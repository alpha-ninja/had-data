                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">This is a UV monitor based around the SI1132 light sensor and an ATMEGA168. It uses a sound-based interface, where beeps signal settings for skin type and use of sunscreen, if any. This is to save the power and cost of a display, and also in recognition that the device will be used outdoors in bright sunlight, where only reflective displays would be usable.<br /><br />SI1132 was chosen as it produces a 'calibrated' UV index and takes up very little space. ATMEGA168 was chosen as a cheap uC with hardware I2C support which is supported by the Arduino environment (to allow a broad range of users to easily modify the code as they see fit).<br /><br />Power management relies primarily on either deep sleep via hardware interrupt or watchdog timer interrupt for sleep-wake-sleep monitoring cycles.<br /><br />The current form factor of the hardware represents a balance between ease of assembly and wearability. Next release will get the size to a minimum and allow for remote sensor.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Some notes on building the board:</p><p>I used 0805 passive parts. They aren't the smallest but they are much easier to place by hand than 0603.</p><p>The MCU and SI1132 present a challenge to me as I don't want to do a solder stencil at the moment and the pads are way to small for placing solder paste by hand. You could just lay a line of paste down along the pads for the ATMEGA168 but I don't like cleaning up the resulting solder bridges.</p><p>What I do instead is:<br>- flux the pads (I use a ChemTool flux pen)<br>- wipe the pads with solder (small chisel soldering tip)<br>- flux the pads again<br>- place the parts (3x reading glasses + 10x loupe)</p><p>Then reflow solder as usual with the rest of the parts on the board. This leaves a little darkened solder flux residue on the board but has been 100% reliable so far.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>
