        <div class="container post-content">
            <p><em>Disclaimer: I wasn't originally going to bother posting this idea, but was convinced to by the recent blog post "We Have a Problem: Food Production". I'm not sure there is enough original innovation in this to warrant a prize, however I wanted to get the idea out there in case it inspires someone else with an even better idea. All I'm doing here is aggregating and leveraging other's innovations.</em></p><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><strong>Background:</strong></p><p>There are two related trends I have observed that I think warrant investigation. </p><p>1. As the recent blog post noted, the last century saw massive improvements in food production capacity thanks to technological innovation, saving billions of lives in the developing world. An emerging criticism of this Green Revolution is reports of decreased nutritional value in high yield crops. See: <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Revolution" target="_blank">http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Revolution</a> :Criticism / Quality of Diet</p><p><a href="http://www.scientificamerican.com/article/soil-depletion-and-nutrition-loss/" target="_blank">http://www.scientificamerican.com/article/soil-depletion-and-nutrition-loss/</a><br></p><p>2. Somewhat more recently, market forces have shifted to make "organic" food wildly popular.  This can be seen in the rise of local farmer's markets nationwide.  For more purely anecdotal evidence, look at the stock price of "Whole Foods Markets" compared to the Dow since 1992.<br></p><p>An interesting twist to this is confusion over the term "organic", and a general (though unproven) perception of increased nutritional value.  "A recent study examined the past 50 years' worth of scientific articles about the nutrient content of organic and conventional foods. The researchers concluded that organically and conventionally produced foodstuffs are not significantly different in their nutrient content."</p><p><a href="http://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-living/nutrition-and-healthy-eating/in-depth/organic-food/art-20043880" target="_blank">http://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-living/nutrition-and-healthy-eating/in-depth/organic-food/art-20043880</a></p><p><strong>The Problem:</strong></p><p>Significant market forces and scientific research have indicated a profound need for more nutritious crops.  The relatively bland labels of "organic" and the even more dubious "natural" have been insufficient at driving innovation towards this end.  Further, it seems that while increasing in popularity, the benefits of buying "organic" seem to be largely subjective for the average shopper.  Either they are philosophically pre-disposed to buying organic and are thus willing to pay the higher price, or they aren't.  </p><p><strong>A Partial Solution:</strong></p><p>Two years ago, I had an idea for a solution.  In a free market economy it is generally true that the cheapest product will win out.  Where more expensive products can compete of course is in quality.  A product will survive or thrive if it can offer a benefit in quality that outweighs its added cost.  While this added benefit can of course be subjective, the effect is more pronounced if it can be easily measured and quantified for the customers.  Most of you I'm sure remember the "Megahertz War" of the 1990s, and later the "Megapixels War" in digital cameras.  From these two prominent examples, I realized that massive market forces can be captured if a particular aspect of quality can be quantified and compared to competitors.  </p><p><strong>My solution then is to provide a method of measuring and quantifying the nutritional content of crops. </strong> The success of Whole Foods and farmers markets proves that people are willing to pay more for food that is merely 'perceived' to be healthier.  How much more will they pay for food that is <em>proven</em> to be healthier?  A local farmer who can grow apples twice as nutrient rich as the supermarket's apples, and who can prove it to his customers in an easily quantifiable way, would have a huge competitive advantage.  </p><p>Currently, crops compete largely on cost and aesthetic appeal.  Thus markets have driven the cultivation of cheap, large, shiny, bright red apples because they "look" better on a store shelf.  This has apparently resulted in a massive decline in the true nutritional content of food crops.  If however crops competed based on nutritional content, market forces would drive innovation to instead focus on the cultivation of nutrient dense foods.  </p><p>In summary:  <em>I do not know how to grow more nutrient rich crops.</em>  However, if I provide farmers and customers the means to measure, quantify, and compete on this aspect, market forces will ensure that someone else figures out how to.  Because whoever figures out how to grow the healthiest food first will make lots of money.  And then all of us can buy healthier fruits and vegetables.  Everybody wins!  </p><p>I would hope these techniques could then be spread to the developing world as well.  </p><p><strong>The Second Problem:</strong><strong></strong><strong></strong><strong></strong></p><p>Until last year, I didn't know how to do this.  Nutrition labels today are quantified by large expensive labs.  The process is intense, and is completely out of reach for the local farmer.  It couldn't just be done once either, each harvest and each field would need to be tested, perhaps multiple times.  They would need a small, cheap device that could test each and every harvest, from different sections of their fields so they could optimize the soil content in every part.  How do you shrink an entire nutrition lab into one small device?</p><p><strong>The Solution - Enter Hackaday Prize 2014:  </strong></p><p>Thanks to last year's competition and the brilliant work of fl@C@ (<a href="http://hackaday.io/flatCat" target="_blank">http://hackaday.io/flatCat</a>), just such a device now exists, the RamanPi.  <a href="http://www.eng.ucy.ac.cy/biaolab/Publications/Theses/UG_Thesis_Christos_Moustakas.pdf" target="_blank">It turns out</a>, we can measure the nutritional content of food using Raman Spectroscopy.  </p><p>Thanks to RamanPi, this technology will soon be far more available to the average farmer or extremely avid green thumb.  </p><p><strong>What Remains - This Project:</strong></p>
