                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1>What is Bone Conduction? <br></h1><p>&quot;Bone conduction is the conduction of sound to the inner ear through the bones of the skull. Bone conduction transmission can be used with individuals with normal or impaired hearing...In use, the speaker is strapped against one of the dome-shaped bone protrusions behind the ear and the sound, which can be surprisingly clear and crisp, seems to come from inside the user&apos;s head.&quot; - Wikipedia</p><p><strong><img src="http://www.swimman.com.au/site_media/uploads/b3f041d7be54ffa5d1c806011a4fbab2.jpg"></strong></p><p>Essentially, bone conduction differs from conventional air conduction in that it completely bypasses the outer and middle ear including the eardrum and associated transmission bones and transmits the vibrations of sound straight to the cochlea for sensing by the auditory nerve. Anyone with an abnormality of the pinna, ear canal, ear drum, or the intricate bones of the inner ear, as is the case in many instances of impaired hearing or deafness, is able to hear the sound transmitted through bone conduction incredibly clearly with no distortion. Users with fully functional auditory systems are able to hear the bone conducted sound just as easily, and the effect is incredibly eerie as the sound or music playing seems to come from inside your own head, you are able to hear your surroundings normally in addition, and no one else can hear what you&apos;re listening to even though it sounds as if there is music playing in the room. It appears to be ghostly phantom music, hence the name.</p><h1>The First Prototype</h1><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2832461434295298055.jpg"></p><p>My first prototype to test the phenomenon uses a cheap bluetooth audio module from china to wirelessly receive sound from a connected device which is then passed through an Adafruit amplifier breakout before going to the bone conduction transducers. After initial tests, the volume was too quiet unamplified, so I added the amplifier behind the circuit board (which barely squeezed in) at max gain to boost the signal to drive the 1W transducers. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4556621434295329456.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9543081434295351291.jpg"></p>The case I designed in Autodesk 123D and is 3D printed in strong, flexible plastic by Shapeways. It has screws on each end for me to attach prototype arms that hold the transducers in different places on the skull and the PH&#x39B;NTOM logo on the front.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/745031434295506756.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9253201434295570423.jpg"></p>After some initial tweaks, it works eerily well. When it turns on, it plays some welcome tones, and then waits for a bluetooth connection. Once connected to my phone, I can control music playback with the on board play/pause, skip, and volume up and down buttons, and when I place the transducers on my ear ridges on the back of my skull, undistorted and surprisingly powerful music surrounds me in stereo, seeming to emanate from within my own head. No one else can hear a thing, and my own ears are open to listen to people talking to me and I would be aware of my surroundings on a bike for example. I named the project PH&#x39B;NTOM as the effect is ghostly and strange, as if the music was played by a phantom orchestra or a trick of the mind.<h1>The Final PH&#x39B;NTOM v2.0<span></span></h1><iframe width="500" height="281" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/k81sQgub8TQ" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="width: 500px; line-height: 22.3999996185303px; height: 281px;"></iframe><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6132441439829430409.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6838081439829450430.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4824651439829540249.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9310101439829551726.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7132871439844047348.jpg"></p><p>Scroll down and see project logs for more details about the final prototype!</p><hr><p><span></span></p><p>What I&apos;m entering into the Hackaday Prize is my next version of the device. As well as upgrading the cheap bluetooth module to a high-quality, feature-rich, and longer range module from <strong>Microchip</strong>, their RN52 bluetooth audio module, I will add stereo microphones with automatic gain adjustment to add hearing aid functionality and the ability to take calls over bluetooth. I will also add my own amplification of the signal to boost volume and allow greater adjustment, and slim the design to better act as a seamless wearable device. I will include a higher capacity battery while keeping size down, and take what I&apos;ve learned about the sound response of the first prototype in different skull locations into account.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9425891434296205078.jpg"></p><p>From the response I&apos;ve received from my first prototype, this device has incredible desirability for child and speech therapy, the deaf and hard of hearing, and certain conditions of the ear canal, auditory bones, or eardrum. So many people I have talked to are very excited...</p>
