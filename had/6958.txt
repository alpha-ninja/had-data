                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The polarization of light carries interesting information about our visual environment of which we are unaware because human vision is virtually insensitive to polarization.  Some animals have evolved the capability to see polarization as a distinct characteristic of light, and rely critically on this sense for navigation and survival.  Many fish, arthropods, and octopuses  use polarization vision as a compass for navigation, to detect water surfaces, and to enhance the detection of prey and predators.  Polarization cameras do exist, but at over $50,000, they are mostly research curiosities that have found few practical uses outside the lab. The DOLPi project  aims to widely open the field of polarization imaging by constructing a very low cost polarimetric camera that can be used to research and develop game-changing applications across a wide range of fields; spanning all the way from environmental monitoring and medical diagnostics, to security and antiterrorism applications. </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The project&apos;s blog is at: <a href="http://www.diyphysics.com/2015/07/27/dolpi-a-low-cost-raspi-based-polarization-camera/">http://www.diyphysics.com/2015/07/27/dolpi-a-low-cost-raspi-based-polarization-camera/</a></p><p>A more complete project description including detailed construction instructions and Python code is available in pdf format at: <a href="http://www.diyphysics.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/DOLPi_Polarimetric_Camera_D_Prutchi_2015.pdf">http://www.diyphysics.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/DOLPi_Polarimetric_Camera_D_Prutchi_2015.pdf</a></p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/SRwqPLOwsa8" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="width: 500px; height: 281px;"></iframe><p>The DOLPi polarization camera is based on a standard Raspberry Pi 2 single-board computer and its dedicated 5MP camera.What makes the DOLPi unique is that the camera sits behind a software-controlled electro-optic polarization modulator, allowing the capture of images through an electronic polarization analyzer.The modulator itself is hacked from a low-cost auto-darkening welding mask filter ($9 each).  In spite of its simplicity, DOLPi produces very high quality polarization images.</p>  <p>This is a first-of-its-kind project!I am not aware of any polarization imager ever presented as an enthusiast-level DIY project, yet it holds truly awesome disruptive power for the development of brand new scientific and commercial applications!</p>  <p><strong>The Polarization Modulator</strong></p>  <p>At the heart of the DOLPi camera is the Electro-Optic (EO) polarization modulator. This assembly, which consists of a liquid-crystal panel and a linear polarizer film selectively passes light at different polarization angles under software control.</p>  <p>The liquid crystal panel used for the DOLPi camera is harvested from a low-cost auto-darkening welding mask filter. These are similar in construction to an LCD display, where the whole filter is a single gigantic &#x201C;pixel&#x201D;. In its intended use, the auto-darkening filter allows the user to see through the filter while setting up the pieces to be welded, but as soon as light sensors detect the welding arc, the filter turns dark to protect the welder&#x2019;s eyes.</p><p><span class="redactor-ie-paste"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9204351438132622205.jpg"></span></p><p><span class="redactor-ie-paste"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8878581438132652645.jpg"></span></p><p>Before being able to use the LCP assembly from the welding filter it is necessary to remove the back-side polarizer film.  It peels off easily after lifting a corner with a sharp hobby knife.The remaining polarizer and LCP now form a voltage-controlled polarization analyzer.Let&#x2019;s call this assembly a &#x201C;VCPA&#x201D; for short.</p>  <p><strong>Driving the VCPA</strong></p>  <p>With no bias voltage applied between the electrodes, the liquid crystal molecules in the LCP align parallel to its windows.In this orientation the liquid crystal panel shifts the polarization of incoming light by 90&#x2070;. However, when 5V are applied to the panel, the liquid crystal molecules align with the electric field and tip perpendicular to the windows. In this state, the polarization of incoming light remains largely unchanged.</p>  <p>The welding mask&#x2019;s LCP can be made to rotate polarization to values between its two extremes at 0&#x2070; and 90&#x2070;. Although it is possible to find a voltage that will set the LCP to rotate light&#x2019;s polarization by around 45&#x2070;, changes in the liquid crystal take place with time, so the polarization state will shift with complex, interacting time constants that are dependent on temperature, age, and other factors. For this reason, the drive amplitude to achieve 45&#x2070; rotation of polarization needs to be periodically recalibrated.</p>  <p>The VCPA in DOLPi is driven with 2 kHz square-wave AC.  The signal is generated by a 555-based square wave oscillator and fed to an AC-coupled amplifier through a voltage-controlled resistor implemented from a simple P-type JFET.  The amplifier boosts the drive signal to a maximum peak-to-peak amplitude of 10V.  The Raspberry Pi controls the amplitude of the drive signal through a MCP4725 digital-to-analog converter.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5558401438132716954.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9006671438132771679.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/340531438133057431.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3398541438133108498.jpg"></p><p>A complete bill of materials and more construction information is available in the document: <a href="http://www.diyphysics.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/DOLPi_Polarimetric_Camera_D_Prutchi_2015.pdf"></a><a href="http://www.diyphysics.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/DOLPi_Polarimetric_Camera_D_Prutchi_2015.pdf"></a><a href="http://www.diyphysics.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/DOLPi_Polarimetric_Camera_D_Prutchi_2015.pdf">http://www.diyphysics.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/DOLPi_Polarimetric_Camera_D_Prutchi_2015.pdf</a></p>    <p><strong>DOLPi Software</strong></p>  <p>After loading required libraries and initializing the Raspberry Pi&#x2019;s ports, the device runs the auto-calibration procedure by sweeping through LCP drive amplitudes between 0V and...</p>

                    <p>My understanding is that the LCP works like a variable phase plate, with the &quot;active&quot; axis nominally set at 45 degrees to the two polarizers in the &quot;welding goggle&quot; application. &#xA0;So at &quot;0&quot; its a 0 wave plate, and no polarization rotation. &#xA0;At &quot;90&quot; it&apos;s a half-wave plate, so 90 degrees of rotation. &#xA0;That would mean that in the middle, it&apos;s a quarter-wave plate, not a 45 degree rotator,&#xA0;so what you are measuring through the final polarizer is either RHC or LHC circular polarization intensity, not the&#xA0;45 degree linear component. &#xA0;This is the reason for the somewhat complicated arrangement of the Bossa Nova Salsa camera cited, which used a fixed quarter wave plate in addition to Two LCP&apos;s. &#xA0;You could confirm this either way using an external polarizer at 45 degrees, but one needs to be careful, as most polarizers for photographic applications include a quarter-wave plate to circularly polarize the light after it passes through the initial linear polarizer to avoid polarization interactions with the various beamsplitter coatings in the camera.<br>I am very interested in whether the LCP is a polarization rotator or variable phase plate, as I have been working a similar concept, but mechanically rotating the filters. &#xA0;A &quot;solid state&quot; solution has a lot of appeal, and even if the LCP is a variable wave plate, the 45 degree linear measurement could be made by a smaller&#xA0;45 degree rotation of the final polarizer. &#xA0;And the RHC measurement in the &quot;between&quot; state then gives you the information to measure all the Stoke&apos;s parameters and completely characterize the polarization state in the image.</p>
