                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21810"><p>I found that the fastest, easiest way to get a rich user interface was to use HTML5. The Raspberry Pi is configures with an internal web browser and kweb is setup to launch in fullscreen mode after startup. This also allows for a smart phone to connect to the web-server externally for future control.</p><p>To make development faster, I am using jQuery Mobile to make the interface. cgi is used to launch python scripts that fire the servos and read the sensors.</p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7019/log/21891-the-most-difficult-part-3d-printing-tolerances">The most difficult part: 3D printing tolerances</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">18 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7019/log/21891#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21891"><p>The most critical part of this machine is the 3D printer mechanics that actually hold and organize the individual pills. I went through several iterations of this before settling on the current version (that I already want to improve). The first version was a snake of sorts that would slide around the base of the box and could align one of the many individual storage boxes above a pill insertion port. The main challenge I had wasn&apos;t getting the pills in but getting them out efficiently.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/692691438303812018.png"></p>I went through a couple iterations of the snake version but couldn&apos;t converge on anything that worked reliably and could make sure the pills never fall out of their containers by accident. <p>The latest version I ended up using for this project is based on a rotating barrel. As you can see below in the blown out view, a barrel (blue) with 16x4 slots rotates in a drum (orange). The drum keeps the pills in their individual slots while it rotates. There are holes in the barrel at the bottom and top. Pills can enter the barrel at the top and doors can open to allow the pill to simply fall out with gravity when selected by doors (pink). The selector at the top (green) slides back and forth to select which of the 4 holes to insert the pill into when loaded.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/419341438304208084.png"></p>The biggest challenge with actually building this is the tolerances in my 3D printer. For example, when I print the circular barrel it actually ends up being more oval shaped. This makes it hard to maintain a tight tolerance from the drum to the barrel to make sure the pills don&apos;t fall out when rotating. Also, the doors at the bottom have to fit perfectly in place to make sure the pills don&apos;t get caught at the joints when the drum is rotating. After it was all printed I ended up having to manually un-warp some of the prints and shave off some of the extra material to get the doors at the bottom to fit perfectly.<p>If you want to explore the design in 3D you can download an STL of the blown out design here: <a href="https://www.dropbox.com/s/slu101yfu7tsvtf/full%20machine%20blown%20out.stl?dl=0">https://www.dropbox.com/s/slu101yfu7tsvtf/full machine blown out.stl?dl=0</a> and a 123D design file here: <a href="https://www.dropbox.com/s/i82wmfc3rblamnl/full_system.123dx?dl=0">https://www.dropbox.com/s/i82wmfc3rblamnl/full_system.123dx?dl=0</a></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7019/log/22274-custom-servo-and-power-board">Custom Servo and Power Board</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">11 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7019/log/22274#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22274"><p>In the original design I wanted to use a custom made optical encoder. I decided to spin my own board for this and figured I might as well integrate regulators and a PWM chip to drive the servos. Below is the board layout. On the side back side in blue are the infrared photodiodes that I originally planned to use as an optical encoder through the rotating barrel. There are 8 Servo connection at the top that drive the motors. There are two regulators: one that powers the servos and one that powers the Raspberry Pi. The regulators can be enabled/disabled to shutdown the Pi and save power. They are separated to avoid brownout on the Pi&apos;s supply when multiple motors are consuming a lot of power.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/423441438901718801.png"></p><p>Here&apos;s the parts list:</p><ul><li>5 RR1206 IR photo diodes</li><li>2 MCP1826 5V regulators</li><li>1 PCA9685 PWM IC</li><li>2 4.7 uF caps</li><li>1 10uF cap</li><li>2 1uF caps</li><li>5 10k pots</li><li>8 220-ohm resistors</li><li>3 10k resistors</li><li>Male headers</li></ul><p>Here&apos;s the associated schematic:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8267711438901644176.png"></p></div>
