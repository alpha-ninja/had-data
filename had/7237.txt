                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">For the past couple years, I Jimmy Locke have been developing a household air quality control device that can be used for scrubbing particulates and volatile organic compounds out of the air.  This invention is of vital importance to households as indoor air quality is often very poor and not regulated by the EPA therefore it is up to the individual to ensure their air is clean.  I originally invented this as a portable cooking ventilation appliance for kitchens that don't have outside venting range hoods. I believe this is the best application so far as the problem is wide spread.<br /><br />As I developed the idea I soon found that there are several other household applications. Besides this being a generic air purifier, it is a great wood dust collector when paired with a dust cyclone as a pre-filter. This also can be used for scrubbing solder or welding fumes.  Ultimately I would like to implement this as an appliance that allows one to smoke indoors without consequence.<br /><br /></div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><strong>What Is This?</strong><br></p><p>This is my invention of an electrostaticly augmented self-induced spray cyclonic wet scrubber. It is a device that purifies the air. These type of devices are typically used in industrial applications like coal fired power plants or garbage incinerators but I strongly believe they need to be implemented into homes in one or more ways. This invention is of vital importance to households as the air quality in households are not regulated by the EPA and it is up to the individual to ensure their air inside is clean.  More often than not, indoor air quality is much worse than outside air due to dust, smoke from cooking, allergens, or VOC&apos;s that leach from building materials.  Serious chronic health conditions can arise because of indoor air quality ranging from asthma, bronchitis, to lung cancer.  My invention would be especially useful in areas like Beijing where smog and PM2.5 air particles are at high levels.  Being an asthmatic myself and having a non-smoker friend recently being diagnosed with lung cancer, this invention is quite personal to me and I find  indoor air quality a very pressing issue to address.<br></p><p> It would be awesome to leverage this technology to build devices such as a centralized unit in homes, or to be sold as a stand alone air filter, or as a kitchen or laboratory recirculating fume hoods, or as an appliance that allows people to smoke indoors comfortably. </p><p><strong>Why Not Use A HEPA?</strong><br></p><p>I chose wet scrubber technology over a typical HEPA filters for three reasons. One because you can theoretically get the same results as a HEPA and not require purchase of expensive filters, just the water and electricity to run it. Second wet scrubber technology can be used for applications where humid or oily particulates need to be cleaned from, ideally the kitchen. And third wet scrubbers can absorb volatile organic compounds or gases that are soluble in water.  </p><p><strong>How Does It Work?</strong><br></p><p>The keys to my invention lies in the geometry of the wet scrubber and the use of electricity to boost its scrubbing power.  My design is very scalable allowing this device to have a small form factor approaching that of a counter top appliance and does not require a huge tower sized structure to be erected to get good performance.</p><p>It operates first by ionizing the incoming particulate laden air via a high voltage charging grid. The air then passes this through a highly turbulent spiral path through scrubbing liquid that is either held to ground potential or charged to the opposite polarity.  The particulates will separate from the air as it impacts the scrubbing liquid.  Fine particles are attached to the scrubbing liquid through means electrostatic attraction.  To scrub VOC&apos;s, the air is passed through an adsorbent bed of activated carbon in a separate stage after the wet scrubber.</p><p><strong>How Does It Perform?</strong></p><p>At the moment my latest prototype can scrub most particles greater than 2um in diameter due to the wet scrubber section.  I&apos;m currently having trouble with the electrostatic charging section shorting out before is effectively pre-charging the particulate laden air.  My end goal is to filter everything above 0.3um particles at a 99% collection efficiency. </p><p>QuarterFinals Video:<br></p><p><iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/paTJLtdfuK8" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><br> </p><p><strong></strong></p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7237/log/23028-my-2min-project-submission-video-testing-results">My 2min Project Submission Video &amp; Testing Results</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7237/log/23028#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/paTJLtdfuK8" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><p>Above is my submission video...</p><p><strong>First Tests of My Latest Prototype</strong><br></p><p>I tested my latest prototype first by filling it with water and turning on the electricity and the vacuum motor.  ZAAAP!! the high voltage electricity applied to the cylindrical charging grids  kept shorting out to the water that the wet scrubber was submerged in and the lights kept flickering in my lab.  This did not bode well....  I had to go plan B and add a wired brush inside the inlet pipe to perform the charging and disconnect the cylindrical charge grids that were inside the hollow walled tube of the wet scrubber.  I was a little sad as plan B does not allow the incoming air much time to be ionized before reaching the wet scrubber nozzles.  </p><p>Moving onwards I turned on the electricity with plan B using the inlet pipe charging brush.  It made a nice satisfying HISS! and I could hear the air being ionized with an electric wind.  At least plan B works, even if it is only a tiny bit.  </p><p>I went on to test my prototype by piping in activated carbon dust into the inlet and placing a paper towel at the exit port to see what got through.  I first created a control sample by bypassing my entire wet scrubber filtration and sucking the carbon dust directly into a paper towel.  This made a very black sample.  </p><p>I then went ahead and tested the wet scrubber without electrical augmentation using the carbon dust and paper towel to sample. For the final test, I turned on both electrical augmentation and wet scrubber. <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9837791439799118334.jpg"></p><p>The data showed that the wet scrubber filtered most of the large particulates, and the electrical augmentation had only a slight effect in capturing more particulates. The samples were not pure white however which was disappointing. The negligible performance of the electrical augmentation was expected to some degree as there was barely any time for the incoming air to ionize traveling at high velocities. Had the original design not shorted perhaps, the cyclonic spiral motion would give a longer path and more time for the air to properly charge.  Furthermore, part of the disappointing performance may have resulted from the fact that putting a paper towel at the exit port places a huge air resistance to the system causing the wet scrubber to not work as efficiently so I may have botched my results by reducing the air flow. </p><p>I performed multiple tests at different speeds. Strangely however, the system seemed to favor lower speeds as the samples for lower speeds seem whiter. That is counter intuitive as higher wet scrubber velocities tend to mean better performance.  This may mean that the method of how I was piping in the carbon dust into the inlet port was flawed.  I simply stuck a pipe into a container containing loose carbon pellets with cloth barrier preventing the large pellets getting sucked in so only the carbon powder would get through. This probably caused greater air resistance than the paper towel and therefore slower speeds simply sucked in less carbon dust than higher speeds. A picture of the jury rigged canister of loose carbon is shown here below.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5710901439800965664.jpg"></p><br><p>I now know where to focus my efforts next time and that is to get the electrostatic booster stage working properly without plan B. There does exist a trade off condition that higher air flow improves the wet scrubber performance due to higher impaction velocities but lower air flow improves the electrostatic charging as it gives more time to ionize particles.  It will be quite a challenge to balance these two factors in my next design. I also must improve upon how I am testing the system so my data is not as confusing and counter intuitive.   </p>

I had abandoned the constraint of keeping the prototypes to a counter top form factor simply for ease of construction. During operation, the entire assembly is placed in scrubbing liquid (water) with the liquid level being above the inward spiraling nozzles.  Note that there must be sufficient number of holes so there is not a great air resistance placed on the blower motor.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2587871439693943836.jpg"></p><p>I planned to insert the ionizing grid inside the hollow walled section of the tube. This allows a cylindrical path for the air to be ionized before it enters the inward spiraling nozzles. To give the incoming particulate laden air more time to ionize, I piped the inlet nozzle tangentially to the inside of the hollow walled tube causing the incoming air to follow a longer cyclonic path before it enters the spiraling nozzles.  </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9795581439793309187.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5087641439793369868.jpg">For the mist eliminator section, I took another slice of the ABS pipe and drilled a bunch of inward spiraling holes into it and flipped it over so it spirals outwards compared to the nozzles at the bottom.  These holes help continue the spiraling motion of the misty air in the inner tube until it exists in an outward spiraling fashion.  </p><p><img style="display: block; margin: auto;" alt="top view of wet scrubber looking into the hollow walled tube" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5477831439793677212.jpg">A bowl like container is placed on top of the inner tube sealing it forcing the misty air to exit the outward spiraling nozzles.  As the misty air exist the outward spiraling nozzles at the top of the hollow walled tube, it will crash into the bowl that is placed over the nozzles, thereby separating the mist from the air.  Note the number of holes on the outward spiraling nozzles must be equal to or greater than that  of then inward spiraling nozzles so to let the misty air exit out the top and not escape out the bottom of the hollow walled tube and bypass the scrubbing action.<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/728991439793817505.jpg">The charge grids had a wire that ran through the air inlet pipe and terminated at a screw on a PVC &quot;T&quot; joint.                             <h2><a href="/project/7237/log/22973-second-pass-redesign-of-wetscrubber-w-prototypes-testing">Second Pass: Redesign of Wetscrubber w/ Prototypes &amp; Testing</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7237/log/22973#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>In 2014 up till today I have been reading and researching best practices in wet scrubber design.  In my next iteration I want my design to have greater air flow without compromising performance.  For my wet scrubber to be effective in removing odor, VOCs (volatile organic compounds), and smoke particles as I have intended, IT NEEDS TO BE ABLE TO REMOVE PARTICULATES THAT ARE LESS THAN 1 MICRON (UM) IN SIZE. Conventional wet scrubbers have a practical limit to the size of particles it can scrub from the air.  The best wet scrubbers can scrub 99% of particles above 1um.  For sub micron particles, another technique is typically used.  ESP&apos;s (Electro Static Precipitators) have excellent performance for sub micron particles, matching that of HEPA.  Wet scrubbers can have also have electrostatic augmentation, which is done by electrifying the dirty air before piping it through wet scrubbers and/or by oppositely charging the scrubbing liquid in the wet scrubber.  I plan to use this technique of electrical augmentation for my enhanced wet scrubber invention.  </p><p>To provide greater air flow without performance loss in my new wet scrubber design I knew I needed the following:</p><ul><li>larger piping</li><li> higher turbulence between the air and scrubbing liquid</li><li>longer contact time</li></ul><p>On August 14th 2014, a design dawned on me how to do just that.  The wet scrubber would be a large hollow walled tube that has an inward spiraling &quot;sieve plate&quot; which is comprised of many nozzles directed at an angle to the tube surface, forming an inward spiral towards the center of the tube.  This tube is submerged in a tank of water (scrubbing liquid) with the water level above the nozzles. The dirty air would be piped into the hollow tube wall and directed through the nozzles into the scrubbing liquid at an angle. The impact of the air hitting the scrubbing liquid separates particles from the air.  As air travels through the nozzles and into the liquid, it will accelerate the scrubbing liquid into a vortex.  As the vortex of liquid accelerates past the nozzles at an ever increasing speed, it will increase the impaction force between the air and scrubbing liquid, thereby increasing the scrubbing effect.  The mist will then spiral upwards and exit the top of the tube.  The cyclonic action caused by the spiraled nozzles allow longer contact time between the scrubbing liquid and the particulate laden air, as the misty air would travel in a longer spiraling path.  The air flow would be limited only by the number of nozzles and tube diameter.  At the top of the tube would be a large dome to act as a mist eliminator.  The particulate laden water would spiral and impact the dome. The droplets, along with the trapped particulates, would fall  back into the pool of scrubbing liquid and the clean air would exit upwards from the tank.  I don&apos;t think this specific design has ever been done before so I may end up writing a patent application.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7652371439685055715.jpg"></p><p>I quickly began to prototype this new idea using CD spindle cases and coffee stirrers<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9322331439682214211.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3492001439684636153.jpg"></p><p>My initial setup made a huge mess as I did not have a splash dome on top of the tube.  This design however worked wonderfully.<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/54341439684751776.jpg"></p><p>I wanted to see if I could make the design smaller so I experimented with a couple other prototypes of different shapes and sizes.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1030021439685621021.jpg"></p><p>I strived to get the device even smaller to maintain a small counter top form factor.<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7598811439685759405.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6478201439685788267.jpg"></p><p>To test the scrubber&apos;s effectiveness, I had it suck up activated carbon dust, and placed a paper towel at the exit port to see what got through.  </p><p><img style="width: 296px; height: 529px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3708261439685891523.jpg" height="529" width="296"></p><p>I bought a compound microscope to see the results.  What I found was astonishing. </p><p>This image below is the control, piping activated carbon dust unfiltered directly into a paper towel.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9902031439689822228.jpg"></p>This is picture below is after passing through the wet scrubber<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9183201439686325548.jpg"></p> This image below is a zoomed in view of the control, piping carbon directly in to a paper towel.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/373291439689982772.jpg"></p>The picture below is a zoomed in view of the sample taken using the wet scrubber setup.  Only the smallest particles remain.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8066831439686344720.jpg">This is an even more zoomed in...</p>
