                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Thanks to HaD, its sponsors, and Ponoko, I've got a $100 custom laser-cut order coming my way!<br /><br />So I thought I'd see if I could make a cartesian-thing, probably a plotter, possibly a DVD-laserer, maybe a PCB-thing if it's strong enough... plausibly a wonky 3D printer... most-likely just a glorified etch-a-sketch with pen-up/down.<br /><br />It's kinda proof-of-concept, really. I'm not much of a mechanical-engineer, but was always fascinated by an old pen-plotter given to me years ago. Years after we parted-ways, I figured-out how they managed to drive two axes with stationarily-mounted motors, pulleys, and cables. I managed to extend that same concept to three-axes to surprising-precision with Legos. And now, on-paper, I'm pretty certain I could even manage four completely independent pulley-driven axes. Kinda cool.<br /><br />Not sure I'll get that far, but four axes could include both pen-up/down and pen-select...</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>While I like the &quot;etch-a-sketch&quot; idea, being hand-drivable, this is definitely intended to be motor-driveable. Part of the goal is to aid in the porting of the motion-control aspects of  <a data-mention-project-id="3828" href="/project/3828">#commonCode (not exclusively for AVRs)</a> over to the PIC32... </p><p>This could be considered a continuation, of sorts, from <a data-mention-project-id="6450" href="/project/6450">#operation: Learn The MIPS (PIC32MX1xx/2xx)</a> which has been a reasonable success; its heartbeat fades based on a timer, and I&apos;ve even managed to finally get the LED pin shared with a pushbutton. In other words: the default &apos;heartbeat&apos; commonCode starting-point/example is now fully-functional on PIC32, which also implies several other commonThings--&apos;tcnter&apos;, &apos;hfmodulation&apos;, &apos;bithandling&apos;--are also functioning on this new MCU architecture. WOOT! The next step is usually the bitbanged-UART &apos;polled_uar/t&apos;, which is really just a tiny extension from heartbeat; using tcnter for precision-timing, and bithandling for... guess. (done and done). After polled_uar/t  I usually begin a new project. </p><p>So, here we are... a new project! Again, this&apos;ll be a porting to PIC32 (aka &apos;abstracting&apos;) of several additional commonThings, including:</p><ul><li>&apos;pwm&apos; (can this be &quot;ported&quot;, really? As-written It&apos;s pretty specific to AVRs&apos; timer/counters)   </li><li>&apos;coordStuff&apos; (32-bit integer-coordinate system for up to 255  axes)</li><li>motorPoxn (designed for DC-motors with an encoder)<ul><li>(I didn&apos;t commonFile &apos;encoderDirect&apos;?!)</li></ul></li><li>polled_uar/t</li></ul><p>At which point I&apos;ll be able to control the positions of the motors via a computer&apos;s serial-port... Later, I might even implement the full motion-system which includes syncrhonization of ramped-axes, arcs, etc... from several other commonThings including:</p><ul><li>sineTable (a lookup table of Sine values, specifically designed for integer-math)</li><li>sineTravel (for arcs and ramping)</li><li>xyTracker (which creates integer-based linear motion along several axes)</li><li>gotoPoxn (which synchronizes motion between motors on up to 255 different axes, regardless of unpredictable/varying loading)</li><li>gotoRamped (which uses sineTravel for ramping/accelerating/decelerating linear motions)</li></ul><p><strong>This</strong> project has taken on a new life as early design-goals were motivated, in part, by limited prototyping-budget. I visualize an entire (tiny) 2-axis Cartesian system requiring little more than:</p><ul><li>a single 7.1in x 7.1in sheet of laser-cut acrylic (or plywood, etc)</li><li>cable (braided fishing-line)</li><li>pins (for laser-cut wheels/pulleys, most likely just snippings from paper-clips)</li><li>and possibly glue (though, ultimately it&apos;d be pretty cool if it was entirely snap-together)</li></ul><p>I visualize a kit, of sorts, distributed in a single 7.1in x 7.1in sheet of material, and a baggie with paper-clips and cable... A &apos;business-card&apos;, maybe?</p><p>A separate add-on could then be snapped to it with motors, etc. for computer-control.<br></p><p>Of course, for the purposes of porting the motion-control system to PIC32, that motor-add-on is necessary early-on ;)<br></p>
                                
                            </p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6892/log/22408-days-of-mushy-brain">days of mushy-brain</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">8 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6892/log/22408#discussion-list">
                                1 comment
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6522751439199459865.png"></p><p>This is the initial-concept for a snap-together drum/driver-pulley... two &quot;forks&quot; cross-over each other and pass-through each layer of lasered-circles. (despite using transparency, the renderings don&apos;t show the forks passing-through the cylinders).</p><p>I&apos;m trying to figure out how to account for material-thickness variances (they only guarantee 10% tolerance)... so I&apos;m thinking &quot;forks&quot; coming from each side with serrated edges that when compressed together will ratchet into ever-tighter compression. It&apos;s frustrating, at this point.</p><p>Also trying to figure out how these pulleys will actually be mounted... so-far I&apos;m thinking two additional small-circles (like the red ones) on either end (outside the green)... these&apos;ll ride inside a cutout mounting-plate on either-side. It&apos;s hokey, and the amount of drag...? yeah. But it&apos;s the best I can think of. Makes hand-driving a bit difficult, having to maneuver your fingers around the mounting-plates... but, again, it&apos;s the best I&apos;ve come up with. Maybe a crank extending from one of the forks on the outside of the mounting-plate, but then there&apos;s torquing on one &quot;fork&quot; which might cause twisting... I&apos;m not opposed to glue, but I&apos;d *like* to not need it in the long-run.</p>Initial calculations suggest the driver/drum pulley can have a <p>relatively small radius and still have the cable physically attached to </p><p>the drum at one point, just a few (ten-ish?) turns are necessary for full-motion, so no need to worry about slippage...</p><p>Anyways, for attaching the motors, I&apos;m visualizing yet another circle with a +-shaped cutout that slides onto the protruding part of the &quot;forks&quot;... But, the + doesn&apos;t really work with a motor-shaft attached in the center... so then a four-hole mate at the bottom (in the image) with longer fork-prongs...? But, then, there&apos;s the serrated/ratchetting thing that was discussed earlier... so, yeah... my brain&apos;s gone to mush again.</p>In reality, this whole project is ridiculous... and more-so trying to do it without a prototype or ten of these various mechanisms. I&apos;ve got a perfectly good inkjet-printer mechanism just waiting to be hacked into a 2-axis system... attach a dremmel and probably could make many of these parts for prototyping...<p> and, yahknow, what was the original purpose of all this, again? I think, somewhere in all this, was the idea &quot;yahknow, it&apos;d be nice to have access to a CNC-machine to make cases for things, etc...&quot; which would certainly be a heck-of-a-lot easier to do off-the-bat with this lasering-order... </p><p>Oh, and, apparently the bit-banged UART receiver isn&apos;t working yet, which is surprising, to say the least, as I/O is clearly working, and precision-timing is clearly working, as well... Seems like that&apos;d&apos;ve worked pretty much right-off-the-bat. Is it the 3.6V signals feeding into the 3.3V Inputs? No, I tried feeding through a diode and a pull-up at 3.3V... so...? And the outcome is *really* weird... it seems to be echoing *several* 0xff&apos;s each time I transmit *one* byte to it. (and raw transmission from it, rather&apos;n an echo, works perfectly fine). So, yeah.</p><p>(WAIT! Is it the fact this system&apos;s so fast...? I might&apos;ve forgotten to increase those timer-comparison integer-sizes on the receiver, as I had to do on the transmitter.... TODO!)<br></p><p>Yeah, this is all as of several days ago, I&apos;m just trying to remind myself the status. </p>
