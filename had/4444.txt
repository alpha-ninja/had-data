                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1>Summary</h1><p>I have developed a small 'box' that let you use solar energy in a very small scale. It powers just a few household items that run 24/7 like the refrigerator, internet modem, security system, laptop, etc.<br>It is modular, scalable, moveable and it connects any inverter, battery, charge controller, solar panel up to 1500W continuous power. It has wifi and hooks into Home Automation. It could also be labeled as <em>Automatic Transfer Switch</em>.</p><p>Project domain: <a href="http://one-fridge-off-the-grid.com/"></a><a href="http://one-fridge-off-the-grid.com" target="_blank">http://one-fridge-off-the-grid.com</a></p><h1>Hardware Design</h1><p>I have gone from bread board proof of concept into actually manufacturing a small run of PCBs. Most of them are at friends houses who help beta-testing the system in combination with various inverters and charge controllers.</p><p>Here is the list of numbers explaining the components (red boxes in the second picture):</p><ol><li>24VDC supply for external hardware with a max draw of 20Watt</li><li>header pins to communicate with other hardware: Serial1 and I2C</li><li>Connector for a Spark Core or Arduino Nano</li><li>Buzzer</li><li>Temperature sensor</li><li>120V to 24V transformer</li><li>Heavy duty 6 AWG terminals to connect to Solar panel, charge controller, battery and inverter</li><li>120V DPDT relay to switch between 2 power sources which are connected at terminals 10</li><li>Power mosfet bank to switch up to 50A at 30VDC</li><li>Terminals for 10A at 120VAC</li></ol><p>The current revision of the board is fitted to a standard enclosure from Polycase.com to make it easy to install.</p><h1>Software Design</h1><p>The basic functionality which was developed for Rev1 runs stable and has been ported to Rev2. This foundation code runs off-line and there is no interaction required. By switching to the Spark Core I entered the world of IoT which is very exciting but also poses some threats to the stability and reliability. I am currently working on a web page displaying live data coming from my unit.</p><p>I am not entirely sure how to make this unit immune to unwanted hacking. Since the beauty of Spark is the remote flashing of the firmware this is also on the other hand the biggest threat. If your power source is at risk you might feel direct impact on your quality of living.</p><h2>Real-time solar power tracking</h2>Here is a <a href="http://solar-trap.com/solarMonitor/">link</a> to the website to access the live data. The graph shows the power generation (green) and consumption (red) for today and yesterday.Next steps<br> <p>I am using the above setup to run my fridge, TV, stereo, laptops and chargers from solar energy and its running stable for more than a year now. I am now working on setting up a crowd-funding campaign which is a completely different set of problems. I also need to look into UL certification because it hooks into the 120V wall outlet in your room and it seems to be a requirement for hardware projects.</p>I will update this page on a regular basis. If you are interested you can take a look at my project blog called '<a href="http://solar-trap.com">One Fridge off the Grid</a>'.<h1>Project Photos</h1><p><img src="http://solar-trap.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/SAM_1261.jpg">Above is the 'mobile' setup which we used at Burning Man to power our camp. The white box in the upper right is the rev 1 controller in a 3D printed case. The generator was only used 2x for powering a large water heater - for preparing gallons of hot tea :)</p><p><img src="http://solar-trap.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/SAM_1334.jpg">This is the rev 1 controller - after returning from Burning Man. A bit dusty but it survived rain and 122F heat! The chip (ATmega2560) was directly soldered onto the PCB.</p><p><img src="http://solar-trap.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/IMG_9441.jpg">This is my current setup which resides in my kitchen. The controller does not sit in the 'fitted' case yet. It powers these items:</p><ul><li>Refrigerator</li><li>TV, DVD, etc.</li><li>Stereo</li><li>Phones, tablets and other small chargers</li><li>Laptops, printer, desk-lamp</li><li>Cable modem, IP phone</li></ul><h1>Next Steps</h1><p>I want to start my own crowd funding campaign to be able to produce this controller for a lot less money. My goal is to replace one coal power plant by taking one million fridges off the grid.<br></p><p>I am currently working with a producer to get a script written and a video filmed.<br></p><p>[UPDATE 7/11/15]</p><p>So the crowd funding campaign<span class="redactor-invisible-space"> did not get the desired funding (only 11%) but I got lots of requests and contacts.
