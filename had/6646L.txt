(Well make that 3.)</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9051631436300653591.png" width="308" height="274" style="width: 308px; height: 274px;"></p><p>"A little trim please" was the last word I heard, but it was all drown out by the buzzing noise of my Dremel tool.  I guess that was a bit too much as the bond wire was ripped out on the right.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9183431436300735652.png" width="313" height="323" style="width: 313px; height: 323px;"></p><p>The next 2 victims was a bit more open minded.                             <h2><a href="/project/6646/log/20652-voltage-threshold-detection">Voltage Threshold Detection</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6646/log/20652#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20652"><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">There is a sweet spot at the efficiency curve for the haptic motor (which unfortunately don't come with any datasheet like <a href="https://catalog.precisionmicrodrives.com/order-parts/product/304-015-4mm-vibration-motor-7mm-type">304-015 from Precision Motor</a> shown below). The trick is to set the threshold of the voltage detection threshold to just above that voltage. You want just enough energy to spin up the motor and let the motor run in the peak efficiency region while it discharges the capacitor.</span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3470191436178408050.png"></span></p><p>This part is used to detect the charge level across the supercap. When the capacitor is charge above the threshold, the rest of the circuit is energized, motors spins around until the capacitor voltage is below threshold again. It would be a boring project for me if I just copy/paste existing designs from <a href="http://library.solarbotics.net/circuits/se_t1.html">here</a>. Instead I am going to design my own from my parts collection.</p><p>I found some old samples of <a href="http://www.ti.com/product/tps3808g15/samplebuy?keyMatch=TPS3808G15DBVR&amp;tisearch=Search-EN-Everything">TPS3808G15DBVR</a> from TI. This is the ideal part for this project as it has a very low quiescent current of 2.4 - 5uA and the threshold is 1.4V.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8140251436225450370.png"></p><p>I like parts with external timing capacitors and external sense pin as building blocks as they give you a lot of flexibility.</p><ul><li>You can sort of increase the threshold <span class="redactor-invisible-space">adding a series resistor (*) to sense input for a one off/prototype designs.</span></li><li>The timing capacitor allow you to control the wait time after reach threshold.</li><li>You can also add external parts to introduce a large hysteresis to allow the capacitor to charge to a much higher voltage about the threshold. This is probably what I would use here.</li></ul><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5550391436275747465.png"></p><p>On the rising edge of Vcc, /RESET is low, part of the current going through R1 is divert into (open drain output) /RESET via R2. This raises the threshold of the circuit. On the fall edge of Vcc from above the threshold, /RESET is open so there is no current through the R2 branch. R1 also act as pull up for driving a logic high to U2. This is how a large hysteresis can be introduced. Because /RESET is open drain, U2 is needed to buffer the output for driving rest of the circuits.</p><p>I'll have to measure the resistance of my U1 to calculate for R1 and R2.</p><p>Note: It is the ratios of the resistor values in the voltage divider that they tightly control, not the absolute values and that can have very large process/parts to parts variation.</p><p>I have some SN74AUC1G08 samples (Vcc=0.8V-2.7V) if my LVC1G08 causes issues operating at low voltages (i.e. out of spec). Or I may go with something similar to my <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5741-emergency-backup-power-and-wallwarts-eliminator/log/17766-undervoltage-shutdown">undervoltage shutdown</a> circuit from my UPS design using the <a href="http://www.ti.com/product/tlc7701/description?keyMatch=tlc7701&amp;tisearch=Search-EN-Everything">TLC7701</a> (which I also found) and eliminating the need for U2.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6646/log/20653-motor-driver">Motor Driver</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6646/log/20653#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20653"><p>While the 74LVC series electrical performance is specified for supply voltages between 1.65V to 3.3V, they can actually operate them below 1.65V. (It becomes the designer's responsibilities for operating them out of specs.) A large number of them can be wired in parallel to reduce the driver impedance at low voltage.</p><p>The traditional bipolar transistor works very well for low voltages for cases when a bridge driver is not needed. For Bristle bots, the motor direction doesn't matter.</p><p>Here is a design I came up with. It is a discrete oscillator with two complementary outputs. The two output can be used to drive a pair of motors <span class="redactor-invisible-space">represented by resistors: R2, R4</span>. The oscillator are buffered by Q2/Q1. By changing the duty cycle of the PWM, one of them will receive more energy than the other and I can use this for steering with the right mechanical arrangement. Since only one motor is on at any time, The current drain is reduced up to a factor of 2. This reduce the losses due to ESR, I^2R.</p><p>By varying the current going into base of Q4/Q3 and capacitors C1/C2, the duty cycle can be changed. In this example: R7 at 200K vs R8 at 220K, the load R4 gets a higher duty cycle i.e. more time/energy. The R7/R8 values can be controlled directly by using a pair of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoresistor">photoresistors</a> or by two controllable current sources.</p><p>The PWM frequency can be increased by reducing the values of the capacitance so that both motors can be spinning at the same time. This unfortunately effect of some components of the vibration may be partially cancelled out.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/643451436320761225.png"></p></div>
                            </div>

The cheap plastic would be very easy to glue stuff on.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2074821436454093463.jpg"></p><p>Too bad that the vibration isn't along the axis of rotation, otherwise a coin Lithium battery, a round tooth brush head and two of these would make Star Ship Enterprise shaped bristlebot.
