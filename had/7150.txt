                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">This is a simple test rig (mostly 3d printed) based on a design by John Walker (<a href='http://www.fourmilab.ch/gravitation/foobar/' target="_blank">http://www.fourmilab.ch/gravitation/foobar/</a>   Gravity is a weak attraction between two objects (beyond that, things get pretty speculative). This "gravity bar" is a rod with lead weights on the ends. It is suspended from a ladder using 6 pound test fishing line. A "daggerboard" on the bottom of the rod sits in water to dampen rotation. <br /><br />The goal is to enable backyard experimenters to examine gravity by providing information, 3d files (maybe kits?) and ideas.<br /><br />An area with minimal vibration and air currents (like my garage) is best for setting up the apparatus. <br /><br />What will be discovered? I really don't know. After letting my test rig sit for 48 hours (allowing the fishing line to stretch and untwist), I used a flashlight to examine the rig. To my surprise, the rig moved when the flashlight was pointed at an end <a href='https://youtu.be/5n_zkesmr-Y' target="_blank">https://youtu.be/5n_zkesmr-Y</a>  I don't believe it's the light--more study required.</div>
                </div>

                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Place the lead weights on the end of the rod--until the rod is balanced. Place water in a plastic cup and be sure that the fin is in the water while the fin and the bar do not touch the cup. </p><p>Let the rig rest for about 36 hours so that the fishing line will untwist and stretch. </p><p>Place the steel balls adjacent to the lead weights as shown. Very slowly (around 4 minutes), the rod will twist and the weights will move close to one another. </p><p>This rig needs to be in a room without a lot of vibration or air currents (garage or basement without climate control works well).</p><p>For more details, go to quarterfinal video:</p><p><a href="http://www.instructables.com/id/Gravity-Bar/">http://www.instructables.com/id/Gravity-Bar/  </a></p><p><br><a href="http://www.instructables.com/id/Gravity-Bar/"></a></p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7150/log/22826-time-lapse-video">Time Lapse Video</a></h2>

                            <p><p>My &quot;Gravity Bar&quot; seems to be bouncing all over the place--lots of movement. To examine this more closely, I needed a way to perform time lapse video, economically if possible. To try this, I used the built in camera on my MacBook and free software called <a href="http://gawker.sourceforge.net/Download.html">Gawker.  </a></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4044541439596838145.jpg"></p><p>My first attempt showed the bar moving around, but coming to a rest near the mass attraction point after about 3 hours.</p><p><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O26_n5i4I1I">Video of coming to rest.  </a></p><p>Next, I moved the steel balls slightly and ran the time lapse again. A nasty electrical storm passed through at 5:45 (watch the clock) and things really bounced around. Is the bouncing due to vibration or electrical noise?</p><p><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fmGlatBzFiM">Second attempt with thunderstorm intervention.</a><br><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O26_n5i4I1I"></a></p><p><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O26_n5i4I1I"></a><br></p><p><a href="http://gawker.sourceforge.net/Download.html"></a></p>
                                
                            </p>
