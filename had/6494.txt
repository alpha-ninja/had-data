                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1>What?</h1><p>Never heard of scanning probe microscopes? Sound like some boring, esoteric piece of lab equipment? Please read my explanation at the bottom first.</p><h1>Why?</h1><p>A great deal of cutting edge research nowadays in many fields, like microelectronics, biology, and material science to name a few, are only possible thanks to scanning probe microscopes. They, along with a handful of other instruments, are the Swiss Army knives of nanotechnology. I believe that a great number of our future achievements as a species will trace back to the fantastical ability of SPMs to precisely measure and manipulate matter on the atomic scale. As a result, I believe that their commoditization would accomplish two great aims: 1) Lower the barrier of entry for development of nanotechnologies. 2) Speed our progress towards the next great technological revolution.</p><h1>How?</h1><p>Scanning probe microscopes are generally extremely expensive instruments found at universities, only operated by crufty ex-industry engineers or twitchy, sleep-deprived PhD candidates. Making them cheap enough to show up on the desks of hackers and makers seems infeasible. But there are good reasons to think it can be done:</p><ol><li>It has been <a href="http://dberard.com/home-built-stm/">done before</a>, <a href="http://www.chemhacker.com/2010/09/chemhackerstm-0-1/">multiple times</a> and by <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20091028073926/http://www.geocities.com/spm_stm/Project.html">different people</a>. But not yet in a robust, reproducible way that would have direct impact in science and engineering.</li><li>Lasers, optics, microprocessors, sensors, and analysis tools are all free or cheap these days. The design of Automatic Jack leans heavily on the great resources that have been brought to bear in mass-manufacturing of high technology goods.</li><li>Modern computer-aided rapid manufacturing makes putting the pieces together much easier and improves the reproducibility of designs.</li></ol><h2>Challenges</h2><ol><li>Touching individual atoms takes structures on the order of individual atoms. Whether they are physical probes or light waves, they need to be infinitesimal.</li><li>To get atomic-scale data from probes takes mechanisms and electronics of great finesse. Atomic force is measured in probe deflections of tens of angstroms and tunneling current is in the range of pico or nano amps.</li><li>A probe interacting with a single atom returning clear data above the noise is an exciting achievement, but to do useful work - imaging or manipulation - the probe must visit other atoms. So it must be moved precisely in subatomic increments.</li><li>Our everyday, macroscopic world may sometimes feel chaotic. But by nanoscopic standards it may as well be considered standing still. Noise is a huge problem in the way of taking measurements of atoms. Without proper isolation any small motion will completely swamp your measurement. Even the truck idling outside your building is too much.</li></ol><h2>Approach</h2><p>These are momentous challenges and I am not a genius able to solve them from first principles, so Automatic Jack can only be possible thanks to a large stack of hacks. The one that started me down the road to this project two years ago when I was taking apart a stack of CD drives that I had pulled out of the trash. In response to reading <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Diamond_Age">a certain book</a> I had spent most of the previous few days devouring any articles I could find related to nanotechnology on Wikipedia and was itching to do a project based on what I had seen. Adapting the precise linear motion of the drives into a micromanipulator eventually failed, but in fiddling with the drives I had an exciting realization. These devices that I got for free from the trash could read millions of ~800 nm dots per second. They were an example of a technology I had read about - confocal microscopy.</p>Optical Drive Confocal Microscopy<p>In a confocal microscope the light used to take the image is sent through optics that make it into a minute spot on the specimen. Then the light is scattered and some of it returns along the same optical path, back towards the light source in the microscope. A beam splitter redirects this light to a sensor. By moving the spot of light across the specimen an image is slowly built up. The advantages in this approach are increased spatial...</p>

                    <h5>Project logs</h5>
                    
                    <ul class="buillogs-list post-content details-content">
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6494/log/21332-voice-coil-motor-driver-built">Voice Coil Motor Driver Built</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6494/log/21332#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The design for the coarse positioning stage for Jack uses two voice coil motors and a quadrature signal from two Michelson interferometers to get the fine positioning stage (piezoelectric scanner) within about 100 nm of a region of interest. By having two positioning stages using different mechanisms for their motion I&#x2019;m hoping to obtain a much larger scanning area than other DIY scanning probe microscopes have achieved in the past. Instead of about 10 square micrometers for a microscope with only piezoelectric positioning, Jack should be able to cover about 1 square centimeter. Whether it&#x2019;s possible to use both stages at the same time to get atomic resolution over the full square centimeter is something I want to find out.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5866581437590071184.jpg"></p><p>To drive the voice coil stage I&#x2019;ve designed this small circuit board. It has at ATxmega32E5 as its brains. I chose an xmega because: 1) At 32 MHz it&apos;s plenty fast for this job. 2) It has a built-in quadrature decoder. 3) I&#x2019;m a big fan of the AVR family but have never tried the xmegas.</p><p>The xmega talks to four LV8498CT voice coil motor driver chips. These are really neat ICs. They are designed for driving focus coils in cameras or phones with cameras. You give them 5 volts and instructions over I2C and they give your coils a precise amount of current up to 150 mA. I figure that they will be able to scan at rates up to a couple of kilohertz, which is good for experimentation but I&#x2019;ll probably need to design my own coil driver so I can go faster in the final microscope and not have to wait a quarter of an hour per scan.</p><p>Assembling the board was a joy, but there was some fear before I started. If you look at the right side of the picture of the assembled board above you&#x2019;ll see the four coil drivers. You might be confused and think, &#x201C;how is that a whole driver IC? Those parts are smaller than the 0603 resistors and caps on the rest of the board!&#x201D; The reason the drivers are so small is that they are in wafer-level packages. This means they are actually tiny slivers of silicon without the usual epoxy packaging (the picture doesn&#x2019;t show it, but they sparkle beautifully in the right light). There are 6 miniscule solder balls on the bottom of each for the connections. I was not sure if OSH Park could even meet the specs for the package, let alone if I could solder them. But as it turns out you can just lay down some flux, forget the solder paste, position ICs with a needle, and go at them with a hot air gun. The little guys align themselves thanks to surface tension. Since the pads are accessible from the edges the tiny errors in the board fab don&#x2019;t matter.</p><p>Next update will come when I write the first version of the firmware for this board and have some coils moving. I&#x2019;ll be at Artisan&#x2019;s Asylum tomorrow night if you&#x2019;re going to the <a href="https://hackaday.io/event/6775-hackaday-boston-meetup">HaD Boston meetup</a> and want to ask me about this project!</p>

                    <p>Thanks! I agree that rigidity and stability are going to be difficult properties to achieve. My strategy for rigidity is to add mass and use flexures cut out of metal or thick plastic to hide material imperfections. I&apos;m also looking into active stabilization, because although it is&#xA0;complicated to design&#xA0;if it works it will&#xA0;make the project much more reproducible (shifting complexity from tricky-to-machine parts into copy-able software &amp; circuitry). For stability I&apos;m going to implement a temperature control system (closed-loop acting on&#xA0;water). And passive&#xA0;vibration cancelling will come from mass &amp; spring (mass from the water used to temp control and spring in the form of an inner tube that the whole system rests on).</p><p>edit: I didn&apos;t recognize your name and then I looked at your project page.
