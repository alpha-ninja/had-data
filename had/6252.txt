                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Capsule endoscopy involves the use of a pill-sized camera platform to image the gastrointestinal tract, particularly in those areas not accessible by conventional endoscopy. Though these devices do not replace gold-standard techniques for diagnosis and treatment of GI problems, they can be a valuable screening tool in cases where conventional approaches might be unsafe, inaccessible, or inconvenient, such as remote areas or communities in underdeveloped countries. Unfortunately, many of the existing platforms are expensive and/or highly proprietary, making access difficult.<br /><br />The goal of this project is to design and build an open-source wireless capsule endoscope with off-the-shelf parts, relatively simple assembly/fab requirements, and as much open-source hardware and software as possible.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The individual systems that make up a pill endoscope is relatively simple:</p>  <ul><li>Communication &#x2013; short-range, low-power, bi-directional (receive control commands, send status and image data).</li><li>Processor &#x2013; configuration, communication and sensors control, and some basic image processing</li><li>Image Sensor(s) &#x2013; the capsule configuration requires 1 sensor at each pole, for a total of two imagers to capture a contiguous view of the GI tract. Some existing systems may use one image sensor and a rotation mechanism or external magnet but that is beyond the immediate scope of this project.</li><li>Lighting &#x2013; the imager requires a light source (a &#x2018;flash&#x2019;) to acquire images inside the body. A small array of white LEDs usually acts as the light source.</li><li>Power &#x2013; power management is a significant challenge and the capsule must last for at least 24-hours on a coin battery source.</li><li>Receiver &#x2013; Any BTLE-enabled device, including smartphones, tablets, or a purpose-built external data recorder.</li></ul>            <p>The most obvious challenge of this project is to fit the above sub-systems inside of the capsule using off-the shelf parts, packages, and affordable PCB technology. Many existing systems will utilize some combination of flex circuitry, high layer count boards, and custom packages/ASICs to address this challenge. Our goal is to do this with standard packages and 4-layer (or less) boards.</p>  <p>The overall capsule dimensions are planned to be 10 mm (dia.) x 30 mm (len.)</p>  <p>Development will proceed approximately as follows:</p>  <ul><li>Phase 1: concept, initial parts selection and research</li><li>Phase 2: Macro-scale prototyping of individual subsystems</li><li>Phase 3: Design of initial real-scale prototype</li><li>Phase 4: Hardware assembly and test</li><li>Phase 5: further hardware/software updates as needed</li></ul>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6252/log/22927-camera-routing-and-system-block-diagram">Camera Routing and System Block Diagram</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6252/log/22927#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Preliminary routing is complete for the camera plate. One of the challenges was in the connector - many off-the-shelf options would have taken up too much space so the solution was to use bare pads and a board notch for structural support.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2593661439670004973.png"></p><p>The camera modules arrived the other day. I&apos;m excited to test these out soon.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9404811439671553801.jpg"></p><p>The final system block diagram had been pending up until now. Previously I intended for all camera control and communications to be handled by a single chip, but the need to buffer image data, combined with the extra IO this would take, made me reconsider.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8170691439670654338.jpg"></p><p>Schematic and layout of the main control board coming next...</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6252/log/22198-so-many-image-sensors-so-little-time">So many image sensors, so little time</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">12 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6252/log/22198#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Unsurprisingly, selection of the image sensor has been one of the more challenging tasks of the initial design process. There&apos;s no shortage of cell phone camera module parts out there for sale, but their interfaces tend to be complex, undocumented, or both. I actually bought some camera modules used in fruit-company-that-will-remain-un-named&apos;s last-gen cell phone but getting it to work might be an entire project&apos;s worth of work by itself.  I&apos;ve been back and forth for some time and, owing to both my relative inexperience with image sensors as well as some other demands on my time, it took a bit to settle on an imager that I think will work (the OV2640). </p><p>I first came across this sensor while looking through <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/1313-openmv" target="_blank">OpenMV</a> (I really like this project) and figured I might start with an imager that has some precedent in the community. These can be bought for ~$5USD or less on eBay. Unfortunately, this also means that the diameter of the capsule will increase to ~14mm, which is pushing the size of something that can be safely swallowed. Still, I would rather get a working prototype than worry about that for now. </p><p>Here&apos;s a preliminary component placement (prior to routing) for the end plate PCB that supports each camera:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3791631438831797460.png"></p>More to come soon.
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6252/log/21301-prototyping-updates">Prototyping updates</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6252/log/21301#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Most of the key subsystems of this project need to be proven out in macro-scale breakout boards before going into the final design. This also given me a good opportunity to double (triple?) check footprints and new components for any issues that could come up later. Three initial boards focus on power supply, power switch, and LED lights.</p><p><strong>Power Supply - TPS61261</strong></p><p>The TPS61261 provides the 3.3V main supply from the ~1.4V-1.55V main battery supply.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5588291437530661979.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3472831437531405262.jpg"></p><p><strong>Power Switch - CT05-1535-J1 Magnetic Reed Switch</strong></p><p>For safety, the capsule needs to be completely sealed. This poses a problem for switching the system off when it&apos;s not in use. One solution is to use a magnetic reed switch. The switch closes in the presence of a magnetic field, allowing the power-down pin on the main supply to be pulled low. Smaller package options exist (note the SW2 footprint on the board below), but these cost close to $20 in small quantities so I won&apos;t be populating them at this time.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6240341437531253753.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7107721437531273060.jpg"></p><p><strong>LED Flash</strong></p><p>Four LEDs provide the &apos;flash&apos; for the cameras. These are small 5 mA white LEDs arranged in a circle approximately the diameter of the final device (10 mm). The camera sensor itself would be mounted in the middle.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5950821437531292102.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3213721437531304781.jpg"></p><p>More updates are forthcoming, including initial image sensor tests.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                    </ul>
