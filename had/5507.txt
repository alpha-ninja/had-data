                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">XORYA is extremely low cost game console that consists of just 1 chip (in its base configuration) - DIP28 integrated circuit PIC32MX170F256B (32-bit MIPS core, 256K flash, 64K data memory) with cost below $5 and a few capacitors and resistors. XORYA is connected to NTSC TV through composite video input and it's running applications created with help of open source XORLib game library ( <a href='http://xorlib.org' target="_blank">http://xorlib.org</a> ) specifically developed for this platform. XORYA may produce a number of black and white video modes (with 640x200 maximum resolution) and (potentially) stereo audio with 15734 Hz sample rate (through PWM). Also with external crystal 14.31818 MHz and 2 more capacitors it may produce color NTSC signal similar to "composite" mode CGA with 16 colors (actually 15, because 2 gray colors look the same) and resolution 160x200. Finally XORYA may receive inputs from up to 10 push buttons (12 if you count RESET and PAUSE). Goal is to get cheap software/hardware platform for "old school" gamedev.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Project is based on <em>"NTSC TV interface"</em> examples from <a href="http://hackaday.io/project/2032-pic32-oscilloscope" target="_blank">http://hackaday.io/project/2032-pic32-oscilloscope</a> by Bruce Land (Cornell University) that is based on examples from <em>"Programming 32-bit Microcontrollers in C: Exploring the PIC32"</em> by Lucio Di Jasio. I switched text mode to my own 8x8 font and eventually dropped support for original 256x200 resolution and focused on newly introduced 320x200 and 640x200 video modes. Adding color to generated NTSC signal was inspired by <a href="http://www.ze.em-net.ne.jp/~kenken/">KenKen's work</a>, but nothing from that software/hardware was used here because goal of my project was not about getting perfect colors, but to get simplest (and cheapest) circuit generating a number of distinguishable colors in NTSC signal. Accidentally I reinvented "composite mode" used in earlier CGA (when every 4 black and white pixels in the row confused NTSC TV to detect different colors depending on pixel pattern) with the same resolution 160x200 and very similar palette (actually 4 palettes of the same 16 colors are supported: 0 - original XORYA palette, 1 - palette similar to CGA composite mode, 2 - palette similar to Tandy composite mode, 3 - palette similar to PCjr composite mode, where "similar" means color phase is shifted back about 30 degrees versus original):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7045141430593523794.jpg"></p><p> To make video memory compatible with CGA I also changed 32-bit DMA-to-SPI to 8-bit (because SPI sends higher bit first and in case of 32-bit it works as big-endian, but it has to be a little one) and surprisingly it did not negatively affect performance (text mode even became 43% faster because now it is not required to mask single bytes in the word to write a character). And before adding project here the progress was reported on <a target="_blank" href="http://dangerousprototypes.com/forum/viewtopic.php?f=56&amp;t=3085&amp;start=34">DangerousPrototypes forum</a>.</p><p>Current schematics (for generating color NTSC signal, no audio or inputs yet):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5791811430705878617.png"></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                <div id="comment-25229-content">
                    <p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">There is a limit with composite TV signals because they were bandwidth limited to get many channels over air. Both NTSC and PAL have a sound sub-carrier that is not much higher than the chroma/luma so the input circuitry in the TV has cut-off filtering.&nbsp;</p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">The old retro computers squeezed as much as possible out of TV's before abandoning them and going to monitors. The last standard that TV like or compatible was CGA. </p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">Compounding these limits, is the fact that you are using a square wave. It is in fact the attenuating effect of the boundaries of the TV's input filtering that are somewhat 'correcting' this generated signal so that the following chroma/luma circuitry can decode it.&nbsp;</p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">The base frequency of a square wave is the reciprocal of it's period. A rectangular wave has two base frequencies. One each that are the reciprocal of twice that active '1' period and also the reciprocal of twice the in-active '0' period.&nbsp;</p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">By changing the duty cycle so that you have a very low or very high duty cycle you are relying on a much higher frequency component that may well me lost in the TV's input filtering. This is the limit. </p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;"><br><br>The advantages of the the circuit here <a href="http://www.ze.em-net.ne.jp/">http://www.ze.em-net.ne.jp/</a>~kenken/en/videogame.html are that by having a simple DAC the output is less square like and more sine like. The second advantage is that chroma and luma can be generated separately.&nbsp;</p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">Your circuit has no chroma control. The resulting chroma from your circuit is simply the number of '1's in each group of four and that is why you have bands between colours.&nbsp;</p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">So 0011 has chroma of 2 and 1100 has chroma of 2 but the four middle bits between the two nibbles&nbsp; (11 11) have the chroma of 4 and hence the bands. At the same time the phase transition is averaged giving a different color. </p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">It would very interesting to test this with PLL and CPLD/FPGA but for me I am going with VESA standards for (x)VGA.&nbsp;</p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">It will very interesting to see how far you can go with horizontal pixel resolution. PAL ('I' and 'BG') has 625 lines interlaced 2:1 ie 312.5 lines per frame offset by one line so it may give reasonable results. Not all of these are visible.&nbsp;</p><p style="transition: transform 1s ease 0s;">Good luck, I will be watching keenly to see how you go. <br></p>
