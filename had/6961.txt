                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">In early 2014, I started working on simple pendulum flow sensors using Arduino micro-controller boards. In 2015 I realized that by turning those into fully functional underwater data loggers I could open up a variety of other environmental monitoring applications.  Instead of optimizing the electronics, I am using a &quot;jumper wires &amp; breakout boards&quot; plan so that any major component can be replaced by several others. This provides physical layout flexibility and avoids component dependencies.  My performance benchmark is at least one full year of operation on three AA batteries and ongoing experiments with sleep, pin powering, buffering, and faster bus speeds are bringing me very close to that goal.  I have several home built PVC housings on long term real-world deployments now and expect to have a generic open source &apos;platform&apos; including all electronics, connectors and underwater enclosures by the end of the year as a low-cost foundation for many future builds.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>As a relative newbie to electronics, I probably don&apos;t have the chops to go up against many of the amazing projects already listed.  But whenever I talk to people who actually do know what they are doing, I hear the same set of comments about how the logger would be vastly more efficient if I went to a custom PCB with a pic micro controller, or that if I used a 3d printer for the housing it would look more professional.  And that&apos;s all great advice if you are trying to bring something to market, but not so good if you want something that you ( and others ) can really tinker with.</p><p>In the spirit of Sheckley&apos;s Minimum man, I wanted something with lowest common denominator flexibility: so that modifications and part replacements don&apos;t require a significant  re-write of the operating code. This means that you can build one with whatever parts you can get your hands on, and I&apos;m betting that even with $2 eBay parts I will reach that one year &apos;good enough&apos; point for serious research. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8488741438277696360.jpg"></p><p>This includes the tools needed for the build: I have worked hard to come up with a robust underwater enclosure that can be assembled with basic tools (ie: a drill, a hack saw &amp; sandpaper)  A good housing is as important as the electronics because environments that are challenging to observe, such as those underwater and underground, are poorly monitored though our society depends on them for vital resources such as ground water. This is especially true in the developing world where even the cheapest commercial equipment  that can withstand these conditions is out of reach. </p><p>Hopefully this basic approach will enable more people around the world to get into the citizen science game - even if they are total beginners like I was when I started this project. <br><br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                    <div class="section section-components">
                        <h5>
                            
                                Components
                            
                        </h5>
                        
                            <ul class="section-component-list">
                                
                                    
    <li class="component-55886">
        <span class="component-number">1</span>
        <span class="component-x">×</span>
        <span class="component-content">
            Any pro-mini style 3.3v Arduino ( $2-$15 )
            <span class="component-description">i'm currently using Rocket ultra boards, but have working builds with many others including some fairly dubious no-name clones. Note that you must use a 3.3 volt Arduino to connect the SD card directly to the pins! Also note that the pin layouts vary from one board to the next so you will have move some jumpers to accommodate that.</span>
       </span>
    </li>

    <li class="component-55887">
        <span class="component-number">1</span>
        <span class="component-x">×</span>
        <span class="component-content">
            MicroSD card adapter for Raspberry Pi ( $1-3 )
            <span class="component-description">I prefer the ones from Adafruit, but just about any one will do. In fact my first few loggers used the little uSD adapters that came free with the cards, but soldering those was tricky.</span>
       </span>
    </li>

    <li class="component-55888">
        <span class="component-number">1</span>
        <span class="component-x">×</span>
        <span class="component-content">
            DS3231 &amp; 4K I2C eeprom RTC module ( $2 )
            <span class="component-description">I prefer the DS3231 over the DS1307's because they have much  less drift over time. But both work with the same libraries.</span>

                    <div class="section section-instructions">
                        <h5>Build instructions</h5>
                        
                        
                            <ul class="post-content section-instructions-list">
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">1</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p>You can find an extensive parts list at:<br></p><p><a href="https://edwardmallon.wordpress.com/2014/10/07/the-diy-data-logger-v2-with-low-power-shut-down-capability/">http://edwardmallon.wordpress.com/2014/10/07/the-diy-data-logger-v2-with-low-power-shut-down-capability/</a><br></p><p>If you go with the cheapest parts, the basic platform can be built for less than $10. But noname clone boards usually have poor voltage regulators, and tend to draw higher sleep currents: shortening your operating lifespan to between 6-9 months. Boards with better regulators (like the MCP1700 series) can almost double that. The same thing applies to sensor modules where better quality Adafruit/Sparkfun boards often draw considerably lower sleep currents than the eBay stuff. But the basic logger works fine across the range of components so you can build one with whatever you can buy locally.</p><p>Here&apos;s a closeup shot of the three basic components laid out in a flat platform configuration (with Rocket Ultra boards): <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7785381438294513506.jpg"></p><p>(Note: the orange wire in this image is for powering the RTC directly from an Arduino pin 7. This is NOT in the wiring diagram above as I am still conducting experiments to see if this trick to lower the sleep current is robust enough to be adopted on all of my loggers. If you want to give it a try you can read more about it on <a href="https://edwardmallon.wordpress.com/2014/05/21/using-a-cheap-3-ds3231-rtc-at24c32-eeprom-from-ebay/" target="_blank">my RTC page</a>.</p><p>The history of my underwater housings:</p><p><a href="https://edwardmallon.wordpress.com/category/a-diy-underwater-housing/">http://edwardmallon.wordpress.com/category/a-diy-underwater-housing/</a></p><p>It&apos;s been a long road, but the new smaller housings can be built with a few straight cuts, a hole saw &amp; some pvc cement. The design can be extended to any length, making room for more electronics and as many batteries as you need for a long deployment. Here is an exploded view, and only the two surfaces indicated need to be sanded smooth to mate with the o-ring. 2&quot; Formufit <a href="http://www.amazon.com/FORMUFIT-F002ECT-WH-4-Table-Furniture-Grade/dp/B00MNIZ9IA/ref=sr_1_10?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1438099998&amp;sr=8-10&amp;keywords=formufit+end+cap+2+inch" target="_blank">table leg caps</a> provide the struts for the bolts and you can find more supplier links in the &quot;New Housing for 2015&quot; post. Total cost runs about $10-12<span class="redactor-invisible-space">.</span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8976991438275673240.jpg"></p><p>While it&apos;s probably not obvious to someone who isn&apos;t a diver, having a freely sliding top cap means that the o-ring will be more compressed at depth, improving the water tight seal. I haven&apos;t had enough spare units yet to determine their absolute limit, but I am confident these housings will survive long duration deployments at significant depth.<br></p></div>
