                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><strong>Build your own Raspberry Pi Weather Station, WeatherPi!</strong></p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3965941433371604813.jpg" style="width: 310px; height: 624.0589198036006px;"><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><strong>By doing this project you will learn:</strong></p><ol><li><strong>How to build a solar powered system<br></strong></li><li><strong>How to design and size the panels and batteries<br></strong></li><li><strong>How to gather data to analyze your system performance<br></strong></li><li><strong>How to wire up a Raspberry Pi to a solar power system<br></strong></li><li><strong>How to safely turn a Raspberry Pi on and off</strong></li><li><strong>Building 3D Printed Parts for WeatherPi</strong></li></ol><p><em>And most importantly, have fun doing it!</em></p><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4824251433371504284.jpeg"></p><p><strong>What is WeatherPi?</strong></p><p>WeatherPi is a solar powered Raspberry Pi WiFi connected weather station designed for Makers by <a href="http://www.switchdoc.com/" rel="nofollow">SwitchDoc Labs</a>. This is a great system to build and tinker with. All of it is modifiable and all source code is included. The most important functions are:</p><ul><li>Senses 20 different environmental values </li><li>Completely Solar Powered </li><li>Has a full database containing history of the environment (MySQL) </li><li>Monitors and reports lots of data on the solar powered system - great for education! </li><li>Self contained and monitored for brownouts and power issues </li><li>Can be modified remotely </li><li>Download your data to crunch it on your PC </li><li>Can be modified to do SMS (Text) messaging, Twitters, webpages and more </li><li>Has an iPad Based Control Panel </li><li>Easy to connect to Twitter, WeatherUnderground, etc</li></ul><p>This project will show you how to build a WiFi Solar Powered Raspberry Pi Weather Station. This project grew out of a number of other projects, including the massive <a href="http://www.switchdoc.com/project-curacao-introduction-part-1/" rel="nofollow">Project Curacao</a>, a solar powered environmental monitoring system deployed on the Caribbean tropical island of Curacao. Project Curacao was written up in an extensive set of articles in MagPi magazine (starting in Issue 18 and continuing through Issue 22).</p><p>The WeatherPi Solar Powered Weather Station is an excellent education project. There are many aspects of this project that can be looked at and analyzed for educational purposes:</p><ul><li>How do solar power systems behave? Limitations and advantages </li><li>Temperature, Wind and Humidity data analysis. </li><li>Shutting down and starting up small computers on solar power </li><li>Add your own sensors for UV, dust and pollen count and light color</li></ul><p>Follow along on updates to the WeatherPi story on <a href="http://www.switchdoc.com./" rel="nofollow">www.switchdoc.com.</a></p>

                    <div class="section section-instructions">
                        <h5>Build instructions</h5>
                        
                        
                            <ul class="post-content section-instructions-list">
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">1</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p><strong>Building the Box</strong></p><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6115191433454108124.JPG" style="width: 199px; height: 265.2127272727273px;"></p><p>As with most projects, we tend to "breadboard" the circuitry before we put it into the enclosure. With WeatherPi, we spread out the parts, wired them up, made sure each of the major paths worked (and of course, took the obligatory nighttime geek shot) and then started placing them in the box, attaching them with screws and posts through the plastic.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9602671433454368357.jpg" style="width: 451px; height: 338.66px;"></p><p>Putting the WeatherPi into the BUD Industries box was pretty straight forward. We chose to put the solar power part of the circuit on top and the Raspberry Pi and the WeatherPiArduino Sensor array in the box bottom. The parts were all placed and then all the screw holes and outside screws were sealed with silicon caulking.</p><p>We used Gland Connectors to run the wires in and out of the box. Then we sealed the Gland Connectors. The Gland Connectors aren't necessarily waterproof, but they make things tighter and provide a good strain relief. We then used a waterproof disconnectable connector to tie into the WeatherRack weather instruments.</p></div>
                                    </li>
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">2</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7884131433372020169.JPG"></p><p>The WeatherPi Block Diagram looks a lot more complicated than it actually is.</p><p>The first thing to notice that the dashed lines are individual boards (WeatherPiArduino and SunAirPlus) which contain a lot of the block diagram and the second thing is that all of the sensors to the left of the diagram plug into the WeatherPiArduino board which simplifies the wiring. Don't be intimidated!</p><p><strong>The Subsystems</strong></p><p>The <em>Power Subsystem </em>of WeatherPi uses a <a href="http://www.switchdoc.com/sunairplus-solar-power-controllerdata-collector/">SunAirPlus</a> Solar Power Controller which handles the solar panels, charging of the battery and then supplies the 5V to the Raspberry Pi and the rest of the system. It also contains sensors that will tell you the current and voltage produced by the Solar Panels and consumed by the batteries and the Raspberry Pi. Gather that Data! More Cowbell! It also contains the hardware watchdog timer and the USB PowerControl that actually shuts off the power to the Raspberry Pi during a brownout event (after the Pi shuts gracefully down under software control).</p><p>The <em>Sensor Subsystem</em> of WeatherPi uses a <a href="http://www.switchdoc.com/weatherpiarduino-bare-board/" rel="nofollow">WeatherPiArduino</a> as the base unit and then plugs in a bunch of optional sensors such as <a href="http://www.switchdoc.com/weatherrack-weather-sensors/" rel="nofollow">wind speed / direction / rain</a>, lightning detection (how cool is that!), inside and outside temperature and humidity.</p><p>The <em>Software Subsystem</em> of WeatherPi runs in Python on the Raspberry Pi. It collects the data, stores in in a MySQL database, builds graphs and does housekeeping and power monitoring.</p></div>
                                    </li>
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">3</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p><strong>What's On the I2C Bus?</strong></p><p>WeatherPi makes extensive use of the I2C bus on the Raspberry Pi.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/704731433454438564.jpg" style=""></p><p>At SwitchDoc Labs, we love data. And we love I2C devices. We like to gather the data using lots of I2C devices on our computers and projects. Project Curacao has a total of 12, WeatherPi has 11 devices and SunRover (a solar powered rover under development at SwitchDoc - you will see it as an Project in fall 2015) will have over 20 and will require one I2C bus just for controlling the motors. We are always running into conflicts with addressing on the I2C device. Since there are no standards, sometimes multiple devices will have the same address, such as 0x70 and you are just out of luck in running both of them on the same I2C bus without a lot of jimmy rigging.</p><p>To get around this addressing problem (and our conflict with an INA3221 and the Inside Humidity Sensor) we added an I2C Bus Multiplexer to the design which allows us to have many more I2C devices on the bus, irregardless of addressing conflicts. Here is our current list of I2C devices in WeatherPi:</p><p>Device:I2C Address</p><p>BMP180 Barometric Pressure:      0x77</p><p>Real Time Clock DS3231:              0x68</p><p>ATC EEPROM:                                0x56</p><p>ADS1015 Analog to Digital Converter:              0x49</p><p>FRAM non-volatile storage:           0x50</p><p>ADS1015 on SunAirPlus:                0x48</p><p>INA3221 3 Channel Voltage/Current Monitor on SunAirPlus:    0x40</p><p>HTU21D-F Humidity Sensor:          0x40</p><p>Embedded Adventures Lightning Detector:    0x03</p><p>AM2315 Outdoor Temp/Humidity: 0x5C</p><p>I2C 4 Channel I2C Bus Mux:          0x73</p>
