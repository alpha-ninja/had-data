                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description"> With advent of reasonably priced photo voltaic solar cells it is possible to generate power at your home. Solar cells generate direct current (DC) not alternating current (AC). Many things in our homes use DC and most everything could. Converting DC from solar cells to AC and then back to DC is inefficient. Many times 50% of the energy is lost through these conversions.<br /><br />This projects objective is to develop an LED lighting system that will not suffer the conversion inefficiencies caused by converting from DC to AC then back to DC. We want the system to be robust enough to allow existing homes to be upgraded cost effectively.</div>

                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1><br></h1><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/a35VsTbJO9I" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><h1>DC LED Lighting System</h1><h2>This project will concentrate on developing a DC LED lighting system that can be retrofitted into existing home wiring systems. Goals of this system are to:</h2><ul><li>Reduce the load from lighting to minimize the solar panels and battery capacity needed.</li><li>Allow the system to be installed using the existing wiring in the home.</li><li>Increase central control over individual fixtures for convenience and energy savings.</li></ul><h1>Background - The Problem</h1><h2>Current Wars</h2><p>More than one hundred years ago when commercial power distribution was just beginning, two inventors Thomas Edison and Nicola Tesla competed to see who could better supply electrical power economically and efficiently.</p><p><span class="font-size-3">Edison picked Direct Current (DC) for his first foray into commercial power distribution and Tesla picked Alternating Current (AC). It was a rancorous public battle; but Tesla was right, and not just by a little bit. AC power has the distinct advantage that its voltage can be easily increased or decreased with a transformer. This allows the voltage to be increased to thousands of volts for long distance transfers and then decreased to a usable voltage at its destination. Really high voltages decrease the power loss in the wires a lot.</span></p><p><span class="font-size-3"><br></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7241901439590207734.jpg"></p><p><span class="font-size-3"><br></span></p><h2>Shocking Voltage</h2><p><span class="font-size-3">The original incandescent lights in the early days required a voltage of over 100 volts to operate effectively. This determined the minimum voltage that was used in homes and businesses. In some countries like the USA the minimum voltage chosen was only about 120 volts, while in Europe and many other places it was approximately 240 volts.</span></p><h2>What our things need</h2><p><span class="font-size-3">So what has changed since the early days when these standards were set? In the typical home what things need AC power and what things need DC?</span></p><p><span class="font-size-3">Some examples:</span></p><ul><li><span class="font-size-3">DC - TV, Computer, Radio and pretty much anything electronic. Currently AC power is converted to low DC voltages in the unit.</span></li><li><span class="font-size-3">AC or DC - Refrigerator, Washer, Fans, and other things with motors could be designed to work with either.</span></li><li><span class="font-size-3">AC - Incandescent, Florescent, and other resistance lighting could be designed to use either but probably won&apos;t.</span></li><li><span class="font-size-3">DC - LEDs used for lighting gain no advantage from AC.</span></li><li><span class="font-size-3">AC or DC -  Electric heating, water heaters, stoves, and other resistance heating. Either could be used but AC has the advantage since a higher voltage is generally desirable to decrease the size of the wires.</span></li></ul><h2>Solar is coming soon</h2><p><span class="font-size-3">As you can see a lot of the things in our homes use or could use lower voltage DC power, by why do we care? The efficiency of solar cells is increasing and the cost is decreasing at an exponential rate. The cost of power from solar will soon be less than the cost of grid power.</span></p><p><span class="font-size-3">If we generate our own power at our homes it doesn&apos;t need to travel long distances. This means stepping it up to high voltages is unnecessary. Solar cells generate DC current at low voltage and a number of cells are wired in series to increase the voltage high enough for practical use.</span></p><p><span class="font-size-3">In a typical home solar system an inverter is used to convert to the higher AC voltage that is standard.  In many places the home owner is paid to supply energy to the grid if they have excess. This has been an incentive for installing solar but it is not sustainable. As more people install solar excess power will be generated by all of them at the same time.</span></p><p><span class="font-size-3">At some point there will not be enough users needing power when it is generated so the power will be wasted. In areas with a significant number of solar installations the net metering payments are significantly decreased or the approval to connect takes a long time.</span></p><p><span class="font-size-3">We will need on-site batteries for our storage and not rely on selling our excess to the power company to really make a difference.</span></p><h2>Conversion is inefficient</h2><p><span class="font-size-3">The conversion process from the high AC voltage to the low DC voltages is generally 80% efficient at best. The greater the difference in the voltage the less efficient it becomes.</span></p><p><span class="font-size-3">This is also true when converting low voltage DC up to the high voltage AC from the solar cells....</span></p>
