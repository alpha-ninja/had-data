                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Space is fun [citation needed]. How about exploring secrets of the cosmos, but without NASA’s budget? <br /><br />Average shooting stars, also known as meteors, are caused by walnut-sized pieces of space rocks left behind by a comet. Observing those fragments as they ignite in Earth’s atmosphere can give us their orbits, which means you know from which part of the Solar System they came from. In cases of very bright meteors (aka fireballs), it is even possible to retrieve a fallen rock! How cool is that?!?<br /><br />The goal of this project is to observe meteors, by a global network of CCTV cameras pointed at the night sky. Each camera is connected to a Raspberry Pi 2 running open-source software for video capture, compression and meteor detection.<br /><br />In other words, with a meteor station attached to your house you’re exploring the Solar System’s formation and evolution!</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>You know we could get hit by a rock from space, right? I bet a large rock would do some serious damage though. Help us find them while they are still up there!</p><p>Every day hundreds of tons of small interplanetary objects enter Earth&#x2019;s atmosphere. These tiny fragments can offer us unique view on Solar System formation and evolution. Yet, most of them remain unobserved.</p><p>The most common effect that these small objects produce when interacting with Earth&#x2019;s atmosphere are meteors - better known as shooting stars. If a lot of meteors can be noticed coming from the same spot in the sky during a period of time, we call that event a meteor shower.</p><p><img alt="" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5010991438273812819.gif" style="float: right; margin: 0px 0px 10px 10px;"></p><p>Besides offering a good backdrop for summer romances, the most popular meteor shower, the Perseids, are also a very interesting source of information about the Solar System. We know that they originate from the comet Swift-Tuttle. During its many passages close to the Sun it lost a considerable amount of small particles on its trajectory, which the Earth regularly passes through every year in mid-August. </p><p>But are there any meteor showers we don&#x2019;t know from where they originate from, a comet or an asteroid? Can these unknown objects potentially hit Earth? The answer to both of these questions is - yes. Can we do something about it then?</p>We can observe. We must see what is happening on the sky and in our interplanetary neighbourhood.<p>With at least 2 of these systems whose cameras are looking at the same volume of the sky, meteor orbits can be calculated. That means you know from which part of the Solar System that meteor came from.</p><p><img alt="Perseids orbits (CMN data)" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4560951438273690288.jpg" style="display: block; margin: auto;"></p><p>Average meteors are caused by walnut-sized pieces of space rock left behind by a comet or were chipped off an asteroid. Meteors pose not threat to us as they completely &#x201C;burn&#x201D; out in the atmosphere, but their orbits can be a very good indicator of something bigger on the way.</p><p>Very bright meteors are called &#x201C;fireballs&#x201D; and sometimes if the conditions are right, and the piece of rock that entered the Earth&#x2019;s atmosphere and caused the fireball is big enough, you can recover a fragment of that rock on the ground (also called meteorite).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8878801438273744724.png"></p><p>Most of the meteorites that are found today were not observed as fireballs, meaning we don&#x2019;t know their orbits, meaning we don&#x2019;t know where they came from. But if you observe the fall and collect the meteorite, you will hold in your hand a piece of a space rock you know EXACTLY where it came from. No need to send an expensive space probe to collect just a few dust samples.</p><p>Now the ugly part - that rock you are holding now had mostly the same orbit as its parent body (a comet or an asteroid). The trouble is that sometimes we have no idea what and where that parent body is, or if it poses any threat to us. That comet could still be somewhere in the Kuiper belt and on a collision course with Earth. Meteor orbits will help us by pointing us where to look for unknown parent bodies.</p>OK, enough fooling around, we can&#x2019;t hide the truth. The REAL goal of this project is to save the world. But by doing that, we can maybe learn something about the Solar System on the way!<hr><h2>Technology platform</h2><p>Current prototype:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7728191439639210379.png"></p><p>Desired system:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8181051439042626538.jpg"></p>
                                
                            </p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6811/log/22167-compression">Compression</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">13 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6811/log/22167#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Nowthat the question of capturing the data with the RPi2 is resolved, aquestion of data compression arises. If you were to save uncompressedvideo on a disk using the current setup (720x576 @ 25FPS), during anaverage night of 12 hours you would end up with &gt;400GB of rawdata.</p><p>Soit is clear that some kind of compression should be used. Usual lossyvideo compression methods are trying to reduce redundancy in videodata to suit human vision. Regarding video meteors, the problem ismore complex - you need to do your best to preserve meteor features(spatial and temporal resolution and pixel intensity) so you can dometeor detection later.</p><p>Usualvideo meteor setups have wide-angle lenses and relatively smallresolution cameras. Our setup has a 64&#xB0;x48&#xB0; field of view and720x576 camera resolution, which gives a plate constant of about 5arc minutes per pixel. If you consider that an average meteor is atabout 100km distance, one pixel corresponds to ~150m at thatdistance. This means that the real position of the meteor is actuallyquite uncertain and you shouldn&#x2019;t compromise the spatial resolutionany further.</p><p>Themethod we use was first developed by Mark Vornhusen for his Skypatrolsoftware, and later extended by Peter Gural for use with <a href="http://www.imo.net/imc2010/talks/Gural.pdf">NASACAMS meteor network</a>.In essence, you are taking 256 raw video frames (10.24s at 25FPS) andsaving only the brightest pixels as you can assume the meteor was thebrightest event in that portion of time. In addition, you track whenthe brightest pixel occurred (coded as an 0-255 intensity whichcorresponds to the frame number), the average pixel values and theirstandard deviation.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8046381438785716536.png"></p><blockquote>compressed image of a fireball</blockquote><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5643931438785404065.gif"></p><blockquote>reconstructed video from compressed image</blockquote><p>Thus you have reduced 256 frames to just 4 images (1:64 compression ratio) which you can use for meteor detection as the standard deviation can help you determine what is a meteor and what is the background.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7992071438785747144.png"></p><blockquote>tresholding (used for detection)</blockquote>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6811/log/21992-pain-with-meteor-networks">Pain with meteor networks</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">17 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6811/log/21992#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Having a $100 meteor station in our hands (check out our <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6811-asteria-network/log/21285-beginnings">first log</a>), we realized there is a broader issue with meteor astronomy that could be solved. Currently, there are many separate meteor networks, often on national levels. Each one uses different combinations of software, data reduction routines and storage format. Paradoxically, data is often shared by amateur networks, while government funded (with public money!) networks keep their data as a hidden treasure.</p><p><img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/fMPNYIKLaTZl9wnwu-wLDpSRNQudep0TcyyBN1QPBv2fKALvSauQ_eX49ww7UM79zKfaSONZ-hULKtaYnO_Y7sZGBLH5PBI6abII4MuJMPm_H6neVCUHJr5AaNAElU6Y0TSS-BU"></p><blockquote>Meteor radiants plotted on a map of the whole sky (SonotaCo and CMN data). Meteor showers are represented by high-density areas of the same color (Perseids are the orange blob at the upper-center).<br></blockquote><p> Regardless of what we humans think, universe doesn&#x2019;t care about national borders. If we were to set out to understand meteors, and through them our Solar System, these limitations cannot get in our way. A truly global network is needed. Such large network raises problems that can only be solved with community effort and open solutions. We are strong proponents of open data, which is still a taboo topic in meteor science.</p><p>As the goal price of a single system is very affordable, we hope that many amateur astronomers, hackers, citizen scientists and students will be interested in the project. The system is envisioned to be very simple to install, use and maintain, with minimal knowledge of astronomy and computer use. We aim to expand that knowledge and introduce people to science and show them the importance of meteor astronomy and foremost - programming.</p><p>We want to provide every high school or university that is interested one or more of these systems, and form a student science team which will work on data collection and processing. As the data will be shared via a central data hub, groups will be encouraged to cooperate with other participants on the project.</p>On the other side, many amateur astronomers buy specialized equipment, expensive all-sky cameras and record meteors for their own satisfaction. We are here to offer a low-priced solution with free software which will enable them to fulfill their satisfactions and yet provide useful data to the community.
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6811/log/21863-to-sbc-or-not-to-sbc">To SBC, or not to SBC...</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">19 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6811/log/21863#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p><span></span>&#x2026; that never was the question. Rather, the question was which SBC shall it be?</p><p> During the last year we have tested quite a few Single Board Computers, as our project has specific requirements. During that time, SBCs have evolved enough to satisfy our needs. In this post, we&#x2019;ll try to chronologically document our experiences with different boards.</p><p><strong>Raspberry Pi B</strong></p><a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/products/model-b/" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5232611438268661181.png" width="297" height="145" alt="" style="width: 297px; height: 145px; float: left; margin: 0px 10px 10px 0px;"></a><p>Being the most popular SBC at the time, it was obvious we had to test it. Unfortunately, RPi still had <a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/forums/viewtopic.php?f=28&amp;t=12097">serious USB issues</a> and fairly limited computing power (700MHz single core CPU, 512MiB RAM). With that hardware, we couldn&#x2019;t reliably capture video with USB frame grabber device nor perform real-time compression. Although there is powerful GPU present (<a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/help/faqs/#performanceSpeed">24 GFLOPs</a>), there was no way to utilize it.</p><p><strong>Banana Pi</strong></p><p><a href="http://www.bananapi.org/p/product.html" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2827411438268277586.png" alt="" width="263" height="164" style="float: right; width: 263px; height: 164px;"></a></p><p> Shortly afterwards, Banana Pi came out. The same price range as RPi B, but with a 1GHz dual core CPU and 1GiB RAM. Also, there was a SATA port for HDD. We thought that it will surely be able to do capture on one core and compression on the other. If we could just get that USB frame grabber to work&#x2026;</p><p><strong>Raspberry Pi 2</strong></p><a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/products/raspberry-pi-2-model-b/" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1233281438268710982.png" alt="" width="253" height="131" style="float: left; width: 253px; height: 131px; margin: 0px 10px 10px 0px;"></a><p>After some time, RPi2 came out. With 900MHz quad core CPU, 1GiB RAM and most importantly a strong community, it caught our attention. This time, we tested it with different USB capture hardware, finally finding an acceptable solution. At that moment software development started and software is growing rapidly ever since. Not only that we had to settle on the capture and compression solution, which would still require us to do detection on a separate machine, but it was possible to completely match capabilities of desktop machines with previous software solutions. Furthermore, we still had enough processing power left to implement new features.</p><p> Same powerful GPU as on RPi B (VideoCore IV) was still used on new board. Turns out that it it&#x2019;s possible <a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/blog/accelerating-fourier-transforms-using-the-gpu/">to do some</a> general purpose computations on it, certainly it&#x2019;s not an easy task. Raspberry Pi Trading: please include OpenCL in next generation RPi! :)</p><p><strong>Odroid C1</strong></p><a href="http://www.hardkernel.com/main/products/prdt_info.php?g_code=G141578608433" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/543361438268861520.png" alt="" width="280" height="214" style="float: right; width: 280px; height: 214px; margin: 0px 0px 10px 10px;"></a><p>Although we were already deep into development with RPi2, we still wanted to test other available hardware. With same price of $35, but 1.5GHz CPU, Odroid C1 definitely was worth a shot. Amount of available RAM is equal as in RPi2 (1 GiB), but divided across two chips.<br></p><p> Surprisingly, our compression timing tests showed that it&#x2019;s actually slower than RPi2 (we&#x2019;re running RPi2 @ 1GHz). We guess that this is caused by different core (A5 versus A7) or RAM frequency. Another issue is that the very moment when RAM fills up over 512 MiB, it suddenly crashed. The problem here is most certainly in 2 RAM chips and bad memory management between them.</p><p><strong>Orange Pi Mini 2</strong></p><a href="http://www.orangepi.org/orangepimini2/index.html" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8930201438269353251.png" alt="" width="252" height="156" style="float: left; width: 252px; height: 156px; margin: 0px 10px 10px 0px;"></a><p>$25 for a quad-core Cortex A7 @ 1.6GHz CPU?!? We had to try it. It crashed as soon as we started our timing tests and CPU core went to 100%. Turns out that placing your finger on SOC is same as touching a hot oven. Coolers didn&#x2019;t help. We didn&#x2019;t found a way to limit the CPU freq. We&#x2019;ll post an update when we get our hands on liquid nitrogen&#x2026;</p><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><span></span><strong>Conclusion</strong></p><p>We decided to use Raspberry Pi 2 as the heart of our station as it ticks every single box on our requirements list. Our software will perform faster on next-gen SBCs, but for now we&#x2019;ll stick with RPi2.</p><p><a href="http://linux-sunxi.org/">Linux-suxi</a> project seems promising, but the <a href="http://linux-sunxi.org/Buying_guide">list of officially supported hardware</a> is quite small, with devices mostly having a higher price and lower processing power. Banana Pi M2 and Banana Pi M3 might be interesting with OpenCL, but community support is nowhere close to the RPi.</p>
                                
                            </p>
