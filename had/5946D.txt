        <div class="container post-content">
            <p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5155041432430433830.jpg"></p><p><strong>Summary</strong><br></p><p>Last year I designed the first iteration of an open source computed tomography (CT) scanner.  It was a lot of fun to design, having many atypical design problems in it, from the mechanical design of the rotary gantry to pairing an appropriate radioisotope source with a modified extra-sensitive radiation sensor. Something about it being essentially a <em>radioactive desktop Stargate that lets you see inside of things</em> also seems to get people very excited, and so I&apos;ve received an eclectic bunch of e-mails asking about the scanner from folks as diverse as radiology professors and biomedical folks to makers to those hoping I&apos;d open up Dr. Jansen&apos;s back-alley CT scans to have a look at some strange bump they have (please go see your doctor!). </p><p>But I feel that for all the excitement, to quote <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surely_You%27re_Joking,_Mr._Feynman!">Feynman</a>, that first open CT design feels a bit like a dog that walks on two legs &#x2014; it&apos;s not that it does it <i>well</i>, it&apos;s that it does it at all that gets people excited. It&apos;s essentially a working model of the <a href="http://clinical.netforum.healthcare.philips.com/global/Explore/White-Papers/CT/Development-of-CT-imaging">first generation of CT scanners</a>, but with a much weaker source, and so it takes a very long time to get even a single slice of an image. I&apos;ve been wondering what it would take to move the idea of a desktop CT scanner from a proof of concept into something that does the job well, or at least substantially better.<span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/144041435559642541.jpg"></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>OpenCT2</strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">The OpenCT2 is my attempt to move the design much closer to the tomographic systems in use today, and make a complete <a href="http://tech.snmjournals.org/content/35/3/115.full" target="_blank">third generation</a> desktop CT scanner that uses a large parallel array of detectors. This should decrease the acquisition time substantially, acquiring low-resolution images on the order of an hour, and tomographic scan times on the order of a day.</span></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/705231439784008467.jpg"></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>Design Goals and Constraints</strong></span></span></p><ul><li><strong>Extremely low intensity source:</strong>  Instead of using an x-ray tube, for safety the system is designed to use a low-intensity radioisotope source.  The 10uCi Barium-133 source used with this system is the most intense radioisotope source that one can purchase without a license in the US.  This constraint makes the design much safer than using an x-ray tube, but it&apos;s also the major limiting factor in acquisition time -- because the source is of such a low intensity, the acquisition times are essentially bound by the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shot_noise" target="_blank">shot noise</a> associated with photon counting.  For those professionals extremely well trained in radiographic safety, with appropriate lab facilities and comfortable modifying open designs, it should in principle be possible to mount a small x-ray tube in the design, decreasing scan times from the order of a day to a few minutes. </li><li><strong>Compact, and in it for the long haul:</strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"> The design should be small, self-contained, and attractively fit on the edge of ones desk. The scan times are limited by physics and safety.</span></li><li><strong>Mechanically simple:</strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"> The tomographic platform is designed to make use of a simple rotating sample tube and single linear axis, instead of a complex rotating gantry.</span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>Modular:</strong>  The three main components of the system (radiation sensors, imaging array, and tomographic platform) are modular -- so whether you&apos;re simply looking for a design for an inexpensive high-energy particle detector, or a tomographic platform to mount your infrared or terahertz system upon, there&apos;s something for scientists and engineers of many interests. </span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>Easy to assemble:  </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">The system is being designed to be capable of being assembled by a keen undergraduate with modest surface mount assembly skills. The modular detectors  generally make use of SOIC ICs and 0603 passives.  To make the tomographic platform as general as possible, it&apos;s designed to be assembled using largely off-the-shelf components, and basic through-hole soldering skills. </span></span></li></ul><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>Contributions</strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><br></span></span></span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">The project has three main contributions: </span></span></span></p><ol><li>An inexpensive modular radiation sensor suitable for low-resolution spatial imaging <em>(nearing completion). </em> These modules have pixel sizes of about 2.5mm square (7.5mm^2 area), and are designed to be placed in close proximity to form one dimensional imaging arrays. The single-quantity BOM for these sensors is about $30, not including assembly. <img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1583361435560325919.jpg"></li><li>A small imaging array, composed of about 16 modular detectors <em>(in progress).</em> The array can be easily connected to a microcontroller, with designs included for interfacing with an Arduino Pro Mini, which  offers a USB-&gt;Serial connection for easy integration into a lab setup. <img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/380541435560339076.jpg"></li><li>A mechanical tomographic platform, suitable for mounting the above imaging array, or any other small imaging system (e.g. infrared, terahertz) of interest by researchers or tomography enthusiasts <em>(in progress).</em><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/847091435560485929.jpg"></li></ol>
