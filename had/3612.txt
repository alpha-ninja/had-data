                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">It´s a really tiny satellite. You can put BASIC code on it and run it in orbit. There is a magnetic field sensor (UPDATE: 9-dof-IMU magnetometer, accelerometer and gyro, all XYZ), magnetic coils for attitude control and a new feature: imaging sensor.<br />You can transmit and receive within the coordinated frequency band (around 437MHz).<br />A simulator will be available soon.<br />After a crowdfunding campaign which will hopefully be succesful, i will offer real hardware in form of a development kit, so you can test your code before putting it into orbit.<br />After having put the first 1U PocketQub satellite into orbit, it´s now time to make You part of the space community.<br />A deployer will release 50-100 of the personal Chipstar-Sats into low earth orbit from the ISS.<br />( Submitted to the 2015HackadayPrize )</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>I submitted the project for the Hackaday Prize 2015.</p><p>But what exactly can a tiny satellite do for the community?</p><p>The CHIPSTAR is a platform - what it´s used for is up to you - you can program it and come up with a concept that may be beneficial for all of us.</p><p>Here is one idea:</p><p>Free communication network: 4 billion people in the world are offline. A satellite network could be a great solution to provide free worldwide communication. Use the CHIPSTAR to develop communication protocols, stationkeeping, intercommunication, routing etc.</p><p>Another point is the accesibility of space launches to a wide range of enthusiasts:</p><p>The costs for a cubesat launch are about 100.000$, a PocketQub reduces them to 30.000$. <br>We are trying to push them down to 1500$  - for one CHIPSTAR, and of course we hope for fundings to make a first launch possible.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

Only 30 lines are sent - one line contains 120 pixels representing 40 blue/green/red pixels each.<br>Now I am working on a converter to melt them together into a RGB image.<br>Transmission time is two seconds on a standard audio line.</p><p>It´s funny: Just holding the handheld radio in front of the PC microphone is enough to receive that picture within 2 seconds<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3144471428431063545.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7017101428473114073.png"></p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/3612/log/15895-reaction-wheel">Reaction Wheel</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/3612/log/15895#discussion-list">
                                1 comment
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>A big change occured in the new board: Instead of three coils there will be one permanent magnet to align the satellite to the earth´s magnetic field.</p><p>This will have the advantage of detumbling and stabililzing the satellite.</p><p>The remaining axis can be controlled by a reaction wheel (mounted on the right side).</p><p>It´s basically a small motor which changes the rotation around the mounted axis.
