                    <div class="description">An affordable open capillary electrophoresis (CE) platform with wireless C⁴D detection. By bringing the cost of a USD $~40, 000 laboratory instrument to under $500, citizen scientists and biohackers will be capable of real world feats such as:<br /><br /> • Continuously measuring anions and cations in hydroponic nutrients/waterways.<br /> • Measuring the nutritional content of food<br /> • DNA sequencing (selective breeding of crop varieties)<br /> • PCR product analysis (low cost biotech teaching)<br /> • Cloud analysis of environmental parameters (community awareness)<br /><br />To show the potential of C⁴Derpillar to solve water-related health issues in the third world, we are building a reference implementation of it as an automated, cloud-connected water testing device, capable of measuring the concentrations of individual anions and cations (including heavy metals) directly from water sources such as rivers.</div>

                            <p><p>The team at C&#x2074;Derpillar have set these goals for the 2015 Hackaday Prize:</p><ul><li>Develop an open-source Capillary Electrophoresis (CE) Capacitively-Coupled Contactless Conductivity Detection (C&#x2074;D) platform for under USD $500.</li><li>Demonstrate the effectiveness of the platform by developing an affordable water testing unit capable of taking automated measurements of nutrient levels in a water system.</li><li>Effectively communicate our use of open-source technology and academic research to contribute back to the open community and establish a public global monitoring network.</li></ul><hr><iframe width="420" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/35-2XdD4sAU" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><p><strong></strong></p><h2>What problem are we solving?</h2><p><a href="http://www.un.org/apps/news/story.asp?NewsID=34150">The United Nations Secretary General Ban Ki-moon states</a> that every year, more people are killed by unsafe water, than all forms of violence, including war. It is thought that the primary source of water pollution is <a href="http://www.un.org/waterforlifedecade/quality.shtml">inadequately managed industrial and agricultural waste</a>. This disproportionately affects developing countries, which are industry-rich but lack the resources to effectively police strict environmental regulations.<br></p><p><a href="http://www.unwater.org/wwd10/downloads/water_quality_human_health.pdf">The Executive Director of the United Nations Environmental Programme concedes</a> that the lack of resources for monitoring and assessment make it difficult to obtain a global picture of water quality. Whilst the technology exists to analyse chemical levels in water, these processes suffer from significant limitations. Traditional techniques, such as ICP-AES and ICP-MS use expensive (USD $50, 000 - $0.5mil), complex machines that require the supervision of an expert operator. These operational requirements make it impossible to obtain regular readings over a wide range of remote monitoring sites, limiting the conclusions we can draw from water quality data.</p><p>The same publication laments that traditional methods lack the spatial and temporal resolution to draw effective conclusions regarding water quality. This information gap is not only an issue in developing countries, as the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_Gold_King_Mine_waste_water_spill">Animas river incident</a> of August, 2015 has shown us. An accidental spill of from an abandoned mine by the US Environmental Protection Agency led to the release of <a href="http://www.denverpost.com/news/ci_28614946/epa-taking-damage-claims-toxic-spill-animas-river">3 million gallons of industrial waste</a> which affected 3 states. The EPA was criticised for delaying the release of an analysis conducted on the water while confusion existed in the community as to the safety of the bright yellow water. Eventually it was revealed that the water contained high levels of arsenic, cadmium and lead. Towns were advised that the water was unfit for consumption, after a large, unknown number of residents unwittingly exposed themselves going about their daily activities. </p><p>These compounds are not easily removed from the body and accumulate; <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_toxicity">causing ailments</a> such as birth defects, intellectual impairments and degenerative diseases like osteoporosis. It is a fundamental belief of our research group that access to potable water sources is an inalienable human right. This coincides with legislative instruments such as the <a href="http://ec.europa.eu/environment/water/water-drink/legislation_en.html">European Union Drinking Water Directive</a>, which mandates water quality parameters for EU member states. Unfortunately, the Directive does not require sources serving less than 50 people to adhere to the guidelines. This is reflective of the high level of resourcing current analysis methods require.</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/w6OdBeG.jpg" width="232" height="236" style="width: 232px; height: 236px;"></p><p>Animas River: before and after.</p><h2>Where does C&#x2074;derpillar fit in?</h2><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span><span class="redactor-invisible-space">By reproducing a ~USD $40, 000 ($12, 000 used) Capillary Electrophoresis device for under USD $500 and implementing several advances from the academic and open source communities, we aim to make it  a simple process for citizen scientists (and academics) to exploit the unique advantages CE offers. We aim to generate high resolution environmental data for the first time and are particularly interested in using big data processing techniques with this high quality data to devise unique solutions to water pollution challenges.</span></p><p>Imagine a system that sent push notifications to warn villagers that their drinking water was contaminated with arsenic from an illegal waste dump, while at the same time drawing...</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6835/log/23409-separation-chemistry">Separation chemistry</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">11 hours ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6835/log/23409#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>tl;dr</p><ul><li>Samples must be dissolved in a separation buffer, which contains 3 primary components dissolved in water.</li><li>Background electrolyte provides stable reference signal for the conductivity detector and does not migrate in an electric field.</li><li>pH buffer resists changes in pH driven by the electrolysis of water at the anode and cathode.</li><li>Chemical modifiers bind to specific analytes, allowing superimposed bands to be resolved.</li><li>Electroosmotic flow is of concern when analysing anions, and can be modulated with cationic surfactants</li></ul><p>When a sample is introduced into the analysis system, it is usually mixed with a separation (or run) buffer. An ideal buffer needs to perform the following functions:</p><ul><li>Maintain solution pH at a level that ionises (gives charge to) analytes of interest.</li><li>Be weakly conductive to facilitate current flow between the electrodes but limit Joule heating within the capillary.</li><li>Provide a stable baseline signal to the detector over the course of a run.</li><li>Facilitate effective separation of analytes of interest.</li></ul><p>These ends can be achieved through the addition of a background electrolyte (BGE), pH buffering agents and optional chemical modifiers.</p><p><strong>Background electrolyte (BGE)</strong></p><p>As explained in <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6835-cderpillar-open-ce-cd/log/22994-what-is-capillary-electrophoresis-ce">Project log: What is Capillary Electrophoresis</a>, capillary electrophoresis separates ions by differences in their migration speed through an electric field. Bands of ions are detected as they cross a wireless conductivity detector at different times.</p><p>In order to minimise interference due to stray capacitance and impurities, a background electrolyte (BGE) is usually included in the separation buffer. BGEs are usually weakly conductive and serve to provide a stable reference signal for the detector. An ideal BGE is an ampholyte [<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphoterism">wiki</a>] &#x2013; a molecule that can contain negative and positive charges, depending on the pH. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histidine">Histidine</a> is commonly used as a BGE in capillary electrophoresis as it exists as a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zwitterion">zwitterion</a> (a form having equal positive and negative charges) at the pHs used in most electrophoresis protocols. This prevents it from being affected by the applied electric field, maintaining a uniform distribution of BGE throughout the capillaries over the run.<br></p><p>Histidine is a relatively safe chemical that can be purchased at low cost, making ideal as a BGE in our system. 250g can be purchased for USD $40, enough for thousands of runs.</p><p>Histidine can be purchased from here:</p><p><a href="http://www.ebay.com/itm/BulkSupplements-Pure-L-Histidine-Base-Powder-/201301522344?var=&amp;hash=item2ede8177a8">http://www.ebay.com/itm/BulkSupplements-Pure-L-Histidine-Base-Powder-/201301522344?var=&amp;hash=item2ede8177a8</a></p><p><strong>pH buffer</strong></p><p>As <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water">electricity is being applied to the water</a>, reactions at the anode and cathodes will generate hydroxyl (OH&#x207B;) and hydronium (H&#x2083;O&#x207A;) ions. The local build-up of these species creates zones of varying pH within the electrolyte. This phenomena significantly affects the reproducibility of readings in capillary electrophoresis and as such, a pH buffering agent [<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_solution">wiki</a>] is usually included in the sample buffer. Buffers are able to resist the addition of acid or base and maintain the solution at a constant pH. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MES_(buffer)">MES</a> finds use in the majority of electrophoresis publications as a pH buffer <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good%27s_buffers">for a few reasons</a>. It is also readily available and inexpensive &#x2013; perfect for us! Using MES as the pH buffer will allow us to adjust the pH from around 5.15 to 7.15. </p><p>MES can be purchased from here:</p><p><a href="http://www.ebay.com/itm/MES-Free-Acid-1-kg-Chemical-for-Molecular-Biology-/161073003402?pt=LH_DefaultDomain_0&amp;hash=item2580b2fb8a">http://www.ebay.com/itm/MES-Free-Acid-1-kg-Chemical-for-Molecular-Biology-/161073003402?pt=LH_DefaultDomain_0&amp;hash=item2580b2fb8a</a></p><p><strong>Chemical Modifiers (optional)</strong></p><p>Sometimes two ions may have similar electrophoretic mobilities, which means that they will migrate down the capillary at very similar speeds. If the length of the capillary is not sufficient, these bands will not separate (resolve), giving a superimposed peak on the detector. It is impossible to identify and quantify analytes of interest if they are part of a superimposed peak, therefore chemical modifiers are sometimes introduced into a separation buffer. Modifiers bind to their targets, usually a particular class of...</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6835/log/22994-what-is-capillary-electrophoresis-ce">What is capillary electrophoresis (CE)?</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6835/log/22994#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>tl;dr</p><ul><li>Conventional water monitoring systems do not provide meaningful information for environmental monitoring and citizen science applications.</li><li>This is because samples usually contain many ions and the sensor cannot &#x2018;see&#x2019; ions individually to measure them.</li><li>A separation procedure can be used with our wireless conductivity detector to measure ions individually.</li><li>Capillary electrophoresis (CE) separates ions based on differences in their electrophoretic mobilities.</li><li>CE is a simple technique that has been in use for decades and simple protocols are available in the literature for most analytes of interest.</li></ul><p>The C&#x2074;D detector used in our design detects the conductivity of almost all dissolved substances, which is used to infer the total dissolved ion concentration. This technique is commonly implemented in applications that only require knowledge of the total dissolved solids [<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_dissolved_solids">wiki</a>] (referred to as TDS or &#x2018;ppm&#x2019;) in a solution, such as in hydroponics, where a user is primarily concerned with aggregate nutrient thresholds for plant growth. When testing the quality of a water supply, we are interested in the levels of particular pollutants that are harmful at small levels, like arsenic. The maximum safe level of arsenic in a water supply is considered to be <a href="http://water.epa.gov/drink/contaminants/basicinformation/arsenic.cfm#What%20are%20EPA%27s%20drinking%20water%20regulations%20for%20arsenic?">~10 &#x3BC;g/L</a>. The traditional TDS-based approach can barely detect a noticeable change in total ion concentration at these levels, let alone single-out arsenic from the jumble of dissolved ions &#x2013; making TDS measurements functionally useless for most environmental monitoring applications.</p><p>Effective detection of specific analytes (arsenic, mercury, pesticides, etc) is best achieved through separation of the sample into its chemical constituents, allowing them to be identified and quantified individually by a single system (the C&#x2074;D detector). </p><p><strong>Separation techniques</strong> </p><p><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_process#List_of_separation_techniques">Separation techniques</a> are not new and usually rely upon variations in a physical property among the ions of a solution. The separation procedure employed in this build is capillary electrophoresis (CE) [<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capillary_electrophoresis">wiki</a>]. CE sorts chemicals by differences in <a href="http://apps.who.int/phint/en/p/docf/">electrophoretic mobility</a>, a property unique to each molecule. This is accomplished by drawing the sample into a very thin (10-100&#x3BC;m) ID <a href="http://www.wisegeek.org/what-is-a-capillary-tube.htm">capillary tube</a> and subjecting it to high voltage (2-30kV) DC. The capillary serves to create an environment with a high surface area/volume ratio, allowing more interactions that facilitate the separation to take place. Separation occurs to a point where ions migrate in independent &#x2018;bands&#x2019; sequentially past a stationary detector, enabling them to be identified and measured.</p><p> <img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7509371439709735800.png" style="width: 526px; height: 311px;" width="526" height="311"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></p><p>Adopted from: <a href="http://theses.ulaval.ca/archimede/fichiers/24330/apa.html">http://theses.ulaval.ca/archimede/fichiers/24330/apa.html</a></p><p><br>The above image represents the expected migration order of ions inside a capillary when it is exposed to an electric field. Note that small, highly charged ions migrate to the electrodes at a higher rate than larger ions with less charge. Anions migrate toward the anode, while cations migrate towards the cathode.<br></p><p>The signal intensity of the C&#x2074;D detector enclosing the capillary tube can be charted over time to identify and measure the concentrations of individual ions with high repeatability and precision. This can be represented on a graph as an electropherogram. Each peak represents an analyte passing the detector and its magnitude is proportional to the concentration of each substance. Peaks are assigned to a particular analyte by comparing readings to a separation conducted on a standard solution of known constitution. An example electropherogram is shown below:</p><p><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/eGoqveAlFDQeRmpYkKDb2KFQNRA96-Bqnufo_UODnBlyJq940t5CLPW6UpTJMcERGtKOyhbj8PIsEBJWOdrNBdrkSb57nJ4qFwd3X7AI9IX9One9Ek_4ppJns-30BmJvMIbGCoY"></p><p>Source: <a href="http://www.dionex.com/en-us/webdocs/4234-an91.pdf">http://www.dionex.com/en-us/webdocs/4234-an91.pdf</a></p><p>So what is actually making this happen in there? As with standard spherical particles, the velocity of charged ions moving in an electric field is the proportional to of the electrokinetic energy introduced to the particleFEK by the electric field <strong>E</strong> and the particle&#x2019;s drag <strong>Fd</strong> in a viscous solution (<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_law">Stoke&#x2019;s law</a>).</p><p></li></ul>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6835/log/22684-conductivity-detection-cd">Conductivity Detection: C⁴D</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6835/log/22684#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><h2>Capacitively Coupled Contactless Conductivity Detection C&#x2074;D</h2><p>tl;dr</p><ul><li>Electrolytic conductivity can measure the dissolved contents of a solution</li><li>Combined with Capillary Electrophoresis (CE) individual ions can be detected</li><li>Contact with the solution hindered the development of conductivity detection based CE</li><li>The expense and inherent limitations of light-based (UV) detection is prohibitive for amateurs </li><li>Capacitively-Coupled Contactless Conductivity Detection (C&#x2074;D) provides a simple, low-cost detection method </li><li>Our implementation of C&#x2074;D</li></ul><hr><p>The detection technique we have chosen for this project is based on the measurement of Electrolytic Conductivity [<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conductivity_(electrolytic)">wiki</a>], which is a solution&apos;s ability to conduct electricity. This measure contains highly valuable information on the contents of a solution as its conductivity is proportional to the sum of the Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) [<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_dissolved_solids">wiki</a>]. Measurement of TDS is typically used as an aggregate indicator of chemical contaminants in the study of water quality, particularly relating to agricultural runoff and industrial pollution. Our system utilises this principle in combination with a separation technique to detect specific substances within the TDS. We apply a technique called<a href="http://chemwiki.ucdavis.edu/Analytical_Chemistry/Instrumental_Analysis/Capillary_Electrophoresis"> Capillary Electrophoresis</a> (CE) [<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capillary_electrophoresis">wiki</a>] which separates the solution into a series of ionic bands that migrate past a stationary detector at varying times, allowing for individual substances to be identified and quantified.</p><p>Conductivity detection has been employed in CE and chromatography for some time however the initial iterations employed a contact-based approach, typically a fixed eC/TDS probe, which came into direct contact with the solution. While this technique is highly sensitive the electrode is subject to degradation, this causes measurement drift due to heterogeneous oxidation of the electrode surface which diminishes the machine&#x2019;s precision and operational lifespan.The full potential of the technology was realised with the introduction of contactless detection systems, which led to the development of the technology we have chosen: Capacitively-Coupled Contactless Conductivity Detection (C&#x2074;D). C&#x2074;D utilises the wall of the capillary as a dielectric to wirelessly transmit an electrical signal through the solution which is attenuated by the change in resistance due to the ion concentration. </p><p><img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/_WbvANaUCMbtx-jjTEZvdnZWu-EchOqApUzgWBbpDNR7MMWj2r8r_sfXPeo_iCQ8J-oTSeVEbFAL7_30STWjzJwQy9mhWd6Em2PYrTEDYp1H0wZMFCJGkbfMjHDw9QkDgIQbk6g"></p><p>The C&#x2074;D technique allows for a much simpler transmission and receipt of the excitation signal over typical light (UV) and contact-based (TDS) detection, without expensive image sensors and complex capillary windows. The versatility of using C&#x2074;D with the CE separated samples means that by manipulating only a few simple parameters of the system almost any substance can be identified and measured. This replicates the function of most Ion Specific Electrodes (ISEs) by utilising a separation technique to get the desired solute by itself, in place of specific electrodes for each solute. The relative simplicity of this technique makes it a highly versatile yet low-cost way to detect the presence of substances in a sample that is highly suited to automated sampling and reporting of water quality.</p><p>The measurement of electrolytic conductivity is achieved by measuring the resistance of the solution. Measuring the resistance involves passing an electrical signal through the solution and measuring the attenuation of the signal as different ions pass the electrodes. Contactless detection in a Capillary Electrophoresis (CE) systems couples electrodes to the capillary forming a picofarad capacitor, which requires a high frequency input signal to transmit current through the solution. As ions pass through the electrode the change in resistance of the solution R causes a change in the resultant current I=V/R. The current of the output signal is converted to a voltage by a transimpedance op-amp (current-voltage conversion) which is then synchronously demodulated into a DC voltage, cleaned of noise and sampled...</p>
