                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">A box that reads online newspapers to blind people through TTS. Navigation (select, back, next, previous) is through a series of buttons. Headlines are read from an RSS feed, articles themselves are retrieved from the link embedded in the RSS feed. The trick is to clean up the HTML so that only the actual article content is output and not the plethora of navigation links and other stuff that they eye would simply glance over when an article is rendered in a browser.<br />Note that the latest version of Doug Gore's port of the Android TTS engine to the Raspi has been cleaned of some copyrighted files and needs some work before it becomes fully operational. An earlier version works fine, though.<br />The goal for the next few months is to document the build and expand the number of papers that can be read.<br />As a little extra gadget the reader speaks out the air pressure when starts up. The current user, like many other elderly people, suffers under the weather and likes to track the pressure.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The first prototype uses a BananaPi with three navigation buttons in a repurposed Amazon cardboard box. The Raspi B+ was running near max CPU capacity with the TTS engine so I switched to th eBPi. However, the Raspi 2 that has since been released should have enough power to handle this. </p><p>A headsets turn out to be the best audio output. Initially a speaker was installed in the box but the sound quality of the speaker combined with the sometimes hard to understand TTS was too inconvenient for the test user. An inexpensive 10 Euro Phillips<span class="redactor-invisible-space"> headset from Media Markt turns out to be the best solution</span>. A rotary encoder is used to adjust the volume. </p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>
