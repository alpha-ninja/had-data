                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The cutting-edge microelectronic components of the 2010&apos;s are generally smaller and more powerful.<br /><br />These new chips feature an exposed metal pad for heat output. Commonly the metal pad is soldered to copper layers in the PCB for cooling. This technique is limited in power handling. In most cases chips generating more than 1 Watt of heat can be problematic. <br /><br />Alternatively: The PowerPeg thermal connector solders directly to the component through a plated hole in the PCB. A variety of heatsinks and coolers can be attached after soldering. This two-part system provides limitless possibilities for cooling. <br /><br />Electronics are higher performing, longer lasting, and more energy efficient when properly cooled.<br /><br />The goal of this project is to bring PowerPeg to mainstream electronics design, and provide off-the-shelf cooling accessories.</div>

                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><strong>A brief history</strong><br></p><p>PowerPeg thermalmanagement was conceived in September of 2011. I was in the processof engineering a circuit for digital control of a stepper motor withthe <a href="http://www.ti.com/product/drv8811" target="_blank">DRV8811</a> integrated circuit from Texas Instruments. I was almostdone reading the documentation, and ready to move on to PCB designwhen I stumbled upon <a href="http://www.avayanelectronics.com/" target="_blank">Avayan Electronics</a> on the web. I was pleased tofind a wealth of information about stepper motors, driver chips, andcomplete open-source schematics. The circuit I was about to designwas already complete...</p><p>The <a href="http://www.avayanelectronics.com/Products/AE-STPR8811/ae-stpr8811.html" target="_blank">AE-STPR8811</a> is aprofessionally designed stepper driver module by <a href="https://hackaday.io/avayan" target="_blank">Jose Quinones</a> atAvayan Electronics. I got in contact with Jose to talk about thedesign. To my surprise I learned that despite the 2.5 Amps advertisedby TI, Jose&apos;s design is only capable of delivering approximately 1.6Amps. The tiny chip overheats unless proper thermal management isprovided. Jose did a great job of designing the PCB to spread heatusing the ground plane, and over 100 vias. The bottom line is:the PCB simply did not have enough surface area. </p><p>So Jose and I begancollaborating on an improved heatsink for his PCB. Originally Idesigned a heatsink made of solid copper complete with air fins onone side, and a round post on the other end. The idea was tofabricate a PCB with a hole located beneath the IC. The post wouldsolder directly to the under-side of the chip through the hole. Solidcopper did a fantastic job at transferring the heat direct to the airfins, and the DRV8811 could operate at it&apos;s fullest potential.</p><p>We ran into aproblem, though. Jose explained he could not solder the large copperheatsink directly to the PCB. Heatsinks function to spread heat away,while the soldering process requires rapid heating of the PCB.</p><p>So I went back tothe drawing board to design a two-part heatsink. A small thermalconnector is <a href="https://youtu.be/FxO8Opb7cVs" target="_blank">easily soldered</a> into the PCB. Then a large,high-performing heatsink can be attached after soldering. <em><strong>PowerPegThermal Management</strong> </em>was born.</p><p>After re-designing the PCB to include a PowerPeg heatsink, the driver was able to operate safely at <a href="http://ebldc.com/?p=464" target="_blank">3 Amps</a>. Since then, Jose went on to integrate the PowerPeg with his entire line of <a href="http://www.avayanelectronics.com/Products/AE-CNC8825-TP/ae-cnc8825-tp.html" target="_blank">CNC products</a>.</p><p><strong>Progress</strong></p><p>Since 2011 I have centered my career around PowerPeg. </p><p>In 2014 I was awarded <a href="https://www.google.com/patents/US8929077" target="_blank">US Patent 8929077</a> for my thermal connector.</p><p>I work full time as Lead Engineer and CEO at <a href="http://tem-products.com/index.php/aboutus.html/" target="_blank">TEM Products Inc</a>. I&apos;ve partnered with a nearby factory where we produce mid-sized production batches of the TEM products on a limited budget.</p><p>Today several quality components are available in our web store. I have one size of thermal connector in stock, and three matching coolers to choose from. Ultimately there will be many thermal connectors to choose from based on the component, and many coolers to choose from based on the amount of heat. </p><p> If I win the Hackaday 2015 prize I will use the money to modernize our factory and make PowerPeg Thermal Management available through mainstream distribution.</p><p>I am motivated to make PowerPeg the best it can be because thermal management plays a big role in energy efficiency, and overall quality of electronics.</p>
