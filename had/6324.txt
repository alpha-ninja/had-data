        <meta name="twitter:description" content="This project is designed to allow anyone, anywhere to have a modular, aesthetically pleasing, and simple device to grow food in their own kitchen.

Having long enjoyed growing my own food I searched for a commercial unit to allow me to grow vegetables , herbs and micro-greens/sprouts year round.  I found commercial products to  significantly lack the control and enclosed-nature I desired to grow what I want, when I want without buying their proprietary seeds/pods/modules.  

It uses a peltier driven wine cooler into a grow chamber and powered it with a simple Arduino for control.  The LCD displays the relevant parameters (or alarms) at a glance.

The unit can control the temp, monitor humidity and soil moisture for any plants.  A simple LED spotlight provides sufficient light.

Hopefully this open-source build will inspire others to grow their own food at home.  Perhaps this will allow anyone to achieve some self-reliance for a brighter future regardless of where they may be.">

                    <div class="description">This project is designed to allow anyone, anywhere to have a modular, aesthetically pleasing, and simple device to grow food in their own kitchen.<br /><br />Having long enjoyed growing my own food I searched for a commercial unit to allow me to grow vegetables , herbs and micro-greens/sprouts year round.  I found commercial products to  significantly lack the control and enclosed-nature I desired to grow what I want, when I want without buying their proprietary seeds/pods/modules.  <br /><br />It uses a peltier driven wine cooler into a grow chamber and powered it with a simple Arduino for control.  The LCD displays the relevant parameters (or alarms) at a glance.<br /><br />The unit can control the temp, monitor humidity and soil moisture for any plants.  A simple LED spotlight provides sufficient light.<br /><br />Hopefully this open-source build will inspire others to grow their own food at home.  Perhaps this will allow anyone to achieve some self-reliance for a brighter future regardless of where they may be.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Full video overview:</p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/YZIATF9IRiY" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><p>I have always wanted to grow my own food indoors but our Canadian winters make it difficult. I tried a couple basic DIY methods and then graduated to the AeroGarden commercial counter-top unit. They have proprietary CF (compact fluorescent)  lighting, no climate control as they are only open air and rely on you buying their proprietary &quot;seed pods&quot;. The results were less than I expected...</p><p>I decided there must be a better way and in the fall of 2014 I began creating my own counter-top garden. I simply took the perfectly enclosed (and insulated) wine cooler and controlled it via a basic Arduino.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3645261434434117013.jpg" width="443" height="594" style="width: 443px; height: 594px;"></p><p>This unit allows for any grow media to be used, all parameters such as temp, lighting etc are controllable. No fancy chemicals/fertilizers are required. Just Some counter space in your kitchen or any other room in your home.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5045911439003618221.jpg" width="442" height="332" style="width: 442px; height: 332px;"></p><p>The commercial units I have used in the past require you to place them in an unused room as the light spillage is INSANELY bright.  With this enclosure and the tinted glass door- most of the light remains contained within the unit- less waste and no annoyance.  This was exactly what I wanted.</p><p> The initial LED lights I used for test worked far better than anticipated considering they were never designed for &quot;grow lights&quot; .  These can easily be changed for any commercial &quot;grow&quot; lights which have many colors/wavelengths.  I won&apos;t be changing mine as they are working too well currently.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7852841434433913665.jpg" width="478" height="360" style="width: 478px; height: 360px;"></p><p>Through the winter of 2014/2015 I have tested multiple grows with no issues whatsoever.  In fact- I can&apos;t seem to get rid of the original basil plant I used for testing :)</p><p>The basil plant is NOT the best form factor.  The best results seem to be from trays which you can plant a variety of greens and herbs.  Using radish, chives, mustard greens and many more mixed make for a fantastic salad while maintaining a high density of growth in the chamber.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8132821434434019283.JPG" width="498" height="411" style="width: 498px; height: 411px;"></p><p style="margin-left: 240px;">Here is lettuce at 10 days old:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9047451439831119054.jpg" width="566" height="426" style="width: 566px; height: 426px;"></p><p>I wanted this setup to appear &quot;at home&quot; in any kitchen.  By using the already somewhat &quot;classy&quot; design for wine coolers I believe I was able to hit that target.  I really like the look of the unit.  Somewhat sleek and unique while still at home in any kitchen.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4646721434434169655.JPG"></p><p>This project is released<strong> fully</strong> open-source in hopes others will take the design and code to further the self-sustaining food production truly needed for a sustainable future.</p><p>Here is the Urban Garden live on the Adafruit Show and Tell show (aired Feb 2015).   Fast forward to <strong>8:09</strong> for my appearance since the direct link doesn&apos;t work:</p><p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/ViQ3sIayOfE" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br></p>

                    <div class="section section-instructions">
                        <h5>Build instructions</h5>
                        
                        
                            <ul class="post-content section-instructions-list">
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">1</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p>To recreate your own counter-top Arduino controlled garden the process is actually quite simple.</p><p>1.  Procure a wine cooler.  These Peltier cooled units can often be found on classifieds or at second-hand stores for a very reasonable cost.  Here are just some of the types you can get. You can even use a compressor style cooler with the same control circuit/code I have provided just the relay connection will be slightly different.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3516391438132282397.JPG"></p><p>If you would like to grow taller plants like dwarf tomatoes, large herbs, peppers etc- get the same tall &amp; slim style cooler you see I have used.</p><p>If you would like to grow more &quot;greens&quot; like lettuces etc- I would suggest getting one of the lower &amp; wider units as this will maximize your grow area for that crop. Something like this one:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7365841438132555749.JPG"></p><p> It is totally up to you!  Now on to the modification!</p></div>
                                    </li>
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">2</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p>Disconnect all the on-board control equipment PCB&apos;s etc . For cooling only function (using the code to be shared here) you need only to re-use the 12V fans (usually 2) and the peltier.</p><p>This is quite easy.  In the back of the units you will find the wiring for the 2 fans (internal and external) as well as the TEC device (peltier junction which cools and can also heat).</p><p>At the end you should have 3 positive wires and 3 negative wires. like these:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3371011438132859491.JPG"></p>Let&apos;s hook them up!</div>
                                    </li>
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">3</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p>3. Connect the N.O. (normally open) relay output terminal to the 2 fans + and petier +.  These are both hooked to a single terminal as we use them all at once for control.  The other side of the N.O. connection is hooked to B+ from your external 12V power supply.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7790251438133101842.JPG"></p>When done yours make look a bit like mine here: (you may be using different wires etc of course)<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8509281438133173819.JPG"></p>The large wire is just &quot;daisy chaining&quot; power from one relay to the other to simplify splices etc.<p>Connect the negative leads from your fans and peltier to the negaive side of your 12V power supply.</p></div>
                                    </li>
