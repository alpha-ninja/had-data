                            <h2><a href="/project/7254/log/22933-initial-inspiration-and-statement-of-purpouse">Initial inspiration and statement of purpouse</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7254/log/22933#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22933"><p>This project has been in the back of my mind since the bush administration.  Thanks to some very bad typing habits I have mild carpal tunnel syndrome in my right hand.  My left hand has been spared so the idea of having a keyboard that can offload the work to the undamaged hand is a compelling one.  </p><p>The main problem with most one-handed chorded keyboards is the lack of a way to move a mouse.  Most designs (including the original Engelbart model from the Mother of All Demos) assume one hand for the keyer and one hand for the mouse.  Both are less useful for people who only want to use one hand for both or be using the keyer to control a mobile platform.  </p><p>The first idea was a simple one; Add a thumbstick like you find on modern game controllers and have that be used to control the mouse.  The second idea is the big one.  The mouse thumbstick is not in use while typing, and the more keys added to the keyer the greater complexity both in the chords and in the construction.  Instead of having a large number of keys and combinations or  a small number of keys and the requirement for multiple keystrokes to type lesser used symbols the thumbstick can, in typing mode, be used as an additional set of shift bits to allow other symbols, numbers or control keys to be accessed.  This allows some chords to be reused in an easily memorized way, shift-space is tab, shift-delete is backspace, etc.  </p><p>People who, like me, have carpal in one hand and not the other or limited mobility in their hands can also benefit.  The travel distance for a chorded keyboard is significantly less than for a conventional keyboard and using low force switches you can have minimal force for nerve-damaged hands while still having an audiable &quot;click&quot; for feedback.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7254/log/22964-the-keyer">The Keyer</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7254/log/22964#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22964"><p>We have the basic layout, two buttons under the index fingers, one each for the other fingers and the thumb stick under the thumb. </p><p>Sourcing the parts was easy.  I had a lot of left over keyboard cherry switches from my  <a href="http://ohbah.com/mods/chairkey/" target="_blank">Chair Mounted Keyboard</a> project and several dead ps/3 controllers a soldering iron away from donating their joysticks. </p><p>Figuring out a way to place them under the proper fingers was a bit trickier.  The idea for the index finger controlling a combination of two keys was stolen from the <a href="http://www.chordite.com/">Chordite</a> keyer, he generally uses limit switches and coathangers for his keyer and my using cherry swtiches somewhat limits the applicability of his construction methods.  Working  with what materials I had at hand I decided to roll out a sheet of shapelock and mold it to my hand.</p><p>This was less painful than expected, but still probably not the best way to make ergonomic human interface devices. </p><p>The keys were placed on the molded plastic, attached with strips of shapelock, and solder wires run.  For simplicity I chose to have a common ground and all individual strips have their own wire as opposed to any sort of multiplexing.  Hopefully this won&apos;t come back to bite me down the road</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7254/log/22971-the-layout">The Layout</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7254/log/22971#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22971"><p>So we have the basic design, 5 keys, the basic layout, Pinkey, Ring, Middle, Index Front and Index Rear.  We need to figure out what chord goes with what key.  There&apos;s several standards, Engelbart&apos;s design, cykey, twiddler, but other than Engelbart none of them are designed expressly for 5 keys, and Engelbart was not optimized in any meaningful way.   Like all good engineers do when faced with multiple competing, non compatible standards, I decided to add another competing non compatible standard. </p><p>I spent a few hours clicking in random combinations to try and figure out what fingers worked better with what combinations.  I didn&apos;t have much of a problem where bending one finger caused another to bend enough to move a second finger, found zero issues with using the index finger to actuate the front, rear or both keys and found the hardest button to press was the pinky.</p><p>I decided to write a very small, very dirty python script to help provide suggestions.  Each key was weighted, a letter frequency pulled off of Wikipedia and the permutations calculated and sorted by the expected difficulty of chording.  The output was analyzed, tweaked and tested and the end result put in a handy reference sheet.  I threw together a layout for the remaining symbols based off of a bit of practise.  Time and testing will tell if this is the optimal layout.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7254/log/23077-got-it-working-in-labjack">Got it working in labjack</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7254/log/23077#discussion-list">

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23077"><p>We have the hardware, we have the layout, now we just need to do something useful with it.</p><p>I decided to prototype the keyer with my LabJack U12 and python. Since I run Linux interfacing python to generate keyboard events is as easy as writing commands to <a href="http://linux.die.net/man/1/xte">xte(1)</a> to run the keyboard and the mouse.  Now, I&apos;m not going to lie, this is an ugly hack and I should be ashamed of publishing it, but working 55 hours a week in customer service I found shame to be a liability.</p><p>The wiring is entirely free form, hence the somewhat scattered look on the diagram. </p><p>It seems to work well enough.  Going from 60 wpm on qwerty to  hunt and peck is painful but there&apos;s always a learning curve.</p><p>Still need to think of a good name for the keyer, maybe etaim after cyclilng through the keys?</p></div>
