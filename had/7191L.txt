                            <h2><a href="/project/7191/log/22825-oxidation-of-hydrogen-at-the-anode">Oxidation of Hydrogen at the Anode</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7191/log/22825#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22825"><p>It would appear either a nickel heme or Platinum catalyst is required at the anode to oxidize hydrogen getting it to give up its electrons and become a proton to be transported to the cathode where it will be reduced and combine with oxygen to form water.</p><p>Iron oxides and vanadium are also oxidizing catalysts. </p><p>Asphaltenes just so happen to have Vanadium and Nickel in trace amounts.  So I will proceed after thinking I was dead in the water.<br></p><p><a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0016236102001047">http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0016236102001047</a><br></p><p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphaltene">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphaltene</a><br></p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7191/log/22980-sulfonation-of-aromatic-amines">Sulfonation of Aromatic Amines</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7191/log/22980#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22980"><p><a href="http://pubs.acs.org/doi/abs/10.1021/ja01199a009">http://pubs.acs.org/doi/abs/10.1021/ja01199a009</a></p><p>Sulfonation of aromatic amines found in kevlar is difficult as H2SO4 dissolves kevlar in high enough concentrations. This is useful for casting thin films from bulk para-aramid but I would like to sulfonate the para-aramid as is in its woven fabric state.</p><p>In order to do this tests where conducted to day by sonicating the para-aramid in dilute sulfuric acid and then heating the bulk with a paint stripper to dryness.  The high temperatures produced by the paint stripper heat gun should allow the sulfuric acid to sulfonate some of the Amide groups upon which it has been adsorbed.  The question lies in how much is necessary.</p><p>This has to be done outside as this produces cyanide gas if the para-aramid is vaporized and will also produce sulfuric acid fumes.  Normally a fum hood would be required but I do not posses one anymore.</p><p>A visible change has occurred where the Para-aramid was sufficiently wetted by the dilute sulfuric acid, producing an almost translucent darker colored bulk.  I suspect this is the result of sulfonation or some or most of the aromatic amines. </p><p>Thin films will be cast upon completion of the first prototype allowing greater proton transport by reducing distance they must travel from the anode to the cathode and reducing resistance to proton flow.   These thin films should be sulfonated by baking as is espoused in the above article. </p></div>
                            </div>
