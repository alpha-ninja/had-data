                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">This is currently just a glimmer in my mind's eye. The goals for this project are--hopefully--simple enough to achieve with commonly available technology. This design effort will focus on three things:<br /><br />1) cost of materials; keeping the price down will be my primary goal, after all there are things like, oh, real life to pay for before my hobbies get funded. ;)<br /><br />2) size; the whole mill (enclosure and all) ought to take up not more space than a commercial desktop printer (dimensions from an older model Dell were something like 30" wide x 28" deep x 30" tall).<br /><br />3) relative precision; I have a preference for sub mil (0.0001") precision. While possible--maybe--I doubt I will be able to achieve that goal. I'll settle for +-.001" precision if I have to.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Given the design considerations above (in the description) a few ancilliary design restrictions can be required:</p><ol><li>Utilize as many off the shelf components as possible. This will have a number of consequences, including minimizing the design of original components.</li><li>Utilize open source designs for as many of the components that required design. As above, this will minimize design time for components that required design and fabrication.</li><li>Consider the tools available to the average hobbyist and design for those fabrication capabilities.</li></ol><p>Specific Design Requirements (these are necessary for completion)</p><ol><li>Must completely fit within a W24" x L30" x H30"</li><li>Must have 5 axes; 3 linear (x, y, and z), and 2 rotational (a and b)</li><li>Must be able to effectively mill hardened steel, Inconel, Monel, etc.</li><li>Must use as many off-the-shelf parts as possible.</li><li>Must utilize as many open-source efforts as possible.</li><li>Other requirements pending Collaboratorium 2.</li></ol><p>Specific Design Goals (these are icing on the cake)</p><ol><li>May interface with PLCs.</li><li>May allow for auto tool changing.</li><li>May run via network/wireless.</li><li>May have touchpad/other interface on the machine.</li><li>May have auto material placement and removal.</li><li>May detect mill-bit sharpness.</li><li>May have tool magazine.</li><li>May have general parts library (based on OpenSCAD or similar parametric design tool).</li><li>Others? Input is welcome.</li></ol>
                                
                            </p>

                            <p><p>My light literature review continues. In this rather short episode I consider more advanced math that I am rather unprepared to tackle. Huzzah for brain-pain!</p><hr><p>Park, Sung-Ryung, and Seung-Han Yang. 2010. &#x201C;Design of a 5-Axis Machine Tool Considering Geometric Errors.&#x201D; International Journal of Modern Physics B 24 (15n16): 2484&#x2013;89. doi:10.1142/S0217979210065131.</p><p><strong>Overview</strong></p><p>This paper presents a kinematics drive approach for modeling and predicting volumetric error within a 5-axis mill&apos;s movement. Three mill configurations are considered, analyzed, and the critical design factors contributing to volumetric error are identified.</p><p><strong>How is this study useful to this project?</strong></p><p>This study may represent a way to identify components and assemblies that are critical to precision very early in the design phase. Further, because the math is available it may prove useful during computer driven design revisions for the identification and selection of components that minimize volumetric errors. </p><p><strong>What problems will utilizing this research present?<strong></strong></strong><strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong></strong></span></strong></p><p><strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></span></strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Aside from my distinct lack of training in mathematics the largest challenge will be to appropriately integrate this into some form of computer driven task. A deeper understanding of the method and its outcomes will aid in figuring out where this will fit in the design process and what decisions it will contribute to making.</span></span><strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span><strong></strong></span></strong><br><strong><strong></strong></strong></p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/3725/log/22970-lit-review-lite-frame-design">Lit. Review Lite: Frame Design</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/3725/log/22970#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p> This is a lighter literature review than some applications call for. I am excusing myself from anything more deptailed until I understand the math behind most Mechanical Engineering. Currently I lack a background in Physics, Statics, Dynamics, Calculus (differential and partial differential equations), Linear Algebra, and applications there-of.</p><p>I am taking steps to rectify these gaps in my knowledge. I have enrolled in a Calculus course at my local community college. I am enrolled in an online course for Linear Algebra and its applications (specifically in programming). I am completing a course in programming (CS50 through edX.org) after which I will be approaching Physics.</p><p>And there is probably more that I haven&apos;t mentioned. Keeping all this straight is a task unto itself and it can be rather time-consuming.</p><p>Complaining aside; here is what you&apos;ve skipped everything above for:</p><hr><p>Hung, Jui Pin, Yuan Lung Lai, Tzuo Liang Luo, and Hsin Chuan Su. 2013. &#x201C;Analysis of the Machining Stability of a Milling Machine Considering the Effect of Machine Frame Structure and Spindle Bearings: Experimental and Finite Element Approaches.&#x201D; International Journal of Advanced Manufacturing Technology 68 (9-12): 2393&#x2013;2405. doi:10.1007/s00170-013-4848-6.</p><p><strong>Overview</strong></p><p>Analysis of the Machining Stability of a Milling Machine Considering the Effect of Machine Frame Structure and Spindle Bearings: Experimental and Finite Element Approaches (hereafter &quot;the study&quot; or &quot;the paper&quot;) specifically considers the combined dynamics of a vertical milling machine frame and spindle. An application of finite element analysis was used to predict the dynamics of the milling machine and spindle and an experiment was used to confirm the analysis.</p><p><strong>How is the study useful to this project?</strong></p><p>In the introduction the author&apos;s clearly state how this application of experimental and mathematical analysis could be directly useful to an end-user, saying that, &quot;The prediction [...] of the abnormal vibration is of great importance for the selection of the free chatter machining conditions.&quot; This study aims to specifically address the dynamic conditions that result from using a high-speed spindle in combination with the mill&apos;s frame. A complete solid model is used for the analysis, rather than a simplified model (as other studies have done).</p><p>Items that are specifically useful are the mathematics and derived equations, the mathematic modeling methodology used for the spindle unit and mill unit.</p><p>The math presented will allow me to <em>not</em> have to experiment to find the modal frequency by experimentation and will thus allow a program to handle the heavy lifting (iterative design via algorithm) once the design phase has begun.</p><hr><ol><li>Huo, Dehong, Kai Cheng, and Frank Wardle. 2010a. &#x201C;Design of a Five-Axis Ultra-Precision Micro-Milling Machine-UltraMill. Part 1: Holistic Design Approach, Design Considerations and Specifications.&#x201D; International Journal of Advanced Manufacturing Technology 47 (9-12): 867&#x2013;77. doi:10.1007/s00170-009-2128-2.</li><li>&#x2014;&#x2014;&#x2014;. 2010b. &#x201C;Design of a Five-Axis Ultra-Precision Micro-Milling Machine-UltraMill. Part 2: Integrated Dynamic Modelling, Design Optimisation and Analysis.&#x201D; International Journal of Advanced Manufacturing Technology 47 (9-12): 879&#x2013;90. doi:10.1007/s00170-009-2129-1.</li></ol><p><strong>Overview</strong></p><p>These studies walk through the design and implementation process of an ultra-precise (&quot;nano-meter finish&quot;) 5-axis CNC milling machine. The first article focuses on the existing ultra-precision machines, component options, and the specifications for the proposed &quot;UltraMill&quot;.</p><p>The second article focuses on the modeling and simulation of the &quot;UltraMill&quot; with an emphasis on machine dynamics.
