                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Did you know that 60 to 80 % of people diagnosed with Multiple Sclerosis show extreme sensitivity to heat? When the core body temperature increases, some of the most common MS symptoms worsen, including  fatigue, limb weakness, visual problems, pain and numbness and cognitive dysfunctions. Devices to cool the core body temperature of those suffering from MS are few. They include a cooling vest that requires the wearer to insert cold packs into vest pockets and only last a few hours. Other devices are everyday fans or people sitting in air-conditioned vehicles or rooms. <br />My device is a wearable shirt that circulates cooling gel through silicone tubing. By using mini Refrigeration the shirt will provide cooling for up to eight hours. The hardware will be enclosed in a small backpack. The shirt is actively controlled by an embedded system that monitors body temperature and cooling liquid temperature. This will allow the wearer to maintain a normal body temperature in hot environments.<br /></div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>This project has six systems that make up the cooling pack system.</p> <ol><li>Power Management: <ol><li>Charger/Li-ion cell balancer.</li><li>Dual battery pack management.(implemented in later models)</li><li>Li-ion charger and DC/solar power input.</li><li>TI MSP430 microcontroller power control brain.</li></ol> </li><li>Thermal Management: <ol><li>Heat Exchanger temperature monitoring.</li><li>Mini Compressor running temperature.</li><li>Coolant tubing temperature monitoring.</li><li>User body temperature monitoring.</li><li>Ambient temperature monitoring.</li></ol> </li><li>Pump, Compressor, Valve, and Fan Management: <ol><li>Refrigeration compressor BLDC driver.</li><li>Coolant pump driver.</li><li>Servo valve controller.</li><li>Cooling fan driver.</li></ol> </li><li>Display and User Input: <ol><li>Acme systems Arietta G25.</li><li>Buttons or touch screen.</li></ol> </li><li>Mechanics: <ol><li>Backpack.</li><li>Shirt tubing.</li><li>Electronics system PCB/wiring.</li><li>Cooling chamber and plumbing.</li><li>Insulation.</li></ol> </li><li>Brain/Main Control: <ol><li>Microcontroller MSP430.</li></ol> </li></ol><p>The way the cooling system works is as follows:</p><p>The system has three main parts: The power system which supplies 24 volts to the compressor and 12V, 3.3V, 5V volts to the fans, microcontrollers, and servo valves. The mini refrigeration system which uses R134A refrigerant to cool the liquid coolant. Lastly, the liquid coolant circulation and control system. This system circulates the coolant through the shirt, controls the servo valves, and monitors the temperatures. </p><p><img style="width: 475px; height: 297px; margin: auto; display: block;" alt="Battery Power System" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1094491439836407385.png" height="297" width="475"></p><p>This is the refrigeration system block diagram shown below which has four main parts: the compressor, Condenser, Expansion valve, heat exchanger evaporator coil, and Accumulator. I am not going to discuss how a refrigeration system works in this project, since it is something easily researchable. This systems job is to cool the liquid coolant flowing into the heat exchanger to the desired cold temperature. The heart of the system is the Aspen Q-series low noise, miniature, rotarty BLDC compressor. This compressor runs of 24V, has a 360W cooling capacity, 40dBA noise level, and weighs 900g.</p><p><a target="_blank" href="http://www.aspencompressor.com/Download%20Pdf/Aspen_Universal_Quiet_Compressor_Spec_Sheet_Jan_2014.pdf"><img style="float: right; margin: 0px 0px 10px 10px;" alt="Aspen Compressor datasheet" src="http://www.aspencompressor.com/Download%20Pdf/Aspen_Universal_Quiet_Compressor_Spec_Sheet_Jan_2014.pdf"></a> </p><p><img style="width: 430px; height: 288px; margin: auto; display: block;" alt="" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7179161439826357533.png" height="288" width="430"></p> <p>The cooling liquid, which will be an ethylene glycol and water mixture, will be pumped through 1/4&quot; tubing. This tubing will be attached to a shirt that will fit snugly to the body. This cooling liquid will be cooled in a heat exchanger that houses the refrigeration evaporator copper coil. The heat exchanger chamber will allow the liquid coolant to flow around the evaporator coil allowing it to cool the liquid that is being pumped into the chamber. Then the coolant will be pump out via the servo valves to each cooling tube circuit. The pump and valves there then turn off to save power. At this point the cooling tubing circuits temperatures are monitored by the main microcontroller. As the coolant heats up, it passes a programmed threshold. The microcontroller will activate the pump and valves to pump the warm coolant out and new cool coolant into the tubes. This duty cycle-based system will save power and is projected to run 8 hours.</p><p>This project is being fully funded and endorsed by the National MS Society through the MS Entrepreneurs Grant. In June of this year, II was selected to receive the MS entrepreneurs grant to build this project. <br></p><p><img style="width: 482px; height: 309px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2577871439842237592.jpg" height="309" width="482"></p>

                            <p><p>This is my prototype expansion valve that I have made for the refrigeration system. I have incorporated both a flow control needle valve and a 0.010&quot; flow controlling orifice. The idea behind the orifice is to make the liquid R134A spray into the evaporator coil. As stated in previous project logs, I am using AN fittings to connect the various parts of the refrigeration system together. <br></p><img style="display: block; margin: auto; width: 520px; height: 394px;" alt="" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3099311439838011677.png" height="394" width="520"><p><img style="width: 506px; height: 380px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6178651439838118124.png" height="380" width="506"></p><p>As pictured above is the whole expansion valve and the evaporator coil.                             </small>
                            <p><p>As stated in the last project log detailing the TEC experiment,  this has become a dead end for this application. I am now going with a new plan of using phase change for my source of cooling. I am going to make a mini refrigeration unit. This system will be capable of 360W of cooling power. With the mini compressor I will be putting roughly the same amount of power into the system as with the TEC&apos;s but I will be getting 360 watts of cooling power. This will be a vast improvement in power efficiency because I will not need to run the compressor all the time to reach my cooling temperature goals.   The mini compressor is going to run 24V with a max current consumption of 10amps. The picture below shows the mini compressor and the micro condenser. I am going to use R134A refrigerant.     <br></p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8483601439254725295.jpg"><p>In an attempt to get away from the complexities of HVAC brazing I have decided to use AN fittings to connect up the refrigeration system.
