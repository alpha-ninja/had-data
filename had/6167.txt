                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The Muffsy Phono Preamp PP-3 is a hifi pre-amplifier for vinyl turntables with MM and high output MC cartridges. With its no-nonsense, straight forward design that focuses on both performance and cost, this project caters to audiophiles and music lovers alike. <br /><br />For once, there is no magic involved in a true audiophile piece of equipment. You get spectacular RIAA compliance (+/- 0.025 dB, 1-100.000 Hz), ultra-low noise, a wonderful great sound stage and superior detail. All done with one small PCB and standard, off the shelf components. <br /><br />A kit is available on Tindie and Muffsy.com:<br /><a href='http://phono.muffsy.com' target="_blank">http://phono.muffsy.com</a><br /><a href='http://muffsy.com/store.html#kit' target="_blank">http://muffsy.com/store.html#kit</a></div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/07/06/50-winners-using-texas-instruments-parts/">award winning Muffsy Phono Preamp PP-3</a> and the successor to the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/4788-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-2" target="_blank">Muffsy Phono Preamp PP-2</a> gives you one more level of gain, optional input capacitors, a symmetrical design and identical length audio paths that have been shortened even further. <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19345-whats-new">Look here</a> for more details.</p><p>The <a href="http://muffsy.com/build-it.html">build instructions</a> will bring you all the way to a complete working phono stage. The project uses only through-hole components, and is suitable for both beginners and experienced DIYers.</p><p>This project is complete, the <a href="http://muffsy.com/store.html">kit can be ordered on the Muffsy home page</a> or <a href="https://www.tindie.com/products/skrodahl/cnc-phono-stage-kit/" target="_blank">on Tindie</a>.</p><p><img                             <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6167/log/20773#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Instead of buying <a href="https://www.tindie.com/products/skrodahl/cnc-phono-stage-kit/">the PCB with the Muffsy Phono Preamp kit</a>, you can etch your own!</p><p>This is a <strong><em>single layer version</em></strong> of the Muffsy Phono Preamp PP-3, with ground fill. It will offer proper protection against interference, and almost no copper has to be etched away.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6404371436532588112.png"></p><p>Download the copper side and silkscreen PDFs from here:</p><ul><li><a href="https://app.box.com/s/d4j05nwdohxaa3yowx1kgyg9d6ztlvp1">Muffsy Phono Preamp PP3 - PCB</a></li></ul><p>They are in A4 format, so make sure you don't stretch them if you're using a Letter format printer.</p><p>A text file included in the zip archive will explain the orientation of the layers.</p><p><strong>Note:</strong></p><p>Do not use the picture above to etch the PCB, it's just for illustration.</p>
                                
                            </p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6167/log/19611-enclosure">Enclosure</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6167/log/19611#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>You can pretty much build the Muffsy Phono Preamp into any enclosure you can imagine. This will not cover all possibilities, instead I'll show you how I mounted my phono stage in a B-0905 enclosure that I found on eBay.</p><p>The way I found it was simply by searching for "<strong>0905 enclosure</strong>" on eBay. A couple of weeks later, this showed up:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1635901434468660144.jpg"></p><p>These are the dimensions of my enclosure:</p><ul><li><strong>Width</strong>: 92 mm</li><li><strong>Height</strong>: 47 mm</li><li><strong>Depth</strong>: 158 mm </li></ul><p>The nice thing about this enclosure is that it has grooves on each side for mounting PCBs, and both the Muffsy Phono Preamp and the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5676-muffsy-hifi-dual-power-supply">Muffsy Power Supply</a> slides right into them. No need for drilling or special mounting.</p><h2>Assemble the Parts</h2><p>I have prepared the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19341-audio-wiring">phono stage</a>, <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19288-powering-the-phono-stage">power supply</a> and the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19289-back-panel">back panel</a> (click on the links for instructions).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7009331434469122684.jpg"></p><p>Notice that I have the holes in the screw terminals facing inwards, to make sure no cables are touching the cabinet walls:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1385281434469760993.jpg"></p><h2>Insert the PCBs</h2><p>From here it's just a matter of sliding the PCBs in place:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7034921434469334598.jpg"></p><h2>Fasten the Back Panel</h2><p>With the PCBs in place, just fasten the back panel:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3500581434469578892.jpg"></p><h2>...and We're Done!</h2><p>Mount the top piece of the enclosure, and you've got yourself a phono stage:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2284111434469678369.jpg"></p>
                                
                            </p>

                        <h5>Build instructions</h5>
                        
                        
                            <ul class="post-content section-instructions-list">
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">1</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><h1>Let's Build a Phono Stage</h1><p>These instructions will guide you from beginning to end. If anything is unclear, let me know in the comments section.</p><p>You will be able to build the Muffsy Phono Preamp, even if you've never soldered anything before in your life.</p><p>Now let's start with some basics:</p><h1>Before You Begin</h1><ul><li>Soldering the Muffsy Phono Preamp PP-3 will take about one and a half hour.</li><li>Make sure you have enough time, and an environment without distractions. Don't stress, just enjoy building.</li></ul><ul><li>Take the time to read and understand the instructions.</li><li>Make sure you understand each step. If not, read again or ask a question in the comments section.</li><li>Notice that many components must be oriented a certain way.</li><li>Clicking on a picture will bring up a high resolution version. Use this to make sure your components are placed right.</li></ul><h1>How to Solder</h1><p>If you're just starting out soldering, or if you need an update on how to solder,<a href="http://www.sparkfun.com/" rel="nofollow">SparkFun</a> has a <a href="https://learn.sparkfun.com/tutorials/how-to-solder---through-hole-soldering" rel="nofollow">great tutorial for soldering through-hole components</a>.</p><p>If you have never soldered before, I strongly recommend these two videos from <a href="http://www.eevblog.com/">EEVBlog</a>:</p><p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/J5Sb21qbpEQ" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="width: 500px; line-height: 22.3999996185303px; height: 281px;"></iframe><iframe width="500" height="281" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/fYz5nIHH0iY" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="width: 500px; line-height: 22.3999996185303px; height: 281px;"></iframe></p></div>
                                    </li>
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">2</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><h1>Info about the Kit and Instructions Overview</h1><p>The Muffsy Phono Preamp PP-3 comes as a <a href="https://www.tindie.com/products/skrodahl/cnc-phono-stage-kit/" target="_blank">kit that you can order on Tindie</a>, that includes everything needed to build a complete phono stage.</p><p>To make it do anything useful, you need to complete it with a <a href="https://www.tindie.com/products/skrodahl/muffsy-hifi-dual-power-supply-pcb/">power supply</a>, <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19288-powering-the-phono-stage">cables</a> and <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19341-audio-wiring">connectors</a>, and an <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167/log/19611-enclosure">enclosure</a>.</p><p>Make sure that you have all the components, depending on which option you chose when ordering the kit. All required components are listed in the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167/components">bill of materials</a>.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7778871434579168008.jpg"></p><p>Going through these steps, you'll be mounting the components above onto the PCB below. You'll also be able to change the circuit to suit your needs, get a few options for powering the phono stage, and finally an example of how to install your project in an enclosure.</p><h1>Build Instructions Contents</h1><p>These instructions contains detailed illustrations to guide you through a complete phono stage build. Follow them, and there will be no room for error.</p><p>Here are the contents of the guide:</p><ul><li>Steps 3 through 11 show the actual soldering of the board</li><li>Step 12 shows the back panel</li><li>Step 13 shows the audio and power supply wiring</li><li>Step 14 handles the enclosure</li><li>Step 15 describes the different settings on the board<span></span></li></ul><h1>Illustrations Explained</h1><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5235401433714293185.png"></p><p>The picture above shows the actual layout of the PCB. It will be used to show which components to solder for each step.<br></p><p>For each step, you'll also get a picture of how the board should look like when the step is completed. This will help you make sure you haven't missed anything. Like this example:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7027571435876603962.jpg"></p><p>You can click on all pictures to get high resolution versions.<br></p><h2>About the PCB</h2><p>The high quality printed circuit board has plated-through holes. Do not attempt to drill these holes to make them bigger. Doing so will break the board.</p><p>It is safe to drill the four screw holes (one in each corner) to make them larger.</p></div>
                                    </li>
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">3</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><h1>Solder the Resistors</h1><p>Before you start soldering the resistors, consider if you want to:</p><ul><li>Change the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19286-input-impedance">input impedance</a></li><li>Change the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19334-variable-gain">variable gain</a></li><li>Use other <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3/log/19345-whats-new">resistors in the audio path</a></li></ul><p>With these decisions made, go ahead and solder all the resistors.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4335681434298750527.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4092091435874093909.jpg"></p><p>Click on the pictures for high resolution versions.</p><p>Make sure that the right resistor goes in the right place. Orientation does not matter for the resistors. </p></div>
                                    </li>
