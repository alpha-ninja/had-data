                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">I am building a volumetric 3D scanner based on X-ray computed tomography.<br />The goal is to keep the total material cost under 100$.<br /><br />The scanner will have the size of a backpack and will run on batteries.<br /><br />It will scan a true 3D image with features inside the object, so you will be able to virtually cut inside the object and see features an optical scanner can´t see.<br /><br /><br /><br /><br /></div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>SYSTEM DESIGN DOCUMENT</p><p>Computed tomography is still considered to be an overexpensive huge machine standing in hospitals.<br></p><p>I want to make this technology accessible to everyone.</p><p>This small version might be used to examine archeological artefacts or replicate complex objects that an optical scanner cannot view.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4390661439555105522.jpg"></p><p>Basically, a volumetric 3D image can be obtained from a series of 2D X-ray images assumed the object is rotated by a few degrees for every picture.<br>The X-ray source is an old radio tube (available for 0.50$-3$) which is driven by a high voltage source made of two car ignition coils. There are still plenty of these tubes available online.</p><p>The X-rays are made visible by the so called &quot;intensifying screen&quot;. It&#xB4;s a sheet plated with a chemical that emits visible light when hit by X-rays.</p><p>I am currently using intensifying screens previously used in a veterinary clinic - but there is a way to make them DIY which I will not focus on.</p><p>I was able to generate X-rays with the radio tube:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1175051439557583948.jpg"></p><p>The system is almost completetely assembled now in a transport box, including PCBs and wiring.</p><p>WORKING TOWARDS</p><p>I&#xB4;ll finish assembling the system first and will start test runs to obtain the correct exposure times.</p><p>The filament of the radio tube has to be heated to increase the current. This will be done by induction.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Here is the (hopefully) final control board and power switch board dsign. It&#xB4;s sent for production.</p><p>The controller board contains a relais for the camera trigger, a motor driver for the rotary table, a buzzer to warn you when the x-ray powers up and an interface for the switch buttons and the coil drivers.</p><p>The power board contains two isolated power-mosfets to drive the ignition coils and the transformer for the tube&#xB4;s filament heating.</p><p>I decided to put the power board near the ignition coils &amp; filament heater to reduce the EMI on the controller board. The coils spit lots of unwanted EMI, maybe I&#xB4;ll need to replace the stepdown converter by a simple LDO, because it showed that switching converters get dizzy by the EMI (induction in the switching coils?) Let&#xB4;s see. Chopping the coils induces  high voltages on the primary side( (&gt;400V) which are indended to occur to develop a high enough output voltage, so they should stay on short paths to the battery. Varistors are included for protection of the control board, just in case.</p><p>^^ The filament of the vacuum tube should be heated to ensure a higher current flow. Because two ignition coils are necessary to get a voltage that is high enough to generate hard x-rays, there is a potential of up to 30kv on the heated cathode against the supply rails. So if you want to heat up the filament, you can&#xB4; t just wire it to the control board. A solution would be inductive coupling by two air coils isolated by a plastic wall.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1261651436821437659.jpg"></p>
