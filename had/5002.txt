                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Mount a rail system up an incline (or between two elevations, like inside a disused grain silo or other tall building. Attach a hydrogen-impermeable lightweight bladder to a lightweight rig that moves along the rails. This rig has a permanent magnet attached. At the top and bottom of this rig are magnetic-sealing transfer connectors. At the top of the rail is a hydrogen storage tank. At the bottom of the rails is a water electrolysis tank. Along the rail are induction coils, all routed in series or parallel to a set of storage batteries at the base of the system. The electrolysis system is powered by a tap from this battery bank. Above the hydrogen storage tank, an internal combustion engine uses the hydrogen from the tank and external air to run a dynamo, the output of which is routed down the the battery bank. The exhaust, which should be pure water, is routed down the rails, through multiple turbines, which also generate power, which is all routed to the electrical storage. </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>As a note- the movement of the rig up and down the rails is governed by the inflation level of the hydrogen bladder. As the electrolysis fills the bladder, it eventually develops enough buoyancy to break free of the magnetic seal and begin travel up the rail. As it moves, it generates current in the coils it passes, which is sent to the storage cells. When the rig reaches the top and connects to the upper hydrogen storage, it empties. As this occurs, the rail coil connections are inverted, so the same polarity is sent to the storage cells. Once the bladder releases enough hydrogen to break the seal, it goes back down, generating more power. This is in addition to the power generated by burning hydrogen at the top and from the turbines being turned by exhaust water.</p><p>The electrolysis unit's exhaust is pure oxygen, and the only other output is electricity.<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>
