                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Most concussions resulting from sports go diagnosed either because the player doesn't recognize the symptoms, or because they are encouraged to "play through" the pain. Most traumatic brain injuries from concussions occur because the patient isn't taken out of the situation.<br /><br />Impact is a device the size of a dime and is powered by a small coin cell. It runs at an extremely low power so that the battery doesn't need replacing often. The small size allows the device to be concealed in sports equipment like football helmets. After a player comes off the field and takes their helmet off, a trainer can see if the LED is blinking, which indicates the acceleration threshold was tripped and the player should be screened for a concussion. A complete data log can be downloaded after the game for further analysis.<br /><br />Concussion details:<br /><a href='http://www.momsteam.com/problem-underreporting-concussions-helmet-sensors-solution-to-problem' target="_blank">http://www.momsteam.com/problem-underreporting-concussions-helmet-sensors-solution-to-problem</a></div>
                </div>

There are various numbers that appear in the literature that are likely to result in a concussion. To download the raw data off of the device, a &quot;docking station&quot; with pogo pins is used to make contact with the test points located on the back of the device, just below the battery. This saves the cost and space of a connector. </p><iframe width="560" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/F74dFpKo86A" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5613/log/22523-enclosure-experiments">Enclosure experiments</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">7 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5613/log/22523#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>If this thing is going to be stuffed in helmets or other equipment, it needs some kind of enclosure to protect the electronics from moisture and dirt. I used a material from Smooth-On called XTC-3D to finish a 3D print recently (which is what that material is designed for), and I think it may work for coating electronics too. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/418911439313904844.jpg"></p><p>I tried it on a test PCB with no parts populated, since I don&apos;t want to pot the one that I&apos;m hacking on, since I need to be able to probe traces and pads. I know that there are potting materials made specifically for electronics, but this stuff is pretty easy to get, a little bit goes a long way, so it&apos;s cheap, and we&apos;ll see if it works well. </p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5613/log/22518-assembling-v2">Assembling V2</a></h2>

                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/715701439312050485.JPG"><p>Here&apos;s V2, fabbed and assembled. It came in slightly larger than V1, but not by much. It uses the same 6 pads for programming and reading out the EEPROM. This is a 2 layer board, and the only reason it&apos;s not smaller is because of the routing. If I made a 4 layer version, I could probably cut the size in half. Although at this point, I don&apos;t know if there would be much to gain from going even smaller. </p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5613/log/22517-version-2">Version 2</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">7 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5613/log/22517#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>I decided to make some changes to the parts I&apos;m using.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7392861439311477869.PNG"></p><p>I switched the old ATTiny85 to the larger ATTiny2313. The footprint is actually smaller, since it&apos;s a QFN part, and there&apos;s more I/O, which makes it easier to program and solves the problems in the last version. I also ditched a few discretes that I didn&apos;t really need. I also changed the battery to a rechargable Li-ion battery, which makes the whole thing a little thinner and that way you don&apos;t need to worry about replacing the battery, only recharging it. The accelerometer and EEPROM are the same.</p>
