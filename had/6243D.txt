            <h1>Introduction<br></h1><p>The number of sensors will reach hundreds of billions in next years. One of the biggest challenges today is to find new ways of powering these. Today&apos;s state-of-the-art in terms of powering mass produced products are Lithium based batteries. These, however, bring a significant environmental impact. It is also popular to produce single-use devices which have irreplaceable batteries.</p><p>Energy harvesting is one of the solutions for this problem. The sensors do not necessarily need to be powered for a long time. It is quite common that they need to be on for just a second each hour. This may be solved with short high-power bursts of energy (either RF or light) provided to the device to harvest just the amount needed for advertising the sensor data. Or the energy can be stored in a greener storage element (e.g. supercapacitor).</p><p>The IoTgreen project wants to develop a new platform, performing both research on different methods - RF energy harvesting, solar harvesting and laser beam harvesting - but also a product-ready and developer-friendly open-source solutions.</p><p>All schematics, PCB layouts and source codes are under CC BY-SA license. Some parts of source codes for nRF51 MCU may be a property of Nordic Semiconductor ASA. This will be specified in the source code.</p><h1>Goals of the project</h1><ul><li>Stage 1 - evaluate whether the proposed harvesting techniques are capable of powering wireless sensor</li><li>Stage 2 - make first prototypes which will consist of a harvesting unit, nanopower power supply control logic and nRF51822QFAB microcontroller</li><li>Stage 3 - make final products (open-source hardware and software)<ul><li>1) A no-battery temperature and barometer sensor powered by RF or powered by laser beam / solar panel.</li><li>2) The energy harvesting development kit with free GPIOs, wireless-enabled microcontroller and harvesting power management for development of your own devices</li><li>3) Product-like kit with water-proof plastic box and interface to connect your own sensor</li></ul></li></ul><p>You can find the System design document on following link: <a href="http://www.iotgreenproject.com/uploads/iotgreen_systemdesign.pdf">www.iotgreenproject.com/uploads/iotgreen_systemdesign.pdf</a><br></p><p>The GitHub repository for nRF51 software is here: <a href="https://github.com/mnovak111/IotGreen_IoTGreenSense1.0_nRF51x">https://github.com/mnovak111/IotGreen_IoTGreenSense1.1_nRF51x.git</a></p><p>The quarterfinals video: <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AFl1Wlfyygs" target="_blank">www.youtube.com/watch?v=AFl1Wlfyygs</a></p><h1>Examples of IoTGreen sensors applications<br></h1><h2>Agricultural sensor networks - sensing soil humidity and other parameters to increase effectivity and yields</h2><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5319401438871631239.JPG"></p><h2>Home IoT - maintenance-free ultra-low-power wireless light powered sensors, both artificial light and sun powered</h2><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3627741438873659835.jpg"><br><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4828491438874751938.jpg"></p><h1>Area of use<br></h1><ul><li>Home security sensors</li><li>Smart home sensors</li><li>Contact-less medical sensors</li><li>Battery-less sensors for dangerous (for example highly explosive) environments</li><li>Sensors installed in moving parts (e.g. crankshafts)</li><li>Swarms of sensors for explorative space missions - the main sattelite equipped with high-power radioisotope thermoelectric generator can power hundreds of batteryless sensors around it</li><li>Battery-less environment sensors - the data can be collected wirelessly from much larger distance that other technologies provide - i.e RFID</li></ul><h1>Licenses</h1><p>All schematic diagrams and board layouts are licensed as CC-BY-SA. This means that you can copy, redistribute and modify but those modifications have to be published under same license and all authors have to be listed.</p><p>The software examples for nRF51 microcontrollers are based on Nordic Semiconductor &quot;ble_app_beacon&quot; example. Thus, the license belongs to Nordic Semiconductor. This is stated at the beginning of each file which has the Nordic Semiconductor&apos;s license. Some files (e.g. sensor libraries) were written by me and they are under same license as hardware. Those files do not have the heading where the Nordic&apos;s license is specified.</p><p>Unless stated otherwise, the other types of published work (including, but not limited to: written materials, graphs, tables, measured data, STL models etc. ) are also published under CC-BY-SA.</p><p>You are welcomed to contribute to the project. If you have any interesting pictures of your own energy harvesting experiments, please share them with me and the community. It is a great source of inspiration.</p><p>Disclaimer: Published hardware and software should be used only for experimental and hobby purposes. The main focus of this project is to explore science and technology. I do not carry any responsibility for losses and injuries caused by using results of this project for - including, but not limited to - industrial applications, medical applications, critical applications etc. <br></p><h1>[COMPLETED] First project phase - theory and first experiments<br></h1><h2>RF harvesting</h2><p>RF harvesting is, quite surprisingly, an old method of energy harvesting. If you are living near FM base station or any other high power antenna, you could be able to light up a bulb using simple diode detector and good antenna. The problem is that as the received power goes down, so does the efficiency and voltage. Another issue is the frequency. The equation which describes this behaviour is called the free space path loss equation. It says that the path loss increases with frequency and distance. The focus of this project was to harvest at least a tiny bit of usable energy even from low received power levels. A good choice of frequency used to transmit power is the key. Let&apos;s do some calculation - how big is the path loss on 1 meter at diferent frequencies:</p><p>2450 Mhz -&gt; 40.2 dB</p><p>1800 Mhz -&gt; 37.5 dB</p><p>900 Mhz -&gt; 31.5 dB</p><p>433 Mhz -&gt; 25.2 dB</p><p>100 Mhz -&gt; 12.4 dB</p><p>From my research, basically you need about -5 to 0 dBm input power <em>(edit on 6/14/2015 - cold start was achieved on BQ25570 and 6 stage RF to DC multiplier at around -7 to -10 dBm - it took about three minutes but the chip repeatedly cold-started) a</em>t the receiver antenna to do a cold start of RF to DC converter. To keep the calculations easy, just assume we need 0 dBm (= 1 mW) and gain of both transmitter and receiver antenna is 0 dB ( = 1). To achieve this, you need EIRP of 40 dBm (about 10 watts!!) at 2.4Ghz, while only 12.4 dBm (0.017 watts) at 100 Mhz. While 10 watts output power is way beyond ETSI and FCC limits, the 12 dBm is a output power level of a normal 868/915 Mhz transmitter. Thus, with 2.4 Ghz transmitter at 10 watts, you are able to power a device wirelessly about a meter away. WIth 10 watts at 100 Mhz, the range rises up to 25 meters.</p><p>Next step is the structure which converts high frequency signal to harvestable voltage. Any diode and capacitor voltage multiplier topology will work, I have used the Cockcroft-Walton multiplier:</p><p><img style="width: 432px; height: 200.073732718894px; float: left; margin: 0px 10px 10px 0px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/323291434034063105.png" alt="Cockcroft-Walton multiplier. Source - Wikipedia"></p><p>The diodes used are Avago HSMS-285x. Those are zero-bias Schottky RF detector diodes. They are designed to work at frequencies up to 1.5 Ghz. The output of such multiplier is DC voltage with quite high output resistance - hundreds of Ohms to kOhms. The output voltage depends on number of stages and the RF input power.</p><p><strong>Efficiency of 6 stage Cockroft-Walton RF to DC converter at different input power levels</strong></p><p>The efficiency was measured at 3 input power levels: +10 dBm, 0 dBm and -10 dBm. The results show that the maximum efficiency is relatively high even at high frequency of 868 Mhz (the diodes work only up to 1.5 Ghz), about 25 to 35%. There is a little problem with the correct implementation of MPPT in energy harvesting. The maximum power point travels with the input power and it is not static. At +10 dBm, the maximum power point is at about 62% of the RF converter open circuit voltage. At 0 dBm, it is 40% and at -10 dBm, at about 90% of the open circuit voltage. Thus, the MPPT shall be adaptive to achieve maximum performance. This is however not supported by Texas Instruments BQ25504 chip, which only allows to set  static MPP point with resistor dividers. On the other hand, the efficiency is quite high over a long voltage range so setting it for e.g. 50% will produce quite good results at a wide range of input power.<br></p><p>Those graphs were produced from values which were measured using following setup:</p><p><span></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9181541437404848661.JPG"></p><p>The RF to DC converter is connected with coaxial cable to the TRXEB board with CC110L module which is used as RF power synthesizer. The output of the RF to DC converter is connected in series with 10 turn 20 kOhm potentiometer and ammeter which measures the maximum value (the TRXEB board is not generating continuous carrier). The voltage on the RF to DC converter is measured by scope. The graphs:</p><p><span></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3388041437398071955.png"></p><p><span></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9843981437398103658.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3410961437398112735.png"></p><p><strong>Schematics of RF to DC converter</strong><br></p><p><strong></strong></p><p><strong><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/537171437405121723.png"></strong></p><p><strong>PCB layout of RF to DC converter (size of the board is in inches)</strong></p><p><em><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6602481437405143107.png"></em></p><p><strong>Eagle files</strong></p><p>The Eagle files can be downloaded from this link: <a href="http://www.mnhs.cz/RFtoDC_Eagle.zip">www.mnhs.cz/RFtoDC_Eagle.zip</a> (Eagle 6)</p><p>Feel free to contact me if you will have any problems with opening the file. </p><p><strong>Assembly instructions</strong></p><p>All capacitors are 47 to 100 pF. To ensure maximum performance, the converter should be matched to the frequency using vector spectrum analyzer. As I am only exploring the area and not making final product, I am not doing it. All diodes are RF detectors Avago HSMS285C. I recommend using hot air gun and solder paste for soldering.</p><p><strong>Photos of RF to DC converter</strong></p><p>(using my new Stickvise - thank you Texas Instruments and Hackaday for giving me this prize :-) )</p><p><em></em></p><p><em><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9842801437405759086.JPG"></em></p><p><em><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1243921437405772957.JPG"></em></p><em><br></em><h2>Laser beam harvesting</h2><p>The operating principle is the same as solar charging. The only difference is that the light beam is much more &quot;concentrated&quot; and thus, instead of solar panel, a photodiode is used. I used BPW34 photodiode with 30mW red laser. The correct position of the laser is crucial. On following photos, you can see the test setup:</p><p>The 50 mW red laser beam is mounted in a vice</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8083441438883244905.jpg"></p><p>The photodiode is approx. 3 meters away</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7495261438883343819.jpg"></p><p>The capacitor (on the rear side) was charging with a current of approx. 3 mA and and the sensor was working properly. Further research will be performed. The overall performance heavily depends on the displacement of laser beam on the photodiode. If a part of photodiode remains dark, the overall current which can be drawn is significantly reduced. Thus, the next research will be performed on diffusors which may improve the performance.<br></p><h2>Solar harvesting</h2><p>The method of solar harvesting in this project is thoroughly described in project logs. It is also well documented in various sources on the Internet. The solar harvesting based sensors are the first ones which I had transformed into a product-look design. One of the examples is the IoT enabled soil moisture sensor:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1933961438886462928.jpg"></p><p>[this part will be expanded in following days, thank you very much for your patience] </p><p><em></em></p>
