            <iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/r6L80RRpv9Y" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/16v3KEWCdIE" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe>Hello everyone,</p><p><strong>What problem are we solving?</strong></p><p>If you buy an electric bike today, it comes with 2-year warranty on its battery. If you buy an electric car, it comes with 8-year. What is the difference between these batteries? Chemically and mechanically, not much - but usage is vastly different.</p><p>Never charging above 90% of safe charge level is the first trick electric cars use. By charging to 90% instead of 100% - longevity can effectively be doubled. 80% - quadruple the life. Diminishing returns kick in around ~58% (<a href="http://batteryuniversity.com/learn/article/how_to_prolong_lithium_based_batteries">See table 3 here</a>).</p><p>Furthermore, li-ion batteries sitting at full SOC quickly degrade. Ebike mfgs practically recommend always keeping the battery topped up. Our goal is to enable batteries with long warranties and to stop this consumption of batteries.</p><p>Our problem statement: Batteries do not live as long as they can. <br></p><p><strong>How do we solve this problem?</strong></p><p>We improve the Battery Management System by adding IoT to it. By connecting the battery to a normal mobile phone, the user can make intelligent choices to lengthen the life of the battery. Protection are turned into control. Most importantly, adjusting the charge threshold of the battery can have a huge impact on longevity. Evivar, our BMS, lets the user set this.</p><p><strong>Where are we now?</strong></p><p>We have developed a new BMS from the ground up, Design for Manufacturing (DFM) style. All the components are selected and we are well underway with coding.</p><p>Right now, the BMS handles 9 - 15 Cells and stream data over Bluetooth Classic (2.1). The idle power consumption is below the self-discharge rate typical to li-ion (1-2 % month). As for safety, all the protection features you might want is implemented.</p><p><strong>Where do we want to go?</strong></p><p>Our focus is small batteries (and especially electric bikes) but our design can be applied to many other applications - each requiring small modifications. To mutual gain for us and others - we want to Open Source our hardware and software.</p><p><strong>What does this mean for the average hacker?</strong></p><p>Our hardware can be used everywhere li-ion batteries are used. Some effort will be required for novel applications, but our building block can be used for EV&apos;s, solar installations, robotics and every conceivable portable product.</p><p><strong>Evivar BMS meets the need for better Battery Management</strong></p><p>Inside a li-ion battery you will find cells (that store energy) and a BMS. The BMS protects the cells from abuse and dangerous conditions. A common BMS:</p><ul><li>Measure all cell voltages, and equalize (balance) these to the same level</li><li>Measure battery temperature, and stop discharge and charge if out of spec</li><li>Measure battery current, to track remaining capacity and detect dangerous current levels</li></ul><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5293771437980495616.png"></p><p><strong>Specifications</strong></p><p>The main spesifications of Evivar BMS is:</p><ul><li>Designed for Ebikes up to 1500 W.</li><li>Supports up to 15 cells. With 3,6V per cell, that is 54V</li><li>Protects against overcharge and overdischarge</li><li>Designed for 30A continuous discharge</li><li>Has a separate 3V3 SMPS regulator, able to supply 50mA</li><li>Measures battery current and protects against short circuit and high-currents</li><li>Measures 2x battery temperatures and BMS temperature (MOSFETS and TCCS)</li><li>Communicates through UART</li><li>Is normally fitted with a Bluetooth RF module</li><li>Stores aggregate data about how the battery has been used</li></ul><p>Number of cells is normally referred to as &quot;S&quot;. 13S and 14S batteries are called 48V. 10S battery, 36V. For now, we have used the biggest TI AFE, in the future we will shrink Evivar to perfectly accomodate 5S and 10S batteries.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7393601437986185151.png"></p><p>In the chart above the main components of Evivar is shown. From the battery comes 2 high current leads and n+1 cell-voltage sense wires. A protection module, containing current sensing and MOSFETs, control charge and discharge. The AFE has a pure digital interface to the MCU and supplies 3V3 through an internal linear regulator. </p><p>A controllable 3V3 RF regulator is included. This is driven directly from the battery and can deliver 50mA. This way the HC-06 can be put to sleep when it is not needed or the battery has low SOC.</p><p><strong>App-development</strong></p><p>The hardware needs good software to go with it. We have our &quot;engineering app&quot; up-and-running, but we want to make something much nicer in time. A sketch of what we are working towards is shown below.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9669391437997599760.png"></p><p><strong>Licences</strong></p><p>All licences are open source, <a href="http://opensource.org/docs/osd">as described here.</a> GPL v3 is the licence that has been used. We&apos;ve selected open, cheap or free development software and tools - with one exeption, Altium Designer. CircuitMaker will be our tool for future revisions to the PCB, but the board will need to be routed again. Our source code for the Atmel SAM D20 microcontroller, Gerber fabrication files, Android application and schematics are published under GPL v3.</p><p>Notable Project Logs</p><p><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6913/log/23062-workings-of-the-firmware">Workings of the firmware</a></p><p><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6913/log/22190-programming-the-evivar-board">Programming the Evivar board</a></p><p><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6913/log/22429-temperature-compensated-copper-shunt">Temperature Compensated Copper Shunt</a></p><p><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6913/log/22492-charge-and-discharge-protection">Charge and discharge protection<strong></strong></a></p>
