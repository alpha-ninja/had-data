                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">I read an article in the Wall Street Journal describing super weeds that have developed a resistance to traditional weed killers. Palmar amaranth, pigweed, can grow 3 inches per day. Currently, soybean farmers are spending around $25. per acre on chemicals to protect their crop. Hand weeding can cost $150. per acre. In the U.S., farmers spent more than 13 billion dollars on chemicals in 2012. <br />This looked like a situation where a robot could run between the rows, plucking weeds to the side. The questions that come to mind include:<br />1) Can my robot visually follow a row?<br />2) Can my robot detect a plant taller than average?<br />3) Can my robot arm grab the offending plant?<br />I was aware of motorized bases, vision sensors, pinchers and the like, but putting them all together in one machine requires custom mounts, brackets and a linear gear arrangement for the pincer. Fortunately, these “not off the shelf” pieces are easy to design and create using a 3d printer.<br /><br /><a href='https://youtu.be/qOuOCalwU6' target="_blank">https://youtu.be/qOuOCalwU6</a></div>
                </div>

                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The latest video is at:</p><p><a href="https://youtu.be/7FO7NUBsNBs">Weed Burner</a><br></p><p>Components:</p><p>These can be obtained from superdroidrobots.com</p><p>Aluminum chassis  TP132-032</p><p>(2) six inch wheel shaft sets  TD-087-006</p><p>(4) 190 rpm 24 volt gear motors TD-015-190</p><p>12 volt lead acid battery TE-177-007</p><p>Motor controller</p><p>From other sources obtain:</p><p>Arduino Uno</p><p>15 watt 12 volt solar panel</p><p>12 volt solar regulator</p><p>Linear fresnel lens</p><p>This is no longer a weed picking robot, but is now a weed seed burning robot. Being contained within a field (I haven&apos;t worked out exactly how to do that yet), the robot will use the sun&apos;s power for propulsion, intelligence and weed seed burning (through the fresnel lens).</p><p>The robot runs and is controllable via arduino and it operates using solar power and a battery. Note that I am using a 12 volt system and 24 volt motors; this reflects available materials and it seems to work right now. </p><p>I need to acquire a linear fresnel lens and mount it at the proper height to focus on the ground. Operating speed will have to be adjusted to burn everything under the lens. Boundaries will probably be set with a string (tied to 4 corner poles). The robot will &quot;bounce&quot; off the string and proceed randomly somewhat like a Roomba vacuum. </p><p>All software that I post as well as 3D designs for mounting, etc. will be open source and freely available for personal or commercial use.<br></p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4739/log/20550-going-about-this-differently">Going About This Differently</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4739/log/20550#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Growing Pigweed and Soybeans was an educational experience.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3419681436222468397.jpg">At first, all went well and I expected the Pigweed to grow rapidly and overtake the soybeans. It didn't happen. The little container garden in my yard was in an ideal spot--too ideal. There was sun and moisture from the irrigation system, too much moisture. In a few days, the soybeans were thriving and all the pigweed died.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9345311436222708279.jpg">This caused me to rethink my philosophy of "snatching teenage pigweed" from the ground. A soybean seed has over 5000 times the volume of a pigweed seed. Pigweeds are very vulnerable when they are tiny. What if we could wipe out the pigweeds during this vulnerable stage? </p><p>When soybeans are planted, the soil is disturbed and tiny pigweed seeds near the surface find conditions favorable to germinate. Suppose we "flame" the soil's surface at this time, killing the pigweed seed but not harming the bigger, stronger, deeper soybean? Instead of a flame, let's use the sun, focused through a linear fresnel lens. Let a robot carry the lens around the field (and let the robot be solar powered so that the whole mechanism runs off the sun). </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9416121436223149227.jpg">I have to work out a method for keeping the robot in the desired area as well as managing solar power and the fresnel lens.</p>
                                
                            </p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4739/log/17712-growing-pigweed">Growing Pigweed</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4739/log/17712#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>It took a while, but I found a source for pigweed seeds, <a href="http://www.riverrefugeseed.com/">River Refuge Seeds.</a></p><p>The pigweed seeds are tiny compared to soybean.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/946591431363980156.jpg"></p><p>I designed and 3d printed <a href="http://www.thingiverse.com/thing:821699">labels</a> (use painter's tape on the labels so that they are reusable), then planted seeds.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6542621431364124778.jpg"></p><p>Now, I'll photograph and measure the differences in growth between my target crop and target weed. This information will be useful for weed identification strategy. Ultimately, I'll use "light break" sensors as part of my process to identify weeds. For light break detection in a sunny (or cloudy) field, I'll place a photocell inside a tube (to shield it from direct sun) and direct a laser to the photocell. A Teensy-LC (or similar device) will be used to detect the significant change caused by path interruption.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9591631431364519956.jpg"></p>
