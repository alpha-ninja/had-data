                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">DISCLAIMER: this project uses radioactive isotopes. Please make sure you take all necessary measures should you intend to replicate it. I cannot be held responsible for any misuse, or accidents that may occur. Be safe!<br /><br />This project is aimed at creating a true random number generator. Why? Because I want to have a concrete use for some Geiger tubes I have. For now they do nothing more than clicking furiously at some radioactive samples...<br /><br />It is well known that random numbers generated only with software algorithms have a massive flaw: they're predictable by nature, should one get access to said algorithm. <br />Radioactive decay is, by definition, completely random: you cannot predict exactly when the next event will occur. This device takes advantage of that and makes an attempt at generating totally random numbers. </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><br><strong></strong></p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/xRSDE2FqVLs" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><p><strong>The generator consists of three main blocks: </strong><br></p><p>-The actual source and Geiger tube (inside a leaded enclosure);</p><p>-A power supply/pulse shaper (which powers the tube with 400VDC, and gather the pulses the Geiger generates);</p><p>-The Microcontroller, which handles the talk with the PC.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3384451439498845129.jpg"></p><p><strong>My project has to meet the following goals:</strong></p><p>-Safety. I want the source to be completely shielded, and still allow me to carry it around without risking to compromise the lead lining. </p><p>-Power. The whole thing has to be powered by USB. That shouldn&apos;t be too much of a problem, as the circuit does not draw more than 100mA at full power. </p><p>-Size. Something small, that fits in a small enclosure.</p><p>-Simple. It should be able to talk with the computer through a COM port, to use the data easily with any program I want... That should be easy to do with an Arduino Leonardo.</p><p>-Lifespan. Geiger-M&#xFC;ller tubes aren&apos;t eternal: they wear out after a certain amount of counts (10^8 typically). The source has to be sufficiently radioactive to generate enough pulses, but not too much that it wears out after a month.<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>
