                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">We are addressing the major frustration of collecting real world data such as pressure, temperature, flow, current, voltage and other analog signals.  Having used dozens of data loggers during product development, we got fed up with their noisy/drifty signals, clunky and expensive form-factor, and the “Windows 95” user interface.  <br /><br />The SiGZiG makes it easier to tap into the analog world and log signals with precision and simplicity.  Our 24bit model even has a noise floor of less than a microvolt.  The software is cross platform with an interface that is simple and beautiful.  The precision electronics are potted and encased in black anodized aluminum.  Plug it into your computer via USB, connect up your sensors and you are collecting data in no time.  (See full specs in the details section)<br /><br />We plan to launch a Kickstarter campaign when the development is complete, you can sign up at Sigzig.com to be notified.<br /></div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><strong><i><br></i></strong></p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/VWLj1dZOh0E" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><p><strong><i></i></strong><strong><i>How the Project Solved Our Specific Problem:</i></strong> </p><p>The SiGZiG is a byproduct of our hardware start-up which received funding from the Defense Department to develop advanced chemical sensors. To read data from our sensors the options were limited: 1) Ginormous data loggers or 2) Non-ginormous data loggers that didn&apos;t have the stability or true resolution sufficient to monitor our sensors or 3) Build our own. </p><p>Going with option 3 (by the way we tried 1 and 2 first), we successfully built hardware that was small, low-power, super low-noise, and very temperature stable. The hardware worked great for our chemical sensors as well as everywhere else around the lab. We love it, so we decided to package it in a nice case to see who else would want one too.</p><p>The idea is to use the SiGZiG to log precise changes in temperature for months or quantify the performance of your voltage reference. Anything that outputs a voltage can now be tapped into with a simple, easy-to-use tool. Had our data logger been available at the beginning of our project, it would have literally saved us more than a year of development time.</p><p> <i><strong></strong></i> <i><strong>Function</strong></i><strong>: </strong><strong></strong></p><p>Our vision is to make this as simple and easy to use as possible. Plug in the USB logger, use standard jumper cables to connect things up and click start on the software you downloaded from our website (or write your own).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6399791428347256036.jpg"></p><p><i><strong>Specs</strong></i><strong>:</strong></p><p>We are working on three initial versions with more to follow: </p> <table> <tbody><tr> <td> <p><strong>4 Channel</strong></p> </td> <td> <p><strong>4 Channel</strong></p> </td> <td> <p><strong>8 Channel</strong></p> </td> </tr> <tr> <td> <p>  18bit</p> </td> <td> <p>  16bit</p> </td> <td> <p>  24 bit</p> </td> </tr> <tr> <td> <p>  4 Differential</p> </td> <td> <p>  4 Differential</p> </td> <td> <p>  8 Differential</p> </td> </tr> <tr> <td> <p>  -5V to 5V</p> </td> <td> <p>  -5V to 5V and -10 to 10V</p> </td> <td> <p>  -5V to 5V</p> </td> </tr> <tr> <td> <p>  Noise @ 1 sample/s: &lt; LSB</p> </td> <td> <p>  Noise @ 1 sample/s: &lt; LSB</p> </td> <td> <p>  Noise @ 1 sample/s: 0.3uV</p> </td> </tr> <tr> <td> <p>  Drift: 15 ppm/C</p> </td> <td> <p>  Drift: 10 ppm/C</p> </td> <td> <p>  Drift: 2ppm/C</p> </td> </tr> <tr> <td> <p>  Up to 200 sample/s</p> </td> <td> <p>  Up to 200k sample/s</p> </td> <td> <p>  Up to 4k sample/s</p> </td> </tr> </tbody></table> <p>Something that we started notice after playing with many commercially available devices is that their stated specs often didn&apos;t mean a whole lot. For example a product would state 24bit resolution but when test it was really more like 16-18bit. All the components used matter to deliver the performance. </p><p>Our engineer Paul hooked a pulse sensor up to the 4 channel logger and here is his pulse taken from his index finger.                         <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5145/log/22745-zig-8-prototype-testing">Zig-8 Prototype Testing</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5145/log/22745#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Most of our focus has been on the Zig-4 lately but I finally found some time to play around with one of the Zig-8 (24 bit, 8 channel) prototypes to do some noise floor and short-term drift test. There is still a lot of work that needs to be done, but the results where still worth posting.  Remember this is a Prototype! </p><p>The setup of the test was basically take three pretty good voltage references, put them on the bench and monitor them along with a temperature sensor and a silver oxide battery (one of the original voltage references<span class="redactor-invisible-space">) for 10 hours once a second.  Not overly scientific <span class="redactor-invisible-space">but educational nonetheless.</span></span></p><p>Below is a plot of the three Vref&apos;s.  This is a 1 hour section after about 6 hours of warmup (you can see the time stamp at the top).  Even though a sample was taken every second there is a little aliasing going on with the way the software displays all the data so you don&apos;t actually see every point (sorry).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9941601439529232475.jpg"></p>The scale on the left is in &#xB5;V and all the plots have been zeroed out for easy comparison.  So what do we see?  Well we are basically looking at the combined noise and drift of the SiGZiG Zig-8 prototype with the noise and drift of the 3 voltage references.  And it looks like, at least over 1 hour, that the combined noise and drift  stays pretty much within +/- 1&#xB5;V<span class="redactor-invisible-space">.  So what part of the noise is from the Zig-8 prototype and what belongs to the voltage references?... well the short answer would be that generally any signal common to all the readings is from the SiGZiG.  That being said there are other things in common, like temperature (for good or bad).</span><p>Lets do some averaging to see if we can better pick out the common signal.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6127431439530390161.jpg"></p>The above data has been smoothed with a running average of 10 samples.  You can kind of make out parts that are in common to all three plots.<p>Okay, this 60 min chunk is getting boring, lets look at the whole thing.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8832621439530608329.jpg"></p>As you can see not all voltage references are created equal.  Now if you look at the yellow plot the real question is which voltage reference is drifting, the one being measured or the one in the Zig-8?  Well if I was only measuring the yellow one it would be pretty hard to say, hopefully now you can see the wisdom in measuring more than just one supposedly great voltage reference.  By monitoring more than one it becomes a statistics game and in this case the yellow one is the odd one out.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3748071439531131379.jpg"></p>Applying averaging again and you can start to make out the commonality between signals.  However looking at what is common is still not a perfect method for recreating the noise and drift signature of the SiGZiG because there are slight variations between each channel as well as the fact that each channel is not sampled at exactly the same moment in time. Overall though it still gives a much clear picture than just monitoring a single voltage source when down at these small levels.<p>So how did the silver oxide battery compare?</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8051501439531707094.jpg"></p>Actually not that bad, we are talking 140&#xB5;V of drift over 10 hours.  Even so it definitely puts the others in perspective.  <p>And what about temperature?</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8257861439531869174.jpg"></p>I noticed some of the changes in temperature seemed to match up with variations in the voltage of the silver oxide battery.<p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">After scaling and overlaying you get this</span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6609401439531978823.jpg"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">If you had a good enough data logger you could almost use a silver oxide battery as a temperature sensor!... okay no you couldn&apos;t but you can obviously<span class="redactor-invisible-space"> see it is affected by temperature.</span></span></span></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>

                    <p>In the world of industrial field instrumentation--flow meters, temperature sensors, level transmitters, gas analyzers, etc--24vdc/4-20mA current loops are the gold standard and have been since they took over from 3-15psig air signals half a century ago. Some instrument manufacturers try to push advanced digital protocols (such as Foundation Fieldbus and Profibus), but the vast majority of&nbsp;facilities I come across (upper Midwest) still use current loops for remote indication, control, and logging.&nbsp;</p><p>1mA - 1A would cover the range, and low uA resolution would certainly be adequate for industrial datalogging. However, you'll find that customers in the instrumentation sector will often specifically ask for a 4-20mA system--it's the only thing they've known in a lot of cases.</p><p>This might not be relevant if industrial instrumentation isn't a market you want to pursue. However, I've been in that world for some time and there is a distinct lack of good, cheap, compact, rugged, easy-to-use dataloggers out there. <br></p>
