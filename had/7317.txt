                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">It is known that White LEDs produce very poor spectrum (=color rendering) because they are effectively blue LEDs with yellow phosphor (missing cyan and half of green wavelengths - right where our eye is the most sensitive). This is probably the last major show stopper for complete LED dominance. <br /><br />I've fixed poor spectrum of white LEDs by adding 495nm cyan LEDs, 520nm green LEDs, and 460nm blue LEDs in first iteration. In second iteration I've used 470nm blue instead of 460nm ones to get even better and flatter spectrum. Now I believe it has better spectrum than one can get from best ceramic metal-halide high-intensity discharge lamps.<br /><br />I've placed spectrum correcting LEDs on the top of the fixture, so that they illuminate the ceiling, giving "blue sky" effect. Your limited RGB display (as well as my limited RGB digital camera) just cannot pass you how beautiful this color is - very tender, mild bluish/cyanish. It literally brings smile to my face every time I turn it on ☺</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Many years ago I wanted to have really bright light. I went way too far with <a href="http://3.14.by/en/read/nuclear-light-fixture-100klm">100&apos;000 lumen HID fixture</a>. It was so bright that I eventually left only 1 out of 7 lamps on which gives &quot;mere&quot; 14700 lumen. The light was nice and white, but it was far from perfect:  it required 5 minutes cool-down to reignite it, 2 minutes warmup and was frying insects... It&apos;s spectrum had no gaps, but several distinct peaks (see first update for spectrum). </p><p>That is when I started thinking about switching to LED with corrected spectrum. <br></p><p>Our eye is the most sensitive in the green region, that is why having corrected spectrum in this area is essential for comfort of our eye. White LEDs give you alot of blue and orange-red colors, but not cyan-green ones. Advertised CRI index is misleading, as it is calculated by precision of rendering of a small, fixed set of colors. This is probably the last obstacle to LED widespread penetration including areas requiring perfect color rendering. Some people just don&apos;t like the hole in the green-cyan. </p><p>I&apos;ve decided to try various combinations of extra LEDs to fill in the gap. Here is how generic White LED spectrum looks like (grey), and how I managed to correct it (blue):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3346941439841068780.png"></p><p>Final correction details are as follows: 40W 4000K white LED, 10x0.7A 495nm cyan, 5x0.5A 470nm blue, 5x0.5A 520nm green. </p><iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/QYaGaQIQk68" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe>
                                
                            </p>

                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7317/log/23602#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>With new 490nm and 470nm LEDs I can finally proceed: </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/966141439845279607.jpg"></p><p>470nm LED came close to specs:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6420701439846630858.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7203471439843458883.jpg"></p><p>With 10x0.7A for Cyan LEDs step-up regulator is taking too much heat, so I had to add few extra radiators:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1932531439846840673.jpg"></p><p>After fine-tuning current in the LED chains I&apos;ve got the final spectra:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9015921439843502383.png"></p><p>Finally there are no missing colors - this is something which could satisfy me. And &quot;blue sky&quot; effect is really nice. As we were mostly adding LEDs near to eye (and camera) maximum sensitivity, light feels much brighter than before (as well as photo exposures). </p><p>Is there a visible difference? Yes, but not looking at spectrum analyzer it is hard to show you on the RGB display with RGB camera. I have cyan shirt for example, and it&apos;s color is visibly different under new lighting - but making and comparing a photo won&apos;t show much - camera renders it bluish, while in reality it is slightly greenish (regular LED on the left, corrected LED on the right) :</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8260241439843778085.jpg"></p>Personally, I am more than satisfied by light itself and I like it much more than my <a href="http://3.14.by/en/read/nuclear-light-fixture-100klm">older 100k lumen HID fixture</a>. <p><strong>Future plans:</strong><br></p><p>This is not the end of the journey though - I ought to replace step-up converters with ballast resistors with a proper constant current ones and make it RF-controlled (nRF24LE01). I am going for nRF instead of hiped WiFi ESP8266 because I personally cannot tolerate wakeup latency. </p><p>Instead of using standalone constant-current ICs I am going to drive mosfets from uC PWM pins while monitoring current. As there are no fast current changes this should do the job with very simple schematic. <br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7317/log/23574-moar-leds">MOAR LEDs!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">9 hours ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7317/log/23574#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So I needed to add more LEDs. Apparently just 5x cyan LEDs cannot cover the hole left by 40W white ones, so I used all 10 cyan I had on first fixture (initially I planned to use 5 on each), and set them to maximum rated current (700mA), tuned 460nm blue die form RGB LEDs to get as flat as possible, and the best I got was this: </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5755671439842979352.png"></p><p>Much better than original white LEDs, but there is still a hole near 470nm, not as terrible as before though. It was clear this is as good as one can get with LEDs I had. If I increase current on any of them - I&apos;ll get overshoot. So I needed 470nm blue LED instead of 460nm one to fill in that hole. I ordered LEDs and started to wait...<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7317/log/23557-base-led-fixture">Base LED fixture</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">10 hours ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7317/log/23557#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>I really liked simple geometrical forms, so I got square 40W 4000K LED fixtures. They were relatively inexpensive (~40$ each), their large area ensures decent cooling and long life. I disassembled one to figure out if it&apos;s possible to stick LEDs inside - but there is just too little space left for high-power LEDs.</p><p>Fixture design is quite clever, like on LED display back light: There is acrylic light-guide with diffuse dots across the area to make it uniformly lit. light-guide is lit from the side.   <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7148551439842185900.jpg"></p><p>After fixing minor issues (like black marker marking on the edge,tighter assembly to improve thermal resistance) I assembled it back. It was clear I can only add LED on the back, facing to the ceiling. That might be interesting effect though. I glued first LEDs using ALSIL-5 glue and generic &quot;heatsink plaster&quot;. </p><p>For first revision I decided to use step-up regulators with ballast resistors to control the current over LEDs. I set the current in &quot;hot&quot; state. Surely in the future I will need to replace it with digitally controllable constant current sources.<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5717961439842410588.jpg"></p><p>First tests shows that with 5x cyan alone spectrum is very far from desired. I needed more LEDs, so I added RGB ones, intending to use green and blue dies. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8011411439843260561.jpg"></p><p>Current adjustment near the ceiling feels a bit tricky:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4365821439845459133.jpg"></p><p>With green on I&apos;ve got the following spectra:<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5873801439842656589.png"></p>There is definitely a progress, but still very far from desired.
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
