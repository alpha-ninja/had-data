Read on in our blog: <a href="https://www.coconauts.net/blog/2015/02/26/watchduino2-first-prototype/">https://www.coconauts.net/blog/2015/02/26/watchduino2-first-prototype/</a></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8052501439675463592.jpg"></p><br></div>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23029"><p>On spring this year (we tweeted about it <a href="https://twitter.com/rephus/status/572543084371820544">https://twitter.com/rephus/status/572543084371820544</a>) we got ourselves a Prusa 3D printer, so naturally we wanted to use to to make a neater printed case for WatchDuino!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3731111439721375964.png"></p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1197091439721411415.jpg">Our main challenge was actually to find a strap wide enough, that looked good with the design of the watch. For the future we want to experiment with printing with flexible filament, so that we are able to redesign the case, including the strap.<br></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7244/log/23032-migrated-android-companion-app-to-cordova">Migrated Android companion app to Cordova</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7244/log/23032#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23032"><p> As you know, much of the heavy lifting of the WatchDuino apps is actually not done in the watch itself, but on the phone. We made a framework for serializing and handling the bluetooth communications from and to the watch. </p><p>For WatchDuino to work, a companion app needs to be running on Android as a service. This companion app contains the messaging framework, plus the bit of logic for every phone app, that usually is in charge of querying some web service and producing a response with data to pass to the watch.</p><p>For the sake of debugging and testing, we made a simple interface to the companion app so that we can easily pair the devices, inspect all the messages going through, plus triggering notifications from each of the apps, or simply ping the watch to test the connection.<br></p><p><img style="width: 407px; height: 726px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4314651439722967671.png" height="726" width="407"></p>
<p>One of our biggest sources of frustration when developing WatchDuino2 has been working on the Android companion app. Android apps need to be programmed in Java, which is not a great language for rapid prototyping.</p><p>So we decided to switch to Apache Cordova (<a href="https://cordova.apache.org/">https://cordova.apache.org/</a>) a couple months ago, which is a framework for writing Android apps as if they were web applications, by providing a set of libraries to access the mobile APIs from HTML5 and Javascript. </p><p>Being web developers we are much more comfortable with this kind of stack, so we migrated all of the functionality of the companion app to a new one written with Cordova.</p><p><img style="width: 390px; height: 694px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1516851439723104156.png" height="694" width="390"></p>
<p>Now we are able to make progress faster, with better code, and less bugs. Much win!</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7244/log/23034-watchduino-now-tells-you-when-your-bus-is-coming">WatchDuino now tells you when your bus is coming</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7244/log/23034#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23034"><p>We live in London, which is a huge city. Moving around can be a challenge with 11 different underground lines and over 600 bus lines (no joking! <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Buses">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Buses</a>).</p><p>Luckily, the TFL (organization managing all things Transport For London) does a wonderful job at having real time data for the status of the transport, and not only that, it also exposes that data though an API. Then other wonderful apps such as Google Maps or Citymapper can make use of it to help you find your way around the city.</p><p>When you are waiting on a bus stop in the middle of winter in London you can&apos;t help but to wonder when your bus is due. And you want to do without taking your gloves off also! Wouldn&apos;t it be great to just know just by looking at your wristwatch?</p><p>Now you can, with WatchDuino2! We&apos;ve built an app that will show a list of the next busses, with times, that will arrive at the bus stop where you are staing. When entering the watch app, WatchDuino will contact your Android phone to request for the bus info. Then the Android app with grab your geoposition and query the TFL API to compute your nearest bus stop and the times of the next buses arriving, and send that info back to the watch.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8140051439724707932.jpeg"></p>5 minutes till bus 19? Uhm, might still have time to grab some chips at McDonals!<br></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>

We first pair the application and then trigger some notifications both from the watch (when we enter the weather app for instance), or from the phone (using the testing buttons in the companion app to simulate a notification).</p><p>Turn on the subtitles for further explanations of what&apos;s going on at each point.</p><p><iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/LVE85OxWGzs" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" width="560"></iframe>Apologies that the development version is not soldered and assembled into a nice and pretty case!<br></p></div>
