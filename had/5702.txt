                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1>Big things come in Tiny Packages<br></h1><p><strong>PICxie</strong> is a very tiny (<strong>1"x1"x1"!</strong>) but feature packed development kit for the Microchip PIC18F series of microcontrollers. Specifically the <strong>PIC18F26J50</strong>.</p><h2>Powerup your Projects with PICxie!</h2><p><strong>PICxie</strong> is a stand-alone development kit, but is also the perfect addition to any project, simply attach it to an I2C bus and you're good to go! <strong>PICxie</strong> is <em><strong>jam packed</strong></em> with tons of features; We hate having to change <em>batteries</em> so as with most things we make that are battery powered this one is <strong>rechargeable</strong> vis USB. <strong>It's also programmed via USB using Microchips preloaded HID bootloader firmware.</strong></p><p>There is a <strong>MicroSD</strong> card slot on the top for transferring data between your PC and <strong>PICxie</strong>, and an <strong>IR Receiver</strong> to receive commands <strong>wirelessly</strong>! <strong>PICxie</strong> includes <strong>full color internal RGB lighting</strong>, making it possible to the color change depending on different conditions such as <strong>orientation, environmental factors, or local magnetic field strength!</strong></p><p>An<strong> Accelerometer/Magnetometer combo</strong> for accurate orientation tracking. <strong>Altimeter, Barometer, and Thermometer</strong> for environmental mapping. And lastly an included<strong> Microphone and Speaker combo</strong>, so <strong>PICxie</strong> can hear you and you can hear it!</p><p>The best feature is the <strong>exposed I2C bus and 3.3v power lines</strong> on the bottom, this means it's easy to make <strong>PICxie</strong> interface with your existing projects <strong>as if it was part of it all along.</strong></p><h2>PICxie Provides Limitless Potential</h2><p>Combining everything you could ever want into one small, self-powered, and portable package makes <strong>PICxie</strong> the last solution you'll ever need for embedded systems development. From environmental monitoring to robotics and beyond, <strong>PICxie</strong> pushes the limits as to what a development kit should be</p>
                                
                            </p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5702/log/22457-the-big-question">The Big Question</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">7 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5702/log/22457#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So if you&#x2019;ve been following the development and news about our latest project you might be left asking yourself one question -</p><h2><strong>What exactly is PICxie?</strong></h2><p><i>&#x201C;PICxie is a ultra-portable development kit for Microchip Technology microcontrollers.&#x201D;</i></p><p>We&#x2019;ve taken the traditional idea of a development kit and spun it around on its head! We&#x2019;ve taken most of the features you&#x2019;d expect in a development kit and shrunk it down and crammed it into a <strong>1 inch cube!</strong><br></p><img src="http://41.media.tumblr.com/57c08ec2cbfb4dc01bbf6bab0423362d/tumblr_inline_nsw081JOPa1sidqgl_500.jpg"><p><strong>PICxie </strong>has a built in <strong>microphone and speaker combination, full-color RGB lighting, a MicroSD card slot, and an internal rechargeable lithium battery and more</strong>! PICxie is loaded with powerful suite of features letting you writing cool programs that can do things like:</p><ul><li>Log the local temperature, altitude, barometric pressure, orientation, acceleration, or any combination thereof! (Think of a something like a blackbox for RC planes!)</li><li>Sound detection and recording</li><li>Servo motor control</li><li>IR signal detector</li><li>Local magnetic field strength detector/logger</li><li>And more!</li></ul><p>And the <strong>4-pin breadboard compatible I2C expansion bus (I2C and 3.3v Power)</strong>and extremely small size make it possible to <strong>power your projects with PICxie</strong>! We&#x2019;ve used PICxie to make interesting things like:</p><ul><li>Model rocket altitude, orientation, and GPS location logger</li><li>Mobile robot</li><li>Burglar Alarm</li><li>Universal Remote Control</li><li>Wireless environmental monitoring</li><li>And MUCH more!</li></ul><h2><strong>Who will benefit from PICxie?</strong></h2><p><strong>PICxie </strong>is a powerful and robust platform for learning embedded development and coding, and is a perfect complement to intermediary STEM courses focused in those areas. It&#x2019;s also great for anyone with a serious interest in those fields, as it will teach you how to interface with things like IR receivers and MEMS devices, and use the I2C bus to interface with your own hardware on a breadboard.<br></p><h2><strong>Why should I choose PICxie over any of the other development kits out there?</strong></h2><p>Three simple words: <i>Size, Cost, and Functionality</i>. When designing PICxie we already had a good idea of what the competition was. Our main realization was that the cheap ones had no features and were simply a bit more than a break-out board for the MCU. The feature rich ones are usually more expensive, and are definitely not as portable.<strong> With PICxie you get 90% of the product in a 90% smaller package!</strong></p><h2><strong>OK&#x2026;but how much will it cost?</strong></h2><img src="http://40.media.tumblr.com/10e6f4fd77cd57028b1e041107620e2d/tumblr_inline_nsw2osq0OH1sidqgl_400.png"><p>If you&#x2019;ve spent anytime near a manufacturing or engineering environment you should be aware that out of the following: <i>Quality, Price, and Features</i>; you can only pick 2. Here at MOARobotics we thought that we could beat that thinking and created a product that could encompass all three categories.</p><h2><strong>$54.99USD!</strong><br></h2><p><strong>PICxie </strong>costs a mere $59.99USD and could potentially get cheaper depending on demand! Right now our two biggest hurdles are the casing and components. Both of those get cheaper in volume and extrapolation tells us that we COULD potentially offer <strong>PICxie </strong>for ~$35-$40.00USD depending on how popular <strong>PICxie</strong>was.</p><h2><strong>Kickstarter - Bringing it all to the masses!</strong></h2><p>We will be launching a Kickstarter for <strong>PICxie</strong>! This project culminated in a product that we feel fills a void in the market for a affordable and portable development kit. We primarily need the Kickstarter for the following reasons:</p><ul><li>Bringing our product out into the market</li><li>Developing new products in the future</li><li>Buying equipment to make our products even more affordable</li><li>Simple publicity</li></ul><p><strong>PICxie </strong>is already set to go we just have to draft up our Kickstarter, get a nice presentation video made, drum up some momentum, and conduct our beta test prior to doing it. We think you&#x2019;ll really like <strong>PICxie</strong>, and with your help we can bring a really great development kit out into the market! Speaking of betas&#x2026;<br></p><h2><strong>PICxie Beta B</strong></h2><p>If you&#x2019;re one of the people who like free stuff (and who isn&#x2019;t :)?) and are interested in getting a <strong>FREE</strong> <strong>PICxie </strong>to try out, please signup here, we have about <strong>87</strong> signups at the time of the writing, but only 10-25 people are going to be selected. Signup today...</p>
                                
                                    <a class="readmore" href="/project/5702/log/22457-the-big-question">Read more &raquo;</a>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5702/log/19880-mplabx">MPLABX</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5702/log/19880#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So we got the new ICs today and immediately switched out the PIC18F26J50 that was bad with a new one and we now have USB! YAY! Unfortuantely we hit another snag because for whatever reason, when our code is compiled with MPLabX it will not run when loaded on PICxie. The bootloader comes up fine, and we can load it, and it verifies perfectly. But PICxie just sits there.</p><p>If we build the EXACT same code but use MPLab v8.92 everything works fine, we're not really sure whats going on, but until we get it resolved we're going back to MPLab v8.92 so we can actually get some demos going!<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5702/log/19654-picxie-is-born">PICxie is born!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5702/log/19654#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So we’ve been putting PICxie through it’s paces, one disappointing thing that seems to have happened is that the PIC18F26J50 that we got seems to have a bad USB transceiver! No matter what we do we can’t get the USB the respond. We have 3.3v on the VUSB pin, the oscillator signal is very clean. There is continuity between the end of the cable and the pins on the PIC. After a lot of head scratching we finally just put a project on that had only this code:<br></p><pre class="hljs cpp"><span class="hljs-constant">UCON</span> = 0;<span class="hljs-constant">UCFG</span> = 0;UCFGbits.UPUEN = 1;UCFGbits.FSEN = 1;UCONbits.USBEN = 1;</pre><br>On the prototype this causes the device to be detected, on the current assembled PICxie nothing happens. All the relevant pins are exactly the same voltage between the two, so at the point we can safely assume the PIC is at fault which isn’t common, but at least now we can order a new one and get the USB bootloader tested.<p>As far as the other hardware it’s all working, and we have the ICSP port so we can test everything still. We took a trip from Columbus up to Johnstown, and it was awesome to be able to log the temperature, altitude, barometric pressure, and magnetic field strength during the trip. We’re already working on a GPS module that you can place PICxie into because we’d like to make it easy to be able to correlate the data to the GPS location as well.</p><img src="http://36.media.tumblr.com/818ec04d0946ec452f2f4c29222e8575/tumblr_inline_nq3exrcePI1sidqgl_500.jpg"><p>I apologize for the colors not being in proper order ^_^ but here is some bonus assembly PICs to make up for it.<br></p><img src="http://40.media.tumblr.com/64d2850a73c47391975e6226f791c2a8/tumblr_inline_nq3fl1pQwh1sidqgl_500.jpg"><p>We’ll have more PICs and hopefully a video or two up this weekend some time so stay tuned!!</p>
