                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The test bench uses the stepper motor to turn a "throttle shaft" approx 90°, stopping every 0.75° to take an ADC measurement of the Throttle Position Sensor being tested. Usually, 120 or more values are measured and then put in an array and analyzed for standard deviation and also some strict bounding to make sure the plot doesn't wave around too much. The bench decides if it's good or bad. If it's good, the response plot is printed out on the Thermal Printer with a summary of the analysis. <br /><br />Started out trying to use a Seeedstudio small-format AVR MEGA, but needed more RAM, so ended up with the ChipKIT uC32. More complete write up is in the Adafruit Forums link until I get more info on here.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Oh, right, almost everything you see in the bench is 3D printed from my Airwolf 3DXL. </p><hr><p><strong>Next:</strong></p><p>Although this test bench is awesome and has found plenty of parts we consider not perfect, Real Life still finds ways to make our parts not work sometimes. So I am planning V3 of the test bench to use the actual throttle bodies from the vehicle for mounting the TPS to be tested. Also, since the serial thermal printer is painfully slow, I want to print to our networked Epson POS receipt printer. It's fast. And has a cutter. And bigger replacement rolls of paper.</p><p>There will probably be another ChipKIT uC32 (no actually <a href="http://www.digilentinc.com/Products/Detail.cfm?NavPath=2,892,1193&amp;Prod=CHIPKIT-WF32" target="_blank">their new WF32</a> - wifi and 128K of SRAM!!) and Adafruit 1.8" TFT. That is unless trying to print to the Epson is too difficult. We already print to it from local ubuntu webserver, so there's a change I'll get infected by the whole raspberry PI thing.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>
