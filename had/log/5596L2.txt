                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5596/log/19298#discussion-list">
                                    31 comments
                                </a>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19298"><p>EMdrive test results. Looks plausible - i think we have thrust :)<br>The platform was initially spun counterclockwise. After a whike (indicated by the red bar) the thruster was switched on. <br>Left: Thruster oriented retrograde (increasing rotation rate decay)<br>Right: Prograde (decreasing rotation rate decay)</p><p>The data of higher rotation rates is a bit jumpy. The reason is that to get the rotation rate without much noise, i sample the duration between the peaks of the platform´s imbalance during rotation. The shorter they are the less precise the measurement - but the trend is clearly visible.</p><p>To get the proper time, devide X by 16.</p><p>Changing from retrograde to prograde was obtained just by turning the cavity around. Cables, batteries and other componets remained in the same place and orientation.<br><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5503941433964812887.png"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5596/log/19330-original-recordings">original recordings</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5596/log/19330#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19330"><p>Here are the original recordings of the first test.<br>The curve shows the rotation rate given by the gyro. What looks like a rectified sine is due to the imbalance of the platform performing an unsmooth rotation. <br>I measured the rate by the distance between these peaks.<br>Maybe it can be measured smoother just by following the overall deviation - but I was afraid that eventual radio frequency drift could mislead the measurement (recorded via SDR, rotation rate is directly transformed into frequency)<br>I´ll implement a digital protocol for the gyro data later, but this SDR technique is what I already had, so I used that.</p><p>I´ll make another (smoother) graphs based on the overall deviation today (RF deviation seems not to be that problem)</p><p>First picture: retrograde, second: prograde, third: same prograde as second pic, but uncut graphics (longer unpowered time)</p><p>The thin line below indicates when the thruster was turned on (its the radio frequency of the remote switch)</p><p>Questions occured what prograde and retrograde exactly means in our setup:<br>Only the cavity orientation changed, other things of the setup remained unchanged.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7480871434013687127.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3454311434013707061.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5407861434014429716.png"></p></div>
