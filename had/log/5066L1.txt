                                <div id="post-body-15951"><p>1.  Get the 3D file right.  Make sure you have considered everything. </p><p>2.  Get  a very huge piece of foam !.  Cut the foam according to the dims on the 3D file.  If you have never done this before, start with very small models &amp; practice a lot.!!..</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9169641427930045071.jpg"></p><p>3. Use the foam cut to make the master mould for the FRP body...  Again, if you have not done this before, find a friend who knows what they are doing.. </p><p>4. Do all the prep work needed before making the mould form the foam cut..  </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9621631427929256222.jpg"></p><p>It eventually starts to look better !</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3806651427929602707.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/758011427929671151.jpg"></p><p>It is absolutely essential that you choose all your accessories, lights, door handles &amp; everything else before you start !.  They all have to fit on the body... <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5276871427929786942.jpg"></p><p>Do not forget to make your doors, book lid &amp; Bonnet cover !!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7838371427929962113.jpg"></p><p>After the foam cut is perfect, it is time to get to the making the mould..  Unless you have serious experience, try this with small scale models first.  I would advise getting an experienced friend to help you or ask advice from any local companies who make fiberglass / FRP / or GRP products.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7349311427986613270.jpg"></p><p>Once the mould is complete it time to start making the actual car body.  Hare we go with the first pics below.  Weight is critical in this project.  Using FRP gives us structural strength &amp; lightness without the need for expensive materials like Carbon Fibre. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6214401427986794122.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9157531427986909401.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8650701427986844546.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2027441427986961264.jpg"></p><p>Now is the time you can really see that advance planning is critical.  We selected all the lights &amp; other accessories at the start of the project.  All the components will slot perfectly into the body.  We can now really see the outline of the car. </p><p>Everything was going a bit too well.  Hit the first design issue.  Installing the automotive glass in the door was easy.  Installing the window winding mechanism was not too hard.  But, the base of the door is too small to allow the window to roll down !. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5724351428090458162.jpg"></p><p>Seems we will have to change the slip direction or add a frame.  Changing the slip direction is likely to work well short term but long term it will cause issues.  So, lets add a frame like this. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/572331428090666123.jpg"></p><p>I thing the frame can become an aesthetic plus feature !. </p><p>Frame idea worked !.  Here we are installing the winding mechanism</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9746431428090835292.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2092891428090911327.jpg"></p><p>We also completed the bonnet lid &amp; the boot lid.  As you can see, we have re-enforced these.  This is not for cosmetic reasons, it is for functional reasons.</p><p>Boot lid &gt;</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1081001428091206409.jpg"></p><p>Bonnet lid &gt;</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8022201428091263837.jpg"></p><p>I will explain about the front &amp; rear windows next....Windows are on a separate project log.</p><p>At the start of the project, we bought 'off the shelf' period parts from UK based classic car sites.  All the parts are available at a number of sites.  The parts come from old UK cars like MGs,  As we planned this early, the parts actually fit the body !.</p><p>1.  Front view showing headlights, wing mirrors &amp; door handles<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2153351429178937744.jpg"></p><p>2.  Side view. The back of the car looks great !</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8603861429179191939.jpg"></p><p>5. Sorry.  Same pic.                             <h2><a href="/project/5066/log/16007-building-the-component-testing-chassis">Building the &#39;component testing&#39; chassis</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5066/log/16007#discussion-list">
                                    3 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16007"><p>Before building any chassis there are  few essentials to think about.  If you ever want the car to be road legal, read up on legislation in your area.  This will vary from country to county, state to state.  Europe has one law applied by all member states.  However, there are entirely different interpretations in all EU member states.  In some member states, getting 'type approval' is nearly impossible.  In others, it is fairly straightforward.  Best if I write a separate log about regulation &amp; approval.</p><p>We designed  3D chassis to fit our body.  However, the project has some severe deadlines so we decided to build 2 chassis.  One is for 'component testing".  The other is the actual chassis.</p><p>Remember the basics a road car has.</p><p>A.  Very stiff chassis</p><p>B.  Keep all the weight low to the ground.</p><p>C.  Balance the weight equally over the 4 wheels</p><p>D.  Great steering, Great Brakes &amp; Great suspension.</p><p>E.  A decent amount of power.</p><p>F.  Keep it safe.  (safety deserves another project log all to itself)<br></p><p>In the case of the LUKA EV, we also have to live within the project goals so we need to keep everything very light.  The chassis also has to accommodate in wheel motors &amp; 2 battery packs .  (Bat packs will also have their own project log)</p><p>Lets start with the 'component testing' chassis....</p><p>Keep it as true to the actual design a possible.  But, do not bother getting it milimeter accurate &amp; leave out any parts that are 'non essential' for component testing.  We got help from a company in the UK.  They build street legal kit cars.   We took their basic 'approved' chassis overall structure &amp; used the same principles in our chassis build.  It starts out fairly simple.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3216981428013210391.jpg"></p><p>This outlines where the 'not entirely usual' bits like the Bat packs &amp; hub motors go.  </p><p>Fabricating this is not rocket science.  Lots of straight lines &amp; easy welding.  There will be a lot of project logs so I will not go into all the details &amp; bore you to death.  Sometime later, the chassis looks like this.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8905041428013663627.jpg"></p><p>This is a bit more than the 'bare bones'.  This has various key components attached.  The drivers seat is attached, the steering wheel in place &amp; the front suspension complete.  We used bought in parts for brakes, suspension, steering because they are not expensive, they are safety certified &amp; they are tried &amp; trusted.  If you are not a car person &amp; do anything like the steering geometry or suspension yourself, get an expert to check it... Remember, you will probably have to test drive the car yourself !!</p><p>On the 'component testing' chassis, we are not bothering with non essentials like a passenger seat.  We are also omitting the 'top half' of the chassis.  While the top half will add extra stiffness, the idea behind the component testing chassis is to ensure hub motors function well, the bat backs fit in their compartments, we can set up the BMS, we can set up the motor controllers, Regenerative braking works etc.  Of course, also test 'normal' systems such as brakes, suspension &amp; steering are suitable.</p><p>The cross beams are essential.  Without them, the car will just flop all over the road.  </p><p>Next, we completed the rear suspension, added some wheels &amp; we have a 'rolling chassis;</p><p>This is a front view of the rolling chassis.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9121231428014668779.jpg"></p><p>We added a floor.  You can see the steering geometry, front &amp; back suspension, brakes etc.  If you look closely, you can see that this car is designed to have no front or read axle.  There will also be no drive train, no gears, no exhaust.  This is all possible because we are using hub motors.   The next work is pipe the brakes &amp; fill / bleed the system. Once that is done, take you 'car guy' hat off.  You are going to need to become an electrical engineer.  The future  steps are about connecting up the hub motors, programing the motor controllers, building the battery pack, configuring the Battery Management System (BMS) &amp; making sure the BMS &amp; motor controllers all communicate well together... </p><p>Here are some of the components for Electrification of the vehicle.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6568331428825919889.jpg"></p><p>&amp; one half of the battery pack.  The car has a front &amp; rear battery pack.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5485821428826009958.jpg"></p><p>Parts of the BMS. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4267841428828132905.jpg"></p><p>Still more BMS parts !</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/850591428828210956.jpg"></p><p>After a lot of time &amp; effort, the motor controllers &amp; both battery packs are installed in the car.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8160451428826175617.jpg"></p><p>Next, a drivers seat !. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7409111428827165772.jpg"></p><p>The chassis is now ready for its first test drive.  We are testing each motor.  In this video, the chassis is only driving with the left side motor. </p> <iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/3p9rI_rc9oA" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/dvDoH3b6cKc" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><br><p>We learned a lot from the first test drive.  It is our first proof of concept.  All the bits work. Interestingly, the chassis performs relatively well driving on a single hub motor.  This may be an interesting 'contingency feature' for the production car.  Similar to an aircraft, if a single engine fails, the vehicle will still be able to drive.  Next, we will get the car back into the shed &amp; start the process of tuning the systems.  The various noises were expected &amp; will be eliminated by aligning motor controller &amp; motor settings. <br></p></div>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16077"><p>Selecting glass is something you need to consider at the design stage of a project.  If you intend to just build one car, you should compromise your overall design to allow 'off the shelf' front, rear &amp; side windows to be used.  Remember, you must use certified auto-glass for safety &amp; certification reasons.  In our build, we intend this as a production car &amp; the shape of the windows in critical to the overall appearance of the car.  Not knowing the first thing about glass &amp; not knowing anyone who knows anything about glass, the only route to go here was to get a contractor to make the glass.</p><p>Unfortunately, I still do not know how they make the glass !.  However, the glass was made &amp; it fits perfectly.  The down side of getting glass made is that the tooling costs a lot.  The upside is that the glass for all future cars is much cheaper than 'off the shelf' parts from existing car models.  Apart from cost concerns, getting custom glass allows you to stay true to your original design principles.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4537551428162229525.jpg"></p><p>Here is one of the machines at the glass factoty</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2985451428162334091.jpg"></p><p>&amp; more factory pics</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5007201428162389111.jpg"></p><p>&amp; the final pic</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/488501428162439772.jpg"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5066/log/16658-iva-individual-vehicle-approval">IVA (Individual Vehicle Approval)</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5066/log/16658#discussion-list">
                                    6 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16658"><p><strong>Getting the car street legal.</strong></p><p>This is for sure the least exciting &amp; possibly most difficult part of our car build.  It is also something we had to spend a lot of time on before we even started.  When you look at it first, it is daunting.</p><p>Although I a based in continental Europe, I decided early on to go for approval in the UK.  The UK has a tradition for 'small series' vehicles that many continental countries do not have.  The actual rules are the same in all European countries.  However, different countries have different ways of looking at the rules. </p><p>In the link below, you can find the simple 299 page document telling us what we need to do to get approval.  Yes, that is not a Typo..  It really is 299 pages.</p><p><a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/354071/M1_IVA_inspection_manual.pdf" target="_blank">https://www.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/354071/M1_IVA_inspection_manual.pdf</a></p><p>In the knowledge that no one will actually read the document, below I give a summary as to how it related to the LUKA EV.  Once you get trough the nonsense, it is broken into sections.  We have to comply fully with all sections.</p><p>Section 1.  Noise.  All about exhaust systems.  We do not have an exhaust so we skip this section</p><p>Section 2a   Visual emissions.  We are electric so no worries for us</p><p>2b.  Metered Emissions.  We are all electric so we can skip this part too..</p><p>2c.  Diesel emissions..  Not for us !</p><p>3a.  Fuel Tanks.   Not for us, we do not have one !</p><p>4.  Rear registration plate space. Tells us how big the reg plate has t be, where it has to go, tht it has to have a light etc.  Easy, just follow the rules.</p><p>5.  Steering effort.  All the rules about the steering.  We use bought in parts.  The steering is form  Mazda MX5 so we know we will pass on steering.</p><p>6.  Door latches &amp; hinges.  Our doors are custom as is our car but the latches &amp; hinges are bought in parts so no worries on this section.</p><p>7.  Audible warnings.  In a nutshell, the car must have a horn. Easy...</p><p>8.  Indirect vision.  All about mirrors.  How adjustable they must be, what size they must be. etc.  Not rocket science, just follow the rules even if the rule is dumb.. </p><p>9a.  Brakes..  The brakes should work...Must have a handbrake too..  We have bought in parts (again Mazda Mx 5) so no worries with the brakes </p><p>9b.  Brakes.  Mechanical components.  As above, bought in parts so no worries.</p><p>9c.  Parking brake..  Must have one.....no problem</p><p>9d.  More brakes...  This time about hydraulic &amp; vacuum components.  Again, no worries as we use bought in parts.</p><p>9e.  Brake performance.  They will test the brakes.  Ours are very good because the car is very light..</p><p>10.  Electromagnetic Compatibility.  They probably put this section in just to confuse anyone who got this far.  The HT ignition system must be fitted with radio interference suppression equipment. </p><p>11..  Section 11 does not exist.  Weird but true.</p><p>12.  Interior fittings.  Quite complex rules about the distance things should be from each other, details about the 'specified zone', bluntness of objects.  Nothing difficult but great attention to detail required.  Section 12 is a minefield !. </p><p>13A. Anti theft / immobilizer .  The car must have one.  Surprising but not difficult.</p><p>13B.  Anti theft / Alarm.  Must have one of these too.  </p><p>14.  Steering.  Must have good steering.  We use bought in steering so no worries on this front.</p><p>15.  Seat strength.  We are using Mazda MX 5 seats so no problems here. </p><p>16.  Exterior projections.  Loads of rules about everything on the outside of the car.  Badges, emblems, handles, bumpers.  Like section 12, nothing is difficult but huge attention to detail is required to ensure you do not forget anything.</p><p>17.  Speedometer &amp; reverse rear.  Quite interesting rules about the speedo.  Th speed it tells you does not have to be right, just within acceptable limits.  If the speedo tells you you are going 70km/h, you must be going at least 57km/h.  Wonder who makes the rules up !.   Speedo must be back light.  Car must have a reverse rear.</p><p>18.  Statutory Plates.  Basics stuff about having a VIN number etc on the car.  Easy..</p><p>19.  Seat belt anchors.  Details about anchoring the seat belts. We are using a non-standard (but ultra safe) anchor method so we could face some issues on this point but we should be OK. </p><p>20.  Installation of lights.  Another minefield.  Absolutely nothing difficult but every box must be ticked.  Many of the rules are unexpected.  It is simply a case of following the rules to the letter.  There are many lights you might forget. The fog lamp, the reverse clear light etc..</p><p>21.  Retro reflectors.  There must be enough of them &amp; in the right places.  Some of the specific rules are good like  "rear reflectors must face predominately to the rear'.     &amp; Jes, I was gonna have them facing to the front.....</p><p>22.  More lights...  Another minefield going into details about things like daytime running lamps..</p><p>23.  Indicators.  Must have them, they must make noise &amp; you must be able to see them on the dash inside the car.  Easy stuff.</p><p>24.  Rear Reg Lamp.  Must have a light over the read registration plate.  Easy...</p><p>25.  Headlamps.  Rules about headlamps.  No particular worries for us.  We have 'retro' headlamps but we replaced the actual lights with LEDs to save electricity.  Should be OK for certification purposes. </p><p>26. Front fog lamps..  Car must have them..  Easy...</p><p>27.  Like section 11, it simply does not exist !</p><p>28. Rear fog lamp.. You have to have one...  Easy...</p><p>29.  Reversing lamps..  Car must have them.  Easy...</p><p>30. Parking lamps.  Car must have them.  They must emit red light to the rear.  Angles of the light emitted etc.  Easy stuff.</p><p>31.  Seat Belts. We use bought in parts from another car so no worries on this score. </p><p>32.  Forward Vision.  The driver must have a clear and un-obscured view of the road ahead and to 90Â° each side of straight ahead</p><p>33.  Identification of controls.  Another nightmare.  Simple stuff but every switch must have the correct symbol on it....All those little symbols you never notice like the one for the headlamp beam.. A lot of our switches re bought in parts so they will have markings.  Te laser etching machine will be busy on the ones that do not have markings.</p><p>34.  Defrost / Demist.  W mush have demist / ventilation.  Not a problem !. </p><p>35.  Wash wipe..  Details about windscreen wipers.  Bought in parts so no problem. </p><p>36.  Heating system.  Approved bought n part. No problem.</p><p>37..  Wheel guards.  Car must  have wheel guards... Easy.</p><p>Sections 38 to 43 do not exist, like 11 &amp; 27...</p><p>44. Masses &amp; dimensions.  Gives the max size &amp; weight of allowable vehicles.  Definitely not a problem for us....</p><p>45.  Safety glass.  Specifications for the glass to be used &amp; the markings on the glass.  We had our glass made to these specifications &amp; it has all the markings so no worries here.</p><p>46.  Tyres.  Specs of tyres.  No problem.</p><p>47, 48,49 do not exist.</p><p>Section 50.  Couplings.  If we were towing anything..  Does not apply to us..</p><p>Sections 51 to 59 do not exist.</p><p>60.  Frontal protection (bull bars). Must have an e mark</p><p> It finishes at 60..  BUT...  There is a section about 'general construction'..This is mush less specific but states than things should not leak etc..  This is probably where they can fail you if you have met all the requirements 'to the letter' but they think you have not met the spirit of the regulations.</p><p>Overall, none of the requirements are very difficult.  There is just a lot of them !.  Success just needs a detailed check list to be checked again &amp; again.  I am sure no one will ever read this project log but I thought I would write it just in case anyone wanted an overview of requirements......<br></p></div>

If we were to achieve our target with just 2 motors, it would be  huge bonus (but, we are fairly sure real world driving may need 4 motors with the back 2 being for normal driving &amp; the front 2 kicking in for hard stuff like overtaking, climbing hills etc....<br></p></div>
