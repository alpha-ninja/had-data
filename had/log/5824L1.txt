                            <h2><a href="/project/5824/log/17974-planning">Planning</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5824/log/17974#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-17974"><p>We didn&apos;t even know where to begin. No money, no parts, only enthusiasm and time.</p><p>To start, we looked at the current models. What&apos;s wrong with them? What can we improve on? What do they do well?</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4610661431701436976.jpg"></p><p>The list was surprisingly long.</p><ul><li>Struggles to pick small things up off the floor</li><li>Heavy objects (like a bag of pasta, 450g) cant be lifted. </li><li>All weight is leveraged on the user&apos;s wrist. Hurts me to lift a bag of pasta.</li><li>Trigger mechanism is difficult to pull, and wobbles around</li><li>Trying to pick up a credit card from the floor would often end up sending the card flying (and under the fridge)</li><li>Good wide jaw </li><li>Rubber handle was comfy</li><li>Very lightweight</li><li>Easily stored</li></ul><p>Overall, we weren&apos;t impressed. My arms began to feel strained after only 5 minutes of messing around. If I was relying on this thing, I would hate it!</p><p>We decided that we would need a list of requirements for our project - that was the next task.</p><p>The group had an after-hours meeting to discuss every aspect of the product we could think of and the vote on what was most important. It is difficult for us to judge this, because we are not the end user, but we decided on factors that we thought would be important to someone in that position.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5824/log/21251-design-development-part-1">Design + Development Part 1</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5824/log/21251#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21251"><p>Since the beginning of the project, a lot of work has gone into making our ideas a reality.</p><p>First of all, we worked hard to nail down a specification. This would be the guiding hand that would shape the project, and give us direction when we lacked it.</p><p>Secondly, lots of research was carried out into topics that we felt we lacked knowledge of. For me, this research covered Inductive Charging (wireless charging), Li-ion batteries, methods of charging li-ion batteries and the usage of servomechanisms. Separate research documents were written up and presented to the rest of the team.</p><p>I have never worked with Wireless charging, servos or Li-ion batteries before, so this research helped me choose the right parts for the job, and brought in new considerations to the design of the PCBs and chassis.</p><p>Next, we could move on to the fun stuff!</p><p>The system is pretty simple (in theory).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8902471437474578042.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8513691437474654904.jpg"></p><p>We have a good idea of how the CyberReach will look. The users wrist and forearm fits inside a 3D printed cylinder, called the Chassis. The chassis is the hub of the modular design and everything else attaches to this hub.</p><p>The Arm is a piece of square aluminium box section with a military-style RIS-rail glued to it. The RIS-rail attaches to either the left or right side of the chassis (there are mounting points on each side) for use with either of the user&apos;s arms.</p><p>The grip slides over the Arm, and its position is adjustable to suit the user. This Grip contains the control mechanism, i.e. a trigger or switch, that the user activates to actuate the grabber. This actuation is supplied by a servo that sits behind the mounting rail on the chassis. It is connected, via cord, to the jaws at the other end of the arm (not pictured). For safety, the servo is covered with a special 3D Servo Cover.</p><p>All of this is powered and controlled by a massive 6Ah Li-po cell and AVR microcontroller inside the Controller, and recharged via an inductive charging coil sat on the downwards facing side of the Controller Lid.</p><p>For ease of manufacturing, we are planning to print the Chassis as a single piece - which  would include the Mount Rail, Controller Mount and Servo Mount. We hope this will increase structural strength and produce a sleek product.</p><p>This should also help to reduce cost as we don&apos;t have access to a 3D printer and we must outsource the design to a company. This really is eating into our development budget, which is funds raised through fundraising events, and is also the limiting factor in how many prototypes we can produce.</p><p>3D Modelling is also very time consuming and the team of 3 in charge of the models are working constantly to hone and perfect the models. Our first print needs to be perfect, we can not afford for it to be wrong.</p><p>As for the electronics, we are looking to manufacture a batch of single-sided PCB&apos;s with chemicals we procured for free. This should also keep costs down, but the health and safety problems associated with carrying out such work is delaying the process somewhat. </p><p>Part 2 soon.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5824/log/21676-3d-models">3D Models</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">22 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5824/log/21676#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21676"><p>PROGRESS!</p><p>We now have the chassis, electronics box and arm mount modelled!<span></span></p><p>We have access to a Statasys Objet Eden260VS on site, with 16micron layer height. Theyre making us pay for it though which kinda sucks but hey, its super high quality gear!</p><p>Heres the models so far.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2855211438099657506.PNG"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2932551438099694080.PNG"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6893621438099712053.PNG"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9852681438099723010.PNG">As you can see we have gone with a picatinny rail commonly seen on military rifles. This is a fantastic method of securing our parts and requires minimal tools to secure.</p><p>We have also incorporated a set of plastic tabs that we hope will hold the aluminium arm in place tightly with no tools.</p><p>The idea is that this can be disassembled easily by the end user if needed, and also to reduce the part count. We have no idea if these tabs will work, I know PLA is quite brittle, but its only a couple of mm.... right?</p><p>If they break its no problem we will just secure another way. Its worth trying!</p><p>We also have the charger built, out of lasercut clear acrylic - it currently looks terrible so i&apos;m not inclined to post pictures of it until we paint it up :)</p><p>Electronics is coming along well, we have a pair of boards designed on Altium and EAGLE that are ready for fabrication. Etchant and developer chemicals are available to us for free, so this will be a DIY job for two lucky team members (probably me).</p><p>The boards are pretty simple, will post them when theyre done.</p><p>We&apos;re nearing the end of our design deadline so expect updates in the next few days.</p></div>
