                            <h2><a href="/project/4628/log/14995-first-thoughts">First thoughts</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4628/log/14995#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-14995"><p>The first thing I want to care about is the most critical part: the radioisotope and Geiger tube, plus shielding.</p><p>My choice of isotopes is pretty limited: </p><p>-U-238, contained in some old Fiestaware plates;</p><p>-Am-241, from smoke detectors;</p><p>-Th-232, from lantern mantles;</p><p>-Ra-226, from radium watch hands.</p><p>My choice is mainly dictated by how safe I want it to be: nothing in a powdered form, I want a solid source, that won't scatter easily if breached. </p><p>The Ra-226 is out of the question, as it is simply painted on zinc sulfide, the contamination risk is way too high. </p><p>Am-241 emits lots of alpha, but much less gamma, so not that great, since my GM tubes are only beta and gamma sensitive. Besides, there's a contamination risk should the source be released from its bracket.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9920921426115691702.JPG"></p><p>I'm still hesitating on Th-232 or U-238: the lantern mantle has the advantage of being easy to wrap around the tube, but can scatter if burned (the cotton burns out, leaving a brittle ceramic structure that breaks easily and gets in the air, causing a serious contamination danger). On the other hand, the Fiestaware plate chip is solid and shouldn't scatter in case of a fire, but is much hotter (read radioactive) than the other samples I have; more shielding may be required.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1560691426066457065.jpg"></p><p>As for the Geiger tube, I'll get started with a си-3бг (SI-3BG) beta and gamma sensor. It isn't very sensitive, that's why I need a source to get enough counts and use the thing. However, this tube is very small, so it should be easy to fit it in a small leaded enclosure. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7142651426115679968.JPG"></p><p>I will use lead for shielding. As Th-232 and U-238 does not emit enough beta to create significant <span class="st">bremsstrahlung</span> radiation, especially with the weak sources I use. Actually such shielding may look like overkill, but I want this thing to fit inside a pocket! I don't want to detect <em><strong>any</strong><strong>thing </strong></em>outside the casing.<span></span><br></p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4628/log/15488-yay-progress">Yay! Progress!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4628/log/15488#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-15488"><p>So... I've decided to use another Geiger Tube. Instead of using the SI-3BG, which isn't very sensitive, I'll use the classical СБМ-20 (SBM-20), it'll generate more counts, but will need a bigger enclosure.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7337981426953551469.JPG"></p><br><p>The first enclosure (which will hold the radioactive sample, shielding plus Geiger tube) is a small plastic box I bought on eBay for about 5 bucks. The tube fits inside nicely!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8556791426953566527.JPG"></p>I'm still working on the 400V power supply. I've already made a first try which includes the clicker (copied directly from Brohogan's design), but I'll chop off that part in the next version, andjust keep the power supply and the microcontroller interface...<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1085201426953700057.JPG"></p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6856751426953729429.jpg"><span></span><br><p>That circuit board should be ready by next week!<br></p></div>

                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4628/log/15623#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-15623"><p>I've finished printing the circuit board, it should be completed by this evening. </p><p>In the meantime, I made some progress on the source. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/182041427285984736.JPG"></p><p>The Geiger tube is wrapped around the lead shielding with the radioactive source. I closed the ends by gluing lead strips around the edges, and used a lab NaI scint counter to check for leakage. Nothing seems to get out of it, we'll see in the long term how the shielding holds. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9129941427286494029.JPG"></p><p>A small, self-contained source plus tube!<br></p></div>

Got HV on the Geiger input!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1097821427628884802.JPG"></p><p>I quickly programmed a small sketch that does the math and counts the pulses in the Arduino Leonard.
