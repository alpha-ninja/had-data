                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20325"><p>I just got the software working for the slaves that acts like Synergy! With all the pixel counting too! Right now it is only on the Teensy 3.1 with the USB Host Shield. It only runs one screen, but its super fast and the mouse cursor runs off one side of the screen to the other to simulate switching. Lets get right to the video:</p><p><iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/nM5UWKJUxMc" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><strong>Side Note:</strong> I just downloaded CamStudio (Open Source). It probably needs some new codecs (ffdshow) installed, so sorry the video is 581MB, 22sec, and 10 lousy frames per second. You will have to believe me that is is much much smoother than it looks, until I can get better screen recording.</p><p><strong>In the entire video, the mouse position is constantly estimated without any drivers or communication with the computer.</strong><br></p><ul><li>Mouse movement is super fast!</li><li>Left, right, and middle buttons.</li><li>Mouse moves to center of screen one time on Teensy startup.</li><li>No set up.</li><li>All Win7 settings, like acceleration, work great.</li><li>No screen switching at protected corners, size adjustable.</li><li>Mouse cursor bumps against the left or right and hangs until you push it more.</li><li>The size/force of the bump forces switching, and its adjustable.</li><li>The screen simulates switching by wrapping the coursor to the other side, like Synergy would.</li><li>The serial monitor shows x/y estimating.</li><li>All calibration for guessing the x/y position is continous and unnoticeable.</li><li>Mice are hot swappable.</li><li>Just as fast as a direct connection (unnoticeable with hand and double click speed :)</li></ul><p><strong>Still A Lot of Work To Do:</strong><br></p><ul><li>Set up I2C for the slaves.</li><li>Sketch needs to be cleaned up and tested on the Teensy-LC.</li><li>Sketch for the Teensy 3.1 as a master.</li></ul><p>The setup, from the last log:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5231151435797783751.jpg"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/2872/log/20789-i2c-challenge">I2C Challenge</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2872/log/20789#discussion-list">
                                    4 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20789"><h2>I&apos;m very happy with
the progress of this right now!</h2><ul><li>Last week was my
first SPI project!</li><li>This week is my
first I2C project!</li><li>I can&apos;t tell you how
happy I am that both are working side by side. 
</li><li>I think I bricked a
Teensy!</li><li>Check out the fast
I2C mouse action in the videos!</li></ul><p>(Turn the sound off &#x2013;
annoying fan noise in background.)</p><p><strong>First up</strong> is one
mouse, a Teensy 3.1 with a Win7 on the left, and a Teensy-LC on an
Ubuntu 14 computer on the right. The two microprocessors
simultaneously act like mice, and the mouse movement data that is
scrolling on both serial monitors is identical.</p><p><iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/NeNT2yIcDd4" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><strong>The second</strong> video is
setup for high contrast. Up until now I was skeptical there would be
some sort of noticeable delay or sluggishness in the mouse movement.
Both mouse cursors seem to move identically as if they were from the
same microprocessor. I was also afraid that SPI and I2C would have
eternal conflicts, but both work great, even when hot swapping the
computers USB ports.</p><p><iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/FiDH2ihF7lo" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><strong>Saved the best for last</strong>. <strong>They are Not going from one screen to the next.</strong> They
are each individually just wrapping the mouse cursor from one side to
the other of the same screen.</p><iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/eHtEdovJTQo" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><p>( Firefox is
displaying Hackaday message notifications on both computers :)<br></p><p><strong>This is a big
milestone for me.</strong> This means all the software will work. It won&apos;t be
long until I have it switching automatically. There is no data being
sent from the computers at all, so it maintains the &#x201C;driverless&#x201D;
feature. Not only are the mice hot swappable, but the computers are
also.</p><strong></strong><hr><h2>My First I2C
experience</h2><p>This was kind of
scary because of what looked like an infinite amount of I2C problems
online, some requiring expensive test equipment to diagnose. There
were a variety of pullup resistor suggestions online ranging from
190ohms to 10K. I found Paul&apos;s wire library information to be simple
and straight forward at <a target="_blank" href="http://www.pjrc.com/teensy/td_libs_Wire.html">PJRC.com</a></p><p>My desk is a mess :)</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4893621436550378029.jpg"></p><p>I had two 4.7K
resistors, but one looked deformed and the other I broke a lead off.
I had some others ready if I had to experiment with different values.
I soldered in two 2.2K resistors and it worked the first time with no
hardware adjustments. I will be ordering better headers for the farm soon.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1179001436550960948.jpg"></p>Bottom view:<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5913851436550281955.jpg"></p>Opps! Is this better? Resistors are on the top side.<h2><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5047631436550506446.jpg"></p>I bricked a
Teensy-LC :(</h2><p>I am hoping I might
find a cure. I have not researched it yet because I have been so busy
with the software. My meter says it has 5V and 3.3V on the board,
otherwise very brickish. It seemed to happen when I was screwing up
the I2C bytes being transferred.</p><h2>My workarounds, for
now.</h2><p>The Teensy 3.1 acts
like the master and as the first slave at same time by mashing the
software together, then the Teensy-LC is added as a second slave. It
works for now, but I want to eventually separate them to leave as
much spare processing power as possible on the master. </p><p>That will
allow more flexibility for hardware accessories like encoders and
other sensors, or software patches like global acceleration and cross
platform gestures for example.</p><h2>I used the basic
Arduino wire library</h2><p>Having very small
functions that are coordinated together worked the best. I used:
write three bytes, end, on receive get three bytes, repeat. I didn&apos;t
know I would have to calculate by own method of transferring 7 bit
bytes on both ends with 8 bit positive and negative mouse data (-127
to +127 for each axis). The Arduino site is usually not much help,
but this worked. I kept it really simple.</p><p><a href="http://playground.arduino.cc/Main/WireLibraryDetailedReference">http://playground.arduino.cc/Main/WireLibraryDetailedReference</a></p><p>I2c and big numbers: <a href="http://forum.arduino.cc/index.php?topic=78528.0">http://forum.arduino.cc/index.php?topic=78528.0</a></p><p><a href="https://www.arduino.cc/en/Tutorial/MasterWriter">https://www.arduino.cc/en/Tutorial/MasterWriter</a></p><p><strong><strong>Translation</strong> of previous paragraph :</strong></p><h2>#include &lt;Wire.h&gt;</h2><p>Master:</p><pre class="hljs cpp"><span class="hljs-keyword">int</span> actualX;

                            <h2><a href="/project/2872/log/22103-transparent-acrylic-inlays">Transparent Acrylic Inlays</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">15 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2872/log/22103#discussion-list">
                                    15 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22103"><p><a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/laser-cutting"><strong>Ponoko Laser Cutting Service:</strong></a><strong> Front Panel Tests.</strong></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/356861438667742401.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/556961438667705587.jpg"></p><p>3mm clear acrylic skull inlays in black (painted) acrylic, with two 2mm red LED eyes. The red LEDs are not connected yet, but look like tiny jewel domes in the light. There are some more pictures below.<br>The inlays can be done in a variety of <a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/make-and-sell/materials">Ponoko&apos;s material combinations</a>. I would like to play with inlays in wood, or in felt for clothes, but those are different projects.</p><h2></h2><h2>&#x201C;Make Your First Product for Free&#x201D;</h2><p>I totally fell for the &apos;sign up now&apos; because I was thinking I would eventually need to for the coupon I won.</p><p><strong>The Catch:</strong></p><p><strong></strong>Anyone can sign up for an account, and to find out there are 10 days left to use a $20US discount towards a laser cutting service only, not including shipping.</p><p>That actually was not a bad deal for me. I have never ordered any cutting or 3D printing before, so I knew for sure I would make a mistake on my first design. Not wanting to mess up a $100 order, I decided to play along and jump through the hoops. I thought the OpenSCAD Skull from the <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/2872-driverless-mouse-and-keyboard-sharing/log/20943-human-interface">Human Interface log</a> would be easy to send off. That might have been easier if it was 3D printed, but the preferred method of this laser cutting service is colored SVG files.</p><p><strong>Ponoko has some great instructions for first time users.</strong></p><p>Following the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/make-and-sell/design-it-yourself">design-it-yourself starter kits</a> has to be fool proof, right? Fearing my LibreOffice Draw would be too quirky, I opted for the free Inkscape option so I could follow the directions. The good news is I learned some Inkscape in a few days. It&apos;s important to understand how lines connect with nodes, and how to watch for tricky connections and overlapping lines. The Ponoko directions in the starter kits are great, but now that I understand SVG files better, I will only be using LibreOffice.</p><p><img style="width: 309px; height: 344px; float: right; margin: 0px 0px 10px 10px;" alt="" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2164301438668022175.jpg" height="344" width="309"></p><p>The upper left image is the 0.010mm width blue cut line for the laser cutter (No square corners).</p><p>The other five are me playing with different line widths to get an idea of what the cut pieces might look like. The cut pieces ended up almost identical. </p><p>The second is the actual laser cut width of 0.40mm (+0.20mm on each side of the cut line.) </p><p>The third is the skull subtracted from material, then material subtracted from skull, overlaying both, and last with the 2mm LEDs I ordered.</p><p>I made two test face plates 150mm x 70mm but without the USB holes. Also some extra skulls, as recommended in the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/starter-kits/inkscape#inkscape_section_7">Ponoko directions with tiny pieces</a> (&lt;5mm can fall through the bed.) Also some circles as bases to mount the skulls. I also found How to <a target="_blank" href="http://blog.ponoko.com/2010/06/17/how-to-make-snug-joints-in-acrylic/">Make Snug Joints in Acrylic</a> helpful to prevent cracking in the corners.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1259061438670344206.jpg"></p><p>Cutting the inlays the same size made for a loose fit (0.4mm on each side). Next time I will get closer by cutting different size shapes outside the panel. Another mistake was not putting through holes through the circle bases for the LED leads, just like I did on the 3D prints also.</p><h2>Inkscape vs. LibreOffice Draw</h2><p><strong></strong>Inkscape shows the instructions outside the boarder, <br>and LibreOffice shows the actual drawing.<strong></strong><br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7250391438668446630.jpg"></p><p>Adhesive paper holds parts in place. Scorch marks on paper only. This saved almost all of the 1.6mm diameter 3mm long cylinders cut out from the eyes!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9247761438668520210.jpg"></p><h2>Material and Cost</h2><p>Ponoko recommends testing designs in cardboard for affordability, but I chose to see the more expensive 3mm anti static clear acrylic, 181 x 181 x 3mm (P1). The edges are sharp and clean all the way through. <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8080621438668807670.jpg"></p><p>It was three days for processing, one day to make and ship it, two day priority mail, making it <strong>six days</strong> total from idea to in my hands. $22 is $2 over the $20 discount, plus 9.55 shipping, means the <strong>total cost was $11.55.</strong> I made a few mistakes, but now I can order custom material inlays with more confidence for a variety of projects, so I would say getting my feet wet with this was worth it.</p><p>Some more test pictures:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6505391438668941362.jpg"></p><p>These LEDs looked so much bigger on the computer screen when I ordered them.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3881501438669130646.jpg"></p><p>This is with the paper backing on both sides:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2594761438669158897.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2575771438669273418.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8838761438669318851.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1138141438669417157.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6465411438671821501.jpg"></p><p>Stacked and extended out 3mm past the panel:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5382731438669554036.jpg"></p><p>Recessed 3mm into the panel:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8413221438669652200.jpg"></p><p>Recessed eyes:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6520751438669717190.jpg"></p><p>That&apos;s all for now. Back to the hardware until I get more case dimensions. <br></p></div>
