                            <h2><a href="/project/4972/log/15721-udp-repeater-or-increasing-range">UDP repeater - or increasing range</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4972/log/15721#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-15721"><p>The main issue with the system is that bluetooth low energy just doesn't seem to have the range. I've tried extending range by <a href="http://www.instructables.com/id/Increase-and-extend-the-range-of-a-USB-Bluetooth-d/">hacking the aerial</a>, but it doesn't make much difference.</p><p>After putting the dongle on a usb extension cable at my parents the range got a lot better. I'm assuming that getting it further away from the wifi dongle (both at 2.4GHz) reduced interference and improved range.</p><p>Strangely, doing the same thing in my (much smaller) flat - it didn't make much difference.</p><p>So how to increase range. Well my laptop is on a fair amount, so while it's on why not use its bluetooth to pass the message on if the wristband is out of range from the pi?</p><p>If the daemon is started with <code>--udp_repeat</code> then it will broadcast any wristband messages (default 192.168.0.255).</p><p>The <a href="https://github.com/mattvenn/energy-wristband/blob/master/daemon/udp_repeater.py">udp repeater</a> program can be started at boot, and will pass on messages received over UDP to the wristband if it's in range.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-17144"><p>Ross gave me back the wristband after a few months of testing. They'd made a <span>nice case at DoES Liverpool (</span><a href="https://twitter.com/cheapjack/status/591216586276605952" target="_blank">https://twitter.com/cheapjack/status/591216586276605952</a><span>) that was based on some real energy data!</span></p><p>I made a little test house so people could try turning on and off various <span>appliances and feel the wristband in action.</span></p><p>One of the problems I had at the start was very unreliable bluetooth <span>communications. When I ran `hcitool lescan` I found hundreds of devices due to </span><span>all the mobile phones nearby! Increasing the timeout used by `gattool` to 20 </span><span>seconds improved reliability a lot.</span></p><p>Some photos of the wristband project at the UK Maker Faire 2015 (<a href="https://plus.google.com/u/0/photos/109869064118515349190/albums/6142516349786315393" target="_blank">https://plus.google.com/u/0/photos/109869064118515349190/albums/6142516349786315393</a>)</p><p>And I've updated the beta tester instructions with a nice picture of all the parts (<a href="https://github.com/mattvenn/energy-wristband/blob/master/docs/closed-beta.md" target="_blank">https://github.com/mattvenn/energy-wristband/blob/master/docs/closed-beta.md</a><span>)</span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2646261430394611136.png"></p></div>
