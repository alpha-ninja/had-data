                            <h2><a href="/project/6872/log/21649-rookie-mistake">Rookie mistake</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">22 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6872/log/21649#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21649"><p>I got a test board today from OSHPark. This one is an LCD with a VCOCXO controlled by a 20 turn pot. The board has a pair of D-PAK 78xx regulators on it from a 2.1mm barrel connector. </p><p>Well, I didn&apos;t do the math properly on power dissipation. The 7833 almost instantly got rocket hot and shut down.</p><p>Let&apos;s review: 1A drop from 6V to 3.3V is 2.7 watts. Turns out, that&apos;s too much to ask for a D-PAK package. I&apos;m going to have to go with a TO-220 through-hole regulator with a heat sink on it.</p><p>Some will ask why I don&apos;t just use a buck converter. That&apos;s a valid choice, and I&apos;ve done them before. But at high currents like that, keeping the power clean is a challenge, and this application is going to be quite sensitive to that sort of thing.</p><p>For this test board, I&apos;m going to just tack on a TO-220 package regulator and see how the rest of it works. The board around the OCXO was getting quite warm, even though the oscillator never actually started properly. That may wind up being an issue down the road as well. We&apos;ll hopefully know more later tonight. </p><p>EDIT: I wasn&apos;t able to get a TO-220 3.3v regulator this evening, but I&apos;ve temporarily separately powered the 3.3v rail from my bench supply. It&apos;s not the most stable thing in the world, but it works. With it, I&apos;ve been able to tweak the 20 turn pot to get it down to exactly 200,000,000 cycles every 10 GPS seconds - within 5 ppb of GPS, and it&apos;s stayed that way for a half an hour so far. Now, I have no idea how much phase noise is being introduced, but at least for this build, that&apos;s not really the point.</p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6872/log/21677-results-of-overnight-ocxo-testing">Results of overnight OCXO testing</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">22 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6872/log/21677#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21677"><p>Recall that last night I assembled a preliminary board that I had designed with a VCOCXO on it connected to a 20 turn trimpot. The output goes into an ATMega328p connected to an LCD. Originally, this was intended to be my final clock calibrating solution. I would periodically load firmware in to check the accuracy of the oscillator while feeding the controller with GPS PPS input, and load other firmware to check the accuracy of the clocks.</p><p>Despite the minor power setback, I did run it overnight with my little bench supply giving out 3.3 volts. It functioned fine. The board does get warm around the oscillator, thanks to its oven, but I don&apos;t think it&apos;s going to be a problem. You certainly don&apos;t want to attempt to dissipate that heat. If anything, it should help the rest of the analog components to be kept in a relatively stable temperature (whatever impact the absolute temperature has should wind up being cancelled out by the GPS feedback). At steady-state, the oscillator takes around 330 mA or so. It requires much more than that - closer to 800 mA - for the better part of a minute when it&apos;s starting cold.</p><p>After running overnight, it was running 1 count fast when I visited it this morning. Thats +5 ppb. A little tweak with the screwdriver brought it back down to 0. Even with a 20 turn pot, it&apos;s quite touchy if you&apos;re trying to manipulate it at the single-digit-ppb level. The spec for stability of the oscillator is only &#xB1;20 ppb, however, so there&apos;s really no way for me to tell whether there are short term aberrations that are averaging out over my measurement interval (in this case, 10 seconds. For the final 10 MHz product, it&apos;ll be 20 seconds), but at least for my use case, none of that is significant.</p><p>Once I have a prototype built, I plan on bringing it into the office to run it on a proper frequency counter to see if there are any such short term effects, and if so, their magnitude. My hope is that the stability will not be out of proportion to the longer term accuracy, but we&apos;ll have to see.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <small class="authorship">21 days ago  &bull;

                                    0 comments

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21736"><p>The first three boards coming will have the D2PAK 3.3 volt regulator.</p><p>I suppose one way to avoid having to just throw those boards away would be to build them with DOT050V oscillators instead of DOC020V ones. The former is a TCXO instead of an OCXO. Its stability is &#xB1;50 ppb instead of &#xB1;20 ppb. There&apos;s no particular reason you couldn&apos;t make the swap, and the TCXO requires vastly less current.</p><p>The downside is that using them in place of the OCXO only reduces the BOM cost by around 10%.</p><p>Maybe I&apos;ll build one just to see if there&apos;s a measurable difference between the two final products. If the discipline is capable of keeping either one under control, then getting rid of the high current and thermal problems would certainly be nice.</p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6872/log/21745-the-dangers-of-surplus-wall-warts">The dangers of surplus wall warts</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">21 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6872/log/21745#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21745"><p>I knew that I was going to need to procure a high current, low voltage power supply for this project. To that end, I went down the row of surplus wall warts at <a href="http://www.halted.com">Halted</a> a few days ago and picked what appeared to be a suitable candidate. It was rated 1A @ 6V.</p><p>Well, it&apos;s been powering the complete prototype project for a few hours now, but I began to hear a high pitched whining noise coming from the oscillator module.</p><p>Well, whenever your project makes a noise it&apos;s not supposed to make, the first thing you should do is put an AC coupled scope on the power supply lead at the component that&apos;s having trouble. Sure enough, there was 9 kHz ripple at ~600 mV P-P in 0.5 Hz pulses perfectly in sync with the noises coming out of the module.</p><p>So... that power brick is fired.</p><p>I&apos;ve moved things back temporarily to my desktop switcher, and the problem went away. I&apos;ll go back to Halted tomorrow and try to find another candidate.</p><p>With the switcher on duty, the power looks very nice - &lt; 30 mV P-P ripple. That ought to do.</p><p>EDIT: Turns out, it doesn&apos;t look like it was the wall wart after all. I came home and the noise and oscillations had returned. Perhaps it&apos;s the LT1117 regulator going into cyclic partial thermal shutdown or becoming unstable or something like that. I dunno. I&apos;m less interested now in tracking that problem down since I&apos;ve more or less decided to go with VCTCXOs for the project.</p><p>EDIT: Adding a larger heatsink to the LT1117 made the oscillations go away for good (on the board with the VCOCXO and the trimpot). But that heatsink is the size of Montana. I&apos;m glad I decided to go with VCTCXOs instead. My hope is that the total current draw for the whole project will be under 100 mA, which means that a D2PAK NCP1117 being heat-sunk by the PCB will be acceptable.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <small class="authorship">21 days ago  &bull;

                                    0 comments

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21771"><p>Yesterday I considered using one of the boards with a VCTCXO just to see if the extra $10 for a VCOCXO bought anything.</p><p>I haven&apos;t run that test yet, but I am beginning to think that the answer to that may be that it does not.</p><p>Looking at the DOT050V and DOC020V datasheets, both claim a short-term stability of 1 ppb (1.0E-9/s). Well, short term stability is all we really want to count on from the oscillator. Long term stability is going to be tied to GPS, after all.</p><p>Maybe that extra $10 and all the power supply hassles are for nothing.</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6872/log/22004-case-options">Case options</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">17 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6872/log/22004#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22004"><p>I&apos;ve started to consider how I might plant this project in a case. I am probably going to just sell the boards on Tindie, but at least being able to show <em>how</em> to box it up would be a nice thing.<em></em></p><p>For one, I&apos;ve designed the final board (not yet ordered - awaiting final electrical design validation when the prototype boards arrive Monday evening) so that the SMD LEDs are spaced to fit on a board-mounted light pipe array. The light pipes &quot;bend&quot; the LEDs 90 degrees so that they emerge from the front edge of the board as 1/4&quot; long 9/64&quot; diameter &quot;plugs&quot; on 1/4&quot; row spacing. The board is 2.55&quot;x1.90&quot;, and there&apos;s a number 4 hole at .15&quot;x.15&quot; inside of each corner. On the left edge, 3/4&quot; inch from the bottom is the power jack.</p><p>The idea is that one would mount the board jammed into the bottom left corner of the case. On the bottom edge, holes would be drilled to match the light pipe positions, and on the left edge a hole would be drilled to match the power jack position. The board would sit on standoffs and be screwed down to the bottom of the box. 3 BNC jacks would be attached to the front panel, and would be wired straight down to the output plug. The hardest part of that is that the BNC jacks fit into &quot;double D&quot; 3/8&quot; holes - that is, holes with a nominal 3/8&quot; diameter, but two &quot;flat spots&quot; on each side to prevent the jack from spinning. Lastly, a hole for a GPS antenna pigtail would go on the back panel.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6872/log/22104-prototype-performance">Prototype performance</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">15 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6872/log/22104#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22104"><p>The first prototype build was successful. I spent this evening debugging the firmware, but at the moment, I believe it&apos;s working quite well.</p><p>After the firmware was operating, it was able to keep fairly easily within 1 count every sample window, where a sample window is 25 seconds. In 25 seconds, there are 250,000,000 counts, so that alone means it&apos;s within 4 parts per billion. But for &quot;reporting&quot; purposes (that is, the front panel), a sample window of 20 samples are kept. That means that the measurement granularity is 200 ppt. The UI rules for that are that the &quot;fast blink&quot; happens when the error is less than or equal to two counts. That really means up to 3 counts, so that means it&apos;s within 600 ppt. The slower blink is when the error is &lt; 20 counts, which is 4 ppb. The next notch down is a super-slow blink, which means an error of 200 counts, which is 40 ppb. If the error is worse than that, the light stays off.</p><p>From a dead cold start, it takes about a minute for a GPS lock. If you add a CR1220 battery to the board, then it really only ever has to do that once. Warm GPS starts take seconds. Once the GPS lock is established, it takes 500 seconds (a bit longer than 8 minutes) for the sample buffer to fill. Once that happens, then the oscillator steering will begin. The prototype&apos;s &quot;sweet spot is a DAC value of around 0x80da or so, which is a voltage of 1.651 volts, so the oscillator I picked turns out to have been dialed in at the factory quite well.</p><p>The output is quite clean on the scope:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4688111438672587153.jpg"></p><p>Of course, that&apos;s how it looks right on the output terminal on the board. What it looks like after a foot or three of 50 ohm cable...? Hopefully not too much different.</p><p>EDIT: After leaving it all night, I believe I can confidently say that it&apos;s stable and accurate to at least 1 ppb on a long term basis. I am going to set up the led indications for 50 ppb, 5 ppb and 1 ppb as being &quot;ok, better, best.&quot; With several pages of deltas, the pattern that emerges is that a sizable fraction of them are 0, with some of them being &#xB1;1. While a delta of one count is a potential error of 4 ppb, it&apos;s also possible that it&apos;s a fencepost crossing of a smaller phase error. Whenever that happens, we give one count of offset to the DAC. Very occasionally we get a larger error, and most of the time, that&apos;s followed in the next interval by a matching opposite error. These likely represent steps of the GPS receiver as it makes some sort of gross adjustment to its own clock.</p><p>But the average error over the 500 second sample window stays comfortably inside of 1 ppb. Combining that with the oscillator&apos;s specification of a short-term stability inside of 1 ppb and I feel comfortable making that claim.</p></div>
