                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16024"><p>One of the most labor intensive aspects of growing tomatoes in Illinois is that you need to germinate the plant inside your home and then transplant the tomato plant to your garden when it is warm enough outside. This seemed like a lot of unnecessary work for me. With the Smart Garden's heating coils, I hope I will be able to germinate the seed outside in the planter without the labor of transplanting. I decided to wrap my planter in the heating coil used to heat bathroom floors.</p><p><a href="http://www.oemheaters.com/p-6109-12v-dc-heat-cable-5-wattsfoot.aspx" target="_blank">http://www.oemheaters.com/p-6109-12v-dc-heat-cable-5-wattsfoot.aspx</a></p><p><img src="http://www.oemheaters.com/images/Product/large/6109.jpg" alt="Close this window"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5037/log/16338-water-consumption-the-plant-knows-best">Water Consumption - The Plant Knows Best</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5037/log/16338#discussion-list">

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16338"><p>Providing the correct amount of water and nutrients to the plant is crucial. Too much water and the plant dies, too little water and the plant dies. For that reason, I will let the plant decide. I placed a liquid level sensor in the planter. When the water level gets low the sensor will tell the Arduino to turn on the water pump. This sensor was found on Adafruit.</p><p><a href="https://www.adafruit.com/product/464" target="_blank">https://www.adafruit.com/product/464</a><br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9223781428628068864.jpg"></p><br></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5037/log/16521-smart-garden-tarp-is-off">Smart Garden Tarp is Off!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5037/log/16521#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16521"><p>With the tarp off, The Smart Garden is starting to show its form! As previously stated, this project is aiming to mimic the science of a car engine. For any mechanics out there, think of the Smart Garden components like this:</p><p>Root System = Car Engine (In this case, I am working with a straight line 3 cylinder)</p><p>Arduino Board = Powertrain Control Module</p><p>8-Channel Relay = Power Distribution Center</p><p>Water Level Sensor = Fuel System Sensors</p><p>Temperature Sensor = Radiator Thermometer</p><p>Heat Cables and Fan = Radiator System</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8324531429036712654.gif"></p><p>You can also see how I am taping up the wiring similar to the wiring in a car engine (I still need to drill the temp sensor and water pump into the planter):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7252151429037218338.gif"></p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5037/log/16524-efficient-space-utilization">Efficient Space Utilization</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5037/log/16524#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16524"><p>When contemplating the utilization of space of my robotic garden, I thought back to my favorite story highlighting wealth creation abilities of my favorite business leader in history:</p><p> While seeking a way to improve the efficiency of the exterior floor plans of the restaurants he operated, this man decided that the heating and air conditioning systems on the side of the building were an inefficient use of space. He called up his HVAC company, and told them that he wanted them to install the machines on the rooftop of his restaurants...they thought his idea was crazy, but ended up fulfilling his odd request. To this day, despite the ample space on the side of most buildings, suburban and rural commercial buildings have these machines on the roof.   </p><p>The Smart Garden's place in the  farming landscape is to provide a backyard appliance for the suburban residential setting, and more importantly, to enhance the viability of the green roof by providing not just energy efficiency to the large building, but also a profitable food byproduct. In this way, the Smart Garden pays homage to this great businessman.   </p></div>
