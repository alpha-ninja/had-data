                            <h2><a href="/project/6485/log/21503-floppy-drive-frequency-response">Floppy drive frequency response</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">25 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6485/log/21503#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21503"><p>While playing songs on the floppy drives I couldn&apos;t help but notice that different notes played at different volumes, and the 2 drives I had had very different responses to the notes.</p><p>To see what was going on, I wrote a quick little program to go through a range of notes and measure the volume of each one, per floppy drive. These are the results:</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/HsDsAe2.png"></p><p>The X axis is the MIDI note number. Look for a MIDI note chart to see what frequencies those are or what piano keys they would be.</p><p>I made measurements in 2 units, RMS and Peak, per drive. Peak is pretty much the highest sound in that 25th of a second or so, averaged over 5 seconds, while RMS is a &quot;Decibel&quot; of sorts. It&apos;s a log scale, but it&apos;s just an arbitrary unit because calibrating it to the Decibel scale would be way too much effort for this little program.</p><p>There&apos;s quite a bit of interesting information on the graph here. Let&apos;s start with drive 1 (green). It&apos;s pretty clear that it&apos;s best range is between 23 and 51. After that it goes surprisingly quietly. Until it reaches note 66, which I would guess is it&apos;s resonance frequency. It&apos;s important to note that this graph only shows volume when the note command is sent. It does not necessarily mean it was actually making that note, as is the case for values above around 72 or so, where the drive starts stuttering from struggling to keep up with the high frequency. At note 84, the motor just gave up and the drive stood completely still. At this point, I need to physically disconnect and reconnect the power to get the drive working again for lower notes.</p><p>Drive 2 (red) is pathetic. It is quite soft for most notes, and gets a little inconsistent in volume where drive 1 starts going softer, though it can actually play notes 65 and 66 properly. It also survives through the whole range of notes, even though it has a much smaller stepper motor.</p><p>I&apos;ll repeat this test when I get more drives. I&apos;d like to incorporate this data into my program somehow, but I&apos;m not too sure how I would do so. One thing&apos;s clear though: Never send note 65 or 66 to Drive 1, unless we&apos;re trying to make a very weird kind of dubstep. Also, avoid notes above 72 which temporarily disable Drive 1.</p></div>

                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6485/log/23426#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23426"><p>I&apos;ve finally decided to solve all my power supply problems. Currently, I have seperate power supplies for my 3D printer, my Raspberry Pi, my thermal printer, my various miscellaneous projects and now I need a power supply for my floppy drives. I plan to fix all this with one big old ATX power supply.</p><p>It&apos;s going to be hooked up to my all purpose Raspberry Pi server, which controls the standby/active state of the power supply with both a physical button and software commands. This power supply will also power my 3D printer, and my floppy and disk drives. I&apos;m sure it won&apos;t be a problem, seeing as floppy drives and HDDs are designed to be powered by these things.</p><p>I also plan to finally integrate something I&apos;ve wanted to do for a while now: put the Pi in charge of the orchestra. I&apos;m going to try and make a little server program that my GUI client would connect to, and send over a processed MIDI file for playing, since my Pi is also an Octoprint server. That way, I won&apos;t need to keep changing what my 3D printer is plugged into.</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/Jo6JpDH.jpg"></p><p>I&apos;ve updated the main page to include a &quot;System Design&quot; reflecting how this would all work. I&apos;ll also include it here because those thumbnails are weird:</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/WEIkSEu.png"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6485/log/23436-possible-new-instrument-doorbell">Possible new instrument: Doorbell?</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6485/log/23436#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23436"><p>I finally found that old doorbell I wanted to perhaps use. It&apos;s a really old fashioned one with a solenoid hitting metal plates for the sound.</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/9cRWiDh.jpg"></p><p>It&apos;s design is simple yet clever. A little double sided hammer sits in the middle with a spring and solenoid around it. When the doorbell is pressed, the solenoid activates and slams it into the shorter metal plate, making a &quot;ding&quot; sound. When the button is released, the solenoid deactivates and the now compressed spring swings it into the longer metal plate on the other side, creating a &quot;dong&quot; sound.</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/QWOf5GJ.jpg"></p><p>An interesting solution to put the delay between the two notes in a &quot;ding-dong!&quot; sound. Now, how to exploit that...</p><p>I could, perhaps, try using PWM to put the hammer in a position where it could slam either of the metal plates just as hard. changing the duty cycle to 100% would play a ding, and putting it to 0% plays a dong. After 50ms or so it would return to whatever PWM value results to it sitting in equilibrium. I&apos;m not sure how well this plan might work, but it&apos;s worth a try. It&apos;s not that far off from how drums currently work, which is a 50ms long LOW-HIGH-LOW pulse.<br></p><p>Getting it to be useful in a song is another problem. It isn&apos;t really percussion, and it can only play 2 notes. I could set it up so whenever those notes play in a certain channel, it would play the note instead of a floppy drive, or alongside a floppy drive. For some reason, I&apos;d imagine the classic Super Mario theme wouldn&apos;t sound too bad if some of the notes were played by a doorbell. I&apos;m not musically talented enough to tell you what notes in the song match, and therefore would be replaced by the doorbell&apos;s notes, but that&apos;s my control software&apos;s job, not mine :D</p></div>
