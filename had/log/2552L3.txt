                            <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21140-welcome-to-the-open-source-future">Welcome to the Open-Source Future</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2552/log/21140#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21140"><p>This blog post was originally published on the <a href="http://go.farmbot.it/2015/01/22/welcome-to-the-open-source-future/">FarmBot Blog</a>.</p><p>A common misconception I have heard when I tell people about the FarmBot Project is that being open-source rules out the possibility of making money. Plain and simple, that is absolutely not true. In my opinion, being open-source is the unique facet that is even allowing me to <em>start</em> a company and movement around FarmBot. There is no way I would have been able to attract a team or get the financial backing I needed by working in a proprietary manner &#x2013; the barrier to entry would have been too high.</p><p>Longer term than the startup phase, there are plenty of ways to monetize an open-source project. I believe that there is <em>always</em> value that can be added (and sold) on top of a free and open-source project. I&#x2019;ll give just a two examples that pertain to this company:</p><ol><li>Software as a service &#x2013; Not very many people want (or have the technical ability) to setup and maintain their own instance of the FarmBot web app. Instead, they will opt to pay a small monthly fee for hosted service for the value of convenience, saved time, the latest features, and support.</li><li>Hardware kits &#x2013; Sure, anyone can look at a FarmBot&#x2019;s bill of materials and buy all of the parts themselves from 50 different places on the Internet. But most people just want to buy one thing from one place with the assurance that everything needed will be in the box with a nice instruction manual and the backing of a company&#x2019;s support. Again, they will be paying for the value of convenience, saved time, and support. Additionally, economies of scale may allow the company to produce the final product at a lower cost and higher quality than possible for an individual to do so. So add in the value of saving money and getting something of higher quality.</li></ol><p>It&#x2019;s clear to me that open-source can be very profitable, even lucrative, if there is a viable business plan of providing value added service or product on top of the open-source base.</p><p>In fact, current trends are showing that open-source is becoming a <em>necessary </em>component to the business model; that in order to compete in the marketplace, a company <em>must </em>make at least some of their product or service open-source. Otherwise, consumers will want nothing to do with it because some value will be missing (such as data freedom, hackability, or a price of $0) that an open-source alternative may offer.</p><h2>Open-source is quickly becoming the <em>gold standard</em> that consumers demand.</h2><p>As open-source ideals become more prominent in mainstream society, they will infiltrate all disciplines and cause radical disruption of all industries &#x2013; there is no going back and no stopping it from happening. Once there is open-source <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux">software</a>that fulfills the same function as the proprietary version, what developer would want to go back? Once there is a <a href="https://www.opengov.com/">government</a>that prioritizes transparency, what else would citizens accept? When there is a <a href="https://bitcoin.org/en/">currency</a> that allows us to use our money in the way we want to, or a <a href="https://diasporafoundation.org/">social network</a> that gives us true ownership of our data, or a <a href="http://www.spacex.com/hyperloop">transportation system</a> that can connect the world, or an <a href="http://wikipedia.org/">encyclopedia</a> that anyone can edit, or a <a href="https://phonebloks.com/en">cell phone</a> open to customization, or a free<a href="http://openfarm.cc/">resource</a> that let&#x2019;s us know exactly how to grow our food, or a company that empowers us to <a href="https://www.ifixit.com/patagonia">repair</a> their products, or<a href="http://openoil.net/">frameworks</a> for taking care of our planet&#x2019;s natural resources, or a school that adopts <a href="http://lumenlearning.com/">open educational resources</a>, or a technology that let&#x2019;s us <a href="http://reprap.org/">make anything</a> we can dream of, who would ever want anything else? Out with the old and in with the new! Proprietary is so last century, welcome to the open-source future!</p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21141-our-launch-strategy">Our Launch Strategy</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2552/log/21141#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21141"><p>Crowdfunding is our strategy of choice to launch the first ever widely available FarmBot hardware. This has actually been the strategy since I wrote <a title="The FarmBot Whitepaper" href="http://go.farmbot.it/whitepaper/">the FarmBot whitepaper</a> about 1.5 years ago and these are the reasons why:</p><ul><li>Being the first to market by launching a completely new type of product is a huge reason to crowdfund something. People are looking for the new, the cutting edge, the exciting when they back a project, and a crowdfunding platform such as Kickstarter sets the stage for making a big splash.</li><li>By creating a campaign that has a goal and finite amount of time to achieve it, you create a sense of urgency with your project that compels people to get involved now rather than later.</li><li>Having a campaign is a big draw for the press to cover the project, ultimately driving many more people to see the project than possible with individual outreach efforts. Your message will be amplified and your project spread far and wide &#x2013; much more so than quietly launching an online store and sending out some press releases.</li><li>Crowdfunding essentially allows you to take pre-sales without having to have the final product ready to ship at the time of collecting money. This will be very important for setting up manufacturing lines, ordering parts in bulk at discount, and building the team&#x2019;s capacity to execute and deliver the product.</li><li>You will be sure to get great feedback and validation on the product from hundreds or thousands of people, as well as new ideas to incorporate.</li><li>Crowdfunding can help build a massive community that is excited to use, hack, and contribute to the technology in a matter of only 30 days.</li><li>If the campaign is successful, you will make your business a whole lot more valuable to potential investors and also make a lot of progress without taking on any investment in exchange for equity.</li></ul><p>In short, a crowdfunding campaign does so much more than just raise money. It is also a fantastic marketing engine, press magnet, and community builder that will bring a lot more value to your project than you may expect.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21143"><p>The FarmBot core team is happy to announce the launch of <em>farmbot-serial</em>, a Ruby library that helps developers interface with FarmBot hardware while abstracting away low level hardware details.</p><p>It features an event based architecture via <a href="https://github.com/eventmachine/eventmachine">EventMachine</a> and will allow developers to create third party FarmBot applications for a variety of use cases.</p><p>The gem is in an public alpha state and we plan for a stable release within the next two months.                             <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21145-genesis-v06-documentation">Genesis V0.6 Documentation</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;

                                    0 comments

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21145"><p>We&#x2019;ve been a little behind on the documentation train and are trying hard to catch up. We just finished up the docs for <a href="http://go.farmbot.it/docs/farmbot-genesis-v0-6/">FarmBot Genesis V0.6</a>, check em out! Notice that these docs are now hosted on this website rather than the <a href="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/" target="_blank">community wiki</a>. Don&#x2019;t worry, we&#x2019;ll copy them over there soon enough, though we do want to make clear the separation between the community owned and edited wiki content and FarmBot Inc&#x2019;s &#x2018;pro&#x2019; documentation that will be here on this website. Basically, we can&#x2019;t guarantee any of the information on the wiki because anyone can write and edit it, but we can guarantee that what we write here is accurate. And if its not, we&#x2019;ll make it right!</p><p>Genesis V0.6 was a very minor update to <a title="FarmBot Genesis V5" href="http://go.farmbot.it/docs/farmbot-genesis-v0-5/">Genesis V0.5</a>, in fact, the only changes are with the Universal Tool Mounting System. For this reason, you may find that this documentation page looks nearly identical to that of Genesis V0.5 and you may just want to skip down to the Universal Tool Mount Section to see what is new. Here is a summary:</p><ul><li>Gluing in the magnets on the V0.5 Universal Tool Mount and Tools was problematic, the V0.6 design uses screws to hold the magnets in place</li><li>Gluing on rubber gaskets to the V0.5 tool mount proved to be unreliable. The V6 liquid lines now use o-rings held in place by a small channel in order to make a seal with the tool.</li><li>Experimented with screw-in stainless steel barbs for the Universal Tool Mount&#x2019;s liquid lines</li><li>Experimented with a miniature Universal Tool Mount and seed injector</li></ul><p>Note: The prototypes produced and seen in some of these photos used acrylic for the plates and was of a very small scale. This worked great for a prototype but should not be used for a real FarmBot because the acrylic is very flimsy. Also, the size is good for testing the hardware, but not useful for growing a significant amount of food.</p><p>Page Contents</p><ul><li><a The UTM is ncessary because it is not feasible to have all tools mounted on the z-axis at one time for several reasons:</p><ol><li>This would be very heavy and create more stresses on all components, as well as necessitate a larger z-axis motor.</li><li>Most tools need to be the &#x201C;lowest&#x201D; thing on the z-axis in order to work. Having multiple tools competing for the lowest position (ex: a temperature probe and a seed injector) would not be ideal and may not work at all. The use of individual tool raising and lowering mechanisms, or a turret style mechanism would be complex, heavy, bulky, and limited in the number of tools it could support.</li><li>The Z-Axis&#x2019;s size must be kept to a minimum in order for it to have minimal impact on the plants, especially when there is not very much space between them.</li></ol><p>The UTM is a single 3D printed component that mounts to the Z-Axis aluminum extrusion using two M5 screws and tee nuts. It features 3 strong neodymium ring magnets to magnetically hold tools in place via washers or other magnets placed in the same configuration on the Tool. The magnets double as the keying system to ensure that tools are properly oriented in the UTM. The magnets also function as the passage ways for water, liquid amendments (eg: fertilizer), and vacuum or compressed air to pass through from the UTM (and the rest of FarmBot) to the Tool. In addition, the UTM has 4 spring loaded screws that make electrical connections with Tools. Two are for power (GND and +5V), and two are for data (0 to +5V).</p><span class="mw-headline">Universal Tool Mount Change Log</span><p>There are many changes from the V0.5 UTM design including the following. For a video overview of the design, <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lnrhFsq8IJg" target="_blank" rel="nofollow">click here</a>.</p><ul><li>Instead of using glue to hold in the magnets, the magnets are now secured on both the tools and the tool mount with M5 screws. This also allows them to be reversed in case one puts them in incorrectly.</li><li>3D printed barbs and a place for o-rings is now integrated into the UTM. In V0.4, the holes of the ring magnets were used to pass liquids, and there were gaskets glued onto the surface of the magnets. This proved to be unreliable and difficult to manufacture.</li></ul><p>Only one version of the UTM was designed and produced this time in order to reduce complexity of options. The cost savings of a specialized UTM for another printing process is marginal, and the added complexity of having different sized screws and other hardware makes it less appealing to support multiple versions.</p><span class="mw-headline">A note about Tools, Power, and Data</span><p>Tim Evers and Rory Aronson had a lengthy discussion in email regarding how to handle communication between the tools and the Arduino/RAMPS board. This discussion can be seen on the <a title="Universal Tool Mount" href="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/wiki/Universal_Tool_Mount">Universal Tool Mount</a> page. The summary:</p><ul><li>Plug the vacuum pump for the seed injector into one of the heating element pin pairs on RAMPS</li><li>Plug the water pump/valve into the the other heating element pin pairs on RAMPS</li><li>Plug the Universal Tool Mount&#x2019;s 2 power wires into the fan pins on RAMPS</li><li>Plug the Universal Tool Mount&#x2019;s 2 data pins directly into the Arduino</li><li>Tools will at first have a single function so communication will be general I/O and not require extra electronics</li><li>Eventually tools may have more functions and require an I2C connection and additional electronics in the tool to support this communication</li></ul><span class="mw-headline">Universal Tool Mount Issues and Proposed Solutions</span><ul><li>The main issue with the V0.6 UTM is that there are too many components fighting to be &#x2018;closest&#x2019; to each other. There are the three O-rings on the liquid lines that need to be sufficiently compressed in order to get a good seal. This is conflicting with the fact that the four screws (three of which are not spring loaded) also are trying to make contact with the tool, and those are not compressible at all.<ul><li>In V0.7, the remedy will be that the O-rings will be the primary mate with the tool, while all electrical connections will need to be spring loaded. This means that the electrical connections will need to be separate from the screws holding in the magnets.</li></ul></li></ul><ul><li><a class="prettyphoto"                             <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21146-new-synchronization-architecture">New Synchronization Architecture</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2552/log/21146#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21146"><p>Since we started building the FarmBot web application, our method to synchronize data with the device has been as follows:</p><ol><li>The user opens up the web application in the web browser. When they do this, all of their scheduled sequences are loaded from the web application&#x2019;s server into the browser.</li><li>The user makes some changes to the scheduled sequences, which are saved in the web browser and also uploaded to the web application&#x2019;s server.</li><li>When the user pressed the sync button, the browser would upload all data directly to the FarmBot device.</li></ol><p>This worked great for <em>manual synchronization</em> whenever the user was actively using the web application. However, this method did not support the FarmBot device synchronizing with the web application <em>automatically</em> without a browser session and the user.</p><p>Today we&#x2019;re happy to announce that we&#x2019;ve rolled out a new architecture for synchronizing data across the web application, the FarmBot device, and the user&#x2019;s web browser. FarmBot now has the ability to synchronize schedules while the user is away from the browser. This is significant for upcoming features, such as the ability to adapt schedules to the weather conditions automatically. For <em>manual synchronization</em> it works as follows:</p><ol><li>The user opens up the web application in the web browser. When they do this, all of their scheduled sequences are loaded from the web application&#x2019;s server into the browser.</li><li>The user makes some changes to the scheduled sequences, which are saved in the web browser and also uploaded to the web application&#x2019;s server.</li><li>When the user presses the sync button, the browser tells the FarmBot device &#x201C;Hey, you have a new package, go pick it up&#x201D;. The FarmBot device then downloads the new schedule from the web application&#x2019;s server.</li></ol><p>Meanwhile, <em>automatic synchronization </em>works as follows:</p><ol><li>The FarmBot device is set to go check for new packages at the web application&#x2019;s server at a certain time interval such as every hour.</li></ol><p>This new architecture sets us up for a more automated and dynamic future of FarmBot control. Want to help us build the future of food production? Check out our web application repository on <a href="https://github.com/FarmBot/farmbot-web-app" target="_blank">GitHub</a>  and stay tuned as we roll out more new features in the weeks to come.</p></div>
