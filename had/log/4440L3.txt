                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21783"><p><a href="http://www.makerfairedetroit.com/">Maker Faire Detroit</a> was amazing!</p><p>Thanks to the<a href="http://isc.umd.umich.edu/"> Intelligent Systems Club - University of Michigan Dearborn</a> for confirming my theory that electric motors are <strong>either</strong> powerful or inexpensive and for introducing me to the really nice <a href="http://www.vexrobotics.com/217-8080.html">Talon SRX</a> motor controller. (Also used by <a href="http://www.usfirst.org/roboticsprograms/frc">FIRST robotics</a> competitors.) </p><p>Thanks to Jim Summers from <a href="http://www.human-electric-hybrids.com/">Human Electric Hybrids</a> for taking time to talk with me about the batteries they are using. The &quot;<a href="http://www.electricrider.com/LiFePO4-Lithium-24V-20AH-Electric-Bike-Battery-p/lif2420-14.htm">LiFePO4 Lithium 24V 20AH Clydesdale Edition Battery for Electric Bikes</a><span class="redactor-invisible-space">&quot; from <a href="http://www.electricrider.com">ElectricRider</a> look very promising. </span></p><p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_iron_phosphate_battery">LiFePO4</a> battery technology is very interesting: &quot;<span class="chemf">LiFePO</span>4 batteries have somewhat lower <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> than the more common <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_cobalt_oxide" title="Lithium cobalt oxide"><span class="chemf">LiCoO</span>2</a> design found in consumer electronics, but offers longer lifetimes, better <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_density" title="Power density">power density</a> (the rate that energy can be drawn from them) and are <em><strong>inherently safer</strong></em>. <span class="chemf">LiFePO</span>4 is finding a number of roles in vehicle use and backup power.&quot; --<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_iron_phosphate_battery">Wikipedia</a> <em>(emphasis added)</em><br><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_iron_phosphate_battery"></a></p><p>And what&apos;s not to love about a <a href="http://hackaday.com/tag/seemecnc/">3D printer with a nine feet by four feet build area</a>?<br><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_iron_phosphate_battery"></a></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4440/log/21846-of-love-and-power">of Love and Power</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">20 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4440/log/21846#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21846"><p>Driving across the state of Michigan on a sunny afternoon, I fell in love with the idea of a solar powered rover. Much like my love of <a href="http://www.polyglotnerd.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/Audrey-Tautou-015.jpg">Audrey Tautou</a>, it&apos;s not terribly practical.</p><p>If I was being practical, I&apos;d use a <a href="http://www.northerntool.com/shop/tools/product_299_299">160cc Honda GX engine</a> and a <a href="http://www.amazon.com/ALTERNATOR-18-310-18-350-18-390-0-124-655-011/dp/B008Y3OEZC/ref=sr_1_431?s=automotive&amp;ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1438243239&amp;sr=1-431&amp;keywords=alternator+24+volt">24 volt, 110 amp alternator</a>. But, I don&apos;t like handling gasoline and I don&apos;t like the noise of engines. Besides, Home Depot is having a great sale on Grape Solar <a href="http://www.homedepot.com/p/Grape-Solar-100-Watt-Polycrystalline-Solar-Panel-for-RV-s-Boats-and-12-Volt-Systems-GS-Star-100W/204211365">GS-Star-100W</a> solar panels. They are less than the UPS Large Package Surcharge dimensions when shipped separately and will fit on a &quot;standard&quot; (40&quot; x 48&quot;) pallet when assembled on a rover. Both of these are design goals.</p><p>Most importantly, I&apos;m doing what I love.</p><p>I could talk about solar&apos;s &quot;<a href="http://thezerobase.com/defense-security/c4isr/">acoustical advantage over fossil fuel generators</a>.&quot; But, I do try to be a bit more genuine than a government system report.</p><p>When I was in grade school, I went on a field trip to see a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HERO_%28robot%29">HERO 1</a> demonstration in the high school auditorium. I&apos;ve wanted my own robot ever since. But, I wanted it to do something more interesting than roll back and forth across a stage.</p><p>What I love about the idea of putting a solar panel on my rover is that it could then &quot;survive&quot; somewhat independently of me. Without a solar panel, I would have to keep it&apos;s battery charged and it would probably spend much it&apos;s time in the garage. With a solar panel, it could &quot;live&quot; outside and at least tell the world <a href="http://aprs.fi/info/a/EW6916">what the local weather is like</a>.</p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4440/log/21865-choosing-a-vendor-letting-go">Choosing a Vendor, Letting Go</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">20 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4440/log/21865#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21865"><p><a href="http://www.human-electric-hybrids.com/">Jim Summers</a> recommended two battery suppliers: <a href="http://em3ev.com/store/">EM3EV</a> and <a href="http://www.electricrider.com/">ElectricRider</a>.</p><p>I discounted EM3EV because they don&apos;t have exactly what I need. They have a  &quot;Request a quote&quot; option, but are located in Hong Kong and don&apos;t list a phone number on their web site. When developing a product, it&apos;s often important to be able to talk with someone on the phone.</p><p>ElectricRider has what I need. But, when I called the good people down in Texas and asked about a solar charge controller, they said their controller works on 120 volts and if my &quot;solar panel puts out 120 volts, it&apos;ll work fine.&quot; <strong>*<a href="http://i2.wp.com/hotnerdgirl.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/02/picard-facepalm1.jpg">facepalm</a>*</strong></p><p>I consulted the great AI known as Google and found <a href="http://www.bioennopower.com/pages/about-us">Bioenno Power</a> whose &quot;products are trusted and employed internationally and domestically by a wide customer base ranging from amateur model makers using our batteries for RC applications to professional aviators trusting our batteries to power critical avionics and Ham Radio operators.&quot; And, they sell solar charge controllers for their LiFePO4 batteries. Wow! Sounds like a perfect fit.</p><p>So, I called them. They were very pleasant and helpful, but insisted that 24 volt batteries need 24 volts of solar power. Inexpensive, small form factor, 100 watt (<a href="https://www.google.com/search?client=ubuntu&amp;channel=fs&amp;q=c%2F5&amp;ie=utf-8&amp;oe=utf-8#channel=fs&amp;q=c%2F5+charge+rate">C/5</a>-ish for 24 volt, 20 amp-hour batteries) pretty much don&apos;t exist.</p><p>Not to be deterred in my quest, I consulted Google a few more times and found a <a href="http://www.batteryspace.com/contactus.aspx">vendor</a> in California that sells a <a href="http://www.batteryspace.com/Charge-Controller-120W-12V/24V-Auto-Detection-10A-Rate-for-SLA-or-LFP.aspx">device</a> that does what I want. But, it&apos;s manual is in poorly translated Chinese, which doesn&apos;t inspire confidence.</p><p>So, I&apos;ve decided to <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ri7cx_73jVA">let go</a> of my desire for a solar panel <strong>on</strong> my rover. This will allow for a smaller, lighter rover that isn&apos;t top heavy. I can charge the batteries from a pair of ground mounted 12 volt panels. I could use a <a href="http://www.amazon.com/SMAKN%C2%AE-Waterproof-Converte-Supply-Module/dp/B00O0EDMES/ref=pd_sim_23_10?ie=UTF8&amp;refRID=0ABJW855FCCQ3RESF35G">DC/DC converter</a> to step up the 12 volt solar panel to 24 volts for the charge controller. But, this is going past the design goal of &quot;keeping it simple.&quot;</p><p><strong>Moral of the story:</strong> follow your heart. It may not lead you to what you see in your dreams, but it may lead you to a better vendor of LiFePO4 batteries.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4440/log/22200-stopping-pollution-before-it-starts">Stopping Pollution Before It Starts</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">13 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4440/log/22200#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22200"><p>An anonymous visitor to my table at the Detroit Maker Faire mentioned that he worked for an undisclosed company that was using proprietary methods for locating valves in underground oil pipelines. These valves were then removed before they sprung a leak.</p><p>He said the company considered using ground penetrating radar, but decided against it because of the cost (&quot;$50,000 to $100,000 per system&quot;).</p><p>I wonder how many potential problems could be located and mitigated with an unmanned ground penetrating radar system that costs 20% of current manned systems?</p><p>I&apos;ll probably need to increase the frequency to be able to see the target signature of valves in a 3&quot; pipe at &quot;backhoe depth.&quot; But, it&apos;s on my growing list of targets.</p><p>First, I&apos;m going after the <a>elephant in the room</a>.</p></div>
