                            <h2><a href="/project/7272/log/23104-so-it-begins-which-magnetometer">So it begins...  Which magnetometer?</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7272/log/23104#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23104"><p>This project is something I&apos;ve had on my mind for a while.  Mike Szcys posted to <a href="http://hackaday.com" target="_blank">Hackaday</a> with 4 days to go to the deadline for the hackaday prize, convinced me to formalize the idea and begin work, now I have to work fast !</p><p>The prerequisite knowledge of the Aurora I already have due to my frequent photography expeditions.  However the one area I&apos;m lacking skills is magnetics / magnetometers, the last few days have been spent researching different types of magnetometers and the features and practicality for this application.</p><p>Many don&apos;t realize that most of the population in the world can see an Aurora 2-10 times during solar maximum, provided you know when and the best location to look from.  Go further North (northern USA) and that becomes 50-100 times,  We&apos;re around solar maximum right now.  Yesterday there was another major storm (hit Purple (Extreme) on my aurora alerter), and it was seen as far south as Wisconsin, Michigan, Massachusetts, Idaho, Minnesota, South Dakota, Montana.</p><p>The variance in the earths field due to the Aurora is mainly in the Y direction and partially in the X, which means that the magnetometer needs to be able to monitor strength and direction of the field.  This  rules out many <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetometer" target="_blank">Magnetometer Designs</a> and results in 2 &quot;practical&quot; possibilities for investigation (i.e. we don&apos;t want to be messing with super cooled magentics and caesium vapor type solutions).</p><p>( On a side note, Auroras are measured in strength by a K-Index (0-9): <a href="http://k9la.us/Where_Do_the_K_and_A_Indices_Come_From.PDF" target="_blank">K-Index Primer</a> )</p><p>Additionally, sensitivity is a concern, sub 1nT resolution is desired, and sub 10nT would be the limit for detection.  This is because the quiet day curve (normal earth magnetic change during the day without an aurora) is around 50nT, and a K-Index of 2 (practical observation level for an aurora) is 10-20nT.</p><p>This puts the IC solutions out of the park.  The most sensitive technology in IC&apos;s currently is AMR (Anisotropic Magnetoresistive) or GMR (Giant Magnetoresistive).  The best state of the art commercially available sensor I could find is an AMR sensor with about a 200nT resolution (<a href="http://www51.honeywell.com/aero/common/documents/myaerospacecatalog-documents/Defense_Brochures-documents/HMC5883L_3-Axis_Digital_Compass_IC.pdf" target="_blank">Honeywell HMC118X</a>); this wouldn&apos;t detect anything but the strongest geomagnetic storms.</p><p>Considering that we want small size, low power usage, high resolution, high temperature stability and vector/scalar capability, this leaves essentially one choice a Fluxgate Magnetometer:  <a href="http://www.geotech1.com/pages/mag/info/sensors/sensors1_150.pdf" target="_blank">Fluxgate Magnetometer</a></p><p>There is also a variation on the fluxgate which consists of one winding and the inductor is used to form a clamped oscillator where the frequency of oscillation is dependent on the earths field, this is called Magnetoinductive:  <a href="http://www.sensorsmag.com/sensors/electric-magnetic/a-new-perspective-magnetic-field-sensing-855" target="_blank">New Magentic Field Sensing Technologies</a>   </p><p>Time to build some fluxgate magnetometer prototypes...</p></div>

                                <div id="post-body-23106"><p>It&apos;s the weekend, need to build a sensitive magnetometer in time for Monday...</p><p>Here&apos;s are the constraints:</p><p>1. Can&apos;t order anything in for delivery on the weekend (digikey/mouser etc. are out), and our local supply houses are useless, can&apos;t get delivery till Tuesday at the earliest (1 day after the deadline)</p><p>2. Has to be quick to prototype</p><p>3. None of the big guys stock magnetometer sensors that are adequate for the needs anyway, these are specialist sensors and I&apos;d need to wait.</p><p>4. Can be breadboarded initially to test, but due to the sensitive nature of the coils, things need to be locked down on a PCB (which can&apos;t be ordered in, in time for Monday)</p><p>Rapid prototyping MacGyver style, I need to get creative.  Found some very old approx 35 gauge (0.15mm) magnet wire and some larger mystery magnet wire in the wire box.  I must have purchased it 30 years ago when I first started electronics and was wrapping AM Radio ferrite rods.</p><p>I have an othermill, so at least I can prototype PCB&apos;s fast.</p><p>I&apos;ve been desoldering everything with any type of ring core or ferrite core in from my stash of kept PCB&apos;s from old equipment.  Old Motherboards, switched mode PSUs and router boards proved fruitful.<br></p><p>They are all mystery cores, tried investigating online to see if the color indicated the core material, but it doesn&apos;t.  Some of the coils had numbers on, but most were made in China and old, so none resulted in a data sheet   :-(      Here&apos;s the resulting stash:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/93891439754412288.JPG"></p><br></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7272/log/23129-better-core">Better core</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7272/log/23129#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23129"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1585651439757950496.JPG"><p>Saturday: Further research into the cores for magnetometers has highlighted that the correct core is very important to detecting the earths magnetic field, &quot;metglas&quot; or something similar with high permeability is required.</p><p>Um, don&apos;t have any, or do I ?   Metglas is found in the tags in library books, however I doubt the local library will donate any, but there is something nearly as good in these types of security tags (top rectangular tag):</p><p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_article_surveillance#/media/File:RFID_and_magneto-acoustic_tags.JPG" target="_blank">Acoustic Tag</a><br></p><p>Remember seeing those sometimes in DVD I buy at Costco.   Looking through my collection I&apos;ve found a black one inside a DVD.  The best thing is it&apos;s free (excluding the DVD).  I won&apos;t trash them in future when I find them on stuff.</p><p>Removed the top bias strip (attached to the sticky tape) from the tag to obtain the core strip (actually have 2 in there).  Removed one and wrapped 500 turns of approx 35 gauge around, not being to fussy about layout (apparently the most important thing is that wrap direction doesn&apos;t change when wrapping, otherwise neatness doesn&apos;t matter too much).</p><p>This is a single wrap, so is tested with the Magentoinductive oscillation technique.  Tried 3 different oscillators based on the following oscillation techniques with this coil:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8785751439758145915.JPG"></p><p>555 Timer: Works well, detects a regular ring magnet about 3 feet away on the scope.  Voltage sensitive and temperature sensitive.  The 0.005% / C thermal stability of a 555 is too poor for this use, so would need to be temperature compensated.  Voltage sensitivity can be solved with double regulation.  Also, a little difficult to tune due to the nature of the 555.  Otherwise preliminary results suggest it would do the job.  Oscillation range can be easily tuned for the best coil saturation.  Part count is low and cheap.</p><p>OP Amp: Couldn&apos;t get to oscillate reliably, tried 2 x ST UA741, and an LM358N.   Suspect inductance is the issue and that the coil needs to have more inductance for this to work.  Given this, it&apos;s likely to be problematic, so abandoning this branch.</p><p>Schmidt Trigger Inverter: Same sensitivity as the 555, but oscillation isn&apos;t too clean and varies significantly in amplitude and noise which makes it through to the output due to the back emf on the coil despite putting a shunt diode in.  Still, would likely be a very simple / cheap solution for a mid range precision compass.</p><p>Now the principle is proven to work, it&apos;s time to wind better quality coils on 3D printed forms for face off testing for the 2 techniques :</p><p>1. Dual parallel wound around a security tag strip (suitable for Fluxgate Technique)</p><p>2. Single wound around a security tag strip (2000 turns) - (suitable for Magnetoinductive Technique)</p><p>3. <a href="http://www3.imperial.ac.uk/spat/research/areas/space_magnetometer_laboratory/spaceinstrumentationresearch/magnetometers/fluxgatemagnetometers/howafluxgateworks" target="_blank">A conventional fluxgate design</a> consisting of one of the mystery toroids for the drive and a slip over form for the sense winding, that can be used to test a few different mystery cores.</p></div>

Windings were stabilized  from unwinding with superglue and some hot glue at the ends.</p><p>Coil 1: One wire wrapped around small 3D Printed former, approx 35AWG (0.15mm), 2000 wraps, security tag strip cut in half lengthways (1/8 x 1.5 inches) inserted inside former.  Resistance: 46.7Ohms</p><p>Coil 2: Two wires wrapped together, around small 3D Printer former, approx 35AWG (0.15mm), 500 wraps, security tag strip cut in half lengthways (1/8 x 1.5 inches) inserted inside former.                             <h2><a href="/project/7272/log/23261-settled-on-fluxgate-configuration">Settled on fluxgate configuration</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7272/log/23261#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23261"><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9692721439794292336.JPG"></p><p>Comparing Regular fluxgate (1 driving coil and 1 sensing coil = 2) and MagentoInductive (1 coil in oscillation), (using a magnet detection distance from the sensor of around 3 feet), I&apos;ve come to the conclusion that Magnetoinductive is not as sensitive as fluxgate, which I&apos;m assuming is because the core is not being driven into saturation with MagnetoInductive (it&apos;s low current).  </p><p>Being that the field drops inversely proportionally to the square of the distance, initial testing indicates that MagnetoInductive is approximately 5-10 times less sensitive.<br></p><p>However MagnetoInductive would be fine for a low budget general compass.  For the purposes of this project, sensitivity is a requirement.</p><p>Now for driving the fluxgate coil.  For testing purposes I&apos;ve been using a transistor oscillator (confusing as MagentoInductive uses an oscillator too), but this transistor oscillator uses both the sense and drive coils.  Ultimately I want to migrate away to a microprocessor drive for accuracy reasons, and the output current of a typical microprocessor isn&apos;t enough....</p><p>To get the cleanest sense signal in a fluxgate, the drive should have it&apos;s field reversed on each cycle.  I.E. 0V-5V, 5V-0V, 0V-5V, so I need a driver that can switch rails (similar to driving a DC motor forwards and backwards).</p><p>So, this evening I&apos;ve designed and CNC&apos;d (othermill) an H Bridge driver, driven by 1 pin (direction) and is complete with transient suppressors and schmidt triggers (although the transient suppressors I need to order so I&apos;m chancing it for now).  All driven by a teensy 3.1 rolling through a frequency spectrum 1KHz - 300KHz for testing purposes.</p><p>The PCB off the mill without components is above.  The following is the PCB populated:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8022371439794469036.jpg"></p><p>Testing the drive outputs (unloaded) to check correctness:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7524341439794541382.JPG"></p><p>Connected to the coil and the Teensy:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5292391439794597726.JPG"></p><p>Running at 10KHz, notice the flux collapse / recovery in the sense coil is <a href="http://www.invasens.co.uk/FluxgateExplained.PDF">text book:</a></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5571411439794712964.JPG"></p><p>Schematic for H-Bridge Driver:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1142241439794775599.png"></p></div>
