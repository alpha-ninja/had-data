                            <h2><a href="/project/5971/log/19637-calibrating-the-calibrator">Calibrating the calibrator</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5971/log/19637#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19637"><p>The last log dealt with testing a 24-bit ADC, the AD7718 , and I may have mentioned that its main aim for now was to serve as a calibration tool.</p><p>However, In order to use it as one, It itself must first be calibrated against some industry standard high resolution DMM.    Even though the datasheet for the AD7718 claims a pretty high accuracy, any measurement tool is only as accurate as its reference, which in this case is the REF196.    The REF196 is an ultra stable voltage reference, but may have an initial offset.</p><p>Another source of error is the OPA2277 level shifting op-amps.</p><p>I have access to a Keithley 2100 multimeter, and am using it for this calibration.</p><p>Using python-usbtmc, it can easily be interfaced .  Code is as simple as</p><pre class="hljs python">import usbtmc
DMM =  usbtmc.Instrument(<span class="hljs-number">0x05E6</span>,<span class="hljs-number">0x2100</span>) <span class="hljs-comment">#V-id,P-id </span>
<span class="hljs-keyword">print</span> DMM.ask(<span class="hljs-string">'*IDN?'</span>)
DMM.<span class="hljs-keyword">write</span>(<span class="hljs-string">'SENS:VOLT:NPLC 1\n'</span>)
DMM.<span class="hljs-keyword">write</span>(<span class="hljs-string">':SENS:VOLT:DC:RES 1E-6\n'</span>)</pre>
<p>For now I've calibrated the unipolar inputs on the PCB.</p><p>Here's a screenshot of a simple calibration program that sweeps a voltage source and records the values read by the Keithley2100, and the AD7718.  The data will be used to generate a fitting polynomial.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/559431434512961185.png"></p><p>At lower voltages, both agree pretty well, but en route to 3.3V, the gap increases to about 1.5mV roughly linearly.  This offset is to be expected from the voltage reference, and calibration will take care of it.  What's important is that the calibration be valid over a wide temperature range, and that's the whole point of using a voltage reference and low drift op-amps.</p><p>Update: After applying the calibration factors, the readings from the AD7718 correspond to professional DDMs to within a +/-100uV margin. Averaging brings it down to &lt;10uV, since the noise floor for this roughly designed board is around 100uV. Moving on to opto-isolation.<br></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5971/log/19652-amplitude-modulation-with-an-analog-multiplier">Amplitude Modulation with an analog Multiplier</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5971/log/19652#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19652"><p>The expansion slots are meant to accommodate more complex instruments, and a lock-in amplifier is one of the add-ons I hope to make. Phase correlated sine waves are part of the package already, so only a multiplier and a low pass filter are needed.</p><p>As a first step, I played around with the AD633 analog multiplier, and tried out amplitude modulation using only the tools available on board.</p><p> The schematic is in the examples section of the AD633 datasheet.  The red trace is the modulated output. A carrier of 10KHz was modulated with 150Hz.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5969521434549674066.png"></p><p>A quick Fourier transform of the modulated wave shows the side bands.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3867721434550118592.png"></p><p>Accurate Fourier transformations require a sampling period much greater than the largest wavelength that makes up the input waveform, so I'm polishing a live streaming protocol that won't rely on handshakes, and will send ADC readings at the maximum rate possible without dropped packets.</p><p style="margin-left: 20px;">Here's a screencast of an old streaming app showing the modulated waveform, followed by an FFT of the same.  The timebase shown is incorrect, and I'll be fixing it.<br></p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/vSVH-kCAFPs" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><br></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5971/log/19731-non-contact-short-range-position-sensing">Non-contact, short range position sensing</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5971/log/19731#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19731"><p>The inductance of a solenoid depends on the properties of its core.  Adding a ferromagnetic material will raise it, and a diamagnetic material will do the opposite.</p><p>The Inductance meter on the device simply pairs the externally plugged in inductor with a high stability capacitor on the inside. The resultant tank circuit is driven at its resonant frequency which is measured by the frequency counter. Frequency of oscillations is given by<img src="/api/latex?tex=%5Ccolor%7BWhite%7D%20%5Clarge%20%5Cnu%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B%5Csqrt%7BLC%7D%7D">Where the inductance L, depends on the solenoid, and its core.  C is 1000pF.</p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/Z0GpltggIuA" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><p>The video shows the frequency counter with a resolution of .2Hz connected to the L meter's output logging variations in a solenoid's inductance.  Inserting a ferromagnetic material changes the frequency( A key in this case gives up to 6000 points after being pushed 1cm  into the core . After calibrating, and accounting for noise, the resolution is less than 10 microns )<br></p><p>This method has tremendous applications in mechanics labs. However , It won't match up to optical methods when it comes to being unobtrusive. The oscillating field of the solenoid should cause a minor perturbation in the ferromagnetic piece attached to the body being recorded.<br></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5971/log/22600-an-even-more-affordable-variant">An even more affordable variant</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">7 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5971/log/22600#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22600"><p><strong><a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490-a-versatile-labtool">https://hackaday.io/project/6490-a-versatile-labtool</a></strong> ! </p>
<p><strong>WHY?</strong><br>
 The present form has several components that significantly add to the 
cost, and may not be required by the majority of users. Many of these 
can be provided in the form of add-on boards, thereby creating the need 
for a modular design.<br></p><p>Most of the Add-on slots in the current 
state are separate Berg headers dedicated to specific data buses. 
Therefore, a unified, durable expansion port that supports all the 
available slots in addition to a few analog I/O has been implemented. 
Some remappable pins as well as the 16Mhz clock used by the DDS have 
been routed to the expansion slot.</p><p><br><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3829801438461667253.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/340451439389763945.jpg"></p><p>          Figure : Present state of development of the <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490-a-versatile-labtool">vLabtool</a>. An optional <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490-a-versatile-labtool/log/21563-standalone-mode-using-a-128x64-oled">standalone</a> mode has been implemented.</p><p>I&apos;m trying to meet the deadline for the Best Product prize, so it will be taking up all of my time.<br></p><p><strong>The gist of differences is here.  :</strong></p><p><strong>+  :  BOM Less than $50 with component costs calculated with element14 prices.</strong><br><strong></strong></p><p><strong>+    : </strong>   <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490-a-versatile-labtool/log/21563-standalone-mode-using-a-128x64-oled">An optional standalone mode</a>.
  If the device detects an ssd1306 OLED plugged into an I2C slot during 
boot-up, it displays a few readings until the PC takes over.  This 
allows it to be battery powered, <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490/log/21958-designing-an-enclosure-with-solidworks-and-kicad">and be made portable</a>.<br></p><p><strong>-    :</strong>      4-channel oscilloscope reduced to 2-channel.</p><p><strong>+    :      </strong>12-bit
 offset control, and up to 32x gain for 8 analog inputs with various 
input ranges.  These include 2x +/-16V inputs, +/-2V input, I2V 
convertor, Power supply ripple monitoring, and four unipolar inputs.<br><strong></strong></p><p><strong>+    :</strong>    <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490-a-versatile-labtool/log/20189-nrf24l01-based-wireless-nodes">NRF24L01+ transceiver added to base board</a>. Firmware routines allow faster data transfer, and automatic node registration.</p><p><strong>-    :     </strong>Inductance
 to frequency converter removed.  It can be easily redesigned as an 
add-on board which also measures capacitance faster than the CTMU.<br></p><p><strong>-   :</strong>       <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490/log/21961-building-blocks-28-bit-sinetriangle-wave-generator">The AD9833 is pricey, and only one of them</a> has been made part of the base board in the vLabtool.</p><p><strong>+  :</strong>      <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490/log/21996-building-blocks-15khz-standalone-sine-wave-generator">A 15KHz fixed amplitude sine wave generator</a>
 has been added as compensation. It can be used in conjunction with     
            the lone DDS for experiments such as amplitude modulation.</p><p><strong>+    :     </strong>A
 2x10 pin expansion connector consisting of SPI, I2C, UART, 2xAnalog 
inputs, 16MHz output, raw output of the                     DDS ,     
etc.    <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6490-a-versatile-labtool/log/21584-4-pole-35mm-socket-for-sensors-that-speak-i2c">A 4-pin, 3.5mm socket dedicated for I2C sensors is also included.</a></p><p><strong>+    :</strong>
     2.5ppm accuracy clock generator Fox925b. This provides much greater
 accuracy than the 30ppm crystals that were previously employed.</p><p><strong>-    :  </strong>   Several hundred banana sockets</p><p><strong>-     : </strong>
   TP4056 Li-ion charging IC. These are easily available as breakout 
boards, and may also be added to the wireless nodes at very little 
additional expense.</p><p>*ESP8266 continues to enjoy PCB real estate as an alternate communication channel.</p><p><strong>-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------</strong></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5971/log/22700-position-sensing-with-a-linear-optical-array">Position sensing with a linear optical array</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">6 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5971/log/22700#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22700"><p>Among non-contact position sensing methods used in mechanics laboratories, high speed cameras plus image processing is fairly common. However, this method is resource intensive, and a less expensive method is presented as a demo.</p><p>The TCD1304AP is a 3648 element optical array available in a DIP package.<br>It&apos;s basically 3648 photosensors aligned in a straight line, and each has a storage capacitor.<br>In order to acquire data, the user first sends a pulse that connects each sensor with its capacitor for a fixed duration ( the equivalent of shutters in cameras).</p><p style="margin-left: 20px;">Once the shutter closes, the voltage on each sensor&apos;s capacitor represents the amount of light it received.<br>A series of pulses must then be supplied by the host, and upon receipt of each pulse the TCD1304 connects the capacitors one by one to its analog output pin. The host must simultaneously read and digitize the output analog data with its ADC.<br><br><br></p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/PIIuwt4ZOh8" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe></div>
