                            <h2><a href="/project/5917/log/18741-the-hydrogen-adapter-safety-modifications">The hydrogen adapter safety modifications</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5917/log/18741#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18741"><p>To add really bulletproof safety measures to the hydrogen adapter it was necessary to run a cable from the binary fuel tank that supported a remote valve shutoff, as well as a sensor that detects leaking hydrogen.</p><p>This is what the adapter looks like now. The white tube is the new fuel inlet point:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7241201433089293265.jpg"></p><p>This is a look at the hydrogen sensor:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8310401433089308346.jpg"></p><p>This is how it looks attached to the engine:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9032341433089323310.jpg"></p><p>And yet another view:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8955441433089341579.jpg"></p><p>Note the additional jacks on the adapter plate-These carry power from the binary fuel tank or generator output to power the sensor circuit. In case of a leak or errant gas detection, the solenoid valve shuts off the fuel supply to the engine and activates the engine kill switch. Despite adding these features, the adapter is still inexpensive to make.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5917/log/18810-upgrades-to-the-binary-fuel-tank">Upgrades to the Binary fuel Tank</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5917/log/18810#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18810"><p>Thanks to the OSH park boards I won during the weekly contests, I was able to redesign some circuitry for the fuel tank controller and really provide a great upgrade in respect to safety measures, monitoring and fuel production. The LCD menu display is also making the unit much more user friendly. Here is a look at the new system module:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2750891433206924444.jpg"></p><p>It can also be noted I got all the closure panels on the top of the big yellow box now!</p><p> The new module is the user interface that allows the pressure and flowrate to be programmed in for a specific engine being used(in this case my lawnmower engine/generator) and only requires the unit to be turned on and the engine started. Before, the pressure had to be set each time the tank was turned on and fiddled with a bit.</p><p> Thanks to the PIC controller and lots of crazy coding I was able to make it simple enough for anyone to use, which would be desirable for an end product. I chose the PIC for this(and almost ALL my other projects) as they are stable, reliable and have a ton of pins that can do magic. They have never failed me and for this project, the choice was a no brainer.</p><p>The tank controller has a stepped menu for the two line display which allows changes for other apps, so it's a "one size fits all" thing now. There is also a manual over ride to still allow the use of the manual system in case an unknown or previously unused engine type is used, and of course in this case for experimenting.</p><p> Once the system is up and running the LCD scroll through the readout settings so you can tell at a glance what's happening inside the system(razzle/dazzle!).</p><p>I still need to make some upgrades and revisions to the OSH boards so I will wait to post the Gerbers until everything is all in place.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>

I will now proceed to print out an entire tank thus allowing easier replication of the system worldwide.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3082101439772596957.JPG"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5275571439772621185.JPG">Unit includes drive motor, auger, seals, tensioning adjustment and lower reservoir storage area. The encoder wheel on the motor will give feedback as to delivery speed.</p></div>
