                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-5034"><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6756451405517661256.jpg"></p><p>While I was waiting on boards and parts I started assembling this. &nbsp;My answer to the Nest thermostat, ugly isn't it. &nbsp;Since I got an email yesterday that sparkfun is filling my order for sd card holders and I have 9 more boards to use up I am going to replace this with my "Power Monitor", a protoshield, and a serial lcd. &nbsp;While the thermostat itself is not smart I will send the data to the database, and make it so that the computer can make the "smart" temperature choices and control the thermostat. &nbsp;This is my second Nest rebuild, after first reading about their products before google bought them I built this...</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6001201405518105711.jpg"></p><p>The april board pulled power from the battery and was connected to the test button and the speaker. &nbsp;It would read the battery level (which this setup would drain in about a half an hour), could remotely test the unit through a web interface, and would provide feedback to my xively feed. &nbsp;The feedback was based on the number of pulses sent to the speaker and could report CO2, Fire, or Test. &nbsp;This would have only been practical if the smoke/co2 alarm was connected to house power, but to my wife's frustration I had fun going to work and hitting the test button. &nbsp;I had broken the link to xively one night playing around with the code and forgot about it the next day. &nbsp;When I was showing it off to a coworker I was disappointed that my feed was not updating, but when my phone rang it proved to be a closed loop system just the same.</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/1150/log/5127-volt-amp-board">Volt Amp Board</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a year ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/1150/log/5127#discussion-list">
                                    1 comment
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-5127"><p>I am worried about this board because there are a couple of components I have never used before on it.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8490571405655051761.png"></p><p>What I am wanting to do is bring out the remaining analog pins on the arduino to audio jacks that also supply 5v and gnd. &nbsp;From there you can plug anything you want into them. &nbsp;This board will have a whip on it with an audio plug on the end, and the audio jack here is so you can plug a current transformer into it, and the connector is to connect wires to the mains ( 1 board for each main line coming in) this will go to an AD629 (the part that worries me) . &nbsp;The other part that has me worried is the BAV99 that I replaced the diodes from my original board with. &nbsp;</p><p>The rocket fuel tank monitor that I used was using two of them as a bridge rectifier (that is my best guess because they were marked A7W and it would seem right, at least to me). &nbsp;That makes me think that they should be fine, but still a little worried about it. (Random tangent - I planned on ordering them anyway to rebuild the power circuit from the tank sensor reciever before I put my documentation online. &nbsp;If I get it right it looks like an easy compact way to power a circuit off of 120vac).</p><p>Here is a rough schematic of the board design...</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3642971405657635154.png"></p><p>The part with the S's and D's is what I am using to represent the AD629 (the datasheet will have to suffice for now) until I create the part in Eagle, the part labeled BAV199 will be saved as BAV99, and the part labeled AD711RN is schematically correct but I am using TS1851IDT ( I can't pretend to know much about any of this, but I chose to keep this op amp on my bench because of the operating range).&nbsp;</p><p>If anyone has any advice before I build this I am all ears!</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/1150/log/5532-power-connector">Power Connector</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a year ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/1150/log/5532#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-5532"><p>This is probably old news for most people, but I just learned something today.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1860551406255486315.jpg"></p><p>On the DC power connector above the red wire is connected to the center pin and the black is connected to the casing contact. &nbsp;When nothing is plugged in, the center connection that is not connected has continuity with the connection with the black wire. &nbsp;</p><p>When a power cable is inserted you lose continuity with the terminal that is not connected. &nbsp;I believe that it is irrelevant now because I have revised my board but this was an issue with the way I layed out v2r0. &nbsp;The chinese arduinos would work as is, but I was having some strange problems with the sparkfun arduinos.&nbsp;</p><p>&nbsp;It is a notable fail on my part because several weeks ago when I assembled the first board I had originally used the sparkfun arduino and ran into the strange problem and assumed that I had created a solder bridge somewhere &nbsp;because I was lacking confidence in my smd soldering abilities. &nbsp;I slavaged some parts and tossed the board aside. &nbsp;</p><p>The second build, the one in the main project picture, I used the chinese board because I did not want to risk damaging a $10 board when I had several $3 boards laying around. &nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>Tonight I did some troubleshooting while building up a couple more boards and found the problem... I am almost always right to assume that if there is a problem it is because I did something wrong, but every now and then I need to take a closer look at the components. &nbsp;I didn't know DC jacks had a switch inside.&nbsp;</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/1150/log/5783-ground-fault-test">Ground Fault Test</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a year ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/1150/log/5783#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-5783"><p>I hooked up the ground fault test box again, and took some measurements. &nbsp;With a calculated ground fault of 0.09A I was getting an peak A rms reading on the arduino of 0.05A it was regular but not constant.&nbsp;</p><p>I was able to measure a calculated 0.1A ground fault with the meter and the arduino. &nbsp;The arduino still &nbsp;measured 0.05A but the meter measured 0.9A. &nbsp;This wasn't a reliable measurement though.</p><p>The detectable amperage is higher than the standard which is 0.06A at the highest, but I didn't set out to build a gfci and I am pleased with the results. &nbsp;</p><p>I did check the arduino at a measured 0.6A calculated load and measured 0.52 so I am happy that it was closer at this level. &nbsp;I will continue to test the circuit and finish with three graphs, Calculated, Meter, and Arduino and compare the results, but I have to get a better dmm first.</p></div>
