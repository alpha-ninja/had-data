                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20144"><p>It will be ultra nice if this card could be programmed through SD card interface. Indeed SD card interface has a definition of "iSDIO" which is different from general storage access, and I imagine and guess if appropriate driver is developed in OS level, direct programming (without USB-TTL) will be implemented. But this implementation is completely out of my capability. So, if you are familiar with iSDIO and know well how to make OS level driver, please help to implement it.</p><p>Actually, developing general iSDIO driver is quite useful not only for SDuino but also all general SD card in order to bring out its capability, and I believe it is worth to do. Of course the driver implementation does not have any bind with SDuino but the developer has its own copyright and if needs it can be sold in consideration of its usefulness.</p><p>You can try the implementation as a separate project in hackaday.io but if it is better, I would like to "invite new contributor". Please let me know..</p></div>

(at this moment, the function is same as previous original one.)</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2517431436005560849.jpg"></p>    <p>First one is, 16MHz clock version using seramic resonator, but the thickness of ceramic resonator is too thick to put into SDcard case, and I have to abandon it.                             <h2><a href="/project/6435/log/20455-spi-eeprom-as-sd-card-storage">SPI EEPROM as SD card storage?</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6435/log/20455#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20455"><p>Some sort of people are not convinced of what they don't confirm experimentally by themselves  but are able to predict naive possibility. Yes I am, too.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9563571436063010903.jpg"></p><p>All the SD card are required to have mandatory lower compatibility to SPI mode. The key is, SD card interface equipped components are not responsible for that. But I naively thought some computer might talk SPI command through SD card interface...  I quickly checked it!</p><p>The above picture shows <a href="http://ww1.microchip.com/downloads/en/DeviceDoc/22064B.pdf">1M-bits EEPROM</a> inside SD card shell. All the terminal are connected to standard SD-card pinout, then,,, what's happening next?</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7523901436063296045.jpg"></p><p>Inserting Asus, Apple.... nothing happens. Device manager and system info at OS X does not show any information, even they seem not detecting something is inserted! Now I would summarise for those who suspicious guy will not consume their precious time.</p><p>Conclusion: SPI EEPROM wired by SD card SPI connection does not work</p><p>By the way, SDuino case, you don't need "SDuino writer" just to run your program. Just inserting is enough. It may have some possibility, like "small present" or "some kind of SDcard sized sensor". Just pre-programming and pass it to your friend and you can ask it to work just inserting to SD card connector!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1245531436063640503.jpg"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6435/log/20708-sduino-sensor-sduino-with-i2c-atmospheric-sensor">SDuino sensor, SDuino with I2C atmospheric sensor!!!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6435/log/20708#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20708"><p>The original SDuino was indeed “just a small Arduino with LEDs”. I have tried several things such as “Tiny Basic Plus” and they are working as expected. This time I added one spice, that is, I2C atmospheric sensor in SDuino!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8863221436442983187.jpg"></p>    <p>The atmospheric sensor is well-known MPL115A2 made by Freescale, and also lots of Arduino library and sketches are already well developed. The good point of this sensor is, quite small size, and well accommodated inside SD card shell, as attached in the picture.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4306781436443009837.jpg"></p>    <p>With such a sensor, I always love to “watch sensored digits and graph” but putting outernal big screen is non-sense! So this time I implement to show the sensored value, actually not simple sensored value, to be presented by LEDs!</p>    <p><a href="https://github.com/kodera2t/SDuino_example/blob/master/SDuino_Weather.ino">In this sketch</a>, Forthcoming climate is predicted by the delivative of pressure value of last 2 hours. Every two hours, SDuino_sensor will sense pressure and taking delivative of current and previous values. First forecast will be shown 2 hours after power supplied.</p>  <p> Result will be shown as:</p>  <p>(1) Getting worse (meaning fine to cloudy, cloudy to rain) by RED LED</p>  <p>(2) Moderate (meaning no change) by GREEN LED</p>  <p>(3) Better (rain to cloudy, cloudy to fine) by BLUE LED</p>  <p>(4) No data (first 2 hours since power-on, or some parts broken) by WHITE LED</p>    <p>In order to see climate change, just applying DC voltage (3.3V) is enough and no need of internet access at all!! This small SD card will predict climate change and just inserting to SD card slot will show you whether you will  need umbrella or even hurricane approach!  Have fun!</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6435/log/21115-more-advanced-sduino-temperature-humidity-pressure">More advanced SDuino, temperature, humidity, pressure!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6435/log/21115#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21115"><p>Now my SDuino gained a bit more advanced sensing using very sophisticated sensor module (BME280) by Bosch. In general, humidity sensor is more space-taking one (as well known as BHT-11 ?) but this sensor is even smaller than general pressure/temperature sensor!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6992601437201484147.jpg"></p><p>Small square metal can package is BME280, and very small means hard for hand-soldering. In my case, I just do it soldering iron as usual.</p><p>By measuring humidity, SDuino can evaluate &quot;discomfort index&quot; ( or temperature-humidity index) by following <a href="http://keisan.casio.com/exec/system/1351058230">equation</a></p><p><img src="/api/latex?tex=%5Ccolor%7BWhite%7D%20%5Clarge%20THI%3D0.81%20%5Ctimes%20T_d%20%2B0.01H(0.99%20%5Ctimes%20T_d%20-14.3)%20%2B46.3">, where T_d is temperature in deg. H is humidity in percent. Color of blinking LED will change for THI&lt;60: blue (cold), THI&lt;75: green (comfortable), and THI&gt;75 (hot!).</p><p>SDuino operation does not need computer and one it is programmed, just applying 3.3 V will show you current index. This type of index is very useful to avoid heatstroke, the fatal disease in summer season!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2874131437202369561.jpg"></p><p>Actual operation can be found in the following movie.. have fun!</p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/xF3c8BM38pM" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe></div>
