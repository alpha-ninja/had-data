                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23542"><p>This all started with a borrowed spectrum analyzer (Thorlabs CCS100, 350-700nm, &lt;1nm resolution). I started measured spectrum of every LED and light source I had around... :</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4182921439841346736.jpg"></p><p>Daylight spectrum:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4122821439841947053.png"></p><p>Just blue light from sky (not including Sun) - we can see it is blue-cyan:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6129571439841965214.png"></p><p>My nuclear light fixture HID lamps were quite funny. Right after turn on - lots of mercury lines:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9442741439845867476.png"></p><p>Heating up...</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8966511439845906451.png"></p><p>And it&apos;s done:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9180501439845923953.png"></p><p>Cyan LED:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3968611439846422866.png"></p><p>Blue 460nm (from RGB):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9216851439846445312.png"></p><p>Green 520nm (from RGB):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5897651439846467300.png"></p><p>Red (from RGB):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/474571439846483247.png"></p><p>And finally white LED:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1169891439846544054.png"></p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7317/log/23557-base-led-fixture">Base LED fixture</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7317/log/23557#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23557"><p>I really liked simple geometrical forms, so I got square 40W 4000K LED fixtures. They were relatively inexpensive (~40$ each), their large area ensures decent cooling and long life. I disassembled one to figure out if it&apos;s possible to stick LEDs inside - but there is just too little space left for high-power LEDs.</p><p>Fixture design is quite clever, like on LED display back light: There is acrylic light-guide with diffuse dots across the area to make it uniformly lit. light-guide is lit from the side.   <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7148551439842185900.jpg"></p><p>After fixing minor issues (like black marker marking on the edge,tighter assembly to improve thermal resistance) I assembled it back. It was clear I can only add LED on the back, facing to the ceiling. That might be interesting effect though. I glued first LEDs using ALSIL-5 glue and generic &quot;heatsink plaster&quot;. </p><p>For first revision I decided to use step-up regulators with ballast resistors to control the current over LEDs. I set the current in &quot;hot&quot; state. Surely in the future I will need to replace it with digitally controllable constant current sources.<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5717961439842410588.jpg"></p><p>First tests shows that with 5x cyan alone spectrum is very far from 
desired. I needed more LEDs, so I added RGB ones, intending to use green
 and blue dies. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8011411439843260561.jpg"></p><p>Current adjustment near the ceiling feels a bit tricky:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4365821439845459133.jpg"></p><p>With green on I&apos;ve got the following spectra:<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5873801439842656589.png"></p>There is definitely a progress, but still very far from desired.</div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7317/log/23574-moar-leds">MOAR LEDs!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7317/log/23574#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23574"><p>So I needed to add more LEDs. Apparently just 5x cyan LEDs cannot cover the hole left by 40W white ones, so I used all 10 cyan I had on first fixture (initially I planned to use 5 on each), and set them to maximum rated current (700mA), tuned 460nm blue die form RGB LEDs to get as flat as possible, and the best I got was this: </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5755671439842979352.png"></p><p>Much better than original white LEDs, but there is still a hole near 470nm, not as terrible as before though. It was clear this is as good as one can get with LEDs I had. If I increase current on any of them - I&apos;ll get overshoot. So I needed 470nm blue LED instead of 460nm one to fill in that hole. I ordered LEDs and started to wait...<br></p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7317/log/23602-new-leds-arrived">New LEDs arrived!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7317/log/23602#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23602"><p>With new 490nm and 470nm LEDs I can finally proceed: </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/966141439845279607.jpg"></p><p>470nm LED came close to specs:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6420701439846630858.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7203471439843458883.jpg"></p><p>With 10x0.7A for Cyan LEDs step-up regulator is taking too much heat, so I had to add few extra radiators:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1932531439846840673.jpg"></p><p>After fine-tuning current in the LED chains I&apos;ve got the final spectra:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9015921439843502383.png"></p><p>Finally there are no missing colors - this is something which could satisfy me. And &quot;blue sky&quot; effect is really nice. As we were mostly adding LEDs near to eye (and camera) maximum sensitivity, light feels much brighter than before (as well as photo exposures). </p><p>Is there a visible difference? Yes, but not looking at spectrum analyzer it is hard to show you on the RGB display with RGB camera. I have cyan shirt for example, and it&apos;s color is visibly different under new lighting - but making and comparing a photo won&apos;t show much - camera renders it bluish, while in reality it is slightly greenish (regular LED on the left, corrected LED on the right) :</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8260241439843778085.jpg"></p>Personally, I am more than satisfied by light itself and I like it much more than my <a href="http://3.14.by/en/read/nuclear-light-fixture-100klm">older 100k lumen HID fixture</a>. <p><strong>Future plans:</strong><br></p><p>This is not the end of the journey though - I ought to replace step-up converters with ballast resistors with a proper constant current ones and make it RF-controlled (nRF24LE01). I am going for nRF instead of hiped WiFi ESP8266 because I personally cannot tolerate wakeup latency. </p><p>Instead of using standalone constant-current ICs I am going to drive mosfets from uC PWM pins while monitoring current. As there are no fast current changes this should do the job with very simple schematic. <br></p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
