                            <h2><a href="/project/4977/log/22186-a-true-hacker-makes-his-own-pcbs">A true hacker makes his own PCBs</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">13 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4977/log/22186#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22186"><p>A true hacker makes his own PCBs. Dual layer. And in the kitchen. Why so? Because doing them as DIY and not paying lots of money to some greedy company is some kind of hack.</p><p>And if it wasn&apos;t for <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/resistive-touchscreen/" target="_blank">all the issues I had with the Portable Environmental monitor</a> along the road, I could have used the nice <a href="https://oshpark.com/" target="_blank">OSHPark voucher</a> I won on <a href="https://hackaday.io/" target="_blank">Hackaday (Thanks again guys!)</a>. But I&apos;ve just finished the PCB design last night:<br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_D_pcb_1.0.101.png"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_D_pcb_1.0.101.png" alt="uradmonitor_D_pcb_1.0.101"></a><br>I know, it looks sweet! And the PCB must do as well. But no time to order them in US, ship them in Europe, solder all parts, test the software and ship the 3 prototypes to Hackaday headquarters before August 17.<br>Instead I checked locally with a few companies, but their price offer was and average of 300USD for 10 PCBs (with a 100% emergency tax added). That seems ridiculous so no thanks. I did however order a few in China, where 3 companies I know charged between $0.5 and $3 per PCB plus shipping, and they offer top quality. Now that&apos;s a huge difference. But all in one, time&apos;s too short to wait for shipping. And as I do have a bottle of Ferric Chloride in the kitchen, here&apos;s how the first <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/4977-portable-environmental-monitor" target="_blank">uRADMonitor-D PCB</a> was made:<br></p><iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/DcVUpxi0MTE" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: Lato, sans-serif; line-height: 24px; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%; color: rgb(102, 102, 102);"></iframe><br>This first one was a single layer, doing the top layer with tiny wires (possible as all wires are either horizontal or vertical). Those under the microcontroller were soldered with just a very tiny cap, that was reduced even further with a fine file. Seeing how good it goes, I decided the next two will be dual layer. And so they were, with the layers perfectly matched for even those tiny holes.<br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_toner_transfer.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_toner_transfer.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_toner_transfer"></a><br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_pcb.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_pcb.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_first_pcb"></a><br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_prototypes.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_prototypes.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_prototypes"></a><br>Almost there!</div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4977/log/22270-the-first-prototype">The first prototype</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">12 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4977/log/22270#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22270"><p>It&apos;s been <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/a-true-hacker-makes-his-own-pcbs/" target="_blank">a crazy run</a>, trying to make this happen. After less then one month I now have the first functional hardware iteration or the first prototype. The <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/4977-portable-environmental-monitor" target="_blank">Portable Environmental Monitor</a> has been brought to life! All that&apos;s left is finalising the enclosure (it will be a nice rugged aluminium enclosure, small enough to fit the palm of the hand, despite the many sensors packed inside) and the software (both the firmware and the server side for centralising data sent via <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/esp8266-troubleshooting/" target="_blank">the esp8266 module</a>). Then there are the small improvements, part of every product&apos;s path.</p><p><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_1.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_1-300x200.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_1"></a> <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_2.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_2-300x200.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_2"></a> <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_3.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_3-300x200.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_first_prototype_3"></a><br>There were a few issues like regulator getting a little too hot, unhappy to have a boost converter as a neighbour, or a wire that had to be cut, because I forgot to design a diode in place to stop the battery driven inverter from charging the battery. Well, these things happen, the fix was easy, and didn&apos;t even damage the PCB:<br></p><iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/DPvsicTXnl0" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: Lato, sans-serif; line-height: 24px; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%; color: rgb(102, 102, 102);"></iframe><br>To comply with Stage 2 requirements of the Hackaday Prize competition, I had to shoot a 2 minutes video, and this was an excellent choice to present the prototype in its current form, and to iterate a few of its construction details:
<iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/KC23fl33ja0" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: Lato, sans-serif; line-height: 24px; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%; color: rgb(102, 102, 102);"></iframe></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4977/log/22712-beta-units-shipped">Beta units shipped</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4977/log/22712#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22712"><p>After <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/the-first-prototype/" target="_blank">a sinuous but exciting road</a>, the Beta prototype came out nice, the entire device is amazingly small: I was able to design it to fit the 110x70mm aluminium enclosure I planned initially without renouncing to any of the sensors nor reducing the 1500mAh lithium battery. See it for yourself, here&apos;s the video presentation for the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/4977-portable-environmental-monitor" target="_blank">#bestproduct hackaday prize competition</a>:</p>
<iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/D-7rgwHWxkI" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: inherit; font-variant: inherit; line-height: inherit; font-style: inherit; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%;"></iframe>Producing the Beta hardware<p>The day before I got an express package from China with the pro PCBs, but my 3 units were already fully assembled. <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/the-first-prototype/" target="_blank">Manually, that is etching the PCBs</a>, drilling tens of holes, soldering tiny 0805 components with the soldering iron, using power tools to drill and mill the strong aluminium of the 3 enclosures. I had no time, nor additional MiCS-VZ-89 sensors for more units. But those PCBs look good:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5709031439499661955.jpg"></p><p>There&apos;s <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/mics-vz-89-air-quality-sensor/" target="_blank">the MiCZ-VZ-89 advanced air quality sensor</a> measuring CO2 and VOCs in air, the neat <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/tube-lnd-712-end-window-alpha-beta-gamma-detector/" target="_blank">LND712 Geiger tube for alpha, beta and gamma radiation</a> including its high voltage regulated inverter, the <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/sharp-gp2y10-dust-sensor/" target="_blank">GP2Y10 dust sensor</a> and the <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/bmp085-barometric-pressure-sensor/" target="_blank">BMP180 for temperature and pressure</a> while the PCB allows soldering an additional BME280.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7978501439499710300.jpg"></p>The Aluminium enclosures<p>First thing to do was designing the final enclosure. The design files can already be used in a factory to produce machined enclosures, including nice silkprint to label the various connectors.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4762371439499757826.jpg"></p><p>Cutting aluminium is easy if it&apos;s thin. I found out my cases won&apos;t take the fast spinning diamond disk, so my solution for milling was drilling as many holes as possible to remove the unneeded material, then use a Dremel with a nice milling head. A CNC router would have done wonders here, yet I was able to do it by hand just fine. For drilling holes, I saw that drills for wood give more precise results in aluminium due to their sharp tip better penetrating the material. For the LCD holders I initially wanted some 3D printed parts, but there was no time for that, so I opted for making them out of plastic sheets and painting them black afterwards<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4964601439499854073.jpg"></p>
<iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/jHa4IHaMS8g" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: inherit; font-variant: inherit; line-height: inherit; font-style: inherit; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%;"></iframe><p><a name="photos"></a></p>The hardware ready<p>For a single man, this was an enormous effort, but after less then one month it gave an amazing product. Finally the hardware is ready in all its beauty:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5998291439499885384.jpg"></p><p>As we speak the three prototypes are on their wait to the Hackaday Headquarts to participate in the <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/03/25/best-product/" target="_blank">Hackaday Prize 2015 #Bestproduct competition</a>.</p></div>
