                                <div id="post-body-16738"><p>Completed some initial market research before embarking on this project.  Most IoT solutions for smart watering solutions do not include any direct sensor feedback.</p><p>Some of the more advanced technologies incorporate at a basic level some weather data from online APIs.</p><p>Most relatively inexpensive solutions rely on manual scheduling of the watering system.</p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4698/log/21477-re-factoring-the-ospi-code">Re-factoring the OSPi code</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">25 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4698/log/21477#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21477"><p>For those of us who&apos;ve tinkered around with DIY sprinkler controllers, the <a href="http://rayshobby.net/ospi/" target="_blank">Open-Source Sprinkler</a> over a <a href="http://rayshobby.net/" target="_blank">Ray&apos;s Hobby</a> wonderful website has been a great resource to get a wealth of information for all (or most) things related to irrigation control.</p><p>It was an obvious decision to stand on the shoulder of giants and use the OSPi software as a starting point for our controller.  However, we encountered several aspects that made it quite difficult to use &apos;as-is&apos;.  We&apos;ve thus far forked the project and started re-factoring some of the code that&apos;s taken this wonderful group of people 2 years to develop---not a trivial task.</p><p>The main things we&apos;ve worked out thus far:</p><p><em>Front-end</em></p><ul><li>Use of AJAX functions to avoid having to do full page refresh</li><li>Use of Twitter Bootstrap 3 for styling to avoid writing too much custom CSS</li></ul><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4351651437768548688.png"></p><p><em>Back-end</em></p><ul><li>Use of Apache mod_wsgi directly rather than running a ReversedProxied web.py webserver (improved security)</li><li>Use of crontab for GPIO access to avoid running the entire process with privileged permissions</li><li>Adding direct wireless access to the controller using hostapd</li><li>Adding an API for logging sensor data</li><li>Adding charts for visualizing logged data</li></ul><p>Be on the look out for upcoming where we&apos;ll individually showcase each new feature individually!</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4698/log/21537-calibrating-soil-moisture-sensors">Calibrating Soil Moisture Sensors</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">25 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4698/log/21537#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21537"><p>After doing some (scientific) literature review soil science and hydrology, we found several studies comparing inexpensive soil moisture sensors:</p><p><a href="http://www.k-state.edu/irrigate/oow/p12/Chavez12.pdf">http://www.k-state.edu/irrigate/oow/p12/Chavez12.pdf</a></p><p><a href="http://www.iac.ethz.ch/people/mheidi/Mittelbach_et_al_2012">http://www.iac.ethz.ch/people/mheidi/Mittelbach_et_al_2012</a> </p><p>We decided to test the waters [pun intended] with the Model 200SS WATERMARK Sensor from Irrometer (Riverside, CA).  This solid-state electrical
resistance sensing device  is used to measure soil water tension. As the tension
changes with water content the resistance changes as well. </p><p><span></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1344271437849733869.jpg"></p><p>Soil moisture sensors are useful for irrigation scheduling.   The water status of the soil can be measured during the dormant season (for instance, in a vineyard) to determine if winter rainfall is reaching the desired depths or specific areas.   In addition, these sensors can be used to adjust the length of irrigation times to optimize them and avoid over- or under-watering the soil.</p><p>The data provided by soil sensors helps the machine learning algorithms running on the controller (or users interested in mining the raw data) understand how water displaces through the soil as well as what areas around the roots are consuming water most actively.  Proper placement of soil sensors is fundamental for collecting the data that will be processed by the controller in order to suggest optimal irrigation schedules.<br></p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>

                            <small class="authorship">23 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4698/log/21606#discussion-list">

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21606"><p>While working  on the mount that will be used for the Clouden Nodes&apos; enclosures, we realized that our initial design was neither aesthetically pleasing nor entirely functional.  The original metal brackets that came with the mount were longer than the height of the enclosure box.  To allow for a more compact and secure fixture, we re-designed the brackets using SolidWorks, and proceeded to cut them out of acrylic using our inexpensive Chinese 40W CO2 Laser Engraver, which is controlled by the MoshiDraw software.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2679891437978081817.JPG"></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">The new brackets fit the enclosure and the mount quite well, eliminating the overhang of the original ones as well as providing a sturdier fixture for the Clouden Nodes while  improving the overall looks!<br><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4966511437978101703.JPG"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></span><br></p></div>
