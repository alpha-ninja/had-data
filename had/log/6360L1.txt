                            <h2><a href="/project/6360/log/22750-we-won-the-intel-iot-hackathon">We won the Intel IoT Hackathon!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6360/log/22750#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22750"><p>The idea for the project was born during the preparation for the Intel IoT Roadshow Hackathon held in Berlin on April 25, 2015.</p><p>Initially wanting to build an (all-purpose) bike computer, we decided to focus on the aspect of measuring road quality seeing as this had not been done very often before, and it seemed like the perfect fit for the event, both in the time required to build a first working prototype, as well as regarding the hardware we were provided, which included the Intel Edison, an accelerometer, GPS module and other useful bits and pieces.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6666521439534968707.jpg"></p><p><br>After 24 hours of intense coding and debugging (most of the time was spent trying to get *any* program running on the Edison at all) and a quick test run around the block with the device attached to the bike frame and a MacBook in the backpack serving as a $1000 battery pack, we were able to present the project to the judges including actual, real measurement data! They were impressed!</p><p>The mechanical design, a combination of a 3d-printed mounting bracket for the sensor and LEDs and a quick-and-dirty cardboard box hack to house the controller, was a looker, too!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8180251439534889602.jpg"><br>This event did not only provide the spark of inspiration for the project, but also the right tools for the job, the perfect athmosphere for getting things done quickly, and last but not least, the encouragement of the judges to keep on working on this idea after the hackathon</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/80711439534916366.jpg"></p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6360/log/22753-switching-from-edison-to-arduino">Switching from Edison to Arduino</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6360/log/22753#discussion-list">
                                    2 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22753"><p>While we started off with the Intel Edison as the controller for the computer, some flaws or deficiencies soon became apparent:<br>- It was rather big and clumsy<br>- It drew a lot of power<br>- It was a hassle to program, and the IDE (Intel XDK) was extremely slow<br><br>We decided to switch to an Arduino Uno for the next iteration, to address these problems as well as to make the project available to a greater number of people already familiar with the platform. As a long time Arduino user myself, this also greatly sped up the development process, even though it initially meant rewriting everything from scratch.<br><br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8971361439578877610.jpg"></p><p><br>The sensor setup will stay the same for now, as the Grove base module is actually *designed for* the Arduino. Later on we shall develop a custom PCB to house all sensors and displays, to reduce the cost and keep the number of wires to a minimum.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8092511439578962336.jpg"></p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4570261439578918139.jpg"><p>This is a view of the second iteration, still made out of cardboard, but much more compact and neat.<br></p><p>Top: Individual LEDs (left), LED bar graph (right)<br>Right: GPS module and antenna<br>Bottom: Accelerometer module (screw-mounted), USB port<br>Center: Arduino (bottom), Grove base module (top), red electrical tape (everywhere)</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6360/log/22755-improving-the-enclosure-and-mounting-system">Improving the enclosure and mounting system</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6360/log/22755#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22755"><p>While the first version of the enclosure did its job to demonstrate the feasibility of the project, there was clearly room for improvement.<br>Some requirements:<br>- The enclosure should be as water, weather and shock proof as possible<br>- The power source (USB power bank) should be housed together with the electronics<br>- No dangling wires; everything should look neat<br>- Quick access to the controller&apos;s USB port should be possible, as well as the charging port</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4066901439582761023.jpeg"></p><p>Initial sketch.</p><p>The mounting brackets are attached to the bike frame via zip-ties, which proved very reliable and robust during the hackathon. The case housing the electronics can be removed from the bike by releasing four screws, without having to remove the zip ties. Additionaly, a lid can be removed for quick servicing such as a firmware upgrade or for rechargint the USB battery pack.<br><br>Here are some renderings of the new design, created to be 3D-printable, easy to assemble and adressing all the requirements mentioned above:<br><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5258401439580265244.png"></p><p>We will use Adafruit&apos;s Ultimate GPS Logger Shield, as it provides GPS, RTC and SD Card logging, as well as enough prototyping space to attach the accelerometer and WiFi modules (for uploading).<br></p><p>The blue PCBs are the Arduino and the Logging Shield. The case is longer than the PCBs to accomodate the USB battery pack that resides within the lower compartment, and the corresponding cabling.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6785721439580291000.png"></p><p>We keep the inside and outside of the box strictly separated, especially at the mounting holes. We took care to ensure there were absolutely no through holes on the top part of the enclosure, with could be exposed to rain while riding. None of the screw holes and nut inserts for the mounting mechanism provide a way for water to enter the enclosure.</p><p>You can see this in the following pictures. Notice the orange cross sections. The case is actually upside down in the pictures; the PCBs will &apos;hang&apos; from the mounting pads visible in the center.<span></span></p><p>The bottom &apos;lid&apos; will provide a lid to prevent water from leaking in from the bottom side. We will need to perform some rain tests to see whether this setup will stand up to the lovely German weather.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1240911439580226460.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3804501439580207366.png"></p><p>We use keyhole style mounting holes to easily detach the unit from the bike frame (thanks Hackaday for <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/06/24/learn-40-years-of-mech-prototyping-at-lightspeed/" target="_blank">providing the video with the inspiration for this</a>!). Removing the cover on the underside reveals the USB port (for firmware updating) and the USB battery pack (for recharging).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7313941439580565310.png"></p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6360/log/22807-what-to-do-with-the-data">What to do with the data?</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6360/log/22807#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22807"><p>While the measuring hardware is working fine and collecting nice data, we are still working on what to do with that data afterwards. Currently, we are simply saving the maximum peak from the previous time frame (1 s) every time we log a new datapoint. The visualization then draws a line from one point to the next, with the color of the line representing the intensity of the vibration.</p><p>Green areas are smooth roads, whereas orange and red areas represent medium and high surface roughness. It is easy to see how the main streets are rather smooth excluding some small defects, whereas the smaller, perpendicular cobblestone roads instantly stand out as long red lines.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/948311439646116897.png"></p><p>We can probably do a lot more by looking at the detailed waveform of the vibration.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8984551439577809110.png"></p><p>Blue: Raw data from accelerometer<br>Orange: Smoothed envelope of vibration profile<br>Red: Maximum acceleration measured within a certain timeframe</p><p>Left part: bumpy road<br>Right part: single potholes/bumps</p><p>Potholes and bumps can be extracted not only by their short duration, but by the ratio of the red and orange curves.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8809461439577731919.jpeg"></p><p>By looking at the envelope of the vibration over time, we could try to classify different types of roads. Maybe even a Fourier transform to get a detailed profile of a road, to determine whether it is a regular pattern (cobblestone) or a random roughness profile (going over grass, gravel, etc.).</p><p>Detailed peak analysis could reveal features such as curbs and train tracks crossing the road. </p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
