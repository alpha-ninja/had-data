                            <h2><a href="/project/4693/log/18591-new-shield">New shield</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4693/log/18591#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18591"><p>I decided to design a new shield including the spectrophotometer beside some small modifications like gain control for the respiration sensor and a gas sensor to analyze the exhalation gases . After the shield and spectrophotometer is finished, I will focus on software and wetware for the tricorder. Things will slow down a bit as I will soon move to Germany with my family and I have to leave all my equipment and my chemistry lab here in China.</p><p>To save place, the push button interface had to be removed. I am using  a <a href="https://www.sparkfun.com/products/10063">joystick-like interface in a very small package</a> instead: </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5985061432795032666.jpg"></p>X-ray view of the spectrophotometer:<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/117081432795413350.jpg"></p>Partly routed shield:<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7657731432795470135.jpg"></p><p>One reason why I am using a respirator- half-mask for the <strong></strong>respiration rate sensor is that I am able to do a analysis of the exhalation gases at the same time. This can be done by one or more common available MQ-series gas sensors, which are attached to 3-D printed gas chambers with a predefined volume:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5348171433134916981.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1707291433135048904.jpg"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4693/log/21330-medical-tricorder-rev-3">Medical tricorder Rev. 3</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4693/log/21330#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21330"><p>I have started working on the third revision of the medical tricorder. So far I have routed the new shield and the two PCB&apos;s which will carry the color sensor and RGB LED for the spectrophotometer.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/61721437588579988.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1718691437588770081.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2632901437588786447.jpg"></p><p>  The housing for the <span class="redactor-invisible-space">spectrophotometer was </span>produced by applying a particle material (<strong>PMMA </strong>Polymethylmethacrylate) in layers, which is bonded together with a binding agent.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9417321438408142283.JPG"></p><p>Finally the cuvettes arrived:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3128191439012769617.JPG">PCB&apos;s:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4150741439312982803.JPG"></p><p>Partly populated:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4609071439313028425.JPG"></p><p>PCB ready for some first tests:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2176651439413213175.JPG"></p><p>Completely populated shield:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1612411439565659979.JPG"></p><p>Testing the RGB LED of the <span class="redactor-invisible-space">spectrophotometer</span>:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1962421439565724514.JPG"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">The next couple of days I will write code for the <span class="redactor-invisible-space">spectrophotometer. Once done, a short demonstration video will follow. </span></span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">In a first test I will use <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benedict's_reagent">Benedict&apos;s solution</a> to determinate glucose level in<span class="redactor-invisible-space"> urine. Benedict&apos;s solution is prepared as following:</span></span></span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></span></span></p><p>(1) <strong>Benedict A</strong>: Dissolve 1.7 g of CuSO4<span class="st">&#xB7;</span>5H2O in 20 ml of deionized H2O.
</p><p>(2) <strong>Benedict B</strong>: Dissolve 17.3  g of sodium citrate and 10 g of anhydrous Na2CO3 in 70 ml warm deionized H2O.
</p><p>(3) <strong>Benedict&apos;s Reagent</strong>: Pour Benedict&apos;s solution <strong>A</strong> slowly into solution <strong>B</strong> and make up the volume to 100 ml . Mix well.
</p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Use this <a href="http://www2.hendrix.edu/biology/CellWeb/Techniques/Benedicts%20Test.htm">procedure</a><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"> to determinate <span class="redactor-invisible-space">glucose level by spectrophotometer.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span><br></p></div>
