                            <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21130-our-reasons-for-and-paths-to-commercialization">Our Reasons for and Paths to Commercialization</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2552/log/21130#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21130"><p>The primary focus at the FarmBot Project is to build a community around using and developing FarmBot technology much like how the <a href="http://reprap.org/" target="_blank">RepRap Project</a> has done. This means <a title="My Personal Goal" href="http://go.farmbot.it/2014/03/27/my-personal-goal/" target="_blank">community sustainability must be reached</a> whereby the technology grows, the community thrives, and the work is accessible to non-developers and/or non-technical people. This is all very possible and can happen in an expedited fashion when there exists the opportunity for compensation (financial gain) and people capitalize on that opportunity. Such commercialization may take the form of:</p><ul><li>Providing FarmBot software as a paid service</li><li>Selling premium software plugins</li><li>Selling hardware plans, kits and/or accessories</li><li>Selling value-added data</li></ul><p>Therefore my reason for commercialization is to ensure that community sustainability is achieved and to get there faster than it might happen otherwise. The path to commercialization is dictated by what the community needs, which at this time I suspect will be all of the different components required to own and operate a Farmbot:</p><ul><li>Easily assembled, hackable, and fully documented complete hardware kits</li><li>Maintained software as a service suited to non-technical users</li><li>Access to and the ability to share plant growing data (<a href="http://openfarm.cc/">OpenFarm</a>)</li></ul><p>We will need to provide these three components at least until there are other companies or people providing those value to the community as well, otherwise we run the risk of losing community sustainability.</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21131-hardware-monetization-strategies">Hardware Monetization Strategies</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2552/log/21131#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21131"><p>In the last blog post, I outlined our <a title="Our Reasons for and Paths to Commercialization" href="http://go.farmbot.it/2014/09/23/our-reasons-for-and-paths-to-commercialization/">reasons for and potential paths to commercialization</a> concluding that it is commercialization, or monetization, that is a necessary component to bootstrap the FarmBot Project towards <a title="My Personal Goal" href="http://go.farmbot.it/2014/03/27/my-personal-goal/">community sustainability</a>. In this blog post are strategies that could be used by myself, other project collaborators, or future entrepreneurs not yet familiar with the FarmBot Project in order to monetize FarmBot hardware, ultimately strengthening the community by improving the technology and making it more accessible. I am writing this publicly because I want to share which options I have considered and some thoughts on each strategy. I welcome feedback in the comments section or in email.</p><p>In general, hardware monetization will involve getting more &#x201C;skin in the game&#x201D; than with software monetization (blog coming soon). This is because hardware development involves a higher barrier to entry: one must own prerequisite hardware and tools, prototyping costs are higher, and there are many difficulties involved in scaled manufacturing, distribution, and/or service. Here are some strategies for monetizing hardware.</p>The Crowdfunded Approach<p>With the advent of accessible crowdfunding websites like Kickstarter and Indiegogo, there has been an explosion of innovation and product development being driven by consumers themselves. Crowdfunding can allow an idea to get &#x201C;buy in&#x201D; from consumers in order to validate the product&#x2019;s value and need ahead of time. With crowdfunding, funds are transferred up front and in one large sum such that people can be paid to develop the product and make bulk purchases of components, and having time to do so. The model has been proven to be quite successful for <a href="https://www.kickstarter.com/discover/advanced?term=3d+printer&amp;raised=2&amp;sort=magic">dozens of 3D printer projects</a> as seen by the sheer number and consistent overfunding of new campaigns almost weekly. So how can this apply to FarmBot?</p><p>As an example, it might look like creating a campaign to develop a specialized tool for depositing seeds in bulk. Perhaps the design is open-source but too difficult for regular people to manufacture themselves, so setting up a traditional manufacturing line will keep cost low and make the superior design more accessible. Or maybe the design is closed source and so much more innovative and difficult to produce than other designs, that there is hope it will sell and not be easily replicated. No matter the product or if it is proprietary or open-source, it is possible that the FarmBot community will either validate the need and fund the campaign, or deem that there really is no need for the innovation &#x2013; that it provides marginal value &#x2013; and so they choose to not fund it.</p><p>When successful, the crowdfunding strategy can be very effective at quickly building a client base and making a lot of pre-sales, increase exposure and press for the project significantly &#x2013; potentially attracting more contributors, and inspire discussion, validation, and feedback from the community.</p><p>Challenges of crowdfunding hardware are many, including: going from no production to very high production with no ramping, the risk of running out of money and being back at square one by the end of the campaign with no capital to reinvest in a company, and also poor execution which may ruin your brand or community. Of course, these can all be avoided with careful planning and there are many resources and blog posts available to run a campaign successfully. For example, you can see our strategy for and the benefits of running our <a href="http://go.farmbot.it/blog/page/2/kck.st/1yBHkVG">Kickstarter campaign for OpenFarm</a> in our <a href="http://blog.openfarm.cc/2014/09/16/creating-and-running-a-successful-kickstarter-campaign-for-openfarm/">recap blog post</a> (granted, it is not for hardware, but it should still be useful).</p>Venture Capital and Traditional Product Development and Sales<p>For new companies, seeking out capital investment and developing a product in a traditional and likely proprietary manner is of course an option, though I think it is not as good compared with the crowdfunded approach. With this strategy, one gives up equity and control, will not have as great a connection with customers, and may waste far more time if the idea does not meet customer needs as expected and therefore does not sell as expected. This strategy also foregoes the huge marketing hype experienced with a crowdfunding campaign, as well as the potential for volunteer contributions.</p><p>However, let&#x2019;s say a company is already established and has a client base. In this case, running a crowdfunding campaign may be more work than it is worth. Rather, taking on venture capital to increase the capacity of the company may be a better choice. This may allow the company to maintain focus as a whole company and simply tap into their existing client base when the new product is ready. The company&#x2019;s brand and clout may spread the word and drive sales adequately on its own. It all depends on the state of the company.</p>Selling Designs, Not Matter<p>Traditional hardware development costs tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars to initiate due to the high cost of design, setting up tooling and a manufacturing line, bulk ordering of parts, etc. However, with the advent of free and low cost manufacturing tools and resources (3D printing, CAD/CAM software, and facilities such as MakerSpaces and Fab Labs) hardware manufacturing is becoming more democratized. It is quite possible for someone to create a business based on selling only designs rather than physical products, relying on the consumer to do the physical manufacturing.</p><p>This strategy may work well if one&#x2019;s designs truly have a competitive advantage and they continue to innovate, but there is high risk of those designs being quickly copied and made available for free or lower cost. However, that is not to say that it cannot be done to <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xGhj_lLNtd0">stay on the cutting edge of innovation</a>, especially if the design company can build a strong brand and reputation for being the best and the go-to source.</p>Localized Manufacturing and Service<p>Because FarmBots may need to be significantly tailored to the environment that they exist in and the types of plants they are growing, there may be opportunity to setup local &#x2018;FarmBot Dealerships&#x2019;. Such a business could provide on-site assembly and setup of the hardware and offer technical support for whenever something goes wrong. They may have showrooms with different tooling and accessories available. They could offer scheduled maintenance packages to keep the machines running smoothly. Here&#x2019;s an idea: One could setup a company that leases FarmBots to own and while the lease is being paid off, some percentage of the food produced could be owned by the leasing company and sold at the local markets or integrated into a crowd-based-CSA! The possibilities here are endless.</p><p>This strategy would likely be very difficult to scale and reach a mass market. However, it may be a necessary step for the project to achieve its Vision in aiding <em>everyone</em> to grow food, as many folks may not be savvy enough to own and operate a machine without such a service.</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21136-farmbot-sequence-builder-and-web-app-roadmap">FarmBot Sequence Builder and Web App Roadmap</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2552/log/21136#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21136"><p>As the FarmBot hardware continues to improve and we polish up the core functionality of our embedded codebases, it is time to start looking at how users will use their hardware in meaningful and fully configurable ways. We have decided to create something called the &#x2018;Sequence Builder&#x2019; &#x2013; a drag-and-drop interface on our web application where the user can configure the most basic operations that FarmBot can execute, and combine those basic operations to form a longer, more complex sequence of things to do. This was inspired by the <a href="http://mix.bio/">mix.bio</a> interface proposed by the <a href="https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/932664050/opentrons-open-source-rapid-prototyping-for-biolog">OpenTrons project</a>, and we&#x2019;re excited to see it come together. Here is a mockup of what we are building towards:</p><p><img src="http://go.farmbot.it/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Sequence-Builder-Soft-Colors.jpg" alt="Sequence Builder Soft Colors"></p>Our Web App Roadmap<ol><li>Initially, we will build in support for all of the basic operations we currently have in the manual control page: relative and absolute movements, reading and writing pins, sending errors, and waiting. All parameters will need to be manually entered by the user. A sequence can be created from these operations, named, and saved. Saved Sequences can also be used a components of larger sequences.</li><li>Next, we&#x2019;ll make another page on the web app that will allow the user to &#x2018;schedule&#x2019; their sequences to be executed at a certain time, or on a repeating basis.</li><li>Then we&#x2019;ll need the ability for Sequences to have their own parameters, and for parameters of sub-operations and sub-sequences to &#x2018;Inherit&#x2019; their value from their parent sequence. This will allow for a very complex sequence to be applied to one set of coordinates at the top level without having to change all of the individual parameters in the sub-operations as long as they are initially set to &#x2018;Inherit&#x2019;. We will also want to be able to set parameters from a list of variables such as Plant-62_X-Coordinate, or Tool-Bay-4_Z-Coordinate.</li><li>At this point, we may be able to take a Growing Guide from <a href="http://openfarm.cc/">OpenFarm</a> and translate it into a schedule of operations.</li><li>Now we&#x2019;ll need the ability to intuitively create a plant database (where plants are located, their type, and their age). This will be in the form of the &#x2018;Farm Designer&#x2019; page on the web app. This will allow users to schedule complex operations for specific plants or groups of plants. A sequence scheduled for a group of plants will run as a loop for each plant in the group.</li><li>By now a user will be able to fairly efficiently tend to a lot of different plants. We can then start optimizing certain parameters based on other data sources (weather, soil probe, etc) via the Decision Support System (DSS).</li></ol><p>The goal of all of this is to allow the user complete control down to every parameter of every operation that FarmBot executes. By reducing FarmBot&#x2019;s complex functions to the most basic operations, we allow users to easily make the device work with new tools, in new ways, and to the person&#x2019;s exact needs and desires. Here are some of the ideas for how sequences could be created using other sequences and even variables:</p><p><img src="http://go.farmbot.it/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/FarmBot-Block-Sequence-DSS-Architecture-1.jpg" alt="FarmBot Block-Sequence-DSS Architecture (1)"></p><p>Obviously steps 2-6 above are very far off, and we&#x2019;ll be focusing first on step 1, but that is the overall plan in a nutshell. Interested in helping us out? Check out the <a href="https://github.com/FarmBot/farmbot-web-app">web application repo</a> and our production site at <a href="http://my.farmbot.cc/">my.farmbot.cc</a>!</p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/2552/log/21137-the-farmbot-web-app-manual-control-page">The FarmBot Web App Manual Control Page</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2552/log/21137#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21137"><p>We&#x2019;ve been working hard on the design of the manual control page of the FarmBot web app and we want to show you what we&#x2019;ve been up to. As we learn more and more about the design constraints and requirements of the manual control page, we iterate the design. As we hone our design skills and center in on a unified design language, we tweak the styles. The UTM is ncessary because it is not feasible to have all tools mounted on the z-axis at one time for several reasons:</p><ol><li>This would be very heavy and create more stresses on all components, as well as necessitate a larger z-axis motor.</li><li>Most tools need to be the &quot;lowest&quot; thing on the z-axis in order to work. Having multiple tools competing for the lowest position (ex: a temperature probe and a seed injector) would not be ideal and may not work at all. The use of individual tool raising and lowering mechanisms, or a turret style mechanism would be complex, heavy, bulky, and limited in the number of tools it could support.</li><li>The Z-Axis&apos;s size must be kept to a minimum in order for it to have minimal impact on the plants, especially when there is not very much space between them.</li></ol><p>The UTM is a single 3D printed component that mounts to the Z-Axis aluminum extrusion using two M5 screws and tee nuts. It features 3 strong neodymium ring magnets to magnetically hold tools in place via washers or other magnets placed in the same configuration on the Tool. The magnets double as the keying system to ensure that tools are properly oriented in the UTM. The magnets also function as the passage ways for water, liquid amendments (eg: fertilizer), and vacuum or compressed air to pass through from the UTM (and the rest of FarmBot) to the Tool. In addition, the UTM has 4 spring loaded screws that make electrical connections with Tools. Two are for power (GND and +5V), and two are for data (0 to +5V).</p><span class="mw-headline">Universal Tool Mount Change Log</span><p>There are many changes from the V4 UTM design including the following. For a video overview of the design, <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JIVe0dvgx6w&amp;feature=youtu.be">click here</a>.</p><ul><li>Switched from the single central magnet design from V4 to a three magnet design in order to keep the tools from easily being torqued out from the mount, and to increase the holding power.</li><li>The ring magnets are now glued in place instead of held in with screws in order to keep the hole free.</li><li>The ring magnets are now the passageways for air, water, and liquid amendments (eg: fertilizer) to pass through from the UTM to the Tool.</li><li>The outer diameter of the UTM has increased slightly to accommodate the increased number of pass-through components.</li><li>Center screw is now spring loaded too.</li><li>Mounting flange is now 8mm thick instead of 5 for extra strength.</li></ul><p>There are two versions of the Universal Tool Mount this time around. Both are functionally the same, one is simply optimized for the Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) 3D printing process while the other is optimized for Stereolithography or Selective Laser Sintering (SLS). The major change is that the FDM version is much bulkier so that no support material is needed. This means that the part takes a long time to print, but it requires very little to no manual cleanup once done printing.</p><p>The SLS version is significantly smaller in volume, allowing to be printed with less material, thereby saving on time and cost. However, these printers are generally more expensive and not as widely available to the public as FDM printers are such as RepRaps.</p><p>Therefore both versions have great merit and will are made available for download. Make sure to select the correct model before printing depending on the process you choose or have access to!</p><span class="mw-headline">A note about Tools, Power, and Data</span><p>Tim Evers and Rory Aronson had a lengthy discussion in email regarding how to handle communication between the tools and the Arduino/RAMPS board. This discussion can be seen on the <a href="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/wiki/Universal_Tool_Mount" title="Universal Tool Mount">Universal Tool Mount</a> page. The summary:</p><ul><li>Plug the vacuum pump for the seed injector into one of the heating element pin pairs on RAMPS</li><li>Plug the water pump/valve into the the other heating element pin pairs on RAMPS</li><li>Plug the Universal Tool Mount&apos;s 2 power wires into the fan pins on RAMPS</li><li>Plug the Universal Tool Mount&apos;s 2 data pins directly into the Arduino</li><li>Tools will at first have a single function so communication will be general I/O and not require extra electronics</li><li>Eventually tools may have more functions and require an I2C connection and additional electronics in the tool to support this communication</li></ul><span class="mw-headline">FDM Universal Tool Mount Renderings</span><span class="mw-headline">FDM Version</span><ul></ul><span class="mw-headline">SLS Version</span><ul><li><a href="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/wiki/File:V5_UTM_Render_1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="V5 UTM Render 1.JPG" src="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/mediawiki/images/8/84/V5_UTM_Render_1.JPG"></a></li> <li><a href="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/wiki/File:V5_UTM_Render_2.JPG" class="image"><img alt="V5 UTM Render 2.JPG" src="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/mediawiki/images/6/65/V5_UTM_Render_2.JPG"></a></li></ul><span class="mw-headline">Universal Tool Mount Photos</span><span class="mw-headline">FDM Version</span><p>Note: In these photos, there are M3 screws in the mount. Again, do NOT print the SLS version with an FDM printer as seen in these photos, it will not come out well.</li></ul><span class="mw-headline">Universal Tool Mount Issues and Proposed Solutions</span><ul><li>The main issue with the V5 Universal Tool Mount is that the three pairs of magnets directly mating with each other is too much force for the z-axis to be able to separate them. In fact, it is difficult to remove the tool from the mount even by hand! We tried making a tool without magnets, instead using washers in hope that the attraction would be weaker. That worked but posed a new problem: the attraction to the tool&apos;s washers was not strong enough in comparison to the attraction to the tool&apos;s screws for the electrical connections. This resulted in the mount and tool aligning improperly about 50% of the time.<ul><li>Only having metal where the magnets are (no screws enticing a misalignment) will help prevent misalignment. And not allowing the magnets to touch each other directly will reduce the holding force.</li></ul></li><li>Another issue with the magnets is the difficulty of seating them all in the tool mount to the same z-level in order to get good seals between the the tool and the mount. A remedy we tried was supergluing gaskets onto the magnet surfaces of the tool and the tool mount, but that was finicky as a corrective measure, and changed the distances between the tool and mount so the electrical connections no longer worked. That could have been solved with different sized screws or counter bores, but the glued on gaskets we&apos;re still not a great solution.<ul><li>Not having the magnets ever touch might be a better solution.</li></ul></li><li>Gluing the magnets into the mount and tool is also problematic. It means that the magnets cannot be easily removed - a major problem if one glues in a magnet with the wrong orientation such that it repels the tool instead of attracts it. In this case, one must take damaging measures to remove the magnet and flip its orientation.<ul><li>Screwing in the magnets to the tool and the mount is a much better solution as was done with the V4 design. This will be brought back in V6.</li></ul></li><li>Last, depending on the quality of the 3D printed parts, the barbs can be easily broken.<ul><li>This could be solved by replacing the printed barbs with screw-in metal barbs or with higher quality 3D printed parts.</li></ul></li></ul><h2><span class="mw-headline">Electronics</span></h2><p>V5 hardware integrates <a href="http://wiki.farmbot.cc/wiki/Rotary_Encoders" title="Rotary Encoders">Rotary Encoders</a> onto the back of each of the stepper motors.
