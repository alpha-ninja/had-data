                            <h2><a href="/project/7225/log/22926-march-2015-designing-a-low-cost-x-ray-sensor-first-attempt">March 2015 - Designing a low cost x-ray sensor - first attempt</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7225/log/22926#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22926"><p>At this point, I had recovered, or rebuilt most of <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/7225-low-cost-x-ray-systems-for-developing-nations/log/22917-february-2015-losing-everything-in-a-burglary-fighting-depression-and-staring-into-an-abyss" target="_blank">what was lost</a> in February. Feeling (somewhat) better, I set out to design a low cost x-ray sensor to accompany the x-ray source. Film after all, is expensive, messy, and, very logistically troublesome for medical practices in places that have less infrastructure than the USA.</p><p>-</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7029411439669953075.jpg" width="339" height="601" style="width: 339px; height: 601px;">-</p><p>Silicon wafers are expensive, let&apos;s use less of them!</p><p>By using a photostimulable <span class="il">phosphor</span> <span class="il">plate</span>, (ie, a <span class="il">phosphor</span> whose previously stored energy may be released by a low-energy photon), we<br>could in theory, capture a low-dose transmission x-ray, and there-after read it out using an array of 650nm laser diodes linearly focused onto the <span class="il">plate</span> with a cylindrical lens, and a 1D array of CMOS photodiodes which sense the stimulated emission (blue light) via a1D-array of rod lenses and a dielectric, high-pass mirror. </p><p>The gantry containing these optics could be placed behind the <span class="il">plate</span> and moved with a stepper motor much like a flatbed scanner, which would allow us to produce self-contained digital x-ray sensors of any size, at a fraction of the cost of using a 2D array of CMOS photodiodes as is done currently. </p><p>To build this, I used; </p><ul><li>10 Laser Diodes</li><li>Europium-doped barium fluorobromide phosphor</li><li>A floppy disk drive</li><li>A logitech webcam</li><li>Plastic rods and optical cement</li><li>An MSP430 and stepper motor drivers</li><li>Dielectric mirrors from a projector</li><li>Solder</li><li>A cardboard box</li><li>An image intensifier (since the webcam is crappy)</li></ul><p>And, built this assembly below:</p><p>-</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4708651439670274640.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6943161439670284449.jpg"></p><p>-</p><p>Before an x-ray exposure, the laser diodes (on the board with the blue potentiometer) would illuminate and cast a line on the phosphor plate, which scans back and forth a few times to clear any cosmic ray noise that could have been there. </p><p>After an x-ray exposure, the laser diodes pulse briefly, and the phosphor emits a blue light that is reflected off a mirror, focused through two dielectric filters onto the image intensifier, and then finally digitized by the camera. This process repeats 130 times, and a collection of 1D images are recorded in a computer by a python script for later stitching.</p><p>- </p><p>For testing purposes, I built a small copper grille to take an image of. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1958011439670567454.jpg"></p><p>- </p><p>And, this was the result. </p><p>-</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9847601439670775134.png"></p><p>- </p><p>I wasn&apos;t too impressed. Never mind the limited lines / mm. That could be fixed. </p><p>What was unsettling, was the terrible dynamic range. Despite the two filters, ted light overpowered blue, To fix this, I would need very expensive, tuned laser diodes and a very narrow band filter to record *only* blue light. This would make the cost of a real sensor prohibitively high.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7225/log/22932-april-may-2015-designing-a-low-cost-x-ray-sensor-second-attempt">April, May 2015 - Designing a low cost x-ray sensor - second attempt</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7225/log/22932#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22932"><p>Building upon what I learned from the last sensor, I set out again to build a low cost x-ray camera. This time, I excluded the possibility of using any moving parts in the design. </p><p>After thinking about flat panel displays, I hypothesized the following:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7980931439672517210.png"></p><p>Following the total internal reflection principles used in LCD backlight panels, is it possible to capture an image in the same way? That is, to design a prism which folds light coming in at the critical angle, throughout the prism body in such a way that you see the image of its face in the base of the prism. </p><p>If so, I could capture the image on an x-ray phosphor panel with little more than a camera and molded plastic!</p><p>-</p><p>Within a few days, I had learned how to use optical design software. In theory, my models suggested that this could work. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6761951439672668219.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7741491439672655180.jpg"></p><p>-</p><p>But how could I possibly test this in the real world? I never had optically polished anything before, let alone built a prism. </p><p>The answer is, <em>very carefully. </em></p><p><em>-</em></p><p><em></em></p><p><em><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7994171439672986836.jpg"></em></p><em>-</em><p><em>And slowly. </em>This took a few days.</p><p>Eventually, I had my prism.</p><p><em>-</em></p><p><em><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5128521439673013115.jpg"></em></p><p>-</p><p>And my lord, <strong>it worked</strong>. </p>-<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/414751439673061247.jpg"></p>-<p>With this, a phosphor plate and a camera with large pixels, I should be able to capture x-ray images without film, for a substantially less sum than if I were to make a large silicon wafer for the purpose.</p><p>To test this, I made the prism light-tight, and coupled (the same crappy) webcam to it..</p><p>-</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7823251439673293252.jpg"></p><p>-</p><p>And in 200 ms, I had a reasonable x-ray image! I had matched x-ray film in exposure dose. </p><p>-</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2162991439673324427.png">-</p><p>The image is a bit blurry as my optical surfaces were a little less than stellar, but with a diamond turned mold for the PMMA, this is won&apos;t be a problem!</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7225/log/22975-march-2015-building-a-mini-ct-scanner-for-fun">March 2015 - Building a mini &#34;CT scanner&#34; for fun</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7225/log/22975#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22975"><p>With a working, mini x-ray sensor, and a bluetooth-controlled mobile x-ray source on hand, it seemed silly not to capture some 3D / rotated x-ray images of the small electronics trinkets I could get my hands on. </p><p>To do so, I put together a small stepper motor system consisting of a micro controller, a stepper motor driver, a few buttons and a nice base. The microcontroller is programmed to accept some integer number of steps, and will send commands over its serial port to the computer before each step. </p><p>-</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2698511439693570866.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8608701439693578144.jpg"></p><p>-</p><p>On my computer, I bridged the serial port to feed its incoming data out the virtual bluetooth port, connected to the x-ray source. Initially I had a bluetooth radio on the CT scanner itself, but, it proved too unreliable for reasons I haven&apos;t yet figured out myself. </p><p>-</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3157391439693023971.jpg"></p><p>-</p><p>The results were pretty to look at, to say the least. </p><p>I have not yet written a script that preforms the radon transform on the image collections, but, if anyone is willing to take the challenge, I&apos;d love to hear more about it!</p><hr><p><strong>Pololu Stepper driver:</strong></p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/0FiQtjR.gif"></p><hr><p><strong>Teensy 3.0:</strong></p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/XOFkAPL.gif"></p><p>-</p><p>The source files for this small CT scanner <a href="https://drive.google.com/folderview?id=0BwIWrZZX8IW6fjF3LXdmUzlzMUUwWHhiS3VDcDkwV2dTTUttRFFnb1hDMENnYzhiV3JFNkU&amp;usp=sharing" target="_blank">are available here</a>. It uses a standard size stepper motor. </p></div>
