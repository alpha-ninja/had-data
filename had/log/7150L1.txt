                            <small class="authorship">4 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7150/log/22826#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22826"><p>My &quot;Gravity Bar&quot; seems to be bouncing all over the place--lots of movement. To examine this more closely, I needed a way to perform time lapse video, economically if possible. To try this, I used the built in camera on my MacBook and free software called <a href="http://gawker.sourceforge.net/Download.html">Gawker.  </a></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4044541439596838145.jpg"></p><p>My first attempt showed the bar moving around, but coming to a rest near the mass attraction point after about 3 hours.</p><p><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O26_n5i4I1I">Video of coming to rest.  </a></p><p>Next, I moved the steel balls slightly and ran the time lapse again. A nasty electrical storm passed through at 5:45 (watch the clock) and things really bounced around. Is the bouncing due to vibration or electrical noise?</p><p><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fmGlatBzFiM">Second attempt with thunderstorm intervention.</a><br><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O26_n5i4I1I"></a></p><p><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O26_n5i4I1I"></a><br></p><p><a href="http://gawker.sourceforge.net/Download.html"></a></p></div>
