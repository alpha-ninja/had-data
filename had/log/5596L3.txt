Battery got weak after the last on-phase</p><p>On-times:</p><p>97..174, 362..538, 747..803, 998-1208 (weak batt. until 1260), 1437-1541 (weak batt.)</p><p>Blue curve: raw data</p><p>Red curve: low pass filtered data (lowpass seems to be set too low, comes too late)</p><p>Interpretation is difficult for me - torsion 3 test looked very non-ambiguous compared to this one.</p><p>Maybe you can find anything useful in this data.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1885951434625542314.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2220391434624823133.png"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5596/log/20382-swimming-platform">Swimming Platform</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5596/log/20382#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20382"><p>Here´s the new swimming platform.<br>We hope this one will give more useful results.</p><p>The platform is stabilized by two cuboid neodymium magnets, one under the platform and one under the water tank. The magnets keep the platform aligned in the center of the water tank while the cuboid shape works like a weak torsion spring, aligning the rotation angle to a defined state.</p><p>The data acquisition will be made automatically via optical tracking of two LEDs mounted in the edges of the platform.</p><p>Many thanks for your comments and suggestions - we hope to provide some results for you soon.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5489041435915729212.jpg"></p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5596/log/20473-preliminary-tests-swimming-platform">Preliminary Tests (swimming platform)</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5596/log/20473#discussion-list">
                                    8 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20473"><p>Optical markers (LEDs) are tracked with a camera to determine the angle of the platform.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2151831436102467519.jpg"></p><p>It shows that the platform reacts very sensitive to high power loads in the house. When the TV is on, it gets noisy. When people take shower in the morning hours (electric heating here), the angle lowers significantly. Where the jump in the pic below comes from - I don´t know,  maybe a neighbor toggled some power load.<br>For the preliminary test, I took the data from the flat areas of the graph - in this case in the sections before and after the jump.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4610361436102530373.png"></p><p>The cavity has been turned on and off alternately. For the preliminary tests, two ON and two OFF phases were recorded with cavity in CW and CCW direction. Green graphs show cavity OFF, red graphs show cavity ON. Due to the jump (see above), the second pair of values is raised.</p><p>Cavity attached to push CCW:<br></p><p>When the cavity is on, the angle drops below the off-level.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8859791436102700808.png"></p><p>Second test with cavity meant to push CW:</p><p>The angle rises above the off-level.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4305281436102908886.png"></p><p>This looks plausible so far. Anyway this is neither a proof nor a real valuable data.</p><p>Another think I noticed is the poor resolution in determining the angle.<br></p><p>It´s because the marker detection algorithm works only on pixel accuracy. I will improve it to calculate the marker position on subpixel accuracy.</p><p>Although I am happy to see these results, it might be just a coincidence that they look good. I´ll need to perform long duration tests to be sure.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5596/log/20599-trouble-with-sideeffects">Trouble with sideeffects</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5596/log/20599#discussion-list">
                                    5 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20599"><p>I ran two clean tests with cavity mounted CW and CCW.</p><p>What looks like thrust on the first sight turns out to be a parasitic force that turns the platform always in the same direction :(<br></p><p>So it must be some current or thermal effect in the system. My guess are lorentz forces.</p><p>I don´t know how to get rid of this effect, it will make measurements really hard.</p><p>I could compare the differences between the ON/OFF phases of the two tests to get some clues, but because the baseline is always fluctuating it´s hard to see.</p><p>And the baseline also changes when the cavity is turned around due to the different mass distribution on the platform (magnet gets realigned a little and causes slight position change?)</p><p>Do you have any suggestions?</p><p>X-Axis: on/off phases, ca. 30min each</p><p>Y-Axis: platform angle in 1/100°</p><p>Means of the first graph: 11563 11743 11554 11569 11644 11723 11506 11724 11589 11814 11683 11767 11525 11710 11512 11591</p><p>and of the second graph: 11456 11727 11605 11772 11680 11894</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6627651436287055447.png"></p></div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5596/log/20633-redesign">Redesign</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5596/log/20633#discussion-list">

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20633"><p>The current RF source prototype is too shabby, so it´s time to redesign.</p><p>I designed a board for generating a digitally fine tuneable RF source in the 24GHz band capable of delivering 500mW of power. It also contains a feedback antenna.</p><p>I´ll stop the current testing procedures now and will send the cavity to Jo for modification.</p><p>The cavity will be also made mechanically tuneable. Together with the fine frequency tuning we hope to get better results in the future.</p><p>I will use the LightBlue Bean we won recently to communitate with the platform remotely:<br><a href="http://store.hackaday.com/products/lightblue-bean">http://store.hackaday.com/products/lightblue-bean</a><br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6427611436340594262.png"></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5596/log/21069-pcb-for-new-rf-source">PCB for new RF source</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5596/log/21069#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21069"><p>It arrived. The PCB has two antennas. The big one is the probe for feeding the cavity and the small one is for feedback RX.</p><p>We will try to make the antenna position variable inside the cavity.</p><p>About the RF source: A VCO transceiver is controlled with a 16bit DACs The TX frequency is divided by 16*65536 and fed back to the controller. The I component of the RX baseband is fed into the ADC input of the controller.</p><p>A 500mW amplifier is connected to the transceiver&#xB4;s RF output and fed with 5V. A negative bias for the amplifier is generated by a charge pump from another DAC.</p><p>The feedback antenna is shortened for not stealing much energy out of the cavity. It is attenuated by 40dB before going back to the transceiver IC.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7052011437132934731.jpg"></p></div>
                            </div>
