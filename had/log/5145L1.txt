                            <h2><a href="/project/5145/log/22745-zig-8-prototype-testing">Zig-8 Prototype Testing</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5145/log/22745#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22745"><p>Most of our focus has been on the Zig-4 lately but I finally found some time to play around with one of the Zig-8 (24 bit, 8 channel) prototypes to do some noise floor and short-term drift test. There is still a lot of work that needs to be done, but the results where still worth posting.  Remember this is a Prototype! </p><p>The setup of the test was basically take three pretty good voltage references, put them on the bench and monitor them along with a temperature sensor and a silver oxide battery (one of the original voltage references<span class="redactor-invisible-space">) for 10 hours once a second.  Not overly scientific <span class="redactor-invisible-space">but educational nonetheless.</span></span></p><p>Below is a plot of the three Vref&apos;s.  This is a 1 hour section after about 6 hours of warmup (you can see the time stamp at the top).  Even though a sample was taken every second there is a little aliasing going on with the way the software displays all the data so you don&apos;t actually see every point (sorry).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9941601439529232475.jpg"></p>The scale on the left is in &#xB5;V and all the plots have been zeroed out for easy comparison.  So what do we see?  Well we are basically looking at the combined noise and drift of the SiGZiG Zig-8 prototype with the noise and drift of the 3 voltage references.  And it looks like, at least over 1 hour, that the combined noise and drift  stays pretty much within +/- 1&#xB5;V<span class="redactor-invisible-space">.  So what part of the noise is from the Zig-8 prototype and what belongs to the voltage references?... well the short answer would be that generally any signal common to all the readings is from the SiGZiG.  That being said there are other things in common, like temperature (for good or bad).</span><p>Lets do some averaging to see if we can better pick out the common signal.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6127431439530390161.jpg"></p>The above data has been smoothed with a running average of 10 samples.  You can kind of make out parts that are in common to all three plots.<p>Okay, this 60 min chunk is getting boring, lets look at the whole thing.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8832621439530608329.jpg"></p>As you can see not all voltage references are created equal.  Now if you look at the yellow plot the real question is which voltage reference is drifting, the one being measured or the one in the Zig-8?  Well if I was only measuring the yellow one it would be pretty hard to say, hopefully now you can see the wisdom in measuring more than just one supposedly great voltage reference.  By monitoring more than one it becomes a statistics game and in this case the yellow one is the odd one out.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3748071439531131379.jpg"></p>Applying averaging again and you can start to make out the commonality between signals.  However looking at what is common is still not a perfect method for recreating the noise and drift signature of the SiGZiG because there are slight variations between each channel as well as the fact that each channel is not sampled at exactly the same moment in time. Overall though it still gives a much clear picture than just monitoring a single voltage source when down at these small levels.<p>So how did the silver oxide battery compare?</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8051501439531707094.jpg"></p>Actually not that bad, we are talking 140&#xB5;V of drift over 10 hours.  Even so it definitely puts the others in perspective.  <p>And what about temperature?</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8257861439531869174.jpg"></p>I noticed some of the changes in temperature seemed to match up with variations in the voltage of the silver oxide battery.<p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">After scaling and overlaying you get this</span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6609401439531978823.jpg"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">If you had a good enough data logger you could almost use a silver oxide battery as a temperature sensor!... okay no you couldn&apos;t but you can obviously<span class="redactor-invisible-space"> see it is affected by temperature.</span></span></span></p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5145/log/23655-using-input-impedance-to-measure-current">Using Input Impedance to Measure Current</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5145/log/23655#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23655"><p>Input impedance on measurement equipment is usually kept as high as it can in an effort to prevent loading down the signals the equipment is measuring.  For example most general purpose digital multimeters aim for an input impedance of at least 1M ohm.  The Zig-4 has a lower input impedance of 330k ohms but this is still high enough for many/most situations.  That being said it is still important to understand input impedance and its affect on your measurements. </p><p>In this post I thought it would be fun to actually take advantage of the 330k input impedance of the Zig-4 as a sort of low current sensing.</p><p>The goal:  To see if we can use the Zig-4 to detect a change in current in the very low nA range.</p><p>Even though most everything can be calculated, I thought it would also be nice to back up some of the calculations using actual measurements.  I wanted to use a recently acquired Keithley 480 Picoammeter and on the lowest setting so the first order of business was generating a really small known current.  </p><p>Using a 1G ohm resistor and a 1.25V voltage reference we can generate a current of 1.25nA.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8615691439881638130.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6459581439881657929.JPG"></p>The resistor is 1% and the voltage reference is not exactly 1.25V plus the meter could be off a bit... but overall that&apos;s close enough.<p>I then added a 100M ohm resistor in series with the 1G ohm resistor effectively lowering the current to a calculated 1.136nA</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4871311439881866761.JPG"></p>Again, close enough.<p>Now lets swap out the Keithley 480 picoammeter with the Zig-4 and do the same change in current to see what kind of signal we get.  The current in both cases will be a little lower because now there is the additional 330k of the SiGZiG.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5335501439882144282.jpg"></p>Above is the setup except I switched back and forth between the 1G and the 1G+100M ohm resistor.  Below you can see the signal I was able to get.  The Zig-4 does have a small amount of programmable gain, so in all fairness the below plot is with it set to a maximum gain of 8 giving it more bits for the range it was measuring.  <p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9240761439879397120.jpg"></p><p>So by using the input impedance of the Zig-4 to our advantage we can actually use it to monitor small changes in current down to the sub nA range.  In the case above we were able to clearly pickup a change in current of only 115pA (113.5pA to be exact).  </p></div>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23715"><p>It&apos;s not just a label, it is the label that your SiGZiG will don for the rest of its life.  We are being extremely picky about the choice of supplier.
