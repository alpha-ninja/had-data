                            <h2><a href="/project/5466/log/20172-eduball-simulink-model">EduBall Simulink Model</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5466/log/20172#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20172"><p>A few updates,</p><p>I have recently added a Matlab Simulink model to the GitHub including additional code, the model is also available on Matlab file exchange: <a href="http://www.mathworks.com/matlabcentral/fileexchange/51718-eduball-simulink">Simulink Model</a>. </p><p>The model is designed to check the feasibility of the project, in terms of what Physical phenomena i can actually demonstrate with the current hardware setup. The setup is quite simple, The juggling ball is used to measure the Acc/Gyro values and transmit it to a "dongle" (NRF_Receive) which is connected to the computer via USB. The Simulink model reads the values through a COM Port and performs some simple data manipulations. The update rate of the data is set to 100 Hz.</p><p>The simulink model is used to demonstrate Acc/Gyro Raw values, Acc/Gyro Scaled and biased values. Normal Acceleration and Rotation Rate. angle calculation based on Gyro integration and Acc gravity vector calculation. and the final is complementary filter demonstration with comparison between the different approaches.</p><p>See image as to how it all looks:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7343781435570808464.jpg"></p><p>Conclusions:</p><p>From the Acceleration reading it is very clear to distinguish Free fall and average g such as 1g when the ball is still. It is possible to see the high g`s when the ball is hit, but not at enough sample rate to perform any calculations based on the data such as energy conservation when the ball is thrown and caught at the same height)</p><p>The centrifugal forces are also very clear to demonstrate and to actually compare them to the gyro rotation rate, (as long as the gyro isn`t saturated (at 2000 deg/sec).</p><p>finally it seems that the hardware is good enough for most demonstrations, (still there is a lot of work ahead to actually build a class curriculum and run a pilot<span class="redactor-invisible-space">).</span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><br></span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">The current stage of the project:</span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">- Find a big company which is interested in supporting the project</span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">- Test better hardware setup, to get higher sample rate (1 KHz), and with wider range of the accelerometer (200g).</span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">- Find someone interested in education to help with the curriculum<span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></span></p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5466/log/20296-eduball-demonstration">EduBall Demonstration</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5466/log/20296#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20296"><p>I have done some tests with the platform and have plot results to show for (recorded in real time at 100 Hz).</p><p>First image demonstrates filter effects on the measured acceleration, for the test i have put the ball in a plastic bag and rotated it in the vertical plane resulting in the effect seen in the graph. As you can guess the purple line represents raw data and the yellow one, is the filtered data with a simple LPF filter at time constant of 1 second. (the graph scale is in g`s)</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/235821435746201516.png"></p><p>After that i have decided to play some throw and catch. (the graph scale is in g`s).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8910251435746524396.png"></p><p>And for the finish i have decided to test how precisely i can calculate the length of my plastic bag based on the readings of the gyro and the accelerometer. (Centrifugal force) </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/972631435746793447.gif"> (image is taken from this <a href="http://keisan.casio.com/exec/system/1271292951">site</a>.)</p><p>And my results: (the graph units is deg/sec for the gyro, accelerometer at g`s. and length in meters).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8129461435746931759.jpg"></p><p>Surprisingly (and maybe not) the length turned out to be pretty accurate and as you can see i have played with the rotation velocity).</p><p>So go grab your plastic bag, and do some experiments. (be careful, i managed to bump the ball into my head while trying to press the print screen).</p></div>
