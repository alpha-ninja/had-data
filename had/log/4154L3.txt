I have generated the gerber files now, and everything is ready to go, pending no problems found over the next day or so.</p><p>You can find the zip file <a href="https://github.com/thebiguno/microcontroller-projects/raw/master/projects/power_supply/kicad/gerber/power_supply_panelized.zip">containing the gerbers on github</a>.</p><p>Included in this gerber is the power supply (digital control, one channel for either positive or negative supply, and the power distribution rails), plus Rev 1.1 of the <a href="http://hackaday.io/project/3330-ubbb-32u4">UBBB 32u4</a>, and a small MSOP-10 / TSSOP-14 breakout.                                 <div id="post-body-16886"><p><span></span>One month is a long time when you are waiting for boards to arrive!</p><p>As mentioned earlier, my PCBs are ordered and have been shipped a couple weeks ago.  I estimate they will arrive in early May.  Free shipping from China is slow, and I am too cheap to pay for faster shipping.  :-(</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8957871428115526760.png"></p><p>The boards are shown again here... the board with the heat sinks will be used multiple times (once per channel, so I will be using 4 myself), while the other sections will be used only once (unless I can figure out some alternate use for them).</p><p>Furthermore, I am somewhat confident that the PSU channel board can also be used as a dummy load if you skip populating the regulator + headsink, and just short out the IN and OUT lines on the regulator chip.  This needs to be verified still, though.</p><p>In the mean time I have been planning the enclosure.  The faceplate will be brushed aluminum, and will look something like this:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3000781429897109216.png"></p><p>The entire enclosure will be made of wood (with aluminum front and back plates), with an overall look similar to my resource monitor:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6860961429462397099.jpg"><span></span><br></p></div>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-17441"><p>My boards arrived today, so when I got home from work I started to solder one channel up to see if things worked.</p><p>Spoiler alert: it works!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8621341430931177243.JPG"></p><p>It took a couple of tries.  Reflow soldering TSSOP chips is hard, and I still have not got the hang of it - there always seems to be one or two solder bridges hanging around, which are very hard to see even with a loupe.  Also, I don't have the SMD schottkey diodes which I had planned on using, so I had to substitute a through hole diode with the leads trimmed to be a couple of mm in length.</p><p>So far I have found only one error on the board: the D3 diode is reversed.  It shows the polarity pointing towards the bottom of the board, but it should be the other way.  Sorry!</p><p>Below is a quick video showing off the beautiful soldered board in action... it was unrehearsed and done in one take, and hence rambles a bit, but hopefully it gives you an idea of how things look and work.  Enjoy!</p><p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/QRgkthc16F0" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe></p></div>

                                <div id="post-body-17834"><p>I soldered up the main controller last night, but something is a bit off and I can't seem to program it using the ISP headers.  The default bootloader works, though, so I am not quite sure what is wrong.  More investigation needed.</p><p>I did discover one more error: the crystal footprint is rotated 90 degrees from what it should be.  So, instead of placing it in 2.5x2 orientation, you should place it in 2x2.5.  If that makes any sense at all. :-)</p><p>More updates to follow once I figure out what in the world is happening with ISP.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2982591431530138431.JPG"></p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4154/log/17978-more-isp-investigation">More ISP Investigation</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4154/log/17978#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-17978"><p>Well, I did some more investigation into the ISP issues, and while I made some progress I still don't know exactly what is happening.</p><p>I ended up soldering a new controller board with just the AVR, crystal, and 8pF caps, and programmed the bootloader + fuses successfully.  I then soldered the rest of the components, and tried ISP again, and it didn't work.  Something in the circuit is interfering with the ISP programming.  <br></p><p>The confusing part to me is two-fold:</p><ol><li>The circuit is very simple (the ISP pins are shared with the encoder headers, and the only components are a pullup resistor, a small capacitor, and a TVS diode; all programming testing was done with the encoders not installed).</li><li>Obviously the capacitor / pullup could have something to do with this issue, so the first time I encountered it, I removed them: it still didn't work.</li></ol><p>Now, to be fair, I had some clock issues the first time I saw this problem, so it may have been two issues coinciding that produced the confusing results.  Regardless, I am a bit confused still.  I will have to look into options for fixing this in future revisions (anything from a simple resistor to separate the ISP from the encoder, to perhaps a DIP switch to switch between the two).</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4154/log/18232-some-progress-on-the-enclosure">Some Progress on the Enclosure</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4154/log/18232#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18232"><p>On Monday I cut the aluminum plates for the front / back to size.  I used a circular saw, which was terrifying (my brain was screaming 'why are you trying to cut metal with this!?!?'), but it worked out fine.  Next step is to cut the holes (most will be done with a drill, but there are some sections which will require non-circular cutouts, which I will attempt to do with my scroll saw).  I have done this before on a smaller piece, and it worked out fine... here's to hoping that these larger cuts will work too.</p><p>Yesterday I screwed the switch mode supplies into the footprint.  I am using a scrap of particle board for the base of the enclosure, and all the components, including the front and rear aluminum plates, will attach directly to this.  The cover will be of 1/4" hardwood, and will be mostly cosmetic (although it will help to channel the fan's airflow towards the appropriate components).</p><p>Here we have the current plan for the enclosure, as seen from the front / rear / top.  (The rear is still lacking a bit of detail; it will have the AC code plug and a fuse).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2308281432142698905.png"></p><p>Finally I have a couple pictures showing how the actual layout is progressing:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9201531432142736408.JPG"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8708911432142751832.JPG"></p><p>The next step is to cut the front faceplate, attach it to the base, and get one channel wired up, so that I can start doing some real testing on the channel + controller.  (It is hard to test when high current wires are just hanging loose on the bench...)</p><p>Cheers</p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4154/log/18290-footprint-update">Footprint Update</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4154/log/18290#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18290"><p>I decided to change the footprint from 3/4" particle board to 1/4" hardboard.  The reason for this switch is mostly convenience: I have 3/4" #4 screws, but I don't have 1 1/2" #4 screws.  </p><p>I have now also drilled the holes for all four channels' PCBs, mounted one channel, re-mounted the SMPSUs, and mounted the power rails.  I had originally planned for the power rails to use screw terminals, but I think that directly soldering will give more resilience over time as well as make for a better electrical connection, at the expense of losing some flexibility.</p><p>Updated pictures are below.</p><p>Top view with approximate locations<br><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1437001432225314611.JPG">Closeup of power rails.  Two parallel wires connect to each connection in the SMPSU; one set of wires connects to each channel.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3620411432225329918.JPG"></p><p>More wires</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4290631432225339453.JPG"></p><p>Bottom of the footprint.  #4 screws are used to mount all the hardware; rubber feet will keep the screws off the table.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2457691432225347334.JPG"></p><p>Closeup of a single channel mounted to the board.  I still need to get some nylon nuts for the mounting holes right next to the heat sinks (I tried with metal nuts at first, and while it looks like they are not quite touching the heat sinks, and I have tested for continuity between the mounting holes and the heat sinks (there is no connection), I want to be sure that it does not short out later on.  (The heat sinks are connected to live voltage: the transistor collector and the regulator VIN respectively, while the mounting holes are connected to ground).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8694171432225365695.JPG"></p><br></div>
