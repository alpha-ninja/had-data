                            <h2><a href="/project/4731/log/21057-cases">Cases</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4731/log/21057#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21057"><p>Howdy again!</p><p>In between working on the new firmware over the past little while, I have been designing the cases. My intention from the beginning, as you can see in my pictures, was to create a stack up of acrylic to build the case. I know this might not be the quickest way for production, but hopefully the design I have made is relatively easy to produce anyway. </p><p>The main reason for this design is the tools that I have. I have a laser cutter sitting here in my garage, one of the cheapie ones you can buy off eBay, but it has degraded over time and I can no longer cut through the thickness of material that I was planning on using. I also have a 3D printer, it makes spaghetti very well. </p><p>Anyway, I still wanted to go with the laser cut version, and then along came the mini contest from HAD for $100 credit with Ponoko, which I scored! Thanks HAD! Click past the break to see some images.<br></p><p style="
    text-align: center;
"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6241371437111009330.png"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><em>Here it is all compressed up.</em></span></span><br></p><p style="
    text-align: center;
"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8486471437111037613.png"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><em>And an exploded view</em><br></span></span></p><p>I am using three materials overall, 4.5mm black for the main body, 2mm clear for the top and bottom lids, and 3mm clear for the first spacer above the board.</p><p>The plan is for the 3mm clear to capture some of the light given off by the status LEDs. The top and bottom lids in future probably could be a solid colour, but for now, I wanted to see where so many hours of my life had gone :)</p><p>The purple is the OSHPark PCB. </p><p>You can see on two of the layers there are hexagonal holes cut in. The theory is that I can drop an m3 spacer in there that the top and bottom lids can screw into. I&apos;ll probably glue each layer together with acetone or some other glue so they don&apos;t move around too much. This will also stop the hex nut from falling out. </p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">I have updated my github with all the relevant files, so if you want to get your own cut, feel free to download them!</span></p></div>
                            </div>

                                <div id="post-body-21071"><p>Gday!</p><p>I&apos;ve been working away at my ESPLux a lot recently as you can see. My plan is to send off three ESPLux units to compete in the &apos;Best Product&apos; category. Who knows how I&apos;ll go, but one thing is for sure, I plan on giving it a good crack. Sending stuff from Australia over to the US can be sketchy at best, so I want my ESPLux boxes to have the best chance possible at making it over in one piece. </p><p>So I figured I should box them up, not in any box, but a little producty package thing. Here are a few pictures of my first one. This is my first attempt at any kind of papercraft, so bare with me! I made the base box first, followed by the lid, and it sure does show. Hopefully by number 3 I&apos;ll have it down pat.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8643021437134704266.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9544701437134773825.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7916901437134733605.jpg"></p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8571841437134813315.jpg">I just filled the box up with some foam I had laying around. Unfortunately (or fortunately, depending on how you look at it) it is that pluck type foam. This means it is hard to get a decent fit. I&apos;ll play around with this some more I think. Looking at the picture makes me realise how much more work I have to do before August ...</div>

Thanks goes to HAD for providing me with a $100 gift voucher for <a href="http://ponoko.com" target="_blank">ponoko.com</a>, where I got my cases laser cut. Make sure you go in the hotlists in the future if there are more, they are well worth it!</p><p>There are a few things that could be improved from this design, but overall I am very happy with it! First some photos, then I&apos;ll explain what could be improved.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9624541437721727566.jpg"></p><p>There are a few more pictures after the break.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8154981437721793745.jpg"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3162511437721842084.jpg"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9351371437722002962.jpg"><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Two things that could be fixed, first up, i screwed up the location of the Wi-Fi Reset hole. Ah well, it&apos;ll be fixed in the next revision.<br></span></span></span></p><p>The second thing, I engraved the case to allow a bit of air to flow around the rectifier, but it wasn&apos;t deep enough by any stretch of the imagination.                             <h2><a href="/project/4731/log/21610-had-article-improvements">HAD Article, Improvements</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">23 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4731/log/21610#discussion-list">
                                    7 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21610"><p>Wow thanks everyone! I wasn&apos;t expecting <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/07/25/hackaday-prize-entry-smart-low-voltage-lighting/" target="_blank">an article</a> posted about my little project.</p><p>Thanks to <a data-mention-user-id="13306" href="/hacker/13306">@arcol</a> and <a data-mention-user-id="66401" href="/hacker/12011">@bogdan</a> for their input, I&apos;m keen to include your suggestions into my project.</p><p><a data-mention-user-id="13306" href="/hacker/13306">@arcol</a> suggested that I should use different colours for the input and output terminals, which is a great idea, I didn&apos;t realise there were different colours in the same type. He also suggested using spring terminals and having two different locations to be able to wire the DC out into, both which I&apos;ll be looking into in the near future.</p><p><a data-mention-user-id="66401" href="/hacker/12011">@bogdan</a> noted, rightly, that the rectifier will get warm pretty quickly, which is correct. He suggested using active rectification to reduce the amount of heat produced in the process of getting a DC signal out. I hadn&apos;t heard about this before, so I was intrigued. It looks like you can use a couple of MOSFETs to perform the same role. Two downsides that I can see for this are, first, added complexity. This shouldn&apos;t be a problem, just something I need to wrap my head around! The second is the <em>V</em><em>gss</em> value on whatever MOSFET I choose, I need to make sure that it is capable of handling the voltages that I would like the ESPLux to handle. Alternatively I should be able to throw a zener diode in to drop the voltage to an acceptable level. I am super keen to have a shot at getting this working, it&apos;ll increase the number of lights that you can run on one unit. </p><p>Thanks again for everyone who has followed me in the past 24 hours, I&apos;m chuffed at the amount of people who are interested in it!</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4731/log/23329-brief-update">Brief Update</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4731/log/23329#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23329"><p>Hi everyone,</p><p>I&apos;m down in Adelaide for the week for work, so time is fairly limited for me. This is just a brief update on where I am at, and what my next steps are. </p><p>I&apos;m currently playing around with OpenHab, checking out the best way to integrate ESPLux into it. I have a document describing the API for ESPLux on my GitHub here: <a href="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/pretenda/ESPLux/master/Documentation/API%20Commands.pdf">https://raw.githubusercontent.com/pretenda/ESPLux/master/Documentation/API Commands.pdf</a>. I&apos;ve got my home theatre set up in OpenHa, so I&apos;ll probably set up an ESPLux box behind the TV and set up a bit of accent lighting for my home theatre as a small demo.</p><p>I&apos;ve also been playing around with how to reduce the amount of heat generated by the rectification process. There are a few things I am looking into. First up is active rectification. It uses a bunch of mosfets and a handful of jellybean parts. I really like the idea of this, but it does introduce complexity that isn&apos;t there in the current design. I like the current designs simplicity, but I need to weigh up my options here, see what route I&apos;d like to take. </p><p>As an addition to altering the rectification circuit, I&apos;d like the ability to have a DC only version, which will negate the need for any rectification at all. Hopefully I can implement this on the same layout. When you are dealing with just DC, this will save a fair bit on the overall BOM, so I believe this is a worthwhile cause. </p><p>Again, sorry for the lack of updates. Once this week is up, hopefully I can post some meaningful progress on both OpenHab and the rectification stuff. </p></div>
