                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;

                                    0 comments
                                </a>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16009"><p>I knew that my prototype base didn't have power or clearance to operate in a field or on rough terrain. I ordered a base (lots of assembly required) from superdroidrobots.com and just completed the assembly.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3508461428018208654.jpg">This is Nellie atop her new (big) child.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3739881428018277190.jpg">Here is the underside, with gear motors, noise suppression and bearings.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2810161428018352920.jpg">This is the assembled version, with batteries (24 volt) and (for now) RC control.</p><iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/eZ4I285bhlc" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" width="560"></iframe><p>This has the power and clearance to move around. It won't need to perform the spinning acrobatics in the field.  I do have a question for anyone reading this--the set screws that fasten the axle to the motor shaft tend to work loose (lots of stress with these acrobatics I'm performing). I don't want to use red thread lock--I might need to undo the screw to  change a motor or bearing. Any suggestions on how to keep the set screws from backing out; but still be able to remove them if required?</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4739/log/16271-first-test-weed-grabbing-arm">First Test, Weed Grabbing Arm</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4739/log/16271#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16271"><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/Ejmy9HrjclU" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><p>I took a Firgelli high speed linear actuator (10 inch stroke) and paired it with a Jameco Servo Gripper Unit.  The connection between the actuator and the gripper was 3D printed:</p><p><a href="http://www.thingiverse.com/thing:762559">Actuator/Gripper Connector</a></p><p>For the first test run, I mounted the actuator to a scrap of plywood and controlled the gripper with an Arduino Uno. The actuator has 22 pounds of force and moves at 5-9 inches per second. I used a high torque metal gear servo on the gripper unit. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6576571428515052786.jpg">This is what the test arrangement looks like.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1548321428515118437.jpg">I thought the weed might snap off or slide out of the gripper, but I was surprised to see that the actuator and plywood slid backwards as the weed tried to hang on. The weed was mostly uprooted in spite of the push back on my test rig. I think the 25 pound robot base will probably hang tough when yanking weeds--but it is surprising how tough these weeds can be!</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4739/log/17712-growing-pigweed">Growing Pigweed</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4739/log/17712#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-17712"><p>It took a while, but I found a source for pigweed seeds, <a href="http://www.riverrefugeseed.com/">River Refuge Seeds.</a></p><p>The pigweed seeds are tiny compared to soybean.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/946591431363980156.jpg"></p><p>I designed and 3d printed <a href="http://www.thingiverse.com/thing:821699">labels</a> (use painter's tape on the labels so that they are reusable), then planted seeds.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6542621431364124778.jpg"></p><p>Now, I'll photograph and measure the differences in growth between my target crop and target weed. This information will be useful for weed identification strategy. Ultimately, I'll use "light break" sensors as part of my process to identify weeds. For light break detection in a sunny (or cloudy) field, I'll place a photocell inside a tube (to shield it from direct sun) and direct a laser to the photocell. A Teensy-LC (or similar device) will be used to detect the significant change caused by path interruption.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9591631431364519956.jpg"></p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4739/log/20550-going-about-this-differently">Going About This Differently</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4739/log/20550#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-20550"><p>Growing Pigweed and Soybeans was an educational experience.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3419681436222468397.jpg">At first, all went well and I expected the Pigweed to grow rapidly and overtake the soybeans. It didn't happen. The little container garden in my yard was in an ideal spot--too ideal. There was sun and moisture from the irrigation system, too much moisture. In a few days, the soybeans were thriving and all the pigweed died.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9345311436222708279.jpg">This caused me to rethink my philosophy of "snatching teenage pigweed" from the ground. A soybean seed has over 5000 times the volume of a pigweed seed. Pigweeds are very vulnerable when they are tiny. What if we could wipe out the pigweeds during this vulnerable stage? </p><p>When soybeans are planted, the soil is disturbed and tiny pigweed seeds near the surface find conditions favorable to germinate. Suppose we "flame" the soil's surface at this time, killing the pigweed seed but not harming the bigger, stronger, deeper soybean? Instead of a flame, let's use the sun, focused through a linear fresnel lens. Let a robot carry the lens around the field (and let the robot be solar powered so that the whole mechanism runs off the sun). </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9416121436223149227.jpg">I have to work out a method for keeping the robot in the desired area as well as managing solar power and the fresnel lens.</p></div>
