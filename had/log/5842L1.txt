                            <small class="authorship">21 days ago  &bull;

                                    1 comment
                                </a>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21703"><p>With the winning of the business competition in Rwanda, the building of the lightning detector becomes a serious pursuit. We decided on the following selection criteria for the materials, basically based on the idea that we are not professional PCB designers:</p><p>- Avoid surface mount (perhaps a next version?)</p><p>- Use pre-built modules for the RF components: AS3935 lightning detector and GPS module. There are well designed pcb&apos;s on the market that would be hard to beat.</p><p>We will build the PCB to house power supply and CPU and allows for solid mounting or connecting the GPS and AS3935 boards. The power supply will be solar panel based. Most of the time, the module will be asleep, during which about 70 muA is used. Once lightning is detected, the AS#(#% will produce an interrupt to wake up the ystem during which the system needs about 50 mA. We assume thunder storms will not last more than 24 hours, in which case the batteries chosen here should work.</p><p>With these consideration, these are the materials                             <h2><a href="/project/5842/log/21802-pcb-design">PCB Design</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">20 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5842/log/21802#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21802"><p>Here is the first design of the PCB, adjusted after discussion on the Hacker Channel this morning, for which many thanks, and studying the guidelines for PCB design <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6936-pcb-design-guidelines">https://hackaday.io/project/6936-pcb-design-guidelines</a>.</p><p>Considerations:</p><p>- No surface mount (too little experience)</p><p>- Three functional groups: power supply (left), CPU (center), and RF part (Thunder click) as far from the rest as possible (it will actually stick out over the board with its RF part).</p><p>- Made supply lines wider</p><p>- Removed straight angles and replaced them with 45 degrees</p><p>- Hi frequency ground (crystal and GND of CPU) is isolated from the rest of the ground; only connected at one point to rest of GND</p><p>Unfortunately, with apologies to <a href="https://hackaday.io/whygee">Yann Guidon</a>, we do use the bottom side as well. You are probably right that it can be avoided but that will be the next version... Fortunately, at OSH Park, the price for two-sided is the same as for one-sided (about $10 per PCB).</p><img src="https://github.com/nvandegiesen/Team_TAHMO/blob/master/ld_pcb.png?raw=true"><p>Next steps, put all material on Github and order PCB and parts.... </p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5842/log/22469-system-design-document">System design document</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">8 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5842/log/22469#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22469"><p>The system consists of four parts:</p><ul><li>Power supply</li><li>AS3935 Thunder click</li><li>ATMega328</li><li>NEA-M8T GPS Drotek</li></ul><p>The AS3935 Thunder click and the NEA-M8T GPS Drotek are off-the-shelve PCBs. As these involves RF designs, we decided to go ahead with these solution under the motto &#x201C;do not solve a problem that has been solved before&#x201D;. In the final design, we may integrate these PCBs in our own design to save costs. There is an open source design for the AS3935 by Tautic (<a href="http://wiki.tautic.com/Category:AS3935_Lightning_Sensor_Dev_Board"></a><a href="http://wiki.tautic.com/Category:AS3935_Lightning_Sensor_Dev_Board">http://wiki.tautic.com/Category:AS3935_Lightning_Sensor_Dev_Board</a>).</p><p>All parts have been tested but the integrated design, hardware and software, not yet. The ATMega328 provides the intelligence to the system but is asleep most of the time. The AS3935 will be in listening mode most of the time, taking 60 muA at 3.3V. Once lightning is detected, an interrupt wakes up the ATMega328, which starts a timer and wakes up the GPS. The data (energy and time stamp) are sent to the TAHMO station through a SDI12 connection.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2404361439268055995.png"></p><p><em>Power supply</em></p><p>The power supply is relatively straightforward and is based on a design by Jon Viduchic (<a href="http://tahmo.org/going-solar-with-tahmo-stations/"></a><a href="http://tahmo.org/going-solar-with-tahmo-stations/">http://tahmo.org/going-solar-with-tahmo-stations/</a>). We are running a small duration test with the load being a LED and 330 Ohm resistor (15 mW) running on the board. The solar panel is 4cm x 3.5cm.</p><p><em>AS3935 Thunder click</em></p><p>Communication on the new PCB between the <em>ATMega328</em> and the &apos;<em>AS3935 Thunder click&apos;</em> has been tested.                             <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23075"><p>To test whether the small <a href="http://ixapps.ixys.com/DataSheet/SLMD960H12L-Datasheet.pdf">IXYS solar panel</a> would provide sufficient power, we ran the power supply for a week. The load was a simple diode with 330 Ohm resistor, taking 5mA at 3.3V Vcc. The results suggest that the supply is ample, especially because the average power consumption of the detector will be much less (&lt; 1 mA).</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8740511439744179707.png"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7158531439744044707.png"></p><p>In the final design, we may still want to go for a more advanced solution.
