                            <h2><a href="/project/5983/log/18509-25052015-first-experiments-with-chair-version">25.05.2015 First experiments with chair version</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5983/log/18509#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18509"><p>The chair version will work with any user and should be as low cost as possible so that it will be installed on a high scale on any possible chair. Alternatively, if the chairs are dedicated per person they may provide insight on time spend on each chair, like at work, at home or in the car.</p><p>I started doing some experiments with an ultrasonic distance sensor. As it turns out, it is not the ideal thing to use: this particular version requires 5V and quie a lot of power, but it will do for the time being, as proof of concept.</p><p>The circuit is build around an XMEGA32E5 one some header board, with an attached ESP8266 and your tipical HC-SR04 ultrasonic ranging module, nothing special. It is not optimised for power consumption in any way so it will burn a charge of batteries in 3-4 days. For now, it simply uploads the data to emoncms on my website, similar to Led logger V3.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3969.jpg"><img alt="DSC_3969" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3969-398x600.jpg"></a></p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3972.jpg"><img alt="DSC_3972" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3972-600x398.jpg"></a></p><p>As it turns out, putting the sensor on the top of the chair is not a good idea: some fabrics seem to be pretty bad at reflecting ultrasounds and crouching does not detect the person.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3979.jpg"><img alt="DSC_3979" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3979-543x600.jpg"></a></p><p>Next up, i mount this sensor between the seat and back of the chair. It is a delicate position, as a bit higher or lower will cause the sensor to pick up the cushions as obstacles. However this seems to work a lot better, usable with all the types of pants that i have. (well a long wool sweater will still break the thing).</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3983.jpg"><img alt="DSC_3983" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3983-600x381.jpg"></a><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3993.jpg"><img alt="DSC_3993" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3993-398x600.jpg"></a></p><p>Conclusion on ultrasonic: might work, delicate to place, large. The high voltage and high power consumption may be managed to get decent battery life.</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5983/log/18961-03062015-new-ultrasonic-sensor-and-some-data">03.06.2015 New ultrasonic sensor and some data</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5983/log/18961#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18961"><p>It&apos;s becoming obvious that ultrasonic sensors are not the best way. I have failed to make another one work with a different model of a chair, which means they will not work everywhere. Apart from that, they are very bulky, coupled with 3AA(A) batteries will have to create a reasonably large sensor. Time to think of alternatives. <br></p><p>I have just received the new <a href="http://www.e-gizmo.com/KIT/images/ultrasonicsonar/ultrasonic%20sonar%20module%201r0.pdf">US-100</a> ultrasonic sensors from ebay. As they operate from 3V, a 3.3V supply can be common to the microcontroller and WiFi. Current consumption is also lower, I am measuring 1.8mA vs 7.5mA with the older sensor, 4 times less, this should improve overall power consumption.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3999.jpg"><img alt="DSC_3999" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_3999-600x398.jpg"></a>I have set up an emoncms panel to watch over the data. Unfortunately it takes a precise level of zoom for the first graph to look this great: the visualizer decimates the data and then interpolates the remaining points linearly, which usually causes it to draw diagonal lines between some sitting and non sitting event. However, here is a section that looks great:</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/emoncms_data_2.jpg"><img alt="emoncms_data_2" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/emoncms_data_2-600x385.jpg"></a>On Saturday, 30th may I left something on the chair by accident, which caused it to record a lot more hours. Human and non human distinction would be great for new types of sensors.</p></div>
                            </div>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19212"><p>Today I was checking the proximity sensors based on optical reflection, specially the <a href="http://www.vishay.com/docs/83476/vcnl4020.pdf">VCNL4020 </a>which can do 20cm and which I have in my parts box. Or the <a href="https://www.silabs.com/Support%20Documents/TechnicalDocs/Si1145-46-47.pdf">SI1146</a>. It turns out that even though these would allow for a smaller sensor, they have more limited range than the ultrasonic distance sensor. Then, it hit me: capacitive! A simple wire placed under the chair can measure capacitance which should change depending on whether somebody is sitting or not.</p><p>Note: i am not looking at making a chair with built in sensor, rather a sensor as a simple to use add-on.</p></div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5983/log/19360-11062015-welcome-capacitive">11.06.2015 Welcome capacitive</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5983/log/19360#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19360"><p>For the last 2 days I have experimented with capacitive sensing using the method <a href="http://www.atmel.com/images/doc8497.pdf">described in Atmel&#x2019;s Qtouch ADC guide</a>: charge division between external capacitor and internal sample and hold. Things are a little bit trickier that with buttons: normally a capacitive sensor drifts, therefore it needs to be constantly adjusted. For a button it is not an issue, as you expect it to be not pressed most of the time. So, you just compare the current readout with some average over the past and you are done. For the Sit.Up sensor it needs to be able to detect a &#x201C;button press&#x201D; for longer periods of time, so the button methods don&#x2019;t work. I believe I solved this problem differently, but more on that later.</p><p>The new sensor can be a lot smaller than before, this new board sits on top of 2 AA batteries, and will be hidden under the chair anyway.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4697.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4697" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4697-600x398.jpg"></a></p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4716.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4716" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4716-600x398.jpg"></a></p></div>

                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19446"><p>After the first successful tests using capacitive sensors I implemented the thing in the chair. Here are the modules as size comparison. The new module can sit on top of 2 AA batteries and will not require more than 2 wires leaving from it.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4728.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4728" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4728-600x398.jpg"></a></p><p>Due to the nature of the capacitive sensor, having an ISP/serial cable connected to the PC influences the measurement. In order to develop the software I connected an LCD and in the end I thought it should stay on the chair for a while.</p><p>There are 2 electrodes made of aluminium tape: the one towards the back is the ground and the one towards the front is the sense. The capacitance is measured between the two and when a person sits, it increases. I could not find any normal sitting position on the chair which does no detect the person. Here is the whole thing assembled.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4756.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4756" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4756-600x398.jpg"></a></p><p>And of course, some data logging on the LCD:</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4747.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4747" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4747-398x600.jpg"></a></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5983/log/22542-10072015-dedicated-proximity-sensor-a-failure">10.07.2015 - Dedicated proximity sensor - A failure!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">7 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5983/log/22542#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22542"><p>I have done some brief experimenting with dedicated proximity sensors, specially the <a href="http://ww1.microchip.com/downloads/en/DeviceDoc/40001664B.pdf">MTCH101</a>: better sensitivity, but comes with  2 nice &#x201C;features&#x201D;. First, it is designed for momentarily proximity, so after a while it will compensate the person and think there is actually nothing there. Second, it seems to get stuck in &#x201C;detected&#x201D; mode quite often, even the datasheet mentions a &#x201C;Stuck Release Mechanism&#x201D; as a feature! So, no good.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4775.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4775" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4775-600x398.jpg"></a></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5983/log/22546-12072015-e-filed-sensor">12.07.2015 E-filed sensor</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">7 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5983/log/22546#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22546"><p>As my initial capacitive sensor using aluminium tape as electrodes proved successful, I looked into shrinking the size. Compared to measuring the capacitance, another method of detecting a conductive object like the human body around a sensor is by using the <a href="http://www.microchip.com/pagehandler/en-us/technology/gestic/technology/home.html">electric field method: </a>2 electrodes form a transmitter and receiver. When a conductive body comes between them, the amount of signal received changes. I have attempted to use a 75x100mm double sided PCB for this method, at first. In open air the detection distance can be even 20cm, but things change once this plate is placed under a chair: the sensor is very directional and there are ways to sit on the chair without being detected. Swapping the PCB for the original aluminium tape electrodes works better, but<em> there is no point of implementing this method, as it requires extra parts</em>. The system in this case consists of the microcontroller outputting a 1MHz signal on a pin serving connected to the transmitter electrode while the other electrode, the receiver, gets read out by the microcontroller&#x2019;s ADC after passing through a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detector_(radio)">simple detector</a>.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4772.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4772" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4772-600x373.jpg"></a></p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5983/log/22567-10082012-wrapping-things-up">10.08.2012 Wrapping things up</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">7 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5983/log/22567#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22567"><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/all_complete-6-3.jpg"><img alt="all_complete 6-3" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/all_complete-6-3-600x600.jpg"></a><p>With the original capacitive sensor proven to be the best option, it was time to wrap up the project and build the final prototype device and code.</p><p>Software wise, a lot of features had to be added, most importantly the ability to alert the user when sitting for too long. The device alerts by vibration every 15 minutes, but once an hour has passed, it becomes more annoying. A web interface is in the works which allow configuration of parameters. Other updated include  better calibration, low power modes, vibrator patterns, LED blinks, buzzer sounds, LCD disable and a lot of clean-up.</p><p>Hardware wise, the module lost it&#x2019;s LCD for debugging purposes and gained a vibrator motor, to discretely alert the user if sitting too much. Along with the batteries attached under the PCB, the whole thing is a lot more compact, practically invisible when mounted under a chair. The device is build around a general purpose board I have designed for the microcontroller, the complete schematic is available in <a href="https://github.com/electrobob/Sit_UP">the project documentation on GitHub</a>.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4849.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4849" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4849-600x390.jpg"></a><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/simple-activestandby-current-measurement/">Current measuremen</a>t shows up at around 25&#x3BC;A while sleeping, mostly eaten by the EPS8266 module, the micro itself needs only 0.2&#x3BC;A. Make sure to <em>cut the trace of the red power LED on the module</em>, otherwise it will waste most of the power. In active measurement the current drawn is 4mA, going for an average of about 100&#x3BC;A,  should provide 2 years of stand by time. Of course, frequent sitting for long times drains the battery because of the vibrator motor and WiFi. Nevertheless, with typical usage I expect about 6 months from a pair of AAs.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4853.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4853" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/DSC_4853-600x398.jpg"></a></p><p>Schematics for the device are simple, with plenty of pins to spare on the micro, there are no major issues. The buzzer and LED get a pin each, while the vibrator gets 3, to increase the current capability an elude a transistor. Optionally, there is an ESP8266 for data uploading and logging. The capacitive sensor itself only requires to connections to the outside: GND and Touch, no other components.</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/sit_up.png"><img alt="sit_up" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/sit_up-600x437.png"></a>The device, now installed looks much cleaner than before</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/DSC_4921.jpg"><img alt="DSC_4921" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/DSC_4921-600x398.jpg"></a></p><p>Looking at some data from June, I can say I probably spent too many hours on my home chair, even after a day of office work</p><p><a href="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/situp_june_data-2.jpg"><img alt="situp_june_data 2" src="http://www.electrobob.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/situp_june_data-2-600x157.jpg"></a></p></div>
