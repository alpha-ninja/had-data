<h2>The Concept<br></h2><p>To create an inexpensive and accessible way for people with motorised wheelchairs to take control of their mobility via eye movement, using open source methods as much as possible.</p><h2>Our team<br></h2><p>Patrick Joyce - Team captain </p><p>Steve Evans - Test pilot</p><p>David Hopkinson - Documentation and assembly </p><h2>Background <br></h2><p>It is usual for people with MND/ALS in the UK to be provided with electric wheelchairs loaned by the health service, for as long as we need them. Steve and I have wheelchairs, and we both also have eyegaze equipment (which is becoming increasingly more available). At the moment it is not possible to operate one with the other. Eyegaze is technology only for operating a computer, not the chair.<br></p><h2>The Original Idea </h2><p>As I don&apos;t actually own either the wheelchair or the eyegaze, my solution was to make something that would interface with the user&apos;s chair mounted computer, and physically move the joystick. Crucially this would mean not making any modifications to the loaned hardware, and could work with any wheelchair and eyegaze combination.<br><br>I had the initial idea in 2013 and thought about it for several months. The criteria I came up with were as follows. <strong><br></strong></p><p><strong>It needs to be: </strong></p><ul><li><strong>Cheap </strong></li><li><strong>Easy to install and use </strong></li><li><strong>Safe </strong><strong></strong></li><li><strong>Expandable - the system should be able to control other external devices according to the needs of the user </strong></li><li><strong>Totally o</strong><strong>pen, both open hardware and open source </strong><strong>software </strong></li><li><strong>Easy to build at hom</strong><strong>e -</strong><strong> </strong><strong>by an unskilled person</strong></li><li><strong>A cheap manufactured product as well</strong></li></ul><p>I envisaged having two parts - an Electronic Hand unit, and a Brain Box to control it. Making the Electronic Hand should be fairly easy using servos, but I was stuck on what the Brian Box would actually be . . . until I discovered arduino!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7516521438800311010.png"></p><h2>3D Printing<em></em></h2><p><em></em></p><p>Even with this revelation the project was basically dead in the water 
until the advent of accessible 3D printing. So, in the summer of 2014 I 
ordered a 3D printer and the prototyping process began in earnest.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7846871429906882587.jpg"><em></em></p><p><em>The original Eyedrivomatic prototype </em></p><p>I taught myself to program the arduino, and eventually learned how to use processing too. The hardware went through several stages in the later half of 2014 and the early part of 2015. At first I was going to use infrared for pc to arduino communication. This would have had several advantages, but by April this year I had ditched the IR in favour of serial communication through a usb cable. It&apos;s much simpler and has proved more reliable. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7912391435754941606.jpg"><em></em></p><p><em>An early Eyedrivomatic system</em></p><p>I put in a four relay module as standard equipment in the brain box. These relays are for operating external switches of the user&apos;s choosing. Many wheelchairs have tilt/recline functions, and often these can be controlled using buttons plugged into sockets on the wheelchair&apos;s joystick unit. Thus Eyedrivomatic can control all of the chair&apos;s functions. But the chair will only use two of the output sockets on the brain box, leaving two for other purposes. I will use one myself to fire a nerf blaster which will live on the side of the chair. Also, the arduino has plenty of spare pins and processing power. In order to utilise these, there are three empty bays on the brain box, to take servo extension cables. These could be used for a variety of purposes including; </p><ul><li><strong>Enviromental control through IR or RF. Control your tv or close the curtains with your eyes! </strong></li><li><strong>Controlling a robot arm attached to the wheelchair. I am actively developing one for myself, and will post it soon. </strong></li><li><strong>Controlling a fan</strong></li><li><strong>Headlights, taillights and indicators</strong></li><li><strong><a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6584-headphone-robot">Headphone robot.</a> A robot to put my headphones on and off. This may sound silly, but I wanted one, so i&apos;ve built one. </strong></li></ul><p>And so on. I want Eyedrivomatic to change people&apos;s lives, and it&apos;s inherent flexibility will help that process. </p><h2><br>Progress so far</h2><p>At the time of writing this (August 2015), we have reached Mk3 on both the Electronic Hand and the Brain Box. The hardware is at the point where i&apos;m ready to put an instructable up. It can still be improved, but it&apos;s advanced enough for other people to use. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9906901435767092441.png"><em>The Eyedrivomatic system currently fitted to my chair </em></p><p><br><em></em></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3311681435686353202.jpg"><em></em></p><p><em>Mk2 electronic hand </em></p><p>In the development of the brain box design, I have tried to make it easy to build. All the components are available off the shelf, and are connected together using Dupont cables,  re-purposing something  normally only used for prototyping.  This means that no soldering is required in any part of the construction - the intended end users not necessarily being geeks like me. <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2338651435692512113.jpg"><em></em></p><p><em>Component layout in the Mk3 brain box </em></p><p>Of course even this relatively straightforward build process will be beyond many people. Which makes the manufacture of a pre-built production version all the more important. I had hoped to have started looking into the production process by now, but felt I had to finish the self build model first.</p><h2>Software </h2><p>Eyedrivomatic&apos;s software is in two parts, firmware for the arduino in the brain box, and a pc application which I have written in processing. Most eyegaze users will have software on their computer for communication and computer control. Our intention is that the pc software will be accessible through the users own computer control software, or directly, using the eyegaze equipment&apos;s mouse emulation capibilities. At the moment, it only works with one computer control package - Grid2. Grid2 has allowed for rapid development of the pc software, and I intend to perfect it before writing the stand alone pc application.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7874501438800921003.png"></p><p><em></em></p><p><em>The Eyedrivomatic Computer Application. Generation 4c. Main driving screen.</em></p><h2>Open Source </h2><p>Our intention from the beginning was that Eyedrivomatic should be an entirely open project. To this end;<strong> </strong></p><p><strong>All the software for the eyedrivomatic project is copyright 2015 Patrick Joyce, and is free software: you can redistribute it and/or modify it under the terms of the GNU General Public License version 3.For more info see <a href="http://www.gnu.org/licenses/">http://www.gnu.org/licenses/</a></strong></p><p><strong>All the hardware for the eyedrivomatic project, and the 3d design files and stl files necessary to produce the hardware are copyright 2015 Patrick Joyce, and are licensed under a Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International License. For more info see   <a href="http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0/">http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0/</a></strong></p><em></em><h1>Our Goals</h1><p>Before the final in November we hope to have . . . <strong></strong></p><ul><li><strong>Eyedrivomatic Systems being used daily by MND/ALS sufferers worldwide. </strong></li><li><strong>A comprehensive set of open source building instructions available on-line.</strong></li><li><strong>Software available for all platforms </strong><strong><br></strong></li><li><strong>A manufactured version in production. </strong></li></ul><p>Thanks for reading and any help or suggestions would be most welcomed! <br> </p>
