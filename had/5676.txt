                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h2>Hackaday Weekly Prize Winner, Stickvise</h2><p>The Muffsy Hifi Dual Power Supply is one of the <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/06/04/weekly-winners-stickvise/">winners of a Stickvise</a>!</p><h2>What is it for?</h2><p>This regulated power supply is intended for audio applications that don't require a lot of power. Typically pre-amplifiers , fully discrete or based on operational amplifiers.</p><h2>Features</h2><p><em></em>This PSU takes a single 15-18VAC input and gives dual 15VDC output.</p><p>It is dead simple, has fantastic performance, and consists of the following stages:</p><ul><li>Single 15-18VAC input</li><li>Two half-wave rectifiers</li><li>Voltage doublers</li><li>LM317/337 voltage regulators</li><li>Filter capacitors</li><li>Regulated +/- 15VDC output and ground.</li></ul><p>And, as regulated power is limited:</p><ul><li>Unregulated AUX power ~20-23V</li><li>Extra power output with resistor for LED</li></ul><p>By using a wallwart, there is no risk of electrocution or fires.</p><h2>Performance</h2><p>The PSU performs superbly. Used together with the <a href="http://hackaday.io/project/4788-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-2" target="_blank">Muffsy Phono Preamp PP-2</a> or the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6167-muffsy-phono-preamp-pp-3">new PP-3</a>, it has only 0.1 mV ripple on the DC output. Well below the Power Supply Rejection Rate (PSRR) of any operational amplifier, and very suitable for discrete circuits.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8151891431213011935.jpg"></p><p>The PSU gives up to 100 mA of current when used without heatsinks. That's enough to power four phono stages. Mounting heat sinks on the LM317/337 will give you up to 200 mA. </p><p>The AUX power gives an additional (unregulated) 500 mA of current, or more, depending on your transformer.</p><h2>Additional Information</h2><ul><li><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5676/components">Component List</a></li><li><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5676-muffsy-hifi-dual-power-supply/log/18532-schematic">Schematic</a></li><li><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5676/instructions">Build Instructions</a></li><li><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5676-muffsy-hifi-dual-power-supply/log/17546-aux-power-how-and-why">AUX Power</a></li><li><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5676-muffsy-hifi-dual-power-supply/log/18322-change-the-output-voltage">Change the Output Voltage</a></li><li><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5676-muffsy-hifi-dual-power-supply/log/17542-suitable-ac-to-ac-wall-transformers">Suitable AC to AC Transformers</a></li></ul>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5676/log/18322-change-the-output-voltage">Change the Output Voltage</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5676/log/18322#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The output voltage of this power supply can be changed to suit your needs. Let's have a look at how this can be done, and how to make sure that your transformer is able to deliver sufficient voltage.</p><p>Here's the layout of the printed circuit board. There are only two resistors that need to be replaced to change the output voltage, and they are shown here in orange:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9178041432300429269.png"></p><p>With the resistors at 3300 ohms, the power supply will deliver +/-15 volts.</p><h2>Resistor Values</h2><p>You might want less than this. Let's say +/-12.5 volts. Or more, for instance +/-17.5 volts. These output values are well within reach using the recommended 15-18VAC wall adapter.</p><p>This table will give you the resistor values for 12.5, 15 and 17.5 volts dual output:</p><table><tbody><tr><td><strong>Resistor, ohms</strong></td><td><strong>Output voltage, +/- volts</strong></td></tr><tr><td>2.700 (2k7)</td><td>12.5</td></tr><tr><td>3.300 (3k3)</td><td>15.0</td></tr><tr><td>3.900 (3k9)</td><td>17.5</td></tr></tbody></table><p>Other output voltages are easily obtainable, just use a calculator <a href="http://www.electronics-lab.com/articles/LM317/" target="_blank">such as this one</a>. The R1 value to provide in the calculator is 300 ohm.</p><h2>Wall Adapter Voltage</h2><p>The PSU needs a higher input value than the desired output value. Then how can a 15 volt AC-adapter be sufficient for 17.5V output? The answer to this is that the rectified DC voltage will be higher than the AC input voltage by a factor of 1.41. Now, there is some loss in the diodes. To be on the safe side, we'll use the factor 1.3 instead:</p><ul><li><strong>15V * 1.3 = 19.5V</strong></li></ul><p>This is more than 17.5 volts, and you are in the clear!</p><p>So what is the lowest AC voltage needed for a 15V supply?</p><ul><li><strong>15V / 1.3 = 11.54V</strong></li></ul><p>You could actually use a 12V AC-adapter. But, there is the possibility that an adapter rated at 12V will give less than that. Stick with 15V or more, and you will be sure that your PSU works as expected.</p>
                                
                            </p>
