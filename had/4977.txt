        <meta name="twitter:description" content="The portable environmental monitor addresses pollution, the kind that we are unable to see but directly affects our health and can cause life threatening diseases. Airborne toxic chemicals, radioactive dust and radioactive radon are correlated with cases of pulmonary cancer and asthma.
Since our biological senses can do little to warn us of such possible dangers, we plan to design the Portable environmental monitor as a first line detection and warning system.
This is not the regular detector: packed with powerful sensors capable of detecting both the chemical and the physical harmful factors, these devices are designed with Internet connectivity thanks to a 802.11B/G wifi module, and will share all readings to the Global uRADMonitor network. 
Online data allows us to build graph, stats and send automated notifications when certain thresholds are reached. The infrastructure has been developed for the uRADMonitor project, semifinalist of HaD 2014 http://goo.gl/aoWZmC">

                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The portable environmental monitor addresses pollution, the kind that we are unable to see but directly affects our health and can cause life threatening diseases. Airborne toxic chemicals, radioactive dust and radioactive radon are correlated with cases of pulmonary cancer and asthma.<br />Since our biological senses can do little to warn us of such possible dangers, we plan to design the Portable environmental monitor as a first line detection and warning system.<br />This is not the regular detector: packed with powerful sensors capable of detecting both the chemical and the physical harmful factors, these devices are designed with Internet connectivity thanks to a 802.11B/G wifi module, and will share all readings to the Global uRADMonitor network. <br />Online data allows us to build graph, stats and send automated notifications when certain thresholds are reached. The infrastructure has been developed for the uRADMonitor project, semifinalist of HaD 2014 <a href='http://goo.gl/aoWZmC' target="_blank">http://goo.gl/aoWZmC</a></div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><strong>Note</strong>: for a shorter version see <a href="https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/19o9m_u6T5lY1Vuw1UhByDP_UEonske0gD8dlyhWBAT0/edit?usp=sharing">Docs: Project Summary</a> </p><p><strong>Introduction</strong></p><p>Last year I put my spare time to some really good use: I designed my first hardware product from scratch, and after prototyping and fixing software and hardware bugs it <a href="http://www.uradmonitor.com/model-a-production-ready/" target="_blank">moved to production.</a> This is how uRADMonitor was born, the first automated global radiation monitoring system, materialising in a short term a very ambitious goal!<br></p><p>The efforts have been recognised, and <a href="http://www.uradmonitor.com/featured-on-hackaday-as-semifinalist/" target="_blank">uRADMonitor went into semifinals in the HackADay.io 2014 competition</a>. I didn&apos;t conquer the space, but  did a good job at ground level, spreading <a href="http://www.uradmonitor.com/" target="_blank">the network</a> across several continents.</p><p>Now, the stakes are high: <a href="https://hackaday.io/prize" target="_blank">build something that matters</a>! I like challenges, but even more I like putting my knowledge into the service of my fellows, for making life at least a fraction better. Using the previous uRADMonitor experience, the plan is to use the current global network infrastructure and create a new mobile unit device, handheld - but packed with powerful sensors to measure pollution parameters, both chemical (toxic substances) and physical (radioactive substances). All data will be fused to the current uRADMonitor radiation data. Users will have the option to see trends in particular areas (far better then isolated / absolute measurements), or receive alerts when certain limits are reached.</p><p>Alternative uses include car exhaust checking, soil prospecting, or basement radon monitoring. The units act both as low power handheld dosimeters, but can be also deployed as monitoring nodes. </p><p>The rich collection of sensors cover alpha, beta and gamma detectors, and air quality sensors including basic parameters but also flammable and toxic gases. The new devices (called generically uRADMonitor model D) have a generous large colour touchscreen, a rechargeable battery and WLAN 802.11B/G/N connectivity, huge improvements over the radiation-only model A unit, featured in the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/1662-global-radiation-monitoring-network" target="_blank">previous project entry.</a></p><p>A totally new device, pushing the environmental surveillance to the next level, by using a veryfied global infrastructure of fixed detectors. </p><p><strong>About innovation, obstacles and design details</strong></p><p>Soon after building the Radiation Network I  realised there are other problems, with higher occurrence than nuclear incidents. Friends suggested extending the capabilities of this infrastructure to cover pollution, something we&apos;re all facing daily, in our crowded cities. These were the first directions. I also wanted to go for another durable construction, so I had to drop any form of plastic enclosures in favour of rugged aluminium. After all, that&apos;s how the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/1662-global-radiation-monitoring-network" target="_blank">last year&apos;s uRADMonitor units</a> were built as well. It also had to be small enough to fit the palm of the hand for easy use. These initial requirements shaped the general guidelines of the upcoming product, but identifying the right sensors was still problematic. Particularly air quality was a vague term, implying many physical and chemical factors. The available sensors are either prohibitive because of cost or are energy hungry. Yet, a solution became available with the MiCS-VZ-XX sensors developed by a company in Switzerland, capable of detecting CO2 and VOC conveniently. Next step was finding a way of measuring dust and ensuring a dynamic flow through the air channel. That was done by using power dissipated by a regulator to heat the base region of the dust sensor and put air in motion, via a copper sheet. No energy wasted in  vain, and everything was packed as compact as possible, including the LND712 sensitive geiger counter and its high voltage inverter. We&apos;re set to detect all three popular types of ionising radiation and this adds alpha and beta to the previous gamma only detectors, to open the  way for sensing other damaging factors like the Radon gas, an alpha emitter. The PCB was designed to support both BMP180 or the BME280 sensors from Bosch.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2194851439494658103.jpg"></p><p>With all these powerful sensors, I had to offer the best user interaction, and this came thanks to a generous 2.4&quot;...</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4977/log/22712-beta-units-shipped">Beta units shipped</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4977/log/22712#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>After <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/the-first-prototype/" target="_blank">a sinuous but exciting road</a>, the Beta prototype came out nice, the entire device is amazingly small: I was able to design it to fit the 110x70mm aluminium enclosure I planned initially without renouncing to any of the sensors nor reducing the 1500mAh lithium battery. See it for yourself, here&apos;s the video presentation for the <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/4977-portable-environmental-monitor" target="_blank">#bestproduct hackaday prize competition</a>:</p><iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/D-7rgwHWxkI" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: inherit; font-variant: inherit; line-height: inherit; font-style: inherit; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%;"></iframe>Producing the Beta hardware<p>The day before I got an express package from China with the pro PCBs, but my 3 units were already fully assembled. <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/the-first-prototype/" target="_blank">Manually, that is etching the PCBs</a>, drilling tens of holes, soldering tiny 0805 components with the soldering iron, using power tools to drill and mill the strong aluminium of the 3 enclosures. I had no time, nor additional MiCS-VZ-89 sensors for more units. But those PCBs look good:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5709031439499661955.jpg"></p><p>There&apos;s <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/mics-vz-89-air-quality-sensor/" target="_blank">the MiCZ-VZ-89 advanced air quality sensor</a> measuring CO2 and VOCs in air, the neat <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/tube-lnd-712-end-window-alpha-beta-gamma-detector/" target="_blank">LND712 Geiger tube for alpha, beta and gamma radiation</a> including its high voltage regulated inverter, the <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/sharp-gp2y10-dust-sensor/" target="_blank">GP2Y10 dust sensor</a> and the <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/bmp085-barometric-pressure-sensor/" target="_blank">BMP180 for temperature and pressure</a> while the PCB allows soldering an additional BME280.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7978501439499710300.jpg"></p>The Aluminium enclosures<p>First thing to do was designing the final enclosure. The design files can already be used in a factory to produce machined enclosures, including nice silkprint to label the various connectors.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4762371439499757826.jpg"></p><p>Cutting aluminium is easy if it&apos;s thin. I found out my cases won&apos;t take the fast spinning diamond disk, so my solution for milling was drilling as many holes as possible to remove the unneeded material, then use a Dremel with a nice milling head. A CNC router would have done wonders here, yet I was able to do it by hand just fine. For drilling holes, I saw that drills for wood give more precise results in aluminium due to their sharp tip better penetrating the material. For the LCD holders I initially wanted some 3D printed parts, but there was no time for that, so I opted for making them out of plastic sheets and painting them black afterwards<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4964601439499854073.jpg"></p><iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/jHa4IHaMS8g" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: inherit; font-variant: inherit; line-height: inherit; font-style: inherit; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%;"></iframe><p><a name="photos"></a></p>The hardware ready<p>For a single man, this was an enormous effort, but after less then one month it gave an amazing product. Finally the hardware is ready in all its beauty:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5998291439499885384.jpg"></p><p>As we speak the three prototypes are on their wait to the Hackaday Headquarts to participate in the <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/03/25/best-product/" target="_blank">Hackaday Prize 2015 #Bestproduct competition</a>.</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4977/log/22186-a-true-hacker-makes-his-own-pcbs">A true hacker makes his own PCBs</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">12 days ago  &bull;

                                0 comments

                            </small>
                            <p><p>A true hacker makes his own PCBs. Dual layer. And in the kitchen. Why so? Because doing them as DIY and not paying lots of money to some greedy company is some kind of hack.</p><p>And if it wasn&apos;t for <a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/resistive-touchscreen/" target="_blank">all the issues I had with the Portable Environmental monitor</a> along the road, I could have used the nice <a href="https://oshpark.com/" target="_blank">OSHPark voucher</a> I won on <a href="https://hackaday.io/" target="_blank">Hackaday (Thanks again guys!)</a>. But I&apos;ve just finished the PCB design last night:<br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_D_pcb_1.0.101.png"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_D_pcb_1.0.101.png" alt="uradmonitor_D_pcb_1.0.101"></a><br>I know, it looks sweet! And the PCB must do as well. But no time to order them in US, ship them in Europe, solder all parts, test the software and ship the 3 prototypes to Hackaday headquarters before August 17.<br>Instead I checked locally with a few companies, but their price offer was and average of 300USD for 10 PCBs (with a 100% emergency tax added). That seems ridiculous so no thanks. I did however order a few in China, where 3 companies I know charged between $0.5 and $3 per PCB plus shipping, and they offer top quality. Now that&apos;s a huge difference. But all in one, time&apos;s too short to wait for shipping. And as I do have a bottle of Ferric Chloride in the kitchen, here&apos;s how the first <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/4977-portable-environmental-monitor" target="_blank">uRADMonitor-D PCB</a> was made:<br></p><iframe width="800" height="450" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/DcVUpxi0MTE" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="border-width: 0px; font-family: Lato, sans-serif; line-height: 24px; margin: 0px; outline: 0px; padding: 0px; vertical-align: baseline; max-width: 100%; color: rgb(102, 102, 102);"></iframe><br>This first one was a single layer, doing the top layer with tiny wires (possible as all wires are either horizontal or vertical). Those under the microcontroller were soldered with just a very tiny cap, that was reduced even further with a fine file. Seeing how good it goes, I decided the next two will be dual layer. And so they were, with the layers perfectly matched for even those tiny holes.<br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_toner_transfer.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_toner_transfer.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_toner_transfer"></a><br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_pcb.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_first_pcb.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_first_pcb"></a><br><a href="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_prototypes.jpg"><img src="http://www.pocketmagic.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/uradmonitor_d_prototypes.jpg" alt="uradmonitor_d_prototypes"></a><br>Almost there!
