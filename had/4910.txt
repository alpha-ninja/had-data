                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">In the remote parts of the world where they have no power and limited medical equipment I wanted to make a cheap low power heart rate and body temp monitor.<br /><br />After trying to look for a project to enter into the 2015HackadayPrize I stumpbled upon this hackaday <a href='http://hackaday.com/2015/03/19/measuring-heart-rate-with-a-piezo/' target="_blank">http://hackaday.com/2015/03/19/measuring-heart-rate-with-a-piezo/</a> post about measuring heart rate with a piezoelectric vibration sensor on the end of your finger<br /><br />For the body temp I'm going to be using a LM35 Temperature sensor under the armpit which should give me my core temp.<br /><br />Tying all the hardware together with a atmega328. Hoping to make it solar or hand cranked powered for remote use.</div>

                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1><a href="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/main_ths2.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-666 aligncenter" src="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/main_ths2.jpg" alt="Jpeg" width="470" height="473"></a></h1><h1>Video</h1><p>Quick demo video of the hardware in action</p><p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/P-qDKN8uHOM" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe></p><h1>The beginning</h1><p>Another year another hackaday prize, (I hope this is going to be an annual affair). After entering last years contest with a project I just happen to be working on at the time I thought I would put a little bit more effort into this years entrance.</p><p>I drew imperative from <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/03/19/measuring-heart-rate-with-a-piezo/" target="_blank">This</a> post on hackaday where they used a vibration sensor made for robots that uses a piezo speaker/sensor to read vibrations. They repurposed it to read your pulse on your finger. After seeing this I thought I could use it as a medical based sensor/monitoring kit. I racked my brain looking at all the different arduino sensor so see what else I could add to this. The only sensor that made sense to team up with the now heart rate sensor is a temperature sensor</p><h1>Testing the piezo theory</h1><p>Now that I had decided on what my project was going to be I could start fleshing the hardware . I started with the piezo vibration sensor to see if I could get it working for myself.</p><p><a href="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/img_20150326_211437.jpg"><img src="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/img_20150326_211437.jpg" alt="IMG_20150326_211437" width="259" height="209" class="hoverZoomLink" style="width: 259px; height: 209px;"></a></p><p>I copied the code from <a href="http://www.ohnitsch.net/2015/03/18/measuring-heart-rate-with-a-piezoelectric-vibration-sensor/" target="_blank">This</a> blog post to as a starting point, To my disappointment it wouldn&apos;t pick up my pulse with the piezo strapped to my finger. This was a real blow to my project because if I couldn&apos;t get this to work I would have to go back to the drawing board as they say. Not to be too discouraged I tried the piezo sensor on my wrist because this is where you normally take your pulse, IT WORKED I couldn&apos;t believe it. I did have to lower the threshold to make it easier to pick up my pulse.</p><h1>Trials and tribulations with temp sensors</h1><p>The thing I was looking for in a temp sensor was something that was enclosed and water proof so when I seen the DS18B20 I looked just what I wanted.</p><p><a href="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/ds18b.jpg"><img src="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/ds18b.jpg" alt="ds18b"></a></p><p>One trying the example sketch and <a href="http://playground.arduino.cc/Learning/OneWire-DE" target="_blank">library</a> everything looked good until I added the code for the piezo sensor, that&apos;s when I found out that the library was too slow so It wouldn&apos;t let me read the piezo sensor fast enough.</p><p>Back to the drawing board to try and find a replacement sensor. My attention turned to the LM35 as it being a similar size to the DS18B20 without its metal enclosure.</p><p><a href="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/06/lm35.jpg"><img src="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/06/lm35.jpg" alt="LM35" width="298" height="299" class="hoverZoomLink" style="width: 298px; height: 299px;"></a></p><p>My idea was to remove the DS18B20 from its enclosure and put the LM35 in its place. One good things about the DS18B20 is that they use plenty of sealant to ensure its waterproff but that means its impossible to remove. The only way i found to remove it was to drill out of its enclosure, only then I realised that the LM35 was a 1mm too big so I sanded it down a touch but this destroyed the ability of the LM35 to read temperatures. In the end I just settled with using a bit of heat shrink tubing about the base of the LM35.</p><p><a href="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/06/img_20150415_1707461.jpg"><img src="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/06/img_20150415_1707461.jpg" alt="IMG_20150415_170746" width="362" height="331" style="width: 362px; height: 331px;"></a></p><h1>Picking the enclosure</h1><p>Like with all my previous projects I like to try out different things. Because this project didn&apos;t have to be small or portable, It let me be less picky about the enclosure. I wanted it to have a test equipment vibe and had been eyeing up these which would be big but not too big</p><p><a href="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/enclosure.jpg"><img src="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/enclosure.jpg" alt="enclosure" width="321" height="241" style="width: 321px; height: 241px;"></a></p><p>Also what I liked was that it all came apart, The front and back panel would come off and also the top and bottom. This made it much easier to work on the panels and if I messed it up would be easily replaced too.</p><h1>The power solution</h1><p>When most people think about powering a project they initially think of a lipo battery teamed with a cheap lithium battery charger which is a good solution. I like to use 18650 lithium battery which are significantly cheaper for high-capacity but a bit bulky, Also you can rip the guts out of a 18650 phone chargers because the PCB can be used for charging the battery and powering your project all on one board.</p><p><a href="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/18650.jpg"><img src="https://facelesstech.files.wordpress.com/2015/07/18650.jpg" alt="18650"></a></p><p>I went with two 4200mAh battery to give the project a good battery life because it will be used off the grid with charging few and far between.</p><p>As the project might be run off a solar cell or plugged into the mains for long perides of time I wanted a way to power it but not charge the battery all the time. I added a swtich so you could just power #T_H_S or power and charge the battery, That way you wouldn&apos;t have to worry...</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4910/log/20884-powered-by-light">Powered by light</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4910/log/20884#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>When I started this project I always wanted it to be able to be powered by solar. The only experience I&apos;ve had with solar cells is messing about with the garden solar lights but they dont produce much voltage/current.</p><p>I started looking around on ebay to see if I could find a 5v panel with decent output. I picked a 5v 500mAh 2.5 watt panel hoping that it would be able to power <a data-mention-project-id="4910" href="/project/4910">#T_H_S</a> . Being in the UK we dont normaly get many sunny days so on recieving the panel I had to wait a few days before testing the panel out.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/683201436824876795.jpg" width="452" height="255" style="width: 452px; height: 255px;"></p><p>I could get 5.90v with strong direct sunlight. Now I needed a way to boost/regulate the current so it would power <a data-mention-project-id="4910" href="/project/4910">#T_H_S</a> . </p><p>I ordered a 5v DC - DC boost</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9449211436824597346.jpg" width="391" height="286" style="width: 391px; height: 286px;"></p><p>This allowed me to power <a data-mention-project-id="4910" href="/project/4910">#T_H_S</a> as long as it was in strong direct sunlight. I would charge and power the battery&apos;s at the same time but now too sure how long it would take to fully charge them up.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9875921436824842328.jpg" width="424" height="237" style="width: 424px; height: 237px;"></p><p>I mounted the solar cell on a piece of PVC ment for a window ledge so it was L shaped. This alowed it to have a slight angle to chatch the sun a little better</p><p>Next thing I&apos;m waiting on is a hand crank phone charger.</p>
                                
                            </p>

                            <p><p>So finally my PCB turned up from <a href="http://oshpark.com">oshpark</a> and it quickly dawned on my that some of the drill holes looked on the small side. On closer inpection it was confirmed that they were too small. This wasnt really a problem because the drill holes were big enought to solder some wire though so i would just have to break them out that way. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6499651432916654414.jpg"></p><p>It only really affected the FTDI connector and the speaker too.</p><p>Now that I had all my parts I could start thinking about how they were going to go into the enclosure. Started with the hardest part by cutting the hole for the LCD screen.
