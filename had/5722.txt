                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">You should be able to throw your prototypes in a backpack. You should be able to update their programming while at the coffee shop. You should be able to put your prototypes into production immediately. That's a goal of Retro Specification & Retro Modules. The spec introduces a middle-ground between breadboard and production board.<br /><br />This is not about crafting some new full-stack modular hardware interface. This is about quickly putting parts & small circuit boards into hardy little enclosures (i.e. NULL modem enclosures). No need to buy/make a 3D printer. No campaign or production run required. Just start wiring up your own modules.<br /><br />What will WE make?<br />The Maker Movement is a great inspiration. People of all ages and backgrounds are building amazing things independently and in collaborative environments. What about that project three makerspaces want to take on? How should things interconnect?<br /><br />You probably have some of these connectors collecting dust in a box somewhere...</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>You don&apos;t need much to build a Retro Module. For a host module (e.g. one that will house the main microcontroller board), you can start with a female DA-15 Connector. Your first client module would use a male DA-15 Connector. Modules don&apos;t need to be enclosed.</p><p>D-Sub connectors like the DA-15 Connector have three major form factors: solder, removable pins &amp; ribbon crimp. Ribbon crimp connectors, though the most expensive of the three, will save you tons of time.</p><p>Solder-type D-Sub connectors are the easiest to find &amp; are the hardiest. They do get to be a bit of a pain as you add more wires to the connector.<br></p><p>Consider starting with removable pin connectors. The pins do not require solder to assemble &amp; are easy to move around if there is a mistake. You&apos;ll need a D-Sub pin extractor tool if you want to move pins after they&apos;ve been installed.</p><p><strong>Progress:</strong><br></p><p>This project has been progressing very well, though I am eager to finish the spec &amp; put that part of the project behind me. I have been hampered by minor/major revisions to the spec as much-appreciated feedback has poured in. Revisions cause existing hardware to fall out of compliance -- and the update process is a big pain.</p><p>While chainability is certainly a great benefit, I have found greater immediate need of single modules built to keep devices charged and/or route audio to amplifiers/etc. A few of my very-simple afternoon builds see daily use.</p><p>It is very tempting to try and fit all the signals I love into every connector I find. This is not possible, and not the best of temptations. Instead, I have sought to identify connectors professionals from various industries prefer &amp; propose things -- where appropriate -- on connetors they would be well familiar with.</p><p>My immediate goals are to both wrap up the main part of the specification process, and to finish the retromodules.com website. The site will feature tools which will, via the yaml files in the repository, make the module design/build process faster &amp; easier.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5722/log/23259-revision-updates-new-parts-of-the-spec">Revision Updates &amp; New Parts of the Spec</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5722/log/23259#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>After the Maker Fair 2015 revisions were adopted, I went back to work updating a bunch of Retro Modules which were no longer in compliance. This is one of the reasons I&apos;m striving toward a stable pin specification in short order. It is not fun looking at a box full of no-longer-compliant modules. I&apos;ve had to sift through existing modules to find the few which were not affected by the spec revisions. Thankfully, I am gaining momentum again.<br>When I built out the hardware for my &apos;Christmas Truck&apos; project, I used the Retro Modules spec extensively. Those modules will need slight updates now, but even the basic modularity of the install proved useful. I recently traded my truck in for another, and the uninstall process was a breeze. That is -- everything except some of the oldest modifications I made to the truck. The 2012 Tacoma features a LCD screen in the rear-view mirror. It, at times, displays composite video from the stock rear-view camera. I wanted a way to send my own video to the mirror without making a big mess of the mirror housing. I was able to locate the video cable &amp; make a splice quite some time ago. To undo this splice, I had to break out the soldering iron &amp; solder the short segment of wire back together. If I decide to do a similar modification today, I would make it easy to remove any mods. How? I would add connectors (blade, etc) which allow the stock cable to be reconnected to itself with ease. This can increase the cost of the mod, but not by much.</p><p><strong>Headers</strong></p><p><strong></strong>I have spent some more time fleshing out pin header specs &amp; documentation. The very common 26 pin header is hardware compatible with a 25 pin DB-25 connector. The 10 pin header is compatible with the DE-9 connector. This allows the use of old-fashioned header-to-connector ribbon cables. You may have had some of these cables in an older desktop tower.</p><p><em>&quot;AC&apos;97&quot;/&quot;HD Audio&quot;</em><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6775761439794154086.jpg">Another common staple of desktop computing is the &quot;AC&apos;97&quot;/&quot;HD Audio&quot; header &amp; breakout modules. Many modern single-board computers feature analog &amp; digital audio, but are often broken out via consumer audio jacks. These small computers could leverage the AC&apos;97 header with great ease -- reducing circuit-board complexity &amp; increasing design flexibility. A source of confusion for many, however, is S/PDIF headers on motherboards. Legacy graphics cards often shipped with a tiny cable for routing digital audio. S/PDIF headers are not well standardized &amp; may consist of two to eight pins. Given this problem, I <a target="_blank" href="https://github.com/makerblueprint/retrospecification/blob/incoming/connector-collection/header/retro-audio-header.yaml">opted</a> to append five pins to the end of the &quot;AC&apos;97&quot;/&quot;HD Audio&quot; header. This keeps compatibility with off-the-shelf desktop audio modules yet allows S/PDIF in/out on a custom header if so desired.</p><p><br><strong>Pro audio is also an important area to focus on.</strong></p><p>Since part of this spec features analog audio next to other digital signals, there is a greater risk of noise. Many environments do not require pristine audio conditions. Some do. This is why I have included support -- in certain connectors -- for AES Digital Audio. S/PDIF is the consumer version of AES. AES42 has some really great features that quite a few sound engineers enjoy. These signals are generally sent on a standard 3-pin XLR connector. There is risk, though, in sending digital signals on a connector primarily used for analog audio. Audio mixers may get damaged if a AES signal is connected to an analog input. I have proposed <a target="_blank" href="https://github.com/makerblueprint/retrospecification/blob/incoming/connector-collection/pro-audio/xlr6.yaml">a spec for 6-pin IEC XLR connectors</a> to hopefully address this issue -- and add a feature or two. The 3-pin connector only allows for one-way transmission of digital audio signals (generally). A 6-pin connector allows for power &amp; bi-directional AES transmission. This opens up doors for chainable digital effects processors, digital audio pedals, mics &amp; remote monitoring. For instance, an artist performing on stage generally needs a mic, guitar input &amp; monitor mix. This could be accomplished...</p>

                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5722/log/23236-the-last-major-revision">The Last Major Revision</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5722/log/23236#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Maker Faire Bay Area 2015 and the events leading up to it really helped refine the specification in a few very important ways. First, I found many of the features packed into the DB-25 spec should be moved to DA-15. This was in an effort to make the spec more accessible as one gets started. The DA-15 spec is not the best candidate for a plethora of chained modules. If chainability is your main focus, shift over to DB-25 and/or DE-9.<br>The old version of the spec featured USB and lacked CAN. USB is difficult to support when chaining is considered. USB is also a riskier thing to mess around with, since it probably has an expensive computer connected to it. CAN, on the other hand, chains easily and can be run over long distances. It is not as fast, but is sufficient for many electronics experiments. It is used extensively in automotive, automation &amp; robotics environments.<br>Finally, after taking a look at some other multi-pin connectors, I found it wouldn&apos;t be to difficult to support 1080i HD video. While not the cleanest means of transmitting video, it is an ideal transmission method for those who like to tinker. The other connectors I mentiond combined composite, &apos;super&apos; video &amp; component video onto three pins. This pattern has been followed with the <a target="_blank" href="https://github.com/makerblueprint/retrospecification/blob/incoming/connector-collection/d-subminiature/db-25.yaml">DB-25 connector pin spec</a>.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/328471439789445302.jpg">When I decided to make an appearance at Maker Faire, I knew I would need a set of modules to help attract interest. I chose to embed a number of modules into an old flatbed scanner -- along with an Arduino Uno to run everything. The control surface featured all sorts of buttons &amp; fiber-optic-backlit DE-9 &amp; DB-25 connectors. The buttons &amp; LEDs were driven by I2C port expanders &amp; the NeoPixels via the `pixel-bus`. When one (or a few) of the buttons were pressed, the pin holes corresponding to the button label(s) would illuminate. Since I already had a 16x8 I2C LED display, I programmed the Arduino to display button label text there as well. The LED display was behind the scanner glass, while the control surface was mounted on a board well below. The scanner &apos;display case&apos; and other parts of the display were linked by a single 25 connector ribbon cable. There were other means of input &amp; output as well. Important to the design: I needed certain modules to protected, while others could be messed with. Those goals were well achieved.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1128371439789675903.jpg"></p>

                    <p>Hey Bryan! Thanks for your input! I learned about CAN via D-sub 9 just a month ago. The current version of the spec (unlike the proposed version) does not include CAN at all. I2C is currently on pins 2 &amp; 3 of DE-9 &amp; DB-25, which are commonly for RS-232 or RS-422. The proposed version, as you may know, puts CAN on pins 2 &amp; 3 instead. This is a nod to those who have used these connectors for decades. The challenge with CiA 102: pin 9 on a DE-9 connector is generally used for ground. As such, old DE-9 to DB-25 adapters &amp; old printer cables could not be used. I'm not against adopting the CiA 102 standard, but there would be tradeoffs. Please consider submitting a pull request. Others may also desire adherance to CiA 102. I aim to have this spec be as open (and soon: as stable) as possible.<br>You do raise a very good point: there should be a way to use high-quality off-the-shelf tools. I will add `cia-102` into the client device collection. It would be very easy to adapt a CiA 102 module to be compatible with Retro Modules. RS-232 to RS-422 adapters use the same approach -- two DE-9 connectors in a barrel. Pins 2, 7, 3 &amp; 6 on the CiA 102 side would be pins 2, 3, 4 and 9 on the Retro Module side.</p><p>More to ponder...<br></p>
