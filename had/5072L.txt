                            <h2><a href="/project/5072/log/16914-parts">Parts</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5072/log/16914#discussion-list">

                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-16914"><p>A couple of weeks ago I received my order from SparkFun with the HTU21, BMP180, and a small OLED display. I've gotten everything cobbled together, and a picture will be coming shortly.</p><p>I've also been experimenting with running everything at 1.8 volts. Everything seems to be working fine, but I still have the OLED and '328 running at 3.3 volts. This has also spun off a couple of other projects for supplying 1.8 volts from various sources. More on that in the near future.<br></p><p>Last week, I discovered that Bosch now has a single sensor that measures all three environmental parameters that I need. Nobody sells a breakout board for the BME280 yet, so I threw one together in Eagle and sent it off to OSHPark, and also ordered a few of the sensors from Mouser. I'll release the Eagle files once I've gotten the hardware in my hand and validate that it is correct.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5072/log/19732-development-progress">Development Progress</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5072/log/19732#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19732"><p>As I was casting about looking for the parts to make up this device, I discovered that Bosch had come out with the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.bosch-sensortec.com/de/homepage/products_3/environmental_sensors_1/bme280/bme280_1">BME280</a>, a new environmental sensor that combined pressure, humidity, and temperature and could communicate by either I2C or SPI.  It was exactly the three measurements I needed, and in a package that is 3mm square! However, at the time I couldn't find anyone who had developed a breakout board. So, I made my own. I laid out the sensor footprint in <a target="_blank" href="http://www.cadsoftusa.com/">Eagle</a>, put it and two 0402 bypass capacitors onto a two layer PCB that is 0.4" square (only because I wanted room for all 8 pins), and sent it off to <a target="_blank" href="https://oshpark.com/">OSHPark</a>. A week and $0.80 later (yes, that is eighty American cents, with shipping included!), I had three bare breakout boards. I have checked the layout, and everything seems to work, so I'm sharing the design through OSHPark. You can order your own <a target="_blank" href="https://oshpark.com/shared_projects/4wlXZklr">here</a>.</p><p><a target="_blank" href="https://oshpark.com/shared_projects/4wlXZklr"><img style="display: block; margin: auto;" alt="Assembled BME280 Breakout Board" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8598551434687605796.png"></a></p><p>Since then, I found that Embedded Adventures sells the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.embeddedadventures.com/bme280_weather_multi_sensor_mod-1022.html">MOD-1022</a>, a breakout board with the sensor mounted and configured for an I2C interface at their website. Right now, I am using their <a target="_blank" href="https://github.com/embeddedadventures/BME280">open source driver</a> with the <a target="_blank" href="https://www.pjrc.com/teensy/teensyLC.html">Teensy LC</a> I won a few weeks ago, along with an Adafruit color OLED I had lying around, to test out both the sensor and some performance enhancements to the SSD1331 driver for the Teensy.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1624951434687634489.JPG"></p></div>
