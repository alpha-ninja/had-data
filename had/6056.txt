                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">A graphene powered car would benefit the world in many ways. It would have no emissions so it would not cause pollution, it would only take a few minutes to charge. It would be possible to create inductive charging. If that's done, it would be possible to place inductive charging panels in driveways and parking spots that activate when a car has parked on top of them, and charge the car wirelessly. Since they are supercapacitors and not batteries, they would last MUCH longer and you would be able to drive for (we're aiming) at least two thousand miles on a single charge. Using a supercapacitor would be better for vehicles in an emergency situation as well. Let's say your car does, for some reason, lose it's charge. You can install an emergency crank on the engine and charge it using kinetic energy. No more pushing cars! Our plan is to make a multi-celled energy pack containing multiple graphene supercapacitors to replace the batteries currently used in electric cars.</div>
                </div>

                    <p>Hi Tim,</p><p>We have already done this.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; We got our samples tested by&nbsp; the graphene institute at a Czech university.&nbsp; We achieved sheet resistance of 247 Ω/sq on our best sample.</p><p>&nbsp;* experimental conditions:<br><br>The two wire Kelvin resistance measurements were carried out by Keithley 4200 SCS<br>instrument. The measurement step was set to 5 mV, measurement duration was 44s<br>and the voltage was in range from -1V to 1V. The values are the average ±<br>std.deviation from 3 independent measurements on the given sample.</p><p>As far as we know, this is actually much better than any university has achieved to date.&nbsp; But, there are serious issues with scalability of this method.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>So, we did something completely different!!&nbsp; The new method has no 'fiddling' involved &amp; we have achieved sheet resistance of 13 Ω/sq..&nbsp;</p><p>We have made some supercaps.&nbsp; They work fairly well.&nbsp; Charge starts at about 3V &amp; drops off a cliff rapidly, stabilizing at about 1.2V. &nbsp;The biggest Graphene cap we have made so far is 4cm x 8cm.&nbsp; We have stacked 8 of these to give us a lot more capacitance.&nbsp; This takes about 1 second to charge &amp; it will charge a cell phone for about 2 minutes..</p><p>I do not think supercaps will replace batteries in cars.&nbsp; But, they will work really well with batteries.&nbsp; Our plan is to build our supercaps into our LUKA EV <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5066-luka-ev">https://hackaday.io/project/5066-luka-ev&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</a></p><p>Our supercaps cost almost nothing to make.&nbsp; The plan is that the caps will do all the heavy work in the car (like acceleration).&nbsp; They will also be used to capture the energy from our regen braking system. The caps are completely flexible so we think we can eventually build them into the body of the car.&nbsp; If caps are doing all the hard work, the battery will last a lot longer (In all senses, it will give a car a better range &amp; it will extend the overall life of the battery)</p><p>At the end of the summer we will ramp production so we can make about 200 sheets of 8cm x 4cm each day. &nbsp;</p><p>My gut is telling me that many big companies must be working on this so I suspect neither you nor I will be winning the Noble prize. &nbsp;I hope big companies are working on it because it really should be a game changer. &nbsp;</p><p>We are into open sourcing 'everything' but I am not going to tell you our methods yet. &nbsp;But, you are sort of on the right track !. &nbsp;If you keep at it, you might make some wonderful...<br><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5066-luka-ev"></a></p>
