                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>I&apos;m using an Arduino Nano 3.0 and some shift registers to make Snapple bottles, phone cords, and LEDs into a customizable mood light. I think it would be an excellent weekend project, and can probably be completed with parts you have laying around. Also it is highly scalable, you can have more or less LEDs by just adjusting the number of shift registers to match. I&apos;m going to try to explain as much as I can about the inner workings of the project, making it ideal for beginners to learn about the digital world. I&apos;m going to be using it to teach my friends that are eager to learn about binary and code in general, as well as a fancy light for the h@x workbench</p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/CAvUV87Xuks" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/1637/log/15702-hanging-and-mockup">Hanging and Mockup</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/1637/log/15702#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>I finally decided to pick this project back up after a long hiatus. It was originally entered in the 2014 Hackaday Prize, but after I read the theme of this year's prize, I thought it would fit well.</p><p>I left off at finding a mounting system and I have decided to use standard 550 paracord. It keeps with the trash theme, you should be able to find scraps in the lengths needed. Holes are drilled every 120 degrees around the circle and then tied together:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1053621427422483811.jpg"></p><p>Protip for working on paracord: When burning the frayed ends, don't hold the flaming rope over your hand. It will drip molten nylon onto your fingers. Anyway, here's the mockup, although it's missing one of the bottle assemblies, so it's not balanced:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7733871427422841281.jpg"></p><p>Hardware is almost completed, I just have to hit the bottles with the glass frosting paint. After that, it's just code and then I'm in business!</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/1637/log/7218-mountingdrilling">Mounting/drilling</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a year ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/1637/log/7218#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Just a quick update, been super busy registering for my last semester at school. I have drilled the holes in the lid I am using as the base of the project.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1768671408256151740.jpg"></p><p>It was easy to space them out, since there were eight equally spaced notches in the side of the lid to line everything up with.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9565471408256242225.jpg"></p><p>I think this is the best place for the holes structurally, with the support of those ridges on the inside. Also I removed that rubber ring, it is not needed for my application.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1707301408256383606.jpg"></p><p>I chopped one phone cord up and started pulling it through the holes while knotting one end to prevent slip through. Here is all I completed before decided to pass out tonight:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6898241408256535562.jpg"></p><p>I'm going to try to get the skeleton constructed before the deadline to the contest. I know it isn't necessary, but I think it will look better.&nbsp;</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/1637/log/6570-moving">Moving</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a year ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/1637/log/6570#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>I have been moving across town the past couple weeks for cheaper rent and I haven't had much time to work on my projects. Also, half the stuff is in boxes right now and my workbench(I use the term loosely) is empty and waiting to be rebuilt. However, I have decided what I'm gonna use as the base:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8551911407604392758.jpg"></p><p>A lid to a five gallon pickle bucket sourced for free from a local business. They were just going to throw it out anyway, so this goes well with my trash theme.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4125671407604482270.jpg"></p><p>It also has eight evenly spaced notches cut in the lid, which is the same number of lights I will be using. Although I could just measure the angles, this will save time and effort as I can just line up my holes and go. The last part of my project came in the mail a couple days ago:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2255871407604893162.jpg"></p><p>All the resistors I need for the LEDs!!! I have to put one on each LED. I was thinking about just putting one in each three bulb fixture on the ground side, but I may wish to turn on multiple LEDs in one fixture. Plus it is better practice to put the resistors on the hot side of the component. The next update should be me actually constructing the whole thing. I still need to figure out how to hang it, but not too bad so far.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
