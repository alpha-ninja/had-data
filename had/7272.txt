                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">A Geomagnetic Storm similar to the Carrington Event (<a href='https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859)' target="_blank">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859)</a> could move this planet back to the dark ages rapidly, and even less powerful events have been known to destroy power grids in recent times.<br /><br />The Aurora Observatory is a cheap and easy to build compact 3 Axis (X/Y/Z) magnetometer that is sensitive enough to detect minor variances in the earth's magnetic field in the order of 1-10 nT (Nano Teslas), this makes it sensitive enough to detect the Aurora,  the Quiet Day Curve for the earth and has applications for general Earth Field Monitoring, Metal / Mine Detection.<br />It also makes for an Extremely Sensitive 3D Compass that could be used for positioning.<br /><br />The magnetometer communicates with wireless (BLE) (allowing remote location of the magnetometer), and the aurora detector will be Wifi enabled to allow download of the data via the web with an aesthetic alerting system similar to this: <a href='http://www.thingiverse.com/thing:47973' target="_blank">http://www.thingiverse.com/thing:47973</a><br /><br /></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7272/log/23261-settled-on-fluxgate-configuration">Settled on fluxgate configuration</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7272/log/23261#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9692721439794292336.JPG"></p><p>Comparing Regular fluxgate (1 driving coil and 1 sensing coil = 2) and MagentoInductive (1 coil in oscillation), (using a magnet detection distance from the sensor of around 3 feet), I&apos;ve come to the conclusion that Magnetoinductive is not as sensitive as fluxgate, which I&apos;m assuming is because the core is not being driven into saturation with MagnetoInductive (it&apos;s low current).  </p><p>Being that the field drops inversely proportionally to the square of the distance, initial testing indicates that MagnetoInductive is approximately 5-10 times less sensitive.<br></p><p>However MagnetoInductive would be fine for a low budget general compass.  For the purposes of this project, sensitivity is a requirement.</p><p>Now for driving the fluxgate coil.  For testing purposes I&apos;ve been using a transistor oscillator (confusing as MagentoInductive uses an oscillator too), but this transistor oscillator uses both the sense and drive coils.  Ultimately I want to migrate away to a microprocessor drive for accuracy reasons, and the output current of a typical microprocessor isn&apos;t enough....</p><p>To get the cleanest sense signal in a fluxgate, the drive should have it&apos;s field reversed on each cycle.  I.E. 0V-5V, 5V-0V, 0V-5V, so I need a driver that can switch rails (similar to driving a DC motor forwards and backwards).</p><p>So, this evening I&apos;ve designed and CNC&apos;d (othermill) an H Bridge driver, driven by 1 pin (direction) and is complete with transient suppressors and schmidt triggers (although the transient suppressors I need to order so I&apos;m chancing it for now).  All driven by a teensy 3.1 rolling through a frequency spectrum 1KHz - 300KHz for testing purposes.</p><p>The PCB off the mill without components is above.  The following is the PCB populated:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8022371439794469036.jpg"></p><p>Testing the drive outputs (unloaded) to check correctness:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7524341439794541382.JPG"></p><p>Connected to the coil and the Teensy:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5292391439794597726.JPG"></p><p>Running at 10KHz, notice the flux collapse / recovery in the sense coil is <a href="http://www.invasens.co.uk/FluxgateExplained.PDF">text book:</a></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5571411439794712964.JPG"></p><p>Schematic for H-Bridge Driver:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1142241439794775599.png"></p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7272/log/23171-better-coils-with-3d-printed-formers">Better coils with 3D printed formers</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7272/log/23171#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>3D printed coil formers for 3 test coils now completed.  Coils were wrapped fairly haphazardly in a left-right-left-right formation.    Windings were stabilized  from unwinding with superglue and some hot glue at the ends.</p><p>Coil 1: One wire wrapped around small 3D Printed former, approx 35AWG (0.15mm), 2000 wraps, security tag strip cut in half lengthways (1/8 x 1.5 inches) inserted inside former.  Resistance: 46.7Ohms</p><p>Coil 2: Two wires wrapped together, around small 3D Printer former, approx 35AWG (0.15mm), 500 wraps, security tag strip cut in half lengthways (1/8 x 1.5 inches) inserted inside former.  Resistance: 9.6Ohms (per single wrap)</p><p>Coil 3: One wire wrapped around large 3D Printer former, approx 35 AWG (0.15mm), N wraps.  Core left empty to accept toroids inserted inside.  Resistance: 91.8 Ohms</p><p>Top to bottom:  Coil 3, Coil 1, Coil 2</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1691181439768341198.jpg">Showing 3D printed former for the smaller cores, and the finished wound coil showing the placement of the security tag strip (insert fully inside):</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9118211439768112581.JPG"></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7272/log/23129-better-core">Better core</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7272/log/23129#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1585651439757950496.JPG"><p>Saturday: Further research into the cores for magnetometers has highlighted that the correct core is very important to detecting the earths magnetic field, &quot;metglas&quot; or something similar with high permeability is required.</p><p>Um, don&apos;t have any, or do I ?   Metglas is found in the tags in library books, however I doubt the local library will donate any, but there is something nearly as good in these types of security tags (top rectangular tag):</p><p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_article_surveillance#/media/File:RFID_and_magneto-acoustic_tags.JPG" target="_blank">Acoustic Tag</a><br></p><p>Remember seeing those sometimes in DVD I buy at Costco.   Looking through my collection I&apos;ve found a black one inside a DVD.  The best thing is it&apos;s free (excluding the DVD).  I won&apos;t trash them in future when I find them on stuff.</p><p>Removed the top bias strip (attached to the sticky tape) from the tag to obtain the core strip (actually have 2 in there).  Removed one and wrapped 500 turns of approx 35 gauge around, not being to fussy about layout (apparently the most important thing is that wrap direction doesn&apos;t change when wrapping, otherwise neatness doesn&apos;t matter too much).</p><p>This is a single wrap, so is tested with the Magentoinductive oscillation technique.  Tried 3 different oscillators based on the following oscillation techniques with this coil:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8785751439758145915.JPG"></p><p>555 Timer: Works well, detects a regular ring magnet about 3 feet away on the scope.  Voltage sensitive and temperature sensitive.  The 0.005% / C thermal stability of a 555 is too poor for this use, so would need to be temperature compensated.  Voltage sensitivity can be solved with double regulation.  Also, a little difficult to tune due to the nature of the 555.  Otherwise preliminary results suggest it would do the job.  Oscillation range can be easily tuned for the best coil saturation.  Part count is low and cheap.</p><p>OP Amp: Couldn&apos;t get to oscillate reliably, tried 2 x ST UA741, and an LM358N.   Suspect inductance is the issue and that the coil needs to have more inductance for this to work.  Given this, it&apos;s likely to be problematic, so abandoning this branch.</p><p>Schmidt Trigger Inverter: Same sensitivity as the 555, but oscillation isn&apos;t too clean and varies significantly in amplitude and noise which makes it through to the output due to the back emf on the coil despite putting a shunt diode in.  Still, would likely be a very simple / cheap solution for a mid range precision compass.</p><p>Now the principle is proven to work, it&apos;s time to wind better quality coils on 3D printed forms for face off testing for the 2 techniques :</p><p>1. Dual parallel wound around a security tag strip (suitable for Fluxgate Technique)</p><p>2. Single wound around a security tag strip (2000 turns) - (suitable for Magnetoinductive Technique)</p><p>3. <a href="http://www3.imperial.ac.uk/spat/research/areas/space_magnetometer_laboratory/spaceinstrumentationresearch/magnetometers/fluxgatemagnetometers/howafluxgateworks" target="_blank">A conventional fluxgate design</a> consisting of one of the mystery toroids for the drive and a slip over form for the sense winding, that can be used to test a few different mystery cores.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                    </ul>
