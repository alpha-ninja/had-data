                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Emotions are a common part of the human condition, yet there is no clear method of measuring them objectively, or understanding them, beyond expensive, and inconvenient, formal psychological therapy. Bioloop aims to  "read" the conductivity of a user's skin using a simple, cheap wearable device, and help provide real-time, quantifiable insight into everyday feelings, acting as a biofeedback device for both users and mental health professionals. This opens up a wide range of uses, such as coaching users to control and manage anxiety and stress disorders.<br /><br />Licensed under the MIT licence.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Bioloop is a wrist mounted wearable centered around an electrodermal activity, or EDA sensor. It includes a microcontroller to process the data from the sensor, a display to output the readings to the user, some form of user input, and a battery to power it all. </p><hr><h2>EDA sensor<br></h2><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7506821436422645529.JPG" width="100" height="135" style="width: 100px; height: 135px;"><p>The EDA sensor relies on contact with the user&apos;s skin to output reliable readings. The current prototype uses two zinc hex bolts mounted on a lego wristband. These contacts connect to an amplifier circuit. The prototype also uses two LM358 dual op-amps. Two voltage followers, one acting as a reference and one acting as a sensor connect to a differential amplifier with a gain of 1.  I plan to use the op-amps as long as possible dispute their limited amplification capabilities in order to keep cost down and keep bioloop accessible to all.</p><hr><h2>Microcontroller</h2><p>The microcontroller that I chose to use for the prototype is an Arduino pro mini, bulit on the atmega328. I was weaned on the Arduino IDE, so that is what I chose to use. For the final version I will switch to the attiny84 due to it&apos;s low cost. My current prototype only needs an ADC pin, six digital pins, and four PWM pins. Sometime in the near future, I hope to learn how to program ARM micocontrollers and use a STM32F0 or LPC800 in place of the atmel chip. </p><hr><h2>Display</h2><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4998461436422796554.JPG" width="222" height="183" style="width: 222px; height: 183px;"></p><p>The display is based around 13 LEDs with PWM control that form an &quot;X&quot; shape. The center LED has individual PWM control. Each &quot;leg&quot; of the X is connected to three common PWM pins and a ground. This allows me to control the 12 other LEDs with only 3 PWM pins and 4 digital pins. Eventually, I might use transistors to switch the common grounds of each leg, but it isn&apos;t a huge issue right now. The display firmware uses the &quot;X&quot; to form up or down arrows based on the user&apos;s EDA level. The arrows get bigger or smaller with PWM. The other modes are mostly unexciting. I hope to use green LEDs in place of the yellow ones that I had on hand. In later versions, I might switch to a bigger matrix  display so bioloop can act as a regular watch. </p><hr><h2>Interface</h2><p>Bioloop&apos;s interface consists of a single button that is used to wake the watch and cycle between modes. The microcontroller uses a pin change interrupt to detect when the button is pressed during sleep. Future versions may use a capactive touch button instead.</p><hr><h2>Power</h2><p>The prototype for bioloop is powered off of my laptop to reduce power supply noise issues. Eventually bioloop will use a coin cell battery. Because of low power consumption, the battery may last over a year before it will have to be replaced. In this situation, using a rechargeable battery just adds to the complexity and cost.</p><hr><h2>Software</h2><p>Bioloop&apos;s software is fairly simple. The sensor compiles an average of 20 samples and outputs that to another function which uses a array to make a &quot;moving average&quot; of sensor values. This data is fed into the display function which essentially maps that data to the bioloop&apos;s display. The refresh rates are low enough where anyone can see visible change after taking a deep breath. Sleep code manages the power for bioloop. </p><hr><h2>Connectivity</h2><p>This is one section that I have not planned for yet. The attiny84 will have 1 extra pin which I may use as a serial RX to output the sensor reading in microsimens to a computer, which can be used for professionals for more complex data processing. Future versions of bioloop may use bluetooth 4.0, but I don&apos;t own any compatible devices, so this is a no-go for now. </p><hr><h2>Future Prototypes...</h2><p>Right now bioloop is built on a giant breadboard. If I ever get the prototype to work correctly, my next step will be ordering a PCB with all the parts, and a 3d printed wrist mounted enclosure. Bioloop is designed to be worn around, not chained to a desk. With enough work, I can make that happen. </p>
                                
                            </p>

                            <p><p>I don&apos;t understand computer science very well, but I have developed a rudimentary &quot;algorithm&quot; (if you can call it that) to interpret the information from the sensor. It scans for maximum/minimum readings, uses the value in the middle to establish a baseline, and gives the output value as a distance from this baseline. So far, things appear to be a little strange. Data seems to move up and down with no reason given, and I have not been able to get it to respond to induced stress very well. This is kind of a big deal, and I need to get the algorithm to work before adding all of the extras to make the firmware complete, which involves moving to the attiny sometime soon. Other code that I have used looks at the trendline of the data instead, perhaps that would be more useful? Some sources suggest looking for &quot;SCRs&quot;, but my sensor is not nearly good enough to do that. Perhaps I need to see the variation in data, as one website I looked at saw a &quot;cleaner graph&quot; as a sign of reduced stress, and a noisier dataset a sign of more stress. Anyway, I need to get something working ASAP so I can meet the prize deadlines. The main concurn is just making sure bioloop will work well for everyone.</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6701/log/22350-final-amplifier-design">Final Amplifier Design</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">9 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6701/log/22350#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The amplifier for bioloop required quite a bit of trial and error. I thought I had got it working, and the next day it didn&apos;t. After days of testing and many spilled resistors I decided on a final design, based off of a very helpful PDF that I linked. The original &apos;Cornell&apos; sensor is connected to a differential amplifier with a gain of 1, along with a similar voltage divider/follower circuit as shown below. </p><p style="margin-left: 20px;"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4392191439108034644.png">The dividers will be connected directly to a microcontroller pin, which will allow then to be shut on and off, saving power. Otherwise, small amounts of power would trickle through the voltage dividers, draining the battery. </p><p style="margin-left: 20px;">The schematic is from my final PCB design for the bioloop, which has a dedicated analog sensing board to avoid noise from other electronics. It also allows the final design to be very small-only about 1inch by 1inch. I will post more pictures of the  schematics when I get things squared away and working properly with the attiny84. Otherwise, hit up the github.</p>
