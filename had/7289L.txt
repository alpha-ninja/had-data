                            <h2><a href="/project/7289/log/23408-design-decisions-teensy-31">Design Decisions (Teensy 3.1)</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">12 hours ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7289/log/23408#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23408"><p>For this project, I wanted to make the overall experience as</p><p>aesthetically pleasing as possible. This means minimizing any wires</p><p>that may show, and hiding away as many technical details as possible</p><p>from the end user experience.</p><p>In general, I prefer to start with the lowest, simplest	layers of a</p><p>project, and then slowly add complexity	after each layer has been</p><p>tested and assured of working.</p><p>With capacitive	touch sensing and plants especially, I&apos;ve had a	few</p><p>&quot;interesting&quot; experiences.</p><p>Most recently, I did an	installation at	NYC Resistor for the</p><p>Interactive exhibition,	and had	everything stop working completely.</p><p>I didn&apos;t notice until two hours into the exhibit that my project was</p><p>touching a steel ground, and that my power supply was shorting.</p><p>It&apos;s bugs like these that happen in the	physical world that make</p><p>iterative development especially important when	making more complex</p><p>projects.</p><p>In general, the	Teensy 3.1 has one of the best capacitive touch</p><p>sensors available out of the box, and a	bunch of them. With my picture</p><p>frame, one of my bigger	goals is to get	an X and Y point for any touch</p><p>on my plants, and Teensy will allow me to first	ensure touch is</p><p>working consistently, before reaching for that bigger goal.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7289/log/23413-design-decisions-xbee">Design Decisions (XBee)</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">12 hours ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7289/log/23413#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23413"><p>Designing for XBee can be a miserable process, especially if you try</p><p>using it for something it wasn&apos;t designed for.	</p><p>In this	case, we&apos;ve chosed XBee	to send	event type notifications.</p><p>We&apos;ll send a touch even, or a gesture recognition even,	but we&apos;ll</p><p>never try to send raw input data over the wire.</p><p>The reason for this is,	XBee really doesn&apos;t have enough	throughouput</p><p>to handle real time measurements. It serves a much better role as an</p><p>event triggerer, and a minimal network to send communications.</p><p>We&apos;ll use an off the shelf XBee	X-Stick	connected to a Raspberry Pi to</p><p>act as the XBee	coordinator in our network, and it will send	commands to</p><p>our XBee Wall Router to	turn on	and off	our lights.</p><p>By using XBee we&apos;ll reduce a bit of complexity on our end devices we</p><p>connect to our systems.</p></div>
