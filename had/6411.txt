                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Though technology gets cheaper by the day, the prices of health-related tech haven't seen drastic changes in the last years. While I do understand that there is a lot involved in developing live-supporting devices, I feel that there's lots of room to increase affordability in the field.<br />Current prosthetics don't fall short, either.  Current bionic replacements for upper- and lower-extremity amputees can cost several hundreds of dollars. Living in the third world, it's clear to me that such devices simply can't reach all of those who need them.<br />As many other Makers already, I wish to change this situation, even if by the tiniest bit.<br /><br />This project (biohand) aims to develop a cheap (around the U$300 mark) prosthetic hand, that is 3D printable, and uses affordable and easy-to-find components. <br /><br />Still actively on the making!<br /> </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1>CONSIDERATIONS</h1><p>I live in Brazil. Here - unfortunately - lots of components are expensive or hard to get. This constrained a lot of design choices, a few of which are presented below.</p><p>Also, I tried my best to use solely open-source development tools (or, in worst cases, free-of-charge ones). </p><h2>Documentation</h2><p>I just finished <a href="http://martinvb.com/pydio/data/public/biohand-thesis.php">my thesis on the biohand</a><em><a href="http://martinvb.com/pydio/data/public/biohand-thesis.php">.</a> </em>The project is thoroughly detailed there, with relevant steps in mechanism synthesis, electronics design, software structure and prototype assembly. Check it out! :) <br></p><h1>MECHANICS</h1><p>Each finger is actuated individually, and relies on a 4-bar linkage mechanism to synchronize the movement of the proximal and distal phalanges (see picture below). The actuator is fully enclosed in the palm, to guarantee that even patients with <a href="http://www.ottobockus.com/prosthetics/info-for-new-amputees/information-for-upper-limb-amputees-and-their-families/#amputation">wrist disarticulation or transradial amputation</a> can use it.<br><br> Lots of thought went in the choice of the actuator itself. &apos;Proper&apos; DC motors (eg. <a href="http://www.maxonmotorusa.com/">Maxon</a> or <a href="http://www.faulhaber.com/">Faulhaber</a>) are generally too expensive for this application; same goes for linear actuators. Chinese DC motors with enough torque and decent reductions are hard to find and may easily be discontinued - a danger for any design.</p><p><img style="width: 548px; height: 172px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1627961435038281454.png" height="172" width="548"></p><p>I&apos;ve noticed that standard <strong>RC servos</strong>, on the other hand, are available *pretty much* anywhere in the world, and its internals (specially regarding mechanics) are roughly the same across a wide range of manufacturers. With that in mind, I used the DC motor and two gears of a standard metal RC servo to design a 3D printable linear actuator. A standard 25mm M3 screw acts as a leadscrew.</p><p><img style="width: 396px; height: 211px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4728581435038763499.jpg" height="211" width="396"></p><p>While it looks flimsy, it was able to lift  1kg. On top of that, the servo&apos;s potentiometer is used to supply feedback about the finger&apos;s position - something hardly available in commercial-grade hand prosthetics. The long screws in the servo&apos;s enclosure can also be used as dowels for the joints.</p><p><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6411/gallery#f0497c651988006840c5c26b18e1b852">The thumb</a>, uses a common arrangement with 2 degrees of freedom, with a linear actuator moving the first metacarpal joint, and a micro-servo driving a rotary motion for the carpometacarpal joint. </p><p>Lastly, I designed all the finger&apos;s parts in such a way that they&apos;re printable laying flat on the printbed. The part&apos;s orientation during print is of <a href="http://www.sparxeng.com/blog/mechanical/3d-print-orientation-effect-part-strength">great relevance in its final strength</a>.  With this setup, the <a href="http://www.sparxeng.com/blog/mechanical/3d-print-orientation-effect-part-strength">assembled finger mechanism</a> was able to individually hold 2kg in a non-destructive test. </p><p><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6411/gallery#f0497c651988006840c5c26b18e1b852">The prototype still doesn&apos;t feature aesthetic covers</a> (yet to come!), and <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6411/gallery#aec1fe8da8adaed330f0be66ae2e5ac9">it&apos;s being assembled</a>. </p><p>The mechanical designs were made using <a href="http://www.autodesk.com/products/inventor/overview">Autodesk Inventor</a>. While the program isn&apos;t open-source, 3-year Pro licenses are supplied for students. I&apos;m considering moving to <a href="http://onshape.com">Onshape</a> in a (somewhat not so) distant future. </p><h1>ELECTRONICS</h1><p>I&apos;ll be driving the whole system off of a 2S LiPo battery. The thumb&apos;s micro servo is getting a proper regulator (an <a href="http://www.ti.com/lit/ds/symlink/lm317.pdf">LM317</a> or an <a href="http://www.ti.com/lit/ds/symlink/lm1117-n.pdf">LM1117</a>). The DC motors are being driven with TI&apos;s <a href="http://www.ti.com/lit/ds/symlink/drv8801.pdf">DRV8801</a>: 2A RMS current output (<em>for real! Heard that, L298?</em>), internal logic and an amplifier for sense current made it an easy pick. </p><p>For the processor/controller, I went with an <a href="http://www.st.com/web/en/resource/technical/document/datasheet/CD00161566.pdf">STM32F103</a> <em>ARM Cortex M3</em>. I was already familiar with the processor, and ST&apos;s recent lineup of <a href="http://www.st.com/stm32-discovery">Discovery</a> and <a href="http://www.st.com/stm32nucleo">Nucleo</a> boards are a significant step for lowering the entry price for ARM development. I&apos;m currently using an <a href="http://www.st.com/nucleoF103RB-pr">Nucleo-F103RB</a> both as dev-board and SWD probe.</p><p>Electronics are still in its early stages. For now, I hooked up an STM32 (on a breakout board) to the SWD probe [left of the pic], and designed/milled a carrier board for the DRV8801 [right]. <br></p><p><img style="width: 406px; height: 266px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8399111435070049006.jpg" height="266" width="406"></p><p>The complete board is still being designed under <a href="http://kicad-pcb.org">KiCad</a>.</p><h1>SOFTWARE</h1><p>Toolchains for embedded ARM development used to cost a kidney (or be insanely hard to use). In the last years, however, many great open-source tools have become available. I&apos;m currently using a software setup that consists of:</p><ul><li><a href="https://launchpad.net/gcc-arm-embedded">A GNU toolchain for embedded ARM</a>,</li><li><a href="http://openocd.org/">OpenOCD</a> for programming/debugging the target IC,</li><li><a href="https://eclipse.org/cdt/">Eclipse CDT</a> as DE.</li></ul><p>Since the hardware is still being actively developed, I&apos;ve had...</p>

Now that I&apos;ve finished it, I finally have some spare time to invest in the project again. I tought I&apos;d briefly list some of the &apos;To-Do&apos;s I have planned for the upcoming two/three weeks:</p><ul><li>In the previous logs I&apos;ve mentioned that the custom control PCBs had arrived from the board house.                             <h2><a href="/project/6411/log/23323-some-tests-and-results">Some tests and results</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">15 hours ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6411/log/23323#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>As a part of my thesis on the <em>biohan</em><em>d, </em>I ran some relevant tests (mostly still with no control board, which came in a few days later). Some of these tests are presented briefly here. For more detail, <a href="http://martinvb.com/pydio/data/public/biohand-thesis.php">check my thesis!</a> :D</p><h2>Prehensions<br> </h2><p><a href="http://smpp.northwestern.edu/downloads/Design%20Of%20Artificial%20Arms%20And%20Hands%20For%20Prosthetic%20Applications.pdf">(Weir et al., 2003)</a> presents a minimal list of required hand <em>grips </em>(prehensions) to accomplish the majority of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living">Activities of Daily Living</a> (ADLs). This list encompasses a total of 7 different poses, shown below:</p><p><img style="width: 259px; height: 251px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1415681439815314295.jpg" height="251" width="259"></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></p><p>They are:</p><ul><li><strong>a1) </strong>Tridigital palmar prehension (<em>tripod grip</em><span class="redactor-invisible-space">)</span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>a2) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Bidigital palmar prehension (<em>pinch</em><span class="redactor-invisible-space">)</span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>b) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Tip prehension </span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>c) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Lateral grip</span></span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>d) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Hook grip </span></span></span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>e) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Spherical grip</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>f) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Cylindrical grip (<em>power grip</em><span class="redactor-invisible-space">)</span><br></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></li></ul><p>The image below shows the prototype&apos;s CAD model implementing this minimal set of prehensions (numbering is equivalent to the figure above): </p><p><img style="width: 660px; height: 338px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7679531439815611154.jpg" height="338" width="660"></p><p><br>With the 6 active <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom">Degrees of Freedom</a> of the <em>biohand, </em>the list could be achieved with no problems. Following the CAD proof of concept, the poses were tested on the prototype itself. The image below shows some of the tested grips:</p><p><img style="width: 672px; height: 384px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2154121439815728090.jpg" height="384" width="672"></p><p>Those are:</p><ul><li><strong>A) </strong>lateral grip</li><li><strong>B) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">bidigital palmar prehension</span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>C) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">cylindrical grip</span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>D) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">adduction grip: using the space/angle between adjacent fingers to hold thin objects.</span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>E) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">bidigital palmar prehension</span></span></span></span></li></ul><h2>Load test</h2><p>It&apos;s a know fact that parts printed in an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fused_deposition_modeling">FDM</a> 3D-printer have   stronger bonds inside layers than between them (an interesting study shown <a href="http://www.sparxeng.com/blog/mechanical/3d-print-orientation-effect-part-strength">here</a>). All parts in the <em>biohand </em>take advantage of this fact: they are designed to be  printed aligned to the printing bed in such a way, that the main stress and load gets distributed along the layers. </p><p>For example, the image below shows the proximal phalanx of the index finger. The red lines show were the phalanx connects to the MCP (<em>metacarpophalangeal</em>) and PIP (<em>proximal interphalangeal</em>) joints, as well as the linear actuator that moves the finger. The part gets printed with the orientation shown in the figure:</p><p><img style="width: 346px; height: 208px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8717161439816685201.png" height="208" width="346"></p><p>This grants an increased robustness. No destructive tests were conducted, but the test below shows the <em>biohand </em>succesfully whitstanding a load of 40N with no signs of demage: </p><p><img style="width: 324px; height: 602px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1368301439816830781.jpg" height="602" width="324"></p><p>The test was carried out with no power supply, since the fingers employ a non-backdrivable mechanism. <br></p><h2>Speed, strenghts and weight</h2><p>Upper-limb prosthetics still have a hard time achieving speeds and strenghts comparable to those in human arms and hands. To contextualize the <em>biohand&apos;s</em> streght, speed and weight, it shall be compared to two of the market-leading devices: the <a href="http://www.touchbionics.com/products/active-prostheses/i-limb-ultra">i-limb</a> and the <a href="http://bebionic.com/">bebionic v2</a> (the previous version of the current <em>v3 </em>model). The data from these devices is based in the wok of <a href="http://www.rehab.research.va.gov/jour/2013/505/page599.html">(Belter et al., 2013):</a></p><p><img style="width: 446px; height: 337px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7572861439818853691.jpg" height="337" width="446"></p><p>The <em>biohand</em><em> </em>weights in at approx. 335g (planned with the control board and cosmetic cover). This represents 54% and 62% of the the commercial prosthetics. </p><p>The index&apos; angular speed, when measured at the MCP joint (at the base of the finger), reaches a peak of 56&#xB0;/s, which is 58% and 93% of the other two devices. </p><p>Lastly, an individual finger of the <em>biohand </em>is capable of exerting up to 6.3N*, which represents 43% and 83% of the commercial devices.</p><p>Considering that the <em>biohand </em>uses modified RC servos as linear actuators for each finger, I can&apos;t say I&apos;m not pleased with the results. Four fingers working simultaneously can exert more than 36N in a prehension. This is sufficient for the majority of lighter ADLs, as stated in <a href="http://www.rehab.research.va.gov/jour/04/41/2/Smaby.html">(Smaby et al., 2004)</a>.</p><p> The reduction in weight is also very relevant. The work by <a href="http://www.researchgate.net/publication/5798071_Results_of_an_Internet_survey_of_myoelectric_prosthetic_hand_users">(Pylatiuk et al., 2007)</a> shows that excessive weight is a complaint of most amputees carrying some type of prosthetic device. </p><p>* The force test used only 60% of the <em>biohand&apos;s </em>nominal tension, to avoid motor demage during stall (for the maximum-force tests). </p><h2>Cost</h2><p>Lastly, one of the great thriumphs of the <em>biohand </em>is, in my opinion, its very low cost. With the prototype almost completed, the cost table can be calculated as follows: </p><table><tbody><tr><td>Item</td><td>Cost per unit (USD)</td><td>Quantity</td><td>Subtotal (USD)</td></tr><tr><td>Standard RC servo</td><td>12,95</td><td>5</td><td>64,75</td></tr><tr><td>Micro RC servo</td><td>9,95</td><td>1</td><td>9,95</td></tr><tr><td>Black ABS plastic (filament)</td><td>19,19 (kg)</td><td>155g</td><td>2,97</td></tr><tr><td>Other hardware costs (e.g., screws,...</td></tr></tbody></table>
