I entered that into The 2014Hackaday Prize, and this device was split off from it.<br />How it works:<br />- Connect four USB cables for up to four computers of any operating system.<br />- Plug in mouse and keyboard.<br />- No drivers and no software needed.<br />- Mouse pointer moves from one screen to the next by display edge detection.<br />An all hardware solution to Synergy* software without the hassles like LAN connectivity (*See link.)</div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/2872/log/22103-transparent-acrylic-inlays">Transparent Acrylic Inlays</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">14 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2872/log/22103#discussion-list">
                                15 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p><a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/laser-cutting"><strong>Ponoko Laser Cutting Service:</strong></a><strong> Front Panel Tests.</strong></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/356861438667742401.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/556961438667705587.jpg"></p><p>3mm clear acrylic skull inlays in black (painted) acrylic, with two 2mm red LED eyes. The red LEDs are not connected yet, but look like tiny jewel domes in the light. There are some more pictures below.<br>The inlays can be done in a variety of <a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/make-and-sell/materials">Ponoko&apos;s material combinations</a>. I would like to play with inlays in wood, or in felt for clothes, but those are different projects.</p><h2></h2><h2>&#x201C;Make Your First Product for Free&#x201D;</h2><p>I totally fell for the &apos;sign up now&apos; because I was thinking I would eventually need to for the coupon I won.</p><p><strong>The Catch:</strong></p><p><strong></strong>Anyone can sign up for an account, and to find out there are 10 days left to use a $20US discount towards a laser cutting service only, not including shipping.</p><p>That actually was not a bad deal for me. I have never ordered any cutting or 3D printing before, so I knew for sure I would make a mistake on my first design. Not wanting to mess up a $100 order, I decided to play along and jump through the hoops. I thought the OpenSCAD Skull from the <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/2872-driverless-mouse-and-keyboard-sharing/log/20943-human-interface">Human Interface log</a> would be easy to send off. That might have been easier if it was 3D printed, but the preferred method of this laser cutting service is colored SVG files.</p><p><strong>Ponoko has some great instructions for first time users.</strong></p><p>Following the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/make-and-sell/design-it-yourself">design-it-yourself starter kits</a> has to be fool proof, right? Fearing my LibreOffice Draw would be too quirky, I opted for the free Inkscape option so I could follow the directions. The good news is I learned some Inkscape in a few days. It&apos;s important to understand how lines connect with nodes, and how to watch for tricky connections and overlapping lines. The Ponoko directions in the starter kits are great, but now that I understand SVG files better, I will only be using LibreOffice.</p><p><img style="width: 309px; height: 344px; float: right; margin: 0px 0px 10px 10px;" alt="" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2164301438668022175.jpg" height="344" width="309"></p><p>The upper left image is the 0.010mm width blue cut line for the laser cutter (No square corners).</p><p>The other five are me playing with different line widths to get an idea of what the cut pieces might look like. The cut pieces ended up almost identical. </p><p>The second is the actual laser cut width of 0.40mm (+0.20mm on each side of the cut line.) </p><p>The third is the skull subtracted from material, then material subtracted from skull, overlaying both, and last with the 2mm LEDs I ordered.</p><p>I made two test face plates 150mm x 70mm but without the USB holes. Also some extra skulls, as recommended in the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.ponoko.com/starter-kits/inkscape#inkscape_section_7">Ponoko directions with tiny pieces</a> (&lt;5mm can fall through the bed.) Also some circles as bases to mount the skulls. I also found How to <a target="_blank" href="http://blog.ponoko.com/2010/06/17/how-to-make-snug-joints-in-acrylic/">Make Snug Joints in Acrylic</a> helpful to prevent cracking in the corners.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1259061438670344206.jpg"></p><p>Cutting the inlays the same size made for a loose fit (0.4mm on each side). Next time I will get closer by cutting different size shapes outside the panel. Another mistake was not putting through holes through the circle bases for the LED leads, just like I did on the 3D prints also.</p><h2>Inkscape vs. LibreOffice Draw</h2><p><strong></strong>Inkscape shows the instructions outside the boarder, <br>and LibreOffice shows the actual drawing.<strong></strong><br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7250391438668446630.jpg"></p><p>Adhesive paper holds parts in place. Scorch marks on paper only. This saved almost all of the 1.6mm diameter 3mm long cylinders cut out from the eyes!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9247761438668520210.jpg"></p><h2>Material and Cost</h2><p>Ponoko recommends testing designs in cardboard for affordability, but I chose to see the more expensive 3mm anti static clear acrylic, 181 x 181 x 3mm (P1). The edges are sharp and clean all the way through. <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8080621438668807670.jpg"></p><p>It was three days for processing, one day to make and ship it, two day priority mail, making it <strong>six days</strong> total from idea to in my hands. $22 is $2 over the $20 discount, plus 9.55 shipping, means the <strong>total cost was $11.55.</strong> I made a few mistakes, but now I can order custom material inlays with more confidence for a variety of projects, so I would say getting my feet wet with this was worth it.</p><p>Some more test pictures:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6505391438668941362.jpg"></p><p>These LEDs looked so much bigger on the computer screen when I ordered them.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3881501438669130646.jpg"></p><p>This is with the paper backing on both sides:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2594761438669158897.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2575771438669273418.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8838761438669318851.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1138141438669417157.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6465411438671821501.jpg"></p><p>Stacked and extended out 3mm past the panel:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5382731438669554036.jpg"></p><p>Recessed 3mm into the panel:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8413221438669652200.jpg"></p><p>Recessed eyes:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6520751438669717190.jpg"></p><p>That&apos;s...</p>

                <div id="comment-24185-content">
                    I have a device (an AdderLink IP) that has VGA+PS/2 Keyboard+Mouse inputs and outputs and acts as a VNC server. It has a similar problem, in that it has to match the mouse pointer position of the VNC client to the mouse position on the server, among others to ensure that the entire screen of the remote system is actually reachable.

The solution they came up with is to calibrate the mouse behaviour of the system it's connected to. You're instructed to have a plain background in the upper left corner of the screen; to measure the behaviour, the device runs the mouse to 0,0, moves it by various increments and checks the video output where the pointer actually ends up. It turns out that this is surprisingly accurate, in that they detect the type of acceleration (i've seen it detect X11, Windows and MacOS, if I'm not mistaken), and apparently the 1 or 2 additional numeric parameters required to compute the actual pointer position.  They still have a button in their GUI to resync the mouse pointer position in case anything goes wrong.

Obviously, you don't have the luxury of "watching" the video output to be able to chracterize the mouse acceleration. You could however try a calibration routine where the hardware and a python client work together. Afterwards, the Python client could store the resulting parameters in the hardware.

                <div id="comment-24155-content">
                    <p>So for the USB issue, why not do your switching virtually? Use your own hardware to receive and route data from the keyboard and mouse.&nbsp;When a host computer connects, go ahead and create two HID devices for it (a mouse and a keyboard) but leave them idle. When your device decides that computer [x]&nbsp;should be active, it routes all of its intercepted commands to the already-connected USB device. I realize this will likely cause your material cost to increase, but then you can do fun things like duplicate your input to all outputs and your switching time would go away since the computers wouldn't be connecting/disconnecting devices all the time. Also, I've had some instances where the PC would temporarily freeze when USB devices were added/removed and this caused interruptions to other USB connected hardware. Anyway, just an idea.</p>
