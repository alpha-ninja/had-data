                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The ProReg solves a pesky issue...how much propane is left in the tank? The industry standard for determining propane levels is to use a scale to measure the weight of the tank. The problem is that an accurate scale is expensive. The ProReg determines fuel levels by measuring changes in multiple sensors while gas is being released from the tank.  <br /><br /><br />Check out the first transmission of pressure sensor data to my phone:<br /><a href='https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JM6LUr8fhDw' target="_blank">https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JM6LUr8fhDw</a><br /><br />See more detail on the ProReg Facebook page<br /><a href='https://www.facebook.com/pages/ProReg/314099732118701?ref=aymt_homepage_panel' target="_blank">https://www.facebook.com/pages/ProReg/314099732118701?ref=aymt_homepage_panel</a></div>

                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Hacked my barbecue, and went from there...</p><p>Propane Monitoring - Not Easy To Do!</p><p>In order to understand why I believe the ProReg will be a breakthrough in the measurement of pressurized finite fuel systems, it is important to understand the physics behind the liquid and gas mixture that resides inside of your propane tank. When holding temperature constant, the liquid and gas mixture of your tank will always return to an equilibrium pressure. To clarify, a propane tank with 30% liquid will have the same pressure as a tank with 70% liquid on a 70 degree day. Thus, taking an on the spot pressure measurement is useless. However, when we record pressure changes over time and account for temperature fluctuations, it becomes possible to reliably estimate propane consumption. Making this technology work before the IoT revolution was not commercially viable, as each propane monitoring system would require expensive hardware and software at the sight of the measurement. With the proliferation of low energy wireless connectivity, we can finally create a cost effective system that will send data readings to the cloud where a central database computes propane usage and sends that calculation back to the user. Below is an example of the data-set provided by the maiden run of the ProReg V2. It shows an initial reduction in gas with the burners on medium, then the slope decreases as I turned the burners up to high, then pressure starts to increase back to its equilibrium after I turned the grill off, then It begins to decrease again after I turn the grill back on, and eventually flat-lines as the tank runs out of gas.</p><p><a href="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5293951427831425273.png" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5293951427831425273.png"></a></p>

                            <p><p>I am so happy to show in this project log that the physics of the ProReg method of monitoring propane consumption seem to make a lot of sense! The data below represents the utilization of an entire 20lb tank of propane over the course of 8ish hours of grilling:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1628731434762835667.jpg"></p><p>Each segment of declining pressure data represents 1 hour of grilling with all 3 burners on High. It took approximately 8 hours to burn through the entire tank. Each hour long segment of grilling was done on a different day in May, which is why the temperature data is so differentiated between hour long segments. While there remains a significant amount of work to be done, I hope that this chart shows that there is a distinct and measurable reduction in pressure, adjusting for ambient temperature fluctuations, that can be used to create a system for estimating propane consumption.  </p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5025/log/17937-proreg-casing">ProReg Casing </a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5025/log/17937#discussion-list">

                            </a>
                            </small>

                    <p>I'm not trying to discourage you, just want to point out that a tank of propane has propane in two phases, liquid and gas. As the gas phase&nbsp;propane is used the liquid propane undergoes an endothermic phase change from liquid to gas. This is why the tank gets cool as you use it. As the gas is used and the liquid changes phase and turns into gas the pressure will rollercoaster a little but remain relatively level until all the liquid has phase&nbsp;changed to gas, then you will get a pressure drop.&nbsp;</p><p>A way around this would be to track flow and keep a running total from when you start out with a full tank. So, 5 lbs of propane equals 5x(gas expansion for propane) equals Y liters of gas. Differential&nbsp;Flow can be measured&nbsp;with your pressure transducer across a fixed orifice.&nbsp;</p>
