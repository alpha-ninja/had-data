                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Introducing myself and describing things I do can be frustrating, but having an example device on hand tends to make a good first impression.  So let's make a thing specifically for easing the process of meeting new people at shows and things.  Electronic business cards look cool, but I don't want to buy useless electronics to give away to strangers.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>7" is big, almost too big to wear on a shirt, so people should see it.  I can make a switching power supply to run on batteries.  There are some options for storing graphics in memory and supplying processing power.  I'm starting with a Raspberry Pi-based prototype and I hope to quickly move to something more fun.</p><p><span></span></p>
                                
                            </p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5950/log/21597-lacking-progress">Lacking Progress</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">22 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5950/log/21597#discussion-list">

                            </small>
                            <p><p>I keep getting distracted from this.  Plus work.</p><p>And I&apos;m at the difficult point.  It was looking more complex than I expected and my KiCAD skills aren&apos;t what they should be.  I haven&apos;t read as much of datasheets as I need to to understand.  Maybe I should ask someone?  Dunno who.<br></p><p>I think I&apos;ll stick with the prototype for this thing.  Sometime I&apos;ll take up a smaller project to figure out KiCAD.  And then maybe come back to it?</p><p>Some other neat video stuff on Hackaday but not sure if anyone is directly going to a 40ish pin screen the way I was thinking.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5950/log/20130-plans-need-work">Plans need work</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5950/log/20130#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Here's an incomplete schematic:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9160681435514302671.jpg"></p>No switching power stuff on there, but right now I'm more concerned about the PIC and interfaces between SRAM and LCD.  Needs some figuring out how to do still.<p>A couple tips for producivity:  </p><p>MPLAB Harmony (and the Configurator for some smaller PICs) have a pin diagram that you can use to figure out where modules are and line up what goes on your PIC without absolutely scouring the datasheet.  Looks neat so far.  I'll use it later for code too.</p><p>KiCAD parts can be slow to generate, so I use an online "quicklib" tool here <a href="http://kicad.rohrbacher.net/quicklib.php">http://kicad.rohrbacher.net/quicklib.php</a>.  Not as good as getting free Eagle parts, but it fits into the KiCAD design regimen well and I get to customize things at every step.</p><p>Now, the ram chip has a clock pin which I hadn't figured on.  The PIC PMP might be able to use a chip select pin sort of as a clock, not sure, need to read more.  An annoying placement too, I was figuring to use PORTD as most of the LCD color specifying bits.</p><p>Also, 24 bits of connection is way more than I want to actually do now that I look at it in the project.  The PIC port pins don't line up and it's a struggle to use only 3 different ports to make these 16 bits work.  I think this will be plenty.</p><p>It's frustrating but it's interesting!  But I'm getting burned out for this weekend.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5950/log/20086-one-way-not-to-prototype">One way not to prototype</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5950/log/20086#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So, I've made a bit of a prototype.  It was a rush job putting together stuff and the lack of planning shows.  Here is an overall image:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2809051435452155857.jpg"></p><p>I have 4x 3.7v batteries as the main supply, where everything but the RPI can run anywhere from 24V to 7V.  So the RPI gets a 5V buck converter.  No problems with this switcher:  <a href="http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=9SIA6742FN3277">http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=9SIA6742FN3277</a>.</p><p>Did I mention that these 50 pin, 7" TFT LCD displays are called "AT070TN90<strong></strong>"?  So far working very well and available for cheap on the internet.  They go into car monitors for backup cameras and also are intended to work with the Raspberry Pi.  So I bought a RPI driver for it.</p><p>The screen looks amazing (to me anyway).  No sign of a flicker like with the RCA driver.  Very readable, lots of color, nothing like lag or flickering.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/880941435452573445.jpg"></p>There's some downside though.  It's difficult to fit all of this into my pocket.  I really don't want to break that exposed flat flexible connector.  There are things that can short behind the RPI and the driver boards.  And the mounting plate I used can conduct electricity.<p>These things would be really neat for a little display box like everyone is making, but I wanted these things to fit in my pocket.  The HDMI cable isn't flexible enough to bend into there, and the two boards and extra batteries just won't fit.  I've managed to do the stuff that everyone else is doing, but not the stuff that I wanted to do!  I might be able to shift the batteries into another pocket or something.  There are RPI model A boards that might work better on the RPI side.</p><p>We could play with this more, but I'd like to go back to the microcontroller side of the project.  There's about a month before some important stuff in August where I'd like to have this thing to handle introductions for me and I'd rather have the greatly reduced bulk.  Plus I'm bored of buying other people's electronics and I want to make my own.  The wallet protests too.</p><p>More on a schematic maybe this weekend?  I'm thinking of using a PIC and the deadline for the sponsor contest is Monday, so I might burn some midnight oil for that and the August deadline.  I've used some PIC32 in the past, so I expect that to be faster than STM32.  Good for trying a board.</p><p><span></span></p><p><span></span></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
