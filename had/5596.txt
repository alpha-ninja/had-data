                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The EMdrive is a new type of thruster - recently invented by Roger Shawyer.<br />Unlike other propulsion systems which need to repel mass to produce thrust, the EMdrive can convert electrical energy into thrust directly.<br /><br />A working EMdrive would start a revolution in spaceflight, enabling manned deep space exploration.<br /><br />Several builds have been made worldwide (eg Chinese University, NASA), many  show positive results. This topic is still quite new and needs a lot of research.<br /><br />Most EMdrives work with frequencies around 2.4 GHz. This makes them big and hard to test under DIY conditions (big vacuum chamber, liquid metal contacts)<br />So our attempt is to build one which works with 24GHz, which reduces the form factor significantly.<br />A so small EMdrive could be flown to space for  30000$ on a pocketqube satellite.<br /><br />Our first setup implied thrust - Despite the poor setup, it showed that we should continue.<br />Currently we are developing a much better setup which will be tested in September.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>SYSTEM DESIGN DOCUMENT</p><p>The EMdrive is basically a closed microwave waveguide with a changed geometry, so one of the ends is bigger. When RF is fed into the cavity and a resonance is achieved - according to Shawyer a thrust force will occur.<br></p><p>1) Magnetic levitation test rig</p><p>Our first attempt was a magnetically levitated platform which carried the EMdrive. The platform was set into rotation and the rotation rate change was measured by a wireless linked gyro.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4562191439545807593.jpg"></p><p>Advantage: Force can be accumulated continously into rotation rate change</p><p>Disadvantage: Maximum 100g lift force, regulation loop not vibration free</p><p>2) Swimming platform test rig</p><p>Our last test rig was a swimming platform which contained a fixed size aluminium cavity fed with a modified radar module. A force would results in a rotation:<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7508491439541010423.jpg"></p><p>We could achieve extremely fine measurements by magnetically stabilizing and damping the platform and measuring the rotation via computer vision with subpixel accuracy. Cube magnets below the tank and on the platform would hold the platform at the center and apply a weak torsion-like force to a defined idle position.<br></p><p>Both experiments implied a possible thrust (see the logs) - however they were not able to give a clear proof.<br></p><p>Assumed reasons are: Frequency was not tuneable and there was no moving endplate, so resonance conditions could not be controllably achieved. Although the magnetic swimming platform was able to show extremely tiny forces, currents in cables resulted in lorentz forces which rotated the platform always in the same direction and so disturbed our measurements.</p><p>The levitating platform can lift only 100g. We consider to build a levitator which can lift more with a better feedback loop to avoid oscillations.</p><p>WHAT WE ARE WORKING TOWARDS<br></p><p>Our new approach will be a modified milligram scale for direct thrust measurement - alternatively we&#xB4;ll use the swimming platform or a strong magnetic leviatation platform when the scale does not show the desired accuracy. In case of using the swimming platform, we will put it into the basement and far away from electrical sources to avoid mechanical and elecrtical disturbance forces.</p><p>Our new cavity is 3D printed of silver. Silver is a very suitable material for waveguides due to its conductivity.<br></p><p>We will use a tuneable 500mW RF source and a mechanically movable endplate to fine tune into resonance. We are also working on an electrical detection of resonance condition.</p><p>Final step: If our EMdrive works in the lab, we will produce an embedded flight version inside a pocketqub satellite.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5044911439539783292.jpg"></p><p>PS: Many many thanx to all the people who gave us very helpful hints how to improve the system. It has been an exciting ride for us until now, and we hope to provide a functional Baby-EMdrive soon</p><p>Special thanx go to TheTravellerEMD and Marvin Macportain</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>
