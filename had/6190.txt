                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">In summary, this is an ambitious project aiming to initially perform individual character recognition with a 30x30 mouse sensor camera, and ultimately reading text aloud.<br /><br />As most would know, mouse sensors have fast but low resolution cameras used to detect movement. Importantly, mouse sensors are small and much easier to interface with than high resolution cameras. By using a low resolution, the potential to interpret and hence perform optical text recognition for individual characters is theoretically possible. However, till now, I have only seen basic OCR done with numbers on PICs. OCR is not easy, especially with microcontrollers. Hopefully -- yes -- hopefully a Teensy 3.1 will be able to do it.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><strong>Obstacles</strong></p><ol><li>Performing OCR with the mouse sensor and detecting rotation of the text (text cannot always be expected to be upright/vertical).</li><li>Performing OCR at a decent frame rate to allow reading of text</li><li>Shrinking the hardware down to "pen" sized</li></ol><p>Obviously, there's a lot more than that. Consider this the first stage of the project.<br></p>
                                
                            </p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6190/log/19992-positioning-the-mouse-sensor">Positioning the mouse sensor</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6190/log/19992#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>A typical computer mouse has the sensor elevated at just the right height. For the ADNS-9800, this is approximately, this is approximately 2.4mm. HOWEVER this is not a concern for our application as we aren't detecting the small particles of the surface. Instead, it's important to position the sensor at exactly the right distance so that individual letters are captured on the camera. As a result, the "pen" may look less like a normal "pen" but instead a tubular device that needs to be in contact with the paper.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6190/log/19235-working-with-the-adns-9800">Working with the ADNS-9800</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6190/log/19235#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So I started the project today, hoping to get the camera showing some images of text on the computer. I succeeded ... partly. The camera seems to struggle with differentiating between anything but extremely bright and dark objects. In the end, I was only able to recognise bright sources of light (such as the ceiling light) as pictured below.</p><p><img style="width: 182px; height: 182px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8494721433833465654.png"></p><p>(instead of writing a proper application for showing shades, I decided to just send different ASCII characters over serial).</p><p>I've also tried with and without the lens, and it seems that removing the lens gives better results.</p>
                                
                            </p>
