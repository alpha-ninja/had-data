The Eyedrivomatic system takes advantage of existing eye tracking technology to allow users to drive their chairs again - using only their eyes.  It&#39;s a low cost, open source way to give mobility back to people who thought they had lost it forever. 

The Eyedrivomatic team has three main members, myself (Patrick Joyce), Steve Evans, and David Hopkinson. Steve and I both have Motor Neuron Disease, or ALS as it&#39;s also known. We both have electric  wheelchairs, but while I still have some movement in my fingers and right arm, and am just able to operate mine, Steve only has his eyes left, and until Eyedrivomatic, had to rely on his carer to operate his. 

The majority of people with MND will, like Steve, end up quadraplegics, before they die. And there are thousands of people in that position worldwide right now - Eyedrivomatic could help most of them. ">

                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">People who have totally lost the use of their muscles cannot operate their own wheelchairs. <br /><br />The Eyedrivomatic system takes advantage of existing eye tracking technology to allow users to drive their chairs again - using only their eyes.  It's a low cost, open source way to give mobility back to people who thought they had lost it forever. <br /><br />The Eyedrivomatic team has three main members, myself (Patrick Joyce), Steve Evans, and David Hopkinson. Steve and I both have Motor Neuron Disease, or ALS as it's also known. We both have electric  wheelchairs, but while I still have some movement in my fingers and right arm, and am just able to operate mine, Steve only has his eyes left, and until Eyedrivomatic, had to rely on his carer to operate his. <br /><br />The majority of people with MND will, like Steve, end up quadraplegics, before they die. And there are thousands of people in that position worldwide right now - Eyedrivomatic could help most of them. </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/6xbHKPtN2Jo" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe><h2>The Concept<br></h2><p>To create an inexpensive and accessible way for people with motorised wheelchairs to take control of their mobility via eye movement, using open source methods as much as possible.</p><h2>Our team<br></h2><p>Patrick Joyce - Team captain </p><p>Steve Evans - Test pilot</p><p>David Hopkinson - Documentation and assembly </p><h2>Background <br></h2><p>It is usual for people with MND/ALS in the UK to be provided with electric wheelchairs loaned by the health service, for as long as we need them. Steve and I have wheelchairs, and we both also have eyegaze equipment (which is becoming increasingly more available). At the moment it is not possible to operate one with the other. Eyegaze is technology only for operating a computer, not the chair.<br></p><h2>The Original Idea </h2><p>As I don&apos;t actually own either the wheelchair or the eyegaze, my solution was to make something that would interface with the user&apos;s chair mounted computer, and physically move the joystick. Crucially this would mean not making any modifications to the loaned hardware, and could work with any wheelchair and eyegaze combination.<br><br>I had the initial idea in 2013 and thought about it for several months. The criteria I came up with were as follows. <strong><br></strong></p><p><strong>It needs to be: </strong></p><ul><li><strong>Cheap </strong></li><li><strong>Easy to install and use </strong></li><li><strong>Safe </strong><strong></strong></li><li><strong>Expandable - the system should be able to control other external devices according to the needs of the user </strong></li><li><strong>Totally o</strong><strong>pen, both open hardware and open source </strong><strong>software </strong></li><li><strong>Easy to build at hom</strong><strong>e -</strong><strong> </strong><strong>by an unskilled person</strong></li><li><strong>A cheap manufactured product as well</strong></li></ul><p>I envisaged having two parts - an Electronic Hand unit, and a Brain Box to control it. Making the Electronic Hand should be fairly easy using servos, but I was stuck on what the Brian Box would actually be . . . until I discovered arduino!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7516521438800311010.png"></p><h2>3D Printing<em></em></h2><p><em></em></p><p>Even with this revelation the project was basically dead in the water until the advent of accessible 3D printing. So, in the summer of 2014 I ordered a 3D printer and the prototyping process began in earnest.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7846871429906882587.jpg"><em></em></p><p><em>The original Eyedrivomatic prototype </em></p><p>I taught myself to program the arduino, and eventually learned how to use processing too. The hardware went through several stages in the later half of 2014 and the early part of 2015. At first I was going to use infrared for pc to arduino communication. This would have had several advantages, but by April this year I had ditched the IR in favour of serial communication through a usb cable. It&apos;s much simpler and has proved more reliable. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7912391435754941606.jpg"><em></em></p><p><em>An early Eyedrivomatic system</em></p><p>I put in a four relay module as standard equipment in the brain box. These relays are for operating external switches of the user&apos;s choosing. Many wheelchairs have tilt/recline functions, and often these can be controlled using buttons plugged into sockets on the wheelchair&apos;s joystick unit. Thus Eyedrivomatic can control all of the chair&apos;s functions. But the chair will only use two of the output sockets on the brain box, leaving two for other purposes. I will use one myself to fire a nerf blaster which will live on the side of the chair. Also, the arduino has plenty of spare pins and processing power. In order to utilise these, there are three empty bays on the brain box, to take servo extension cables. These could be used for a variety of purposes including; </p><ul><li><strong>Enviromental control through IR or RF. Control your tv or close the curtains with your eyes! </strong></li><li><strong>Controlling a robot arm attached to the wheelchair. I am actively developing one for myself, and will post it soon. </strong></li><li><strong>Controlling a fan</strong></li><li><strong>Headlights, taillights and indicators</strong></li><li><strong><a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/6584-headphone-robot">Headphone robot.</a> A robot to put my headphones on and off. This may sound silly, but I wanted one, so i&apos;ve built one. </strong></li></ul><p>And so on. I want Eyedrivomatic to change people&apos;s lives, and it&apos;s inherent flexibility will help that process. </p><h2><br>Progress so far</h2><p>At the time of writing this (August 2015), we have reached Mk3 on both the Electronic Hand and the Brain Box. The hardware is at the point where i&apos;m ready to put an instructable up. It can still be improved, but it&apos;s advanced enough...</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5426/log/22772-software-hardware-and-the-return-of-steve">Software, hardware, and the return of Steve</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 days ago  &bull;

                                1 comment
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>We&apos;ve got our test pilot back! Steve has finally got over the pneumonia sufficiently to be able to resume his role as test pilot. While he had been dodging his eyedrivomatic duties, i had done a lot of software development, and was supremely confident that software version 4c was the final version. Steve, over the last five days has proved me entirely wrong. So I have been feverishly writing code, which has resulted in version 4d. Unfortunately, the testing has highlighted a more serious problem with the hardware - or more specifically, the electronic hand. The basic design is flawed. At the moment there are two double sided arms at right angles to each other. They move independently, each being controlled by a servo. The joystick then slides between the arms. In order to slide freely, there is a certain amount of slop in the system. This slop, it turns out, introduces an unacceptable degree of inconsistency into the self centring mechanism. <br>So, next week&apos;s task will be a major redesign of the electronic hand. <br></p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5426/log/22164-software-and-testing">Software and Testing.... </a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">13 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5426/log/22164#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Steve hasn&apos;t recoveredenough to help with the testing yet. I&apos;ve been doing my best, but Ihave few opportunities. Software development is going well however. Iam cautiously optimistic that the software is now feature complete. Ihave successfully (I hope) addressed all the problems we&apos;d had withprevious versions of the software. It needs thoroughly testing ofcourse. Here&apos;s a screenshot of the main driving grid. <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6146311438781642871.png"></p><p>The guiding principleof eyedrivomatic is safety. Controlling a wheelchair with eyegaze ispotentially dangerous because of the inherently temperamental natureof eyegaze. The equipment can lose track of your eyes easily,particularly in fluctuating lighting conditions. This canoccasionally cause the mouse to jump about the screen, or stay in oneplace. Irritating if you&apos;re writing a letter, potentially dangerousif you&apos;re driving your chair. Overcoming this hazard was my first jobas designer. <br></p><p>What I did was thefollowing:  To move the chair you click one of the direction buttons.The chair then moves in that direction for a set number of seconds,and then stops. In order to carry on you have to push the continuebutton or a different direction button. If you push the same button twice in a row, nothing happens.  When you&apos;vepushed the continue button, the chair will continue in the samedirection for another duration. Then you have to push the directionbutton again. And so it continues &#x2013; direction, continue, direction,continue, direction etc. This way, the chair cannot get out ofcontrol for more than a few seconds, before it stops on it&apos;s own. Ifyou push the next button before the previous duration is over, thenthe chair continues forward (or turns) smoothly, without stopping. Nice and safe. Well, as safe as I can make it anyway. Any electric wheelchair can be dangerous, no matter how it&apos;s controlled. </p><p>The &apos;electronic hand&apos;has two servos, one controlling forward and backwards movement, andone for left/right.  Movement duration is user selectable for eachservo. Overall speed is user controlled as well, and there is abutton to toggle between driving and manoeuvring modes. A stop buttonof course, and on chairs that support it, there&apos;s also a panic buttonthat turns the entire wheelchair off.</p><p>Currently the softwareonly works in conjunction with the Grid2 AAC package. Once thecurrent software version has been proven in the field (fingerscrossed), I will make a stand alone application for pc and one formac. Meanwhile I&apos;ve put all the files necessary to build it up on thegithub repository. No instructable yet, but watch this space! <br></p>
                                
                            </p>

                            <p><p>Steve is out of hospital now, and over the worst of his pneumonia. ALS makes you fragile however, and Steve is still a bit broken. He&apos;s not up to doing any testing yet, so, with the deadline looming I&apos;ve taken over that role. Straight away I noticed a problem... The mk2 electronic hand attached to the joystick unit with velcro. This was inadequate because, as the user turned left or right, the hand would twist a little bit out of alignment. This meant that the chair then wouldn&apos;t go in a straight line. Steve came up with the idea of a locator plate, that would fix permanently to the chair, and that the hand would then sit on - locating it perfectly every time. When I came to fit my own &apos;Evans Locator&apos; I found that it was hard to align the plate perfectly. So I have made the  Mk2 evans locator plate. This one is adjustable, even after being fixed in place. I&apos;ve printed it, and will fit it to my chair this morning. Then we will be back in business. <br></p><p>Being an inventor with ALS can be frustratiing. To perfect Eyedrivomatic I need to make small changes to the software, then test it - then make more changes and test again, and so on. The problem is that my hands don&apos;t work. I can&apos;t put the electronic hand on myself, and often I don&apos;t have a carer available to help me. I can&apos;t leave the electronic hand in place because I use the wheelchair joystick as a mouse to control the computer. This is why the partnership with Steve worked so well - because he does have a full time carer, and can test whenever he likes. Frustrating. <br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9890711437208190165.png"><em></em></p><p><em>Mk2 Evans Locator Plate.
