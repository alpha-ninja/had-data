                    <div class="description">A Bristle Bot is the simplest form of moving toy that one could build.   To make it more interesting, I'll try to make a solar powered bristle bot with what I have on hand.  This one probably has the lowest carbon footprint in term of minimal material and run entirely on solar energy in "Have a project that moves?" category of the contest. That should account for something, right?</div>

                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>t just happens that I have extra <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5903-sonar-for-the-visually-impaired/log/18341-lets-go-shopping">haptic motors</a> that is due to arrive any time soon from China and I have been wondering what to do with them.  Then I read about the &quot;<a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/07/02/15-quadcopters-up-for-grabs-in-wings-wheels-and-propellers-contest/">Have a project that moves?</a>&quot; contest today.  I guess I&apos;ll have to enter something entirely different and really low budget just to stretch the rules and stir up the contest.</p><p>This is a combination of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BEAM_robotics">Solar B.E.A.M. bot</a> and <a href="http://www.evilmadscientist.com/2007/bristlebot-a-tiny-directional-vibrobot/">Bristlebot</a> using the parts I have on hand and the left overs from my &quot;<a href="https://hackaday.io/project/5903-sonar-for-the-visually-impaired">Sonar for the visually impaired</a>&quot; contest entry.  What I learnt from here might find its use in the Sonar project.</p><p>This is not going to make the world a better place, nor be the next pet rock in time for Xmas. This one probably has the lowest carbon footprint in term of minimal material and entirely runs on solar energy in that category of the contest.</p><p> It is just a simple down to Earth project  to get a break from my other projects and the chance to win a prize.  If you follow my other projects, you know my definition of simple can be very different...</p><hr><p>This is a form of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jumper_%28BEAM%29">B.E.A.M. Jumper</a> using the vibration of a haptic motor and the angled bristles from a tooth brush which acts like a ratchet to translate the vibrations into a linear motion.<br></p><p>Cheap solar panels from toy have very limited power, so it is not feasible to power the motor(s) directly.  A larger panel also means that it is going to be more heavy and requires a larger motor(s). Somewhere there is a trade-off.  I don&apos;t know if the tiny solar cell I have from a credit card size calculator would charge fast enough at this stage without testing.  I have a much bigger one from a solar patio lantern that might work better.  I may even build both just to find out.  :)</p><p>The supercap is charged by the panel and when the voltage reaches a threshold with enough energy for the motor, the control circuits kicks in and allow the haptic motor to twitch until the capacitor is drained.  The process repeats itself.  Until the supercap is charged up again, the bot remains dormant.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3393581436135241697.png" width="303" height="105" style="width: 303px; height: 105px;"></p><h2>Haptic Motor</h2><p>The most common type of haptic motors are the ERM (Eccentric Rotating Mass). The brushed motor has a lower lifetime and lower efficiency vs the other types of haptic actuators, but they are also the least inexpensive. They are commonly found in lower ended mobile devices. </p><p>The motor is a brush DC motor with a coreless winding and a Neodymium magnet.  Brushes providing commutation, these lightweight motors have a low starting inertia for maximum acceleration &amp; minimal power consumption.</p><p>Diagram from <a href="http://www.ti.com/general/docs/lit/getliterature.tsp?literatureNumber=sszb151&amp;fileType=pdf&amp;keyMatch=sszb151&amp;tisearch=Search-EN">Haptics - Solution for ERM and LRA Actuators</a></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6043391436176755820.png"></p><p>The eccentric mass generates acceleration perpendicular to the axis of rotation.<br><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9863941436177959922.png"><br></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></span></p><h2><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6646-solar-beam-bristle-bot/log/20652-voltage-threshold-detection">Voltage Threshold Detection</a> (link)<br></h2><p>This part is used to detect the charge level across the supercap. When the capacitor is charge above the threshold, the rest of the circuit is energized, motors spins around until the capacitor voltage is below threshold again. It would be a boring project for me if I just copy/paste existing designs from <a href="http://library.solarbotics.net/circuits/se_t1.html">here</a>. Instead I am going to design my own from my parts collection.<br></p><h2><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6646-solar-beam-bristle-bot/log/20653-motor-driver">Motor Driver</a> (link)</h2><h2><span></span></h2><p>This is the circuit for delivering power to the haptic motors and responsible for steering the bot.</p><p>This is what the PCB looks like right now with the Voltage Threshold and the Motor Driver (minus 2 resistors for R7/R8)</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1648141436373559501.png"></p><p>Youtube Video.  Not a lot to show except title and PCB</p><p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/cFeKK2F16Ts" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br></p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6646/log/20653-motor-driver">Motor Driver</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6646/log/20653#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>While the 74LVC series electrical performance is specified for supply voltages between 1.65V to 3.3V, they can actually operate them below 1.65V. (It becomes the designer's responsibilities for operating them out of specs.) A large number of them can be wired in parallel to reduce the driver impedance at low voltage.</p><p>The traditional bipolar transistor works very well for low voltages for cases when a bridge driver is not needed. For Bristle bots, the motor direction doesn't matter.</p><p>Here is a design I came up with. It is a discrete oscillator with two complementary outputs. The two output can be used to drive a pair of motors <span class="redactor-invisible-space">represented by resistors: R2, R4</span>. The oscillator are buffered by Q2/Q1. By changing the duty cycle of the PWM, one of them will receive more energy than the other and I can use this for steering with the right mechanical arrangement. Since only one motor is on at any time, The current drain is reduced up to a factor of 2. This reduce the losses due to ESR, I^2R.</p><p>By varying the current going into base of Q4/Q3 and capacitors C1/C2, the duty cycle can be changed. In this example: R7 at 200K vs R8 at 220K, the load R4 gets a higher duty cycle i.e. more time/energy. The R7/R8 values can be controlled directly by using a pair of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoresistor">photoresistors</a> or by two controllable current sources.</p><p>The PWM frequency can be increased by reducing the values of the capacitance so that both motors can be spinning at the same time. This unfortunately effect of some components of the vibration may be partially cancelled out.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/643451436320761225.png"></p>
                                
                            </p>

                            <ul class="post-content section-instructions-list">
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">1</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><h2>Prepwork: - solder the unsolderable!?</h2><p>Solar Cell</p><p>The solar cell I have is from a very old calculator. It is tiny and was much older technology, so there isn&apos;t a whole of current from it. In the afternoon sun. I get about 3.2V open circuit and about 1.6mA short circuit current.<br>They used conductive tape to connect it to the PCB on the calculator. I had to improvise a bit. I used nickel paint and then solder to it. The nickel paint that I had put on was on is very old. I redid the connection via a short wire onto copper tape. It is not pretty, but the old connection still holds. I am not about to peel it off as it is bonded to the conductive metal deposited on the glass slide.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7503801436216751323.png"></p><p>Supercap</p><p>I disassembled a 5V supercap a few years ago. There are actually 2 coin sized (2F 2.5V) capacitors in series for the 5V 1F capacitor. Naturally I had to pull it apart. I was stupid enough to remove the spot welded tabs as I was going to use them as replacement lithium cells. Don&apos;t do what I did and just use the whole capacitor.  Mine is lighter, double the capacitance and half the ESR though.  :)</p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5206711436217086033.png"></span></p><p>I think the can must have been made out of stainless steel and my usual trick of grinding the surface before soldering doesn&apos;t work here.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4608271436217356550.png" width="432" height="351" style="width: 432px; height: 351px;"></p><p>So I did the next best thing - by taping a piece of copper tape onto the surface and soldering a piece of bare wire on one end. I then covered with a sludge of old nickel paint. After it dried a bit, I use the metal barrel of my soldering iron like a steam roller and flatten the nickel paint. The compression and heat packs the nickel particles together reduces the resistance. Anything that is less than a ohm is okay as we are talking about tens of mA current, so the voltage drops are minimal.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7159711436217535176.png"></p></div>
