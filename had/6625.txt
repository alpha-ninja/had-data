                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">I've wanted to build a photo booth for my daughter's birthday parties since we had her first one, and ever since the RasPi camera was released, I've thought it would be the perfect platform! After finally securing a Pi + camera (and now a Pi2), I'm now ready to make it happen!</div>

                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The 10,000ft view of the project is this: Using a python script running on a Raspberry Pi, take picture(s) and print them onto photo paper.</p><p>1-100ft view: I'm using a hacked together python script that uses the GPIO pins on the RasPi to read button presses and take pictures. Each button corresponds with a different 'style' of photo (one, 2x2, 4x1). This is then post-processed and sent to a Canon SELPY C910, which prints a 4x6 photo.</p><p>During the post processing, images are combined/ rotated where needed, and a watermark is added. A header/footer image is also added, to bring the total dimensions to a 3:2 aspect ratio (to match the photo paper.)<br></p>
                                
                            </p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6625/log/21697-project-log-3">Project Log 3</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">20 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6625/log/21697#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So this past weekend was my daughter&apos;s 5th birthday party, the reason for this project.</p><p><img style="width: 247px; height: 370px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/603411438106642181.jpg" height="370" width="247"></p><p>The photo booth was a big hit, but did have a few snags. First, the printer wasn&apos;t printing anything, so I tweaked the code ( I commented out the wait 2 minutes after the file is sent to the printer before you can take another picture. This keeps the printer from being overwhelmed.) That didn&apos;t fix the problem- but a reboot did. I didn&apos;t have time to re-enable the wait time, which led to  another problem- kids kept hitting the buttons right away to take another picture. The photos were captured just fine, but not every picture made it out of the printer successfully.</p><p>The front of the photo booth, seen below mounted to a re-purposed lemonade stand from a prior birthday, is just a plywood board wrapped in fabric. You can see the two lights in mason jars up top, triggered by a relay when photos are taken. The screen in the middle is just a 19&quot; TV. There are three arcade buttons on the bottom left for choosing photos. There is a hole in the center of the board for the camera.</p><p><img style="width: 217px; height: 386px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4222351438107498448.jpg" height="386" width="217"></p><p>I need to take a better picture of the back of the board, so I&apos;ll upload that in my next post.<br></p><p>My plans for improvements are:</p><ul><li>Add an audio cue when a picture has been taken (buzzer or audio file)</li><li>Make a better &apos;ready light&apos; for when you can take a picture</li><li>improve printing - I think lowering the printed resolution might improve the transmission speed to the printer</li><li>Power switch- right now on/off is handled by plugging/ unplugging from mains</li><li>Upload pictures to the web</li><li>GUI (using pygame?)</li></ul>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6625/log/21101-project-log-1">Project Log 1</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6625/log/21101#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>OK, so PL1- the log where I start talking about the Raspberry Pi, python code, and bread boarding. I should state now that despite python being <em>super</em> easy to work with and use, I did need lots of help getting to a working product. I mention those who helped below.</p><p>Physically, my build was initially (and still is, I suppose) a shameless copy of <a href="http://www.instructables.com/id/Raspberry-Pi-photo-booth-controller/?ALLSTEPS">Scott A. Fay&apos;s RPi photobooth</a> wiring setup. In addition to the bread board setup, knowing the <a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/B00HK8V4NC/">Canon SELPY</a> would work with CUPS was important, as the photo printer was the biggest expense in this project. Below is the version 1 of my replica, with only a single &apos;ready&apos; LED. (Ignore the microcontroller on the breadboard- it does nothing with this build.)</p><p><img style="width: 326px; height: 185px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2558881437172080622.jpg" height="185" width="326"></p><p>Now the photobooth setup has 3 buttons (one for each option of photo), along with a relay that turns on the two flash bulbs. I have funny story about my moving from bread board to protoboard, but I&apos;ll save that for project log 3. ;-)<br></p><p>For the code side of things, <a href="https://github.com/jcroucher/pi-photo-booth/blob/master/photobooth.py">John Croucher&apos;s photobooth.py</a> was the corner stone of my code. While I didn&apos;t end up using pygame (partly because I got tired of trying to figure it out), seeing how <a href="http://www.cups.org/">CUPS</a> and <a href="http://pythonware.com/products/pil/">PIL</a> should work  was really handy.</p><p>My pictures come out of the camera at 1920x1080 by default (because I&apos;m using the FMV preview, I assume? I don&apos;t really know why it&apos;s not 5MP stills- it works out though because it means less processing time is required for manipulation.) From there, I use PIL to rotate the photos if needed, then stitch them together on top of a &apos;background image&apos; that also contains the boarder splash images. These are used to bring the image ratio to 3:2, matching the 4:6 photo paper I&apos;m printing on.</p><p>I need to give credit to <a href="http://stackoverflow.com/questions/25592240/raspberry-pi-camera-auto-capture-python-script">Mad_man111 and ConcurrentHashMap</a> credit for figuring out how to take a series of photos.
