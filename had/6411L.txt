                            <h2><a href="/project/6411/log/23323-some-tests-and-results">Some tests and results</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">16 hours ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6411/log/23323#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23323"><p>As a part of my thesis on the <em>biohan</em><em>d, </em>I ran some relevant tests (mostly still with no control board, which came in a few days later). Some of these tests are presented briefly here. For more detail, <a href="http://martinvb.com/pydio/data/public/biohand-thesis.php">check my thesis!</a> :D</p><h2>Prehensions<br> </h2><p><a href="http://smpp.northwestern.edu/downloads/Design%20Of%20Artificial%20Arms%20And%20Hands%20For%20Prosthetic%20Applications.pdf">(Weir et al., 2003)</a> presents a minimal list of required hand <em>grips </em>(prehensions) to accomplish the majority of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living">Activities of Daily Living</a> (ADLs). This list encompasses a total of 7 different poses, shown below:</p><p><img style="width: 259px; height: 251px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1415681439815314295.jpg" height="251" width="259"></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span></p><p>They are:</p><ul><li><strong>a1) </strong>Tridigital palmar prehension (<em>tripod grip</em><span class="redactor-invisible-space">)</span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>a2) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Bidigital palmar prehension (<em>pinch</em><span class="redactor-invisible-space">)</span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>b) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Tip prehension </span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>c) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Lateral grip</span></span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>d) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Hook grip </span></span></span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>e) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Spherical grip</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>f) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">Cylindrical grip (<em>power grip</em><span class="redactor-invisible-space">)</span><br></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></li></ul><p>The image below shows the prototype&apos;s CAD model implementing this minimal set of prehensions (numbering is equivalent to the figure above): </p><p><img style="width: 660px; height: 338px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7679531439815611154.jpg" height="338" width="660"></p><p><br>With the 6 active <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom">Degrees of Freedom</a> of the <em>biohand, </em>the list could be achieved with no problems. Following the CAD proof of concept, the poses were tested on the prototype itself. The image below shows some of the tested grips:</p><p><img style="width: 672px; height: 384px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2154121439815728090.jpg" height="384" width="672"></p><p>Those are:</p><ul><li><strong>A) </strong>lateral grip</li><li><strong>B) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">bidigital palmar prehension</span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>C) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">cylindrical grip</span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>D) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">adduction grip: using the space/angle between adjacent fingers to hold thin objects.</span></span></span></li><li><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><strong>E) </strong><span class="redactor-invisible-space">bidigital palmar prehension</span></span></span></span></li></ul><h2>Load test</h2><p>It&apos;s a know fact that parts printed in an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fused_deposition_modeling">FDM</a> 3D-printer have   stronger bonds inside layers than between them (an interesting study shown <a href="http://www.sparxeng.com/blog/mechanical/3d-print-orientation-effect-part-strength">here</a>). All parts in the <em>biohand </em>take advantage of this fact: they are designed to be  printed aligned to the printing bed in such a way, that the main stress and load gets distributed along the layers. </p><p>For example, the image below shows the proximal phalanx of the index finger. The red lines show were the phalanx connects to the MCP (<em>metacarpophalangeal</em>) and PIP (<em>proximal interphalangeal</em>) joints, as well as the linear actuator that moves the finger. The part gets printed with the orientation shown in the figure:</p><p><img style="width: 346px; height: 208px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8717161439816685201.png" height="208" width="346"></p><p>This grants an increased robustness. No destructive tests were conducted, but the test below shows the <em>biohand </em>succesfully whitstanding a load of 40N with no signs of demage: </p><p><img style="width: 324px; height: 602px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1368301439816830781.jpg" height="602" width="324"></p><p>The test was carried out with no power supply, since the fingers employ a non-backdrivable mechanism. <br></p><h2>Speed, strenghts and weight</h2><p>Upper-limb prosthetics still have a hard time achieving speeds and strenghts comparable to those in human arms and hands. To contextualize the <em>biohand&apos;s</em> streght, speed and weight, it shall be compared to two of the market-leading devices: the <a href="http://www.touchbionics.com/products/active-prostheses/i-limb-ultra">i-limb</a> and the <a href="http://bebionic.com/">bebionic v2</a> (the previous version of the current <em>v3 </em>model). The data from these devices is based in the wok of <a href="http://www.rehab.research.va.gov/jour/2013/505/page599.html">(Belter et al., 2013):</a></p><p><img style="width: 446px; height: 337px;" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7572861439818853691.jpg" height="337" width="446"></p><p>The <em>biohand</em><em> </em>weights in at approx. 335g (planned with the control board and cosmetic cover). This represents 54% and 62% of the the commercial prosthetics. </p><p>The index&apos; angular speed, when measured at the MCP joint (at the base of the finger), reaches a peak of 56&#xB0;/s, which is 58% and 93% of the other two devices. </p><p>Lastly, an individual finger of the <em>biohand </em>is capable of exerting up to 6.3N*, which represents 43% and 83% of the commercial devices.</p><p>Considering that the <em>biohand </em>uses modified RC servos as linear actuators for each finger, I can&apos;t say I&apos;m not pleased with the results. Four fingers working simultaneously can exert more than 36N in a prehension. This is sufficient for the majority of lighter ADLs, as stated in <a href="http://www.rehab.research.va.gov/jour/04/41/2/Smaby.html">(Smaby et al., 2004)</a>.</p><p> The reduction in weight is also very relevant. The work by <a href="http://www.researchgate.net/publication/5798071_Results_of_an_Internet_survey_of_myoelectric_prosthetic_hand_users">(Pylatiuk et al., 2007)</a> shows that excessive weight is a complaint of most amputees carrying some type of prosthetic device. </p><p>* The force test used only 60% of the <em>biohand&apos;s </em>nominal tension, to avoid motor demage during stall (for the maximum-force tests). </p><h2>Cost</h2><p>Lastly, one of the great thriumphs of the <em>biohand </em>is, in my opinion, its very low cost. With the prototype almost completed, the cost table can be calculated as follows: </p><table><tbody><tr><td>Item</td><td>Cost per unit (USD)</td><td>Quantity</td><td>Subtotal (USD)</td></tr><tr><td>Standard RC servo</td><td>12,95</td><td>5</td><td>64,75</td></tr><tr><td>Micro RC servo</td><td>9,95</td><td>1</td><td>9,95</td></tr><tr><td>Black ABS plastic (filament)</td><td>19,19 (kg)</td><td>155g</td><td>2,97</td></tr><tr><td>Other hardware costs (e.g., screws, glue)</td><td>-</td><td>-</td><td>5,00</td></tr><tr><td>STM32F103 processor</td><td>7,14</td><td>1</td><td>7,14</td></tr><tr><td>DRV8801 motor driver</td><td>3,15</td><td>5</td><td>15,75</td></tr><tr><td>Overall electronic costs (e.g., manufacturing) </td><td>-</td><td>-</td><td>15,00</td></tr><tr><td>2S / 1000mAh LiPo battery</td><td>11,49</td><td>1</td><td>11,49</td></tr><tr><td></td><td></td><td>Total:</td><td>132,05</td></tr></tbody></table><p>Prices were checked in Amazon for the LiPo battery, in <a href="http://www.oshpark.com">OSHPark</a> for the board costs and in <a href="http://www.3ders.org/pricecompare/">3ders</a> for the average spool price.</p><p>With a total price of approximately USD 132,05, the <em>biohand </em>costs 200 times less than the average commercial myoelectric/robotic prosthesis. <br></p><br><a href="http://www.rehab.research.va.gov/jour/2013/505/page599.html"></a><h2>Bibliography</h2><p>Weir, R.                             <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-23467"><p>As mentioned before, I&apos;ve spent the last month mostly <a href="http://martinvb.com/pydio/data/public/biohand-thesis.php">working on my thesis</a> (about <em>biohand</em>). Now that I&apos;ve finished it, I finally have some spare time to invest in the project again. I tought I&apos;d briefly list some of the &apos;To-Do&apos;s I have planned for the upcoming two/three weeks:</p><ul><li>In the previous logs I&apos;ve mentioned that the custom control PCBs had arrived from the board house. All wiring (motors, servo and potentiometers) will be hooked up to the control board.</li><li>As clearly visible in the pictures, there&apos;s still no cosmetic covering. The motors and internals are visible and unprotected. A proper cover will then be designed/printed to enclose the <em>biohand&apos;s </em><span class="redactor-invisible-space">palm. </span></li></ul><p>Other modifications are planned in the long-term future too; the thumb mechanism will be fully replaced and the control board will undergo a large revision. However, these more significant changes will only take place after the aformentioned short-term goals are properly consolidated. <br></p></div>
