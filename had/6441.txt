                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description"> I have been making these solenoid engines since the seventies. I have made them out of every material imaginable, and decided to try and make a fully printable one despite the fact I have no printer or know how to make STL files. This will be an open source fun learning project for all ages. It can power vehicles as shown in the video. Print a set of parts, use some common hardware and build your dream vehicle, machine or just learn about how things work!<br /><br /> </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>3D printed solenoid engine. R.E.M.(reciprocating electric motor)</p><p> The engine is a fully self contained machine that can be easily built for propulsion of scale robots, vehicles and experimentation. I am choosing to convert to 3D printing as this will allow for worldwide distribution and file sharing allowing everyone access to the plans to build one themselves and make use of it as a learning tool, and locomotion of their own creative project. It runs on 4 AA batteries making it safe, simple and easy to implement.</p><p><br> I finally am getting this done! I had been making these from acrylic parts for some time in this configuration, but it was a very labor intensive effort-Cutting, gluing, painting parts. And everything of course has be well aligned and fitted.<br> I found a guy who helped me not only make the STL files(which involved lots of napkin drawings and a lot of mindreading on his part!) but also printed all the parts at a very reasonable cost.<br> He is set up for ABS so this engine is all ABS and some hardware plus the solenoid. Goes together quick, works great, all self contained(battery compartment in the base 4 AAs ).<br> Some of the parts still need some fine tuning, but I am very happy with the outcome. I would love to find somebody to print me some parts in PLA to test them(and even help me make more spare parts in ABS), but my budget is pretty well shot for the rest of the year.<br> I have already tested it in a small RC car(well not that small) that I made but powered some other stuff. Very durable and reliable.<br> I still have to make a hardware/extra parts list but otherwise this thing is pretty much documented as well.</p><p>Here is a picture at the heart of the engine, the tubular solenoid itself:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6806771435942079586.jpg">This solenoid at 12 volts produces 16 pounds of pull! But at 6 volts, much less of course. It is available from several sources including Ebay for about 4$ to about 10$, depending on how careful a shopper you are. It is the most expensive part of the engine.<br> I included a photo of the acrylic version so in case you missed my posts on the original design you can see both. <br> Making some rear end gear housings and steering mechanisms as well that turn this into a RC car construction model that's really built from the ground up.<br> I have  PCBs coming to replace the breadboard version on the top.<br>A lot of work, but a dream come true. This is my first project in 3D printing use.</p><p>Here is a picture of the acrylic version I based the printed version on:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9346821435281074014.JPG"></p><p>here is a video of the engine in operation.</p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/GsAfOOQMLrc" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen="" style="width: 500px; height: 281px;"></iframe>
                                
                            </p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6441/log/20490-the-vehicle-interface">The vehicle interface</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6441/log/20490#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The finished engine can be mounted in a variety of ways for driving wheels. This vehicle which I call the crane is actually more of a robotic forklift than a crane, but is important to show the way the engine is mounted on a chassis, connected to a drivetrain, and ready to roll:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1039861436136019427.jpg">This ,again showing the acrylic engine(pre-3D printed) is mounted to a simple flat chassis that supports engine, rear end and steering.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7207441436136101909.jpg">As can be seen, a series of belts and pulleys transfer torque from the engine down to the rear end. These belts are actually O-Rings which work fantastic as drive belts.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8264891436136206010.jpg">Another view of the vehicle. Electronics package is mounted under the engine which processes sensor data, controls engine RPMs, and steers away from obstacles.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8183821436136297854.jpg">This is what the rear end gearing system looks like before finishing. This component can and will also be 3 D printed soon. It uses worm gear set to optimize available torque, and prevents "rollback" when the engine/vehicle is not running. Note the photointerupter module and wheel.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6441/log/20458-a-reconfigurable-transmission">A reconfigurable transmission</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6441/log/20458#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Building this engine can be accomplished for a wide range of vehicle types. For cars, maximum torque at lower speeds is best, but for a boat perhaps less torque and higher speed.</p><p> This is easily accomplished by the gearset in the transmission. Here are some pictures showing a typical transmission gearset for maximum torque:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2450611436067566790.jpg">Two of the three gears used for this configuration are shown-The one on the flywheel shaft is 14 teeth and always remains so. The second interface gear, a compound one can be increased in tooth count to increase/decrease speeds/torque.</p><p>Here is the third gear interfaces with the second:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3150311436067706989.jpg">The final gear is attached to a hub which fixes it to the output shaft. From there it is belted down to the main output shaft.</p><p> The small gears are available from Ebay for very low cost. You can buy 60 of the in an assortment for a few dollars, and virtually select any speed/torque output desired. That versatility greatly enhanced the engines appeal for multiple platforms and vehicle applications.</p><p>These photos show how gearsets are configured and assembled before installation on the transmission plate:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7986031436068011663.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5951461436068021465.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5160591436068033058.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8350821436068043050.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7998301436068050465.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8459161436068057272.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8017261436068063779.jpg"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8108781436068070377.jpg">The final photo shows the final gear attached to a hub and shaft. The screw on the opposite end holds the gear against the transmission plate.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/6441/log/20454-automated-test-jig-for-the-engines">Automated test jig for the engines</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6441/log/20454#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>This is an automated test jig to measure engine performance:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8604861436061736039.jpg">Sensors are mounted at various points of the engines, buffered on a protoboard, and ran into microcontroller ports.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3547181436061808922.jpg">A servo is mounted on the engine to move the throttle through the full speed range and the microcontroller runs various test programs to datalog results throughout the full speed range.</p><p>The pictured show the acrylic engine version-I have yet to begin performance testing on the fully printed model.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8863711436061959070.jpg">Noise, vibration Flywheel RPM, transmission output RPM, driver temp, input voltage, and current demand are some of the data being collected.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
