Inside nearly every li-ion battery is a Battery Management System (BMS). We&#39;re turning protection into control by adding communication between the BMS and your phone.

Our BMS is called Evivar. Evivar lets the user:
- Set the wanted charge level (this dramatically increases lifetime)
- Document battery history
- See the current status of the battey (State of Charge, Temperature, Power etc)
- Secure the battery

                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description"> A li-ion cell is very powerful, but at the same time fragile if used improperly. Subject to overdischarge, overcharge and abuse from temperature - a Battery Management System (BMS) protects against these events. Inside nearly every li-ion battery is a Battery Management System (BMS). We're turning protection into control by adding communication between the BMS and your phone.<br /><br />Our BMS is called Evivar. Evivar lets the user:<br />- Set the wanted charge level (this dramatically increases lifetime)<br />- Document battery history<br />- See the current status of the battey (State of Charge, Temperature, Power etc)<br />- Secure the battery<br /><br />Getting the most out of Li-ion batteries is not only an engineering challenge. Perfect usage depends on a knowledgeable user. The solution is not only technical, but a combination of technology and teaching users how to make good decisions. <br /><br />In the process of striving after the lofty goals - we'll create some very nice hacker hardware that is open and usable.</div>

                            <p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/r6L80RRpv9Y" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><p><iframe width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/16v3KEWCdIE" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe>Hello everyone,</p><p><strong>What problem are we solving?</strong></p><p>If you buy an electric bike today, it comes with 2-year warranty on its battery. If you buy an electric car, it comes with 8-year. What is the difference between these batteries? Chemically and mechanically, not much - but usage is vastly different.</p><p>Never charging above 90% of safe charge level is the first trick electric cars use. By charging to 90% instead of 100% - longevity can effectively be doubled. 80% - quadruple the life. Diminishing returns kick in around ~58% (<a href="http://batteryuniversity.com/learn/article/how_to_prolong_lithium_based_batteries">See table 3 here</a>).</p><p>Furthermore, li-ion batteries sitting at full SOC quickly degrade. Ebike mfgs practically recommend always keeping the battery topped up. Our goal is to enable batteries with long warranties and to stop this consumption of batteries.</p><p>Our problem statement: Batteries do not live as long as they can. <br></p><p><strong>How do we solve this problem?</strong></p><p>We improve the Battery Management System by adding IoT to it. By connecting the battery to a normal mobile phone, the user can make intelligent choices to lengthen the life of the battery. Protection are turned into control. Most importantly, adjusting the charge threshold of the battery can have a huge impact on longevity. Evivar, our BMS, lets the user set this.</p><p><strong>Where are we now?</strong></p><p>We have developed a new BMS from the ground up, Design for Manufacturing (DFM) style. All the components are selected and we are well underway with coding.</p><p>Right now, the BMS handles 9 - 15 Cells and stream data over Bluetooth Classic (2.1). The idle power consumption is below the self-discharge rate typical to li-ion (1-2 % month). As for safety, all the protection features you might want is implemented.</p><p><strong>Where do we want to go?</strong></p><p>Our focus is small batteries (and especially electric bikes) but our design can be applied to many other applications - each requiring small modifications. To mutual gain for us and others - we want to Open Source our hardware and software.</p><p><strong>What does this mean for the average hacker?</strong></p><p>Our hardware can be used everywhere li-ion batteries are used. Some effort will be required for novel applications, but our building block can be used for EV&apos;s, solar installations, robotics and every conceivable portable product.</p><p><strong>Evivar BMS meets the need for better Battery Management</strong></p><p>Inside a li-ion battery you will find cells (that store energy) and a BMS. The BMS protects the cells from abuse and dangerous conditions. A common BMS:</p><ul><li>Measure all cell voltages, and equalize (balance) these to the same level</li><li>Measure battery temperature, and stop discharge and charge if out of spec</li><li>Measure battery current, to track remaining capacity and detect dangerous current levels</li></ul><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5293771437980495616.png"></p><p><strong>Specifications</strong></p><p>The main spesifications of Evivar BMS is:</p><ul><li>Designed for Ebikes up to 1500 W.</li><li>Supports up to 15 cells. With 3,6V per cell, that is 54V</li><li>Protects against overcharge and overdischarge</li><li>Designed for 30A continuous discharge</li><li>Has a separate 3V3 SMPS regulator, able to supply 50mA</li><li>Measures battery current and protects against short circuit and high-currents</li><li>Measures 2x battery temperatures and BMS temperature (MOSFETS and TCCS)</li><li>Communicates through UART</li><li>Is normally fitted with a Bluetooth RF module</li><li>Stores aggregate data about how the battery has been used</li></ul><p>Number of cells is normally referred to as &quot;S&quot;. 13S and 14S batteries are called 48V. 10S battery, 36V. For now, we have used the biggest TI AFE, in the future we will shrink Evivar to perfectly accomodate 5S and 10S batteries.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7393601437986185151.png"></p><p>In the chart above the main components of Evivar is shown. From the battery comes 2 high current leads and n+1 cell-voltage sense wires. A protection module, containing current sensing and MOSFETs, control charge and discharge. The AFE has a pure digital interface to the MCU and supplies 3V3 through an internal linear regulator. </p><p>A controllable 3V3 RF regulator is included. This is driven directly from the battery and can deliver 50mA. This way the HC-06 can be put to sleep when it is not needed or the battery has low SOC.</p><p><strong>App-development</strong></p><p>The hardware needs good software...</p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6913/log/23062-workings-of-the-firmware">Workings of the firmware</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6913/log/23062#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>At this stage, the firmware running on the Atmel SAM20 is built as follows.</p><p><img style="display: block; margin: auto;" alt="State machine, Evivar software overview" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6757551439730371226.png">The firmware is modelled like a semi state machine. There are three states of the firmware, derived by the amount of capacity left on the battery pack. These states are Active, Batt Low and Ship Mode. As of now, X is 2.9V, Z is 2.75V and X+Y is ~3.15V. Between the states Active and Low Batt there are transition states, turning things on and off for the next state.</p><p>In Ship Mode, nothing really happens. This is the mode that BMSs are often stored and shipped in, to avoid uneccecary discharge of the battery cells. In this state, everything is disabled, and the Evivar board will draw only a few micro amperes. We&apos;re making a battery management system, not a battery murdering system ;) To boot the BMS from the Ship state, a voltage above 1V must be applied to TS1 of the BQ769x0. Do this on Evivar by pressing the SW0 button on the board. The BQ will power on it&apos;s internal 3.3V regulator which in turn will make the SAMD20 boot. </p><p><img style="width: 386px; height: 896px; display: block; margin: auto;" alt="The Active state simplified" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2724901439732652293.png" height="896" width="386"></p><p>Active is the state where the battery is operative and can be discharged. Above is a rough diagram of what&apos;s going on. Here the microcontroller is doing regular checks of the battery cells and pack, logging it&apos;s condition (in the future), acting on conditions and faults and communicating with an external device using bluetooth. </p><p><img style="width: 536px; height: 318px; display: block; margin: auto;" alt="Interrupts in the Active state" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2972621439738427263.png" height="318" width="536"></p><p>There are currently three interrupts enabled in Active. They are kept as short as possible, and will set flags that the main loop as shown above will act on. <br></p><p><img style="width: 614px; height: 869px; display: block; margin: auto;" alt="The Low batt state simplified" src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/271191439738288917.png" height="869" width="614"></p><p>The Low batt state will disable bluetooth, disable the discharge mosfet,  turn off the Coulumb counter in the BQ and enter the device into sleep in order to conserve current. A timer wil awaken the micro controller at regular intervals to do some checks (and in the future store the battery condition on flash).</p><p>The details of the code should hopefully be apparent upon reading it. All variables and function names are given pretty descriptive names. Source code will be out in a bit.</p><p>We defined following as the Minimum Viable Product (MVP):</p><ul><li>Monitor battery cells </li><li>Prevent over discharge / charge currents</li><li>Prevent over charging</li><li>Prevent over discharging</li><li>Send real time data to a mobile device using bluetooth</li><li>Enable / disable discharge from the mobile device</li></ul><p>Our prototype does nearly all that. Some tweaks must be done in order to calculate current correctly, but apart from that, it seems to be performing OK. The BMS is yet to be tested outside a lab environment, but extensive tests with a battery pack are planned in the coming period. <br></p><p>Some important things remaining software wise, among them are</p><ul><li>Logging battery data on flash memory using simulated EEPROM</li><li>Enable balancing during trickle charging</li><li>Calculate total amount of cycles</li><li>Error handling</li><li>Extensive testing and debugging</li><li>Save power by enabling the bluetooth in intervals, possibly by pressing external button</li><li>Open battery parameters from file</li><li>Bullet proof communication between external device</li></ul>
                                
                            </p>
