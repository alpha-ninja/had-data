                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">This cute little robot is based on parts that anyone that tinkered with development platforms and electronics is going to be accustomed to. That is why this project is aimed at people who want to learn more about hardware but also at programmers that just want something cheap to run their code on. Imagine having not one but TWO complete robots to experiment with at the price of one Arduino platform ! <br />The cheap factor makes this design suitable for experimenting with swarm robotics. It's easy to build with easy to find, off-the shelf parts or easy to mass-produce. It could be sold in kits or preassembled. Due to the open source and open hardware nature of this project, you or anyone else can copy the design and build one at home or integrate it in a industrial design, free of charge and without restrictions.<br />Hardware stuff :<br />- Atmega328P-PU Microcontroller <br />- L293D H-bridge<br />- NRF24L01+ Communications module<br />- Chinese DC motors with gears<br />Pretty standard, right? </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/2363/log/10171-home-etching">Home etching</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">11 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2363/log/10171#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>For the first prototype, I manually etched the design on plain cooper-clad boards.&nbsp;</p><p>Home etching is something like old arcade games : Easy to pick up, but hard to master.</p><p>Since I<span>only had plain copper boards, I chose the toner transfer method.</span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5272631412269527745.jpg"></p><p>There are many guides on the net, but, unless you have the exact same materials, something is not going to work like you expect it to.&nbsp;</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3607361412269681511.jpg"></p><p>This usually ends in a few days of trial and error. After spending way more time than intended on wasting toner and a lot of paper, my magic combination came to :</p><p>- cheap, printer foto paper (waaaay better than glossy magazine paper.</p><p>-laser printer</p><p>-secret ingedient: A heat press</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6501591412269801827.jpg" alt="" style="display: block; margin: auto;"></p><p>Usually, the toner is actually transfered to the copper when pressure and heat is applied to the paper. This is done with a household iron. However, this method is sloppy and results in poor quality.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/473271412270158039.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5987291412270202417.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5760921412270268345.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9055041412270317167.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3450301412270346570.jpg"></p><p>I solved this problem by chance. When I was checking out a shop that had a CNC, a heated press caught my eye. Since the CNC did not work, we borrowed the heat&nbsp;press and tried that instead of ironing the board. This yelded much better results because of the constant and even pressure. Usually 5 minutes in the press and the toner transfers 99%. &nbsp;</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5451321412270024403.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/50011412270053833.jpg"></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/2363/log/8264-teh-brainzzz">Teh brainzzz</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a year ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/2363/log/8264#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The ATMEL Atmega328 was chosen for the good price, wide availability and</p><p>wide community support. This chip is also used in the Arduino UNO</p><p>development platform that is usually the starter's platform when it comes to</p><p>embedded programming. Using the same chip should ensure a smooth</p><p>transition of newbies and experienced users alike.</p><p>The microcontroller features 21 GPIO pins divided in three ports :</p><p>Port B (PB7:0)</p><p>This port is reserved for the NRF24L01 communications module due to the</p><p>hardware SPI pins. An ICSP connector is also wired on the respective MISO,MOSI,SKC</p><p>and RESET pins</p><p>The clock circuitry is also on this port.</p><p>PB0 drives a on­board LED.</p><p>Port C (PB5:0)</p><p>This port is used as an analog input on the arduino board so we just used</p><p>an 6 pin connector to provide easy access for adding extra sensors and</p><p>inputs.</p><p>Port D (Pd7:0)</p><p>Port D drives the L293D H­bridge, and the enable pins on the L293D are</p><p>connected to PWM capable pins, (PD5 and PD6) to enable individual speed</p><p>control for each motor.</p><p>Also,the RX/TX pins (PD0 and PD1) are reserved for the user and extended</p><p>to an 3 pin connector that also contains the reset pin (PC6).</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>

It's easy to build with easy to find, off­ the shelf parts or easy to</p><p>mass­produce. It could be sold in kits or preassembled. Due to the open</p><p>source and open hardware nature of this project, you or anyone else can</p><p>copy the design and build one at home or integrate it in a industrial design,</p><p>free of charge and without restrictions.</p>
