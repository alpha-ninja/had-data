                            <h2><a href="/project/6811/log/21285-beginnings">Beginnings...</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a month ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6811/log/21285#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21285"><p>At <a href="http://cmn.rgn.hr/">Croatian Meteor Network</a>, we have always strived for a meteor station to be as cheap as possible. By doing so, network quickly grew to over 20 stations. Having multiple stations covering same volume of Earth&#x2019;s atmosphere allows precise orbit calculation. Over the years tedious task of manually examining and processing each image was replaced by automated software. Thus, focus has changed from data collection to data analysis. This has led to more than 100 new meteor shower discoveries.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6361141437511886612.png" width="554" height="631" style="width: 554px; height: 631px; display: block; margin: auto;" alt="Triangulation"></p><p> But, there has never been complete satisfaction with capture hardware nor software. Current software solutions are often <a href="http://sonotaco.com/soft/e_index.html">expensive</a> and/or supporting <a href="http://www.metrec.org/">only specific hardware</a>. Most of the software is written for Windows machines, with few <a href="http://meteor.uwo.ca/~weryk/asgard/">exceptions</a>. <a href="http://fripon.github.io/freeture/">Almost</a> certainly, software is <a href="http://cams.seti.org/">closed-source</a>, no matter how advanced.</p> Currently, if you would like to operate your own meteor station, you&#x2019;ll most likely end up buying expensive cameras (~$400 per camera) and capture hardware (~$200), as well as a powerful enough PC for running obsolete software. We decided to tackle this issue by developing a flexible and open-source software solution based on 21st century methodologies. Recent advances in field of low-cost single-board computers offer us a replacement for expensive desktop machines without compromising real-time processing capabilities. Also, new generation of low-cost CCTV cameras have the same sensitivity as one order of magnitude more expensive branded cameras. While current solutions could cost you several thousands of US dollars we aim for complete video meteor station for under 100 bucks.</div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6811/log/21863-to-sbc-or-not-to-sbc">To SBC, or not to SBC...</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">19 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6811/log/21863#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21863"><p><span></span>&#x2026; that never was the question. Rather, the question was which SBC shall it be?</p><p> During the last year we have tested quite a few Single Board Computers, as our project has specific requirements. During that time, SBCs have evolved enough to satisfy our needs. In this post, we&#x2019;ll try to chronologically document our experiences with different boards.</p>
<p><strong>Raspberry Pi B</strong></p><a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/products/model-b/" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5232611438268661181.png" width="297" height="145" alt="" style="width: 297px; height: 145px; float: left; margin: 0px 10px 10px 0px;"></a><p>Being the most popular SBC at the time, it was obvious we had to test it. Unfortunately, RPi still had <a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/forums/viewtopic.php?f=28&amp;t=12097">serious USB issues</a> and fairly limited computing power (700MHz single core CPU, 512MiB RAM). With that hardware, we couldn&#x2019;t reliably capture video with USB frame grabber device nor perform real-time compression. Although there is powerful GPU present (<a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/help/faqs/#performanceSpeed">24 GFLOPs</a>), there was no way to utilize it.</p>
<p><strong>Banana Pi</strong></p><p><a href="http://www.bananapi.org/p/product.html" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2827411438268277586.png" alt="" width="263" height="164" style="float: right; width: 263px; height: 164px;"></a></p><p> Shortly afterwards, Banana Pi came out. The same price range as RPi B, but with a 1GHz dual core CPU and 1GiB RAM. Also, there was a SATA port for HDD. We thought that it will surely be able to do capture on one core and compression on the other. If we could just get that USB frame grabber to work&#x2026;</p>
<p><strong>Raspberry Pi 2</strong></p><a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/products/raspberry-pi-2-model-b/" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1233281438268710982.png" alt="" width="253" height="131" style="float: left; width: 253px; height: 131px; margin: 0px 10px 10px 0px;"></a><p>After some time, RPi2 came out. With 900MHz quad core CPU, 1GiB RAM and most importantly a strong community, it caught our attention. This time, we tested it with different USB capture hardware, finally finding an acceptable solution. At that moment software development started and software is growing rapidly ever since. Not only that we had to settle on the capture and compression solution, which would still require us to do detection on a separate machine, but it was possible to completely match capabilities of desktop machines with previous software solutions. Furthermore, we still had enough processing power left to implement new features.</p><p> Same powerful GPU as on RPi B (VideoCore IV) was still used on new board. Turns out that it it&#x2019;s possible <a href="https://www.raspberrypi.org/blog/accelerating-fourier-transforms-using-the-gpu/">to do some</a> general purpose computations on it, certainly it&#x2019;s not an easy task. Raspberry Pi Trading: please include OpenCL in next generation RPi! :)</p>
<p><strong>Odroid C1</strong></p><a href="http://www.hardkernel.com/main/products/prdt_info.php?g_code=G141578608433" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/543361438268861520.png" alt="" width="280" height="214" style="float: right; width: 280px; height: 214px; margin: 0px 0px 10px 10px;"></a><p>Although we were already deep into development with RPi2, we still wanted to test other available hardware. With same price of $35, but 1.5GHz CPU, Odroid C1 definitely was worth a shot. Amount of available RAM is equal as in RPi2 (1 GiB), but divided across two chips.<br></p><p> Surprisingly, our compression timing tests showed that it&#x2019;s actually slower than RPi2 (we&#x2019;re running RPi2 @ 1GHz). We guess that this is caused by different core (A5 versus A7) or RAM frequency. Another issue is that the very moment when RAM fills up over 512 MiB, it suddenly crashed. The problem here is most certainly in 2 RAM chips and bad memory management between them.</p>
<p><strong>Orange Pi Mini 2</strong></p><a href="http://www.orangepi.org/orangepimini2/index.html" target="_blank"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8930201438269353251.png" alt="" width="252" height="156" style="float: left; width: 252px; height: 156px; margin: 0px 10px 10px 0px;"></a><p>$25 for a quad-core Cortex A7 @ 1.6GHz CPU?!? We had to try it. It crashed as soon as we started our timing tests and CPU core went to 100%. Turns out that placing your finger on SOC is same as touching a hot oven. Coolers didn&#x2019;t help. We didn&#x2019;t found a way to limit the CPU freq. We&#x2019;ll post an update when we get our hands on liquid nitrogen&#x2026;</p><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><strong><br></strong></p><p><span></span><strong>Conclusion</strong></p><p>We decided to use Raspberry Pi 2 as the heart of our station as it ticks every single box on our requirements list. Our software will perform faster on next-gen SBCs, but for now we&#x2019;ll stick with RPi2.</p><p><a href="http://linux-sunxi.org/">Linux-suxi</a> project seems promising, but the <a href="http://linux-sunxi.org/Buying_guide">list of officially supported hardware</a> is quite small, with devices mostly having a higher price and lower processing power. Banana Pi M2 and Banana Pi M3 might be interesting with OpenCL, but community support is nowhere close to the RPi.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6811/log/21992-pain-with-meteor-networks">Pain with meteor networks</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">17 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6811/log/21992#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-21992"><p>Having a $100 meteor station in our hands (check out our <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/6811-asteria-network/log/21285-beginnings">first log</a>), we realized there is a broader issue with meteor astronomy that could be solved. Currently, there are many separate meteor networks, often on national levels. Each one uses different combinations of software, data reduction routines and storage format. Paradoxically, data is often shared by amateur networks, while government funded (with public money!) networks keep their data as a hidden treasure.</p><p><img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/fMPNYIKLaTZl9wnwu-wLDpSRNQudep0TcyyBN1QPBv2fKALvSauQ_eX49ww7UM79zKfaSONZ-hULKtaYnO_Y7sZGBLH5PBI6abII4MuJMPm_H6neVCUHJr5AaNAElU6Y0TSS-BU"></p><blockquote>Meteor radiants plotted on a map of the whole sky (SonotaCo and CMN data). Meteor showers are represented by high-density areas of the same color (Perseids are the orange blob at the upper-center).<br></blockquote><p> Regardless of what we humans think, universe doesn&#x2019;t care about national borders. If we were to set out to understand meteors, and through them our Solar System, these limitations cannot get in our way. A truly global network is needed. Such large network raises problems that can only be solved with community effort and open solutions. We are strong proponents of open data, which is still a taboo topic in meteor science.</p><p>As the goal price of a single system is very affordable, we hope that many amateur astronomers, hackers, citizen scientists and students will be interested in the project. The system is envisioned to be very simple to install, use and maintain, with minimal knowledge of astronomy and computer use. We aim to expand that knowledge and introduce people to science and show them the importance of meteor astronomy and foremost - programming.</p><p>We want to provide every high school or university that is interested one or more of these systems, and form a student science team which will work on data collection and processing. As the data will be shared via a central data hub, groups will be encouraged to cooperate with other participants on the project.</p>On the other side, many amateur astronomers buy specialized equipment, expensive all-sky cameras and record meteors for their own satisfaction. We are here to offer a low-priced solution with free software which will enable them to fulfill their satisfactions and yet provide useful data to the community.</div>
                            </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/6811/log/22167-compression">Compression</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">13 days ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/6811/log/22167#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-22167"><p>Now
that the question of capturing the data with the RPi2 is resolved, a
question of data compression arises. If you were to save uncompressed
video on a disk using the current setup (720x576 @ 25FPS), during an
average night of 12 hours you would end up with &gt;400GB of raw
data.</p><p>
So
it is clear that some kind of compression should be used. Usual lossy
video compression methods are trying to reduce redundancy in video
data to suit human vision. Regarding video meteors, the problem is
more complex - you need to do your best to preserve meteor features
(spatial and temporal resolution and pixel intensity) so you can do
meteor detection later.</p><p>
Usual
video meteor setups have wide-angle lenses and relatively small
resolution cameras. Our setup has a 64&#xB0;x48&#xB0; field of view and
720x576 camera resolution, which gives a plate constant of about 5
arc minutes per pixel. If you consider that an average meteor is at
about 100km distance, one pixel corresponds to ~150m at that
distance. This means that the real position of the meteor is actually
quite uncertain and you shouldn&#x2019;t compromise the spatial resolution
any further.</p><p>The
method we use was first developed by Mark Vornhusen for his Skypatrol
software, and later extended by Peter Gural for use with <a href="http://www.imo.net/imc2010/talks/Gural.pdf">NASA
CAMS meteor network</a>.
In essence, you are taking 256 raw video frames (10.24s at 25FPS) and
saving only the brightest pixels as you can assume the meteor was the
brightest event in that portion of time. In addition, you track when
the brightest pixel occurred (coded as an 0-255 intensity which
corresponds to the frame number), the average pixel values and their
standard deviation.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8046381438785716536.png"></p><blockquote>compressed image of a fireball</blockquote><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5643931438785404065.gif"></p><blockquote>reconstructed video from compressed image</blockquote><p>Thus you have reduced 256 frames to just 4 images (1:64 compression ratio) which you can use for meteor detection as the standard deviation can help you determine what is a meteor and what is the background.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7992071438785747144.png"></p><blockquote>tresholding (used for detection)</blockquote></div>
                            </div>
