                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">The project aims at finding an alternative way of capturing 2D projections of light fields at either a) high frame rates, b) high resolution, c) or with incredible low-light performance using (in true hacker fashion) as many off-the-shelf components as possible. </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Multiple approaches will be attempted in order to achieve the desired purpose. The first approach will involve a Linear Sensor Array (LSA) capturing light in a radial pattern around a centre and then interpolating the data to obtain an image we can display on a regular rectangular screen. </p><p>The second approach will also utilise an LSA, but rather than spinning it, we will move it back and forth on a set of rails. More details to come.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7324/log/23629-polar-camera-prototype-improvements">Polar Camera Prototype Improvements</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">9 hours ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7324/log/23629#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>There are a number of improvements that can be done, and here I will list the main ones I will be focusing on in the next few weeks. </p><p>1. Removing the slip rings completely as they produce noise and do not work well at high rpm as well as produce vibrations. Instead replace them with induction coils (this is also known as a rotary transformer) and thus wirelessly transmit the information from the camera to the processor. The power will remain on a slip ring, however this will be simplistic and unrestrictive. </p><p> 2. Modifying the slip ring so that instead of wires it uses copper spring loaded pads which dynamically adjust the pressure on the slip ring minimising friction and thus motor current required reducing the vibration.</p><p>3. Modify the entire build to no longer move the sensor circulary, but linearly thus removing the blind spot in the centre of the circle. This allows us to also operate without sliprings as well as require no interpolation, but also results in a rectangular image. However oscillatory vibrations will need to be accounted for at the resonant frequencies.<span class="redactor-invisible-space"><br></span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space"><br></span></p><p><span class="redactor-invisible-space">This project log will be updated with more info as soon as it is available.</span></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7324/log/23628-polar-camera-prototype-1">Polar Camera Prototype #1</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">9 hours ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7324/log/23628#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The initial prototype uses an STM32F072 Discovery board, a stepper motor, a 3D printed, 5 contact slip ring with a bore down the middle and the LSA. The prototype works so-and-so; the excessive vibrations and unreliable slip ring operation make the obtained results hard to reproduce. We send the data from the STM32 to the computer using a simple UART transceiver and implement a serial interface in MATLAB to capture the results. I will have full technical details of the setup including scope waveforms, etc.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8582541439859024808.jpg"></p><p>Every column in the image below represents a scanned line at a single step of a 200-step stepper motor. The sensor has 128 pixels, thus the &apos;image&apos; has 200x128 pixels. The line at the bottom is a speck of dust on the sensor. </p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9710201439845705471.jpg"></p><p>This is the magic part. If we run this image through the MATLAB scattered interpolation sequence described in Project Log 2 we obtain the image below. Note that the fisheye effect is more due to the stock lens used on the sensor rather than the algorithm itself.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1307221439845965922.jpg"></p><p>With a conventional DSLR camera, this is the image taken from *approximately* the same location, but with a much less wide angle:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1568171439846332396.JPG"></p><p>Now let&apos;s take a look at the same picture captured again, after the motor has been dormant for a while and the motor current slightly increased so it doesn&apos;t occasionally stall.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8028611439858578701.jpg"></p><p>The vibrations are very apparent from the periodic nature of the distortions.  The uncleaned dust particle on the bottom half of the image confirms that vibrations are indeed the culprit since it&apos;s jumping from pixel to pixel for different angles. Furthermore there are some &apos;blanking&apos; lines where either the slip-ring contact was imperfect or the vibrations caused bad things to happen.</p><p>Studying this image in closer detail, we discover that some areas have more vibration induced distortions than others which leads us to believe that the centre of vibrations is not centred. </p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7324/log/23619-polar-camera-simulations">Polar Camera Simulations</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">9 hours ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7324/log/23619#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The next natural step is to simulate the concept somehow. I chose to do this in MATLAB, and while it is neither open-source nor free (even though I, as a student, have free, unrestricted access to it from my university) you can likely do everything I did in MATLAB in GNU Octave, which is open-source. </p><p>The MATLAB simulation takes an input image and creates a scattered data interpolant of it. It then samples this image at non-discrete points (using the interpolant) that correspond to the radial sampling pattern of the sensor. It can also simulate sensor concentricity mismatch, the formula for which can be derived using Pythagoras&apos; theorem (I&apos;ll have more detail on this soon). The radial samples are then used to create another interpolant from which the final image will be reconstructed. </p><p>The simulation was set up in such a way that a number of parameters can be changed with ease; sensor pixel count, samples around the circle, and final desired image size.</p><p>Below is a picture showing the original image (on the left) passed to the simulation and the reconstructed image (on the right) obtained at the end of the simulation. Notice how high frequency details (i.e. areas of high contrast or thin details) are lost further out from the centre due to the nature of radial sampling. The original image is the same size as the reconstructed image at 512x512 pixels, and the simulation was done using a sensor 128 pixels long, with 200 samples around the circle.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4129131439844967717.png">A video simulation will be shown soon.</p>
