                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">One handed keyboard.  Five buttons for the main fingers with the pointer finger actuating two and the other three distributed one per finger.  The thumb controls a joystick scavenged off of a game controller.  Typing is done by chords, multiple keys are pressed simultaneously to create a single letter.   <br /><br />"But wait!" you ask, "Five buttons means 2^5-1 possible combinations of pressed keys, 31 active keystates, that's not even enough for letters and numbers let alone punctuation and symbols".<br /><br />That's where my idea comes in.  Instead of having a large number of keys to give a large number of combinations, the joystick can be used as a 4-way shift button.  Suddenly we have 31*5 possible combinations of pressed buttons and joystick directions, 155 possible combinations, or more than enough to emulate a full keyboard.  This also simplifies the chord scheme.  Suddenly there's no separate chord or chord prefix to go from lowercase to capital, just move  the joystick up and press the same</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>There&apos;s three targets for this project:</p><ul><li>People who, for whatever reason, prefer to type with one hand.  This could be damage to the other hand, wanting to have a hand on a keyboard and the other on a mouse or a tablet, or any one of a number of other reasons.</li><li>The ability to move around while typing.  Even on a well designed walking workstation there&apos;s some bounce as you walk.  Keeping the controller on you means that body movement doesn&apos;t effect the relationship between hand and keyboard.</li><li>Mobile typing.  Smaller tablets and smart phones don&apos;t have room for a full sized qwerty keyboard and while portable keyboards exist most are a pain to use unless you have a decent desktop sized surface.</li></ul><p>This keyboard only requires one hand to use properly, stays on you while moving and will eventually be wireless and can hopefully fit in a cargo pocket for easy transport.</p><p>All code released under the BSD licence.  All other content released under the <a href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/">Creative Commons Attribution Licence</a>. </p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/7254/log/23077-got-it-working-in-labjack">Got it working in labjack</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">a day ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7254/log/23077#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>We have the hardware, we have the layout, now we just need to do something useful with it.</p><p>I decided to prototype the keyer with my LabJack U12 and python. Since I run Linux interfacing python to generate keyboard events is as easy as writing commands to <a href="http://linux.die.net/man/1/xte">xte(1)</a> to run the keyboard and the mouse.  Now, I&apos;m not going to lie, this is an ugly hack and I should be ashamed of publishing it, but working 55 hours a week in customer service I found shame to be a liability.</p><p>The wiring is entirely free form, hence the somewhat scattered look on the diagram. </p><p>It seems to work well enough.  Going from 60 wpm on qwerty to  hunt and peck is painful but there&apos;s always a learning curve.</p><p>Still need to think of a good name for the keyer, maybe etaim after cyclilng through the keys?</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7254/log/22971-the-layout">The Layout</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7254/log/22971#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So we have the basic design, 5 keys, the basic layout, Pinkey, Ring, Middle, Index Front and Index Rear.  We need to figure out what chord goes with what key.  There&apos;s several standards, Engelbart&apos;s design, cykey, twiddler, but other than Engelbart none of them are designed expressly for 5 keys, and Engelbart was not optimized in any meaningful way.   Like all good engineers do when faced with multiple competing, non compatible standards, I decided to add another competing non compatible standard. </p><p>I spent a few hours clicking in random combinations to try and figure out what fingers worked better with what combinations.  I didn&apos;t have much of a problem where bending one finger caused another to bend enough to move a second finger, found zero issues with using the index finger to actuate the front, rear or both keys and found the hardest button to press was the pinky.</p><p>I decided to write a very small, very dirty python script to help provide suggestions.  Each key was weighted, a letter frequency pulled off of Wikipedia and the permutations calculated and sorted by the expected difficulty of chording.  The output was analyzed, tweaked and tested and the end result put in a handy reference sheet.  I threw together a layout for the remaining symbols based off of a bit of practise.  Time and testing will tell if this is the optimal layout.</p>
                                
                            </p>
