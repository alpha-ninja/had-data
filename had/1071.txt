                    <div class="description">This project is continued on a new page, please direct all inquiries & interest HERE:<br /><a href='https://hackaday.io/project/4927-hydra-multi-os-gaming-console-controller' target="_blank">https://hackaday.io/project/4927-hydra-multi-os-gaming-console-controller</a><br /><br />And THANKS to all who Skulled & followed this project, I think the best way to thank you all is with some NEW STUFF to read and maybe try on your own....<br /><br />VM's have many advantages over their bare metal counterparts, but the complexity makes these unavailable to most outside the datacenter. I aim to change that.  <br /><br /> The VM accesses the video hardware using IOMMU, which allows a PCI device to be "given" to a VM.  This Virtualization for Directed IO is what makes transparent virtualization possible. <br /><br />And this is the architecture of the HYDRA. Maxed out prototype contains 6 video cards, and gives each of these + USB to 6 different OS's.  Runs Winx, Linux, OSx, Android, SteamOS, and maybe someday Xbox & PS4.<br /><br /></div>

                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The goal here is to make this hardware assisted virtualization platform as easy to use as it's bare metal counterpart.&nbsp; Or,&nbsp; the virtalization should be completely transparent to the end user. They should be "in the Matrix", in a virtual world unaware of the virtual nature of their constructed environment, yet endowed with "powers" that allow all kinds of amazing feats formerly thought impossible.&nbsp;</p><p>The Xbox One is completely "in the Matrix", with all management removed.&nbsp; Boots 2 VM's &amp; that's it, if they break it's back to Day1 snapshot for you.&nbsp; If you're going to be in the "Matrix" you want to be Neo....</p><p><img src="//cdn.hackaday.io/images/3535601399347970753.JPG"></p><p>But datacenter level management comes with the expectation that you at least have your head wrapped around the whole virtualization thing.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>The HYDRA attempts to strike a balance. ESXi boots from the primary gpu, which gets "stolen" for passthrough during boot at which time the screen freezes. The VM assigned to this GPU boots and takes the screen.&nbsp; Starting other VM's or switching the OS on the primary display needs some interface......</p><p>The Hydra was originally developed 1.5 years ago to be a digital menu board system for a sub shop. Since then I have used this system as my primary PC (s) , and have 2 deployed in others use.&nbsp; This allowed me to get feedback and explore alternative, user friendly methods for the tasks a typical end user may encounter, as well as discover all the capabilities. </p><p>First try: Installed a vm with only 1 usb passed through for a wifi card, &amp; created a softAP. Wrote an Android app and got a cheapo Android tablet. This allowed power &amp; snapshot operations, even showed the VMware vga interface during boot over VNC, so if you were stuck in "startup repair" you would see it at the tablet. However, wireless deemed unreliable. </p><p>2nd try: ESXi has python, so I wrote a menu system that is accessed from the console of the server, or by ssh, or over serial. Created installable VIB patch to easily install the menu. This works very well, power, snapshot, even networking settings are available. But, the end user is required to use a text menu at the console.&nbsp; Should be ok, but as it turns out it's NOT.&nbsp;</p><p><img src="//cdn.hackaday.io/images/7738191399348160509.JPG"></p><p>I once accidentally assigned the primary GPU for passthrough on my system, the ESXi screen froze halfway booting.&nbsp; This was an HP Riloe II card, but it wouldn't pass through. I tried an NVidia PCI card and could actually assign it to a vm.&nbsp;</p><p>3rd try: built mgmt VM including all the tools, simple &amp; complex. Added the softAP. Set to autostart.&nbsp; Works very well, just wastes a GPU....</p><p>Redirecting the console to serial seems to be the answer.&nbsp; A microcontroller based serial console could handle all that is needed, as well as maybe perform other functions, such as HDMI KVM switching. </p><p>Current mgmt interface:&nbsp; I redirected TTY2 (techsupportmode) to com1. Now we have 6 GPU's for passthrough. Added bonus: modules loading are shown on the terminal during boot. The python menu will run on a laptop with putty, or even a palm3.&nbsp; I can't really require a laptop be attached for mgmt and there's only so many palm3's out there. Under development is a serial terminal menu device based on a ATMEGA328 with a 4x20 LCD.&nbsp; Very similar to: </p><p><a href="http://hackaday.com/2008/05/29/how-to-super-simple-serial-terminal/">http://hackaday.com/2008/05/29/how-to-super-simple-serial-terminal/</a></p><p>I am however using other libraries, to get the type of menu I want and support for a jog wheel rather than buttons.&nbsp; It will show 4 VM's with power status at a time, highlighting your selection, and have indicators for more above or below.&nbsp; Only power &amp; snapshot operations would be available, due to limited space. The unchangeable management VM would head the list, and allow full management capabilities.&nbsp; But for daily operations, the 4 line lcd will be an interface end users can deal with.&nbsp;</p><p>Like this:</p><p><img src="//cdn.hackaday.io/images/822191399348498047.jpg"></p><p>The HYDRA can be configured with 6 video cards, or with only one.&nbsp; Next prototype will be a mini-itx board with 2 GPUs.&nbsp;...</p>

                            </p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/1071/log/22084-android-vmapp-is-the-best-management-method">Android VM/App is the best management method</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">14 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/1071/log/22084#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>VMWare ESXi being a Datacenter Hypervisor, the management available from VMWare is the VSphere Client, which needs to run from another PC. Or from a VM on your host, but you still need another PC to get <em><strong>that </strong></em>up and running.  You can do it from the console, but probably not without another PC to Google yourself through it.  And the VSphere Client has many options an end-user does not need, and some an end-user should NOT have access to. </p><p>If this is ever going to be a end-user friendly system, I needed some other interface. A VM with the VSphere Client requires Windows, and a license.....So I can&apos;t &quot;include&quot; a mgmt VM without the whole OEM M$ etc....</p><p>So the VSphere Client is of course available from VMWare for those that are comfortable with it. But I needed a simpler management interface that would be always available without extra hardware, needs to communicate via SSH or Serial to send the vim-cmd commands to the Host, and needs to be able to VNC into a VM screen (for building or troubleshooting VM&apos;s).</p><p>First I was using an HP Lights Out card as the primary adapter.  You could remote in and mount ISO&apos;s, and power the system on/off.  The colors on the DCUI were all messed up, but it worked. </p><p>Then I wrote an Android App that would run on a cheapo tablet that I would include. It needed WiFi, so I ran a VM running a soft-AP using a Ralink USB.  They don&apos;t last very long, not surprising considering the source &amp; price ($4).  And when the radio dies, you&apos;re stuck with VSphere.  But it would VNC into a booted VM, so you could get out of &quot;startup repair&quot; if needed. </p><p>Then I wrote a Python Menu, as an installable VIB.  This always worked for enumerating existing VM&apos;s and controlling their power &amp; snapshots.  No VNC from the console,  even tried some frame-buffer VNC without success.  This code is probably useful somewhere though....</p><p>Then I found I could &quot;steal&quot; the primary GPU from the Host, and give it to a VM.  It would not work with the ILO card, so it took me a while to discover this.  The ESXi screen  freezes, and the VM using the GPU appears at the console when booted.  No text mode, so you can&apos;t troubleshoot a non booting VM from here. (so whatever I run here needs to be RELIABLE)  But, this is a PCI (not PCIe) card, and all PCI slots in the Host can only be assigned to 1 VM (so these slots are kind of useless anyway).  I have these populated with a Geforce FX5200 &amp; a USB card.  I connected a touch screen monitor and am running Android x86 ICS with these 2 PCI cards.  Now I can run the Android App from an autostarting VM on every boot.  A nice touch screen interface requires no keyboard or mouse. The VM with the App can be &quot;frozen&quot; at a working state and return to the snapshot on every boot. The same App could also be on a phone or tablet the EU already has - or not.  I could potentially modify the install ISO to include my App and run it live.  I could also put the cheapo Ralink in the free USB and run an Android Tether soft AP in case there is no existing AP.  The DCUI can be accessed over SSH, so that&apos;s not gone.  No M$ license needed either. This seems to be the best EU friendly management interface and I am finally satisfied with it.   </p><p>Now I&apos;m re-writing the App.....<br></p><br>
                                
                            </p>

                    <div class="section section-instructions">
                        <h5>Build instructions</h5>
                        
                        
                            <ul class="post-content section-instructions-list">
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">1</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p>Gather parts to build a system for your intended purpose:</p><p>Research your system board & processor & be double or more sure it is supported.&nbsp; Number of PCIe, PCI should reflect what you want to run. Internal serial HEADER is preferable over DB9 on the back.&nbsp; Avoid onboard video. DL the manual & check in the settings for VT-d, or "virtualization for directed IO"&nbsp; Check the Intel ARK to verify your CPU supports VT-d. Similar process for AMD, no first hand exp here sorry.&nbsp;</p><p>Use these links to select known working verified CPU/MB combos :</p><p><a href="https://docs.google.com/spreadsheet/ccc?key=0Aqp_xYBwP_Y7dE5EclhtaDdIV09lNWxfODd1alRUTlE#gid=0">https://docs.google.com/spreadsheet/ccc?key=0Aqp_xYBwP_Y7dE5EclhtaDdIV09lNWxfODd1alRUTlE#gid=0</a></p><p><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_IOMMU-supporting_hardware">http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_IOMMU-supporting_hardware</a></p><p>Check the ESXi 5.0 HCL to make sure the nic on your board is supported, or that there is at least a patch that makes it work. If you need to apply a patch, additionally get a nic card that IS on the HCL so you have the connectivity necessary to apply the patch. </p><p><br><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_IOMMU-supporting_hardware"></a></p><p>Lots of info here:</p><p><a href="https://communities.vmware.com/thread/297072?tstart=0">https://communities.vmware.com/thread/297072?tstart=0</a></p><p>GPU:&nbsp; ATI HD series are good for general purpose, pass through easy. You need above HD5XXX to run SteamOS, or for crypto currency mining.&nbsp;</p><p>High end NVidia cards work, K4000 tested.&nbsp;</p><p>Low end cards in the PCI slots work, it seems NVidia works better than ATI in this case.&nbsp; </p><p>I used a 2 GB SSD for the ESXi install. You can install ESXi to a USB stick, or even on the Datastore HDD. The SSD's are cheap, boot faster than USB, and best practice is to have your ESXi install separate.&nbsp;</p><p>8 GB ram is about the minimum useful amount.&nbsp;</p><p>Virtual disks can be pre-allocated (faster) or defined as some maximum size that they can grow to.&nbsp; So you can define 6 500GB virtual disks on a 1TB drive, and it's first come first serve as the disks grow.&nbsp; Which means you can over-provision your VM's initially, and move them to larger storage when needed.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>Prior to virtualization, datacenters struggled with "server sprawl", and now we have "virtual server sprawl".&nbsp; Which means since it's so easy to create/copy VM's, they tend to multiply....</p><p>So size your storage accordingly. </p><p>You can use iSCSi, NFS, or ATA over Ethernet if you get the HBA card form Coraid.&nbsp;</p><p>Unless you build a bootable USB drive with the ESXi install, you will need a cd drive. </p><p>I use a Zalman ZM-VE300, which is a 2.5" drive enclosure that allows you to select an ISO file and emulates a USB CDROM drive.&nbsp; Haven't burned a cd (or coaster) since.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>Keyboard, Video, and Mouse (KVM) matching the number of GPU's, or a KVM switch. </p><p><a href="https://docs.google.com/spreadsheet/ccc?key=0Aqp_xYBwP_Y7dE5EclhtaDdIV09lNWxfODd1alRUTlE#gid=0"></a></p></div>
                                    </li>
                                
                                    <li>
                                        <span class="instruction-number">2</span>
                                        <div class="instruction-list-item"><p>At this point you have an assembled system, VT-d capable CPU & Board, 8+ GB RAM, 2+  ATI GPU's of at least HD5xxx, bootable storage for ESXi and storage for VM's, can boot the ESXi installer from cd/usb/iso, and a supported nic.&nbsp; </p><p>Get ESXi installation ISO from here:</p><p>Boot into the bios (or UEFI) and verify:</p><p>VT-d is enabled</p><p>Execute-disable is enabled</p><p>boot order facilitates installing ESXi from your chosen media</p></div>

                    Hi and thanks for the interest.<br/>Most of what you are asking is possible & straightforwardly so. I'm sort of happy with it, had to do some chopping. I am working on an adapter to put a SFF PCIe card in a 1x slot, which will help with the cases.  The expanding case looks to have plenty of cooling options, which may not be necessary in your basement.  <br/><br/>The more questions the better....<br/><br/>
