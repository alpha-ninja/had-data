                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">According to the United States Department of Agriculture 80-90% of water consumption in the United States is for agriculture ( <a href='http://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/farm-practices-management/irrigation-water-use.aspx' target="_blank">http://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/farm-practices-management/irrigation-water-use.aspx</a> ). Of the 156,000 irrigated farms surveyed in 2008, only 14,086 used some type of soil moisture sensor to determine when to apply water (<a href='http://www.ers.usda.gov/dataFiles/Western_Irrigated_Agriculture/Set13/13-3.xls)' target="_blank">http://www.ers.usda.gov/dataFiles/Western_Irrigated_Agriculture/Set13/13-3.xls)</a><br /><br />I propose some of that if there was a cheap and easy to use device that could be used by both large and small farms, water use would be much more efficient. My idea would be a small spherical device with electrical contacts on the outer surface. A microcontroller on the inside would use those contacts to detect moisture levels and then relay that information through some sort of wireless interface. These small devices could be thrown periodically throughout a farm. The data could be gathered and used to control water usage.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>The basic idea of this is to have two metal bands on the external surface of the device. An internal microcontroller will use these bands to measure the conductivity of the soil. A low-power wireless system will allow the microcontroller to communicate the soil moisture level to a receiving device.</p><p>I foresee several concerns with this project</p><ol><li>Battery life - Batteries must be able to last a complete growing season (up to 9 months).</li><li>Wireless Interface Range - Any small, battery powered device attempting to maintain a wireless connection will have limited range. The radio must also be disabled most of the time in order to conserve power.</li><li>Cost - The enclosure I am envisioning would only be cost effective in large quantities.</li><li>Placement - The device needs to be placed in such a way that it can accurately detect the soil moisture levels.</li></ol><p>I have not yet solved any of these issues. As the design progresses and solutions are uncovered, I will update.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5206/log/16567-research-mode">Research Mode</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5206/log/16567#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>Right now, I am researching the best components/methods for this design. The first question I have been focusing on is the radio.</p><p>Digikey has an <a href="http://www.digikey.com/en/articles/techzone/2011/aug/comparing-low-power-wireless-technologies">article</a> written by Phil Smith that compares several wireless technologies on metrics such as cost, ease-of-use and performance. His favorite semms to be Bluetooth Low Energy. It has a nice balance of cost, design simplicity, range and efficiency. With that suggestion I have been looking at some of TI's BLE SoCs.</p><p>Another option I considered was the increasingly popular ESP8266. Phil Smith rules out Wi-Fi options due to power consumption, but I am curious if the ESP8266 can make a stand in the extreme low-power wireless platform.</p><p>The next question I have been considering is the moisture sensing mechanism. My original idea was to go with the conductivity based method. Thus my conceptual drawing shows two metal rings circling the device between which the resistance could be measured. Unfortunately, my research is showing that to be a very poor choice. The readings of this type of sensor are very sensitive to soil temperature. So while I could possibly include a temperature sensor of some sort, I am looking at other methods first. The one that stands out to me is the capacitance probe sensor (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_domain_sensor">Wikipedia</a>). I believe I can still use the metal rings from my original idea, I will just measure capacitance rather than resistance.</p><p>The method for measuring the capacitance will be the next issue. I need to use the most power efficient method I can find that is still fairly accurate. So far, I know of two methods. The first is a simple RC circuit where you measure the charge/discharge time of the capacitor. The second is a circuit described on <a href="http://michaelgellis.tripod.com/68hc11/cap.html">this page</a>. It uses an op-amp to generate a frequency signal where the frequency is proportional to the test capacitance. That circuit brings the 555 timer to my mind, so I may look into using a 555 to implement an equivalent circuit. My first impression is that the frequency counter is likely to be orders of magnitude higher power consumption since it is doing the same thing as the simple RC circuit method only repetitively. However, depending on the accuracy of the RC method (which will depend heavily on the chosen hardware), it may be necessary to sacrifice a little efficiency.</p><p>So...boring post and nothing useful yet. Sorry about that. With any luck, that will change quickly.</p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
