                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Last semester, me and a friend of mine built a demonstration version IEC fusor using MAKE's tutorial: <a href='http://makezine.com/projects/make-36-boards/nuclear-fusor/' target="_blank">http://makezine.com/projects/make-36-boards/nuclear-fusor/</a>    <br />Pictures of our device have been attached. This fall semester, we plan on building a full-scale version that is capable of actual D-D fusion. After we achieve fusion, we hope to be able to try to increase the efficiency of the device (achieving breakeven is probably too ambitious at this point :) . I happen to have an idea for increasing the efficiency (I  am still researching how viable it is). An efficient source of neutrons can be used for a host of applications: <a href='http://iec.neep.wisc.edu/applications.php' target="_blank">http://iec.neep.wisc.edu/applications.php</a><br /><br />A quick video demonstrating our device can be seen here: <br /><a href='https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mD3ZidpluYs&feature=youtu.be' target="_blank">https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mD3ZidpluYs&feature=youtu.be</a></div>

                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Problem: </p><p>The IEC fusor is one the most simple reactors that can be built. However, due to the fact that a very durable vacuum system must be made, it can cost several thousand dollars to construct. Most people would prefer to pay less on a DIY project. </p><p>I would like to try to see if I can constuct a D-D fusion IEC device for no more than $800 (using common components). Having plans for a low- cost fusor will encourage DIY fusion research. </p><p>Also, the IEC fusor is very inefficient. I have an idea for a grid design that is currently under research. Increasing the efficiency may not break even, however, it still can supply a source of neutrons that can be used for many different applications: <a href="http://iec.neep.wisc.edu/applications.php">http://iec.neep.wisc.edu/applications.php</a></p><p>ABOUT: </p><p>The IEC reactor is one of the most simple reactors that can be built. The reactor consists of three basic parts:</p>    <ol><li>A vacuum chamber </li><li>Two concentric metal grids within the vacuum chamber. </li><li>High voltage supply of at least several kilovolts. </li></ol>        <p>The high voltage differential is applied between the two grids. This causes ionization of any substance within the vacuum chamber. For fusion reactions, deuterium would suffice. On the other hand, for demonstration purposes, the remaining air particles within the vacuum can also be ionized. </p>    <p>There are two types of IEC reactors: </p>    <ol><li>Electron injection (Inner grid positive with respect to the outer grid) </li><li>Ion injection (Inner grid potential is negative with respect to the outer grid)    </li></ol><p>We are building a ion injection reactor.   </p><p>I will be posting more theory as I go along, however, here is some initial theory on potential well formation: </p><p>THEORY: POTENTIAL WELL FORMATION </p><p>The two concentric gridswithin the reactor form a potential well for ions. Ions are produced within thechamber in multiple areas. If an ion is born near the outer electrode then thepotential difference the ion travels through is larger than an ion born closerto the inner electrode. Ideally, ions should be born near the outer grid for maximumspeed of the ions. </p><p> To have aqualitative understanding of the potential well formation, please refer tofigure 2. Suppose a voltage is applied to the grid in such a way so as toestablish a small ionic current within the vacuum chamber. Ions will acceleratetowards the inner grid, and fall into the potential well within the grid. Theions will collide, inducing considerable heat.</p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/3413491439773238158.PNG"><p> If the ionic current is increased (thepotential difference remaining constant), the shape of the potential well willchange. Due to the increase in ion density within the spherical grid, what effectivelyresults is another grid, only this grid consists of dense positive ions. Itessentially forms a virtual anode. The virtual anode causes a rise in potentialwithin the inner grid. This is illustrated in (b) of figure 2. </p><p>Realize that nowthe fusor with its virtual anode is now acting as an electron injectionreactor. This causes electrons to accelerate into the virtual anode whichfunctions as a potential well.<br></p><p> If the current isincreased even more, the density of electrons falling into the potential wellwill increase to the point where a virtual cathode is created. Notice how it isback to where it started. In theory, this can process can continueindefinitely, forming many &#x201C;nested&#x201D; well potentials. However, studies haveshown that this is not the case. Typically, only a &#x201C;double well&#x201D; can be formed(part (c) of figure 2) [10]  </p><p>[10] Miley, G., &amp; Murali, S. (n.d.). 2. In Inertial electrostatic confinement (IEC) fusion: Fundamentals and applications. </p>
                                
                            </p>
