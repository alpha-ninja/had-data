                            <h2><a href="/project/4738/log/15122-photos-were-very-poorly-photoshopped">Photos Were (very poorly) Photoshopped</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4738/log/15122#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-15122"><p>Some sort of robot to remind me to take my pills and to measure my blood pressure has been on my mind for a few years. A while back I constructed a rough frame for the Vex treads first using foam board and then with 3mm expanded PVC.</p><p>Here's a <a href="http://letsmakerobots.com/blog/duane-degn/idea-treaded-robot" target="_blank">link to my post on Let's Make Robots</a> about the idea.</p><p>I only had two structures to hold the treads but the robot will have a total of four treads. I Photoshopped photos of the same two treads to show what I have in mind when using four treads.</p><p>Hopefully it's obvious the photos have been doctored. I didn't intend to fool anyone that my robot is further along than it is.</p><p>I'll add another log about what I've done so far very soon. I just wanted to make sure everyone was aware of the "fake" photos.</p></div>
                            </div>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4738/log/15145-initial-work">Initial Work</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">5 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4738/log/15145#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-15145"><p>I uploaded a video where I ramble about my intentions of building this robot.</p><p>I mention some of the parts I plan to use with the robot and give a general overview of my plans.</p><iframe style="width: 500px; height: 281px;" width="500" height="281" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/oGvulUIB59k" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe><p>Here are a couple of photos of the two tread sections I've built so far.</p><p><img src="http://letsmakerobots.com/files/userpics/u14887/VexTreads130928a.jpg"></p><p>The structure closest to the camera was made from expanded PVC (ePVC). The other structure was my first prototype and it was made from foam board. Foam board was used to see if the planned geometry would work correctly. Before making the second prototype, I changed the design of the motor mount in order to increase its strength and stiffness.</p><p><img src="http://letsmakerobots.com/files/userpics/u14887/VexTreads130928b.jpg"><span></span><br></p><p>Below is a closeup of the ePVC motor mount.</p><p><img src="http://letsmakerobots.com/files/userpics/u14887/VexTreads130928c.jpg">I used two thicknesses of 3mm ePVC in some parts of the motor mount. Since making the above prototype, I've purchased some 6mm ePVC which I plan to use in future prototypes.<br></p><p>Vex wheels and sprockets use a square drive hole. To mount the Vex sprocket to the motor's 6mm shaft, I drilled out the center of the sprocket and then drilled a hole from the side of the sprocket to the center hole. I added a 4-40 machine screw with a nut to the sprocket to act as a set screw.</p><p><img src="http://letsmakerobots.com/files/userpics/u14887/VexSprocket130928b.jpg"><span></span><br></p><p>To reinforce the sprocket, I added Polymorph to the open areas on one side of the sprocket.</p><p><img src="http://letsmakerobots.com/files/userpics/u14887/VexSprocket130928c.jpg">The set screw is positioned so it doesn't interfere with the tread. The nut embedded in the Polymorph is necessary since the plastic alone would soon strip and not provide anything for the set screw to push against as it clamps down on the "D" shaped drive shaft.<br></p><p>Here's a photo of the sprocket secured to the motor with the treads installed.</p><p><img src="http://letsmakerobots.com/files/userpics/u14887/VexSprocket130928a.jpg"></p><p>The Polymorph helps the sprocket hold its shape as the set screw is tightened.</p><p>I drew the parts with OpenOffice's Draw program. Here are most of the pieces before they were laid out to fit on a sheet of paper.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9048331426301193276.JPG"></p><p>After the parts were arranged to fit on a sheet of paper and printed, they were cut out and glued to the ePVC. I used a Dremel with a saw wheel to rough cut the shapes. I used a milling bit in a drill press to cut the slots. I finished the pieces with a belt sander and X-acto knife. Cutting the pieces this way was very labor intensive.</p><p>I have a CNC router which is currently not operational. I think I will spend some time getting the CNC router working before continuing to build this robot.</p><p>I also plan to modify the design a bit. The rollers farthest from the motor need more support than the ePVC frame provides. I will likely reinforce this area with some aluminum L-bracket material. I also intend to add bearings to the unpowered sprocket.</p><p>Besides the changes I plan to make, I also have large sections of the robot I haven't begun to design. I have some thoughts about how to articulate the tread structures but I haven't tried any of my plans out in the real world yet.</p><p>I would really like to get the basic hardware finished relatively soon so I can begin working on the code to control the motors and tread movement. I have a feeling getting this robot to climb stairs autonomously will be a very big challenge.</p></div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4738/log/17531-hackable-blood-pressure-meter">Hackable Blood Pressure Meter</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4738/log/17531#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-17531"><p>I have an old blood pressure meter I was able to hack and receive data from a "tx" pad on the PCB. The LCD screen on this meter has come detached. It looks like the paper thin ribbon cable had been glued in place and the glue dried out after all these years (nearly 20). I'm not sure how, but the electrical connection itself appears to have been made with some sort of conducting glue.</p><p>While I considered purchasing a similar meter and hoped to find another accommodating "tx" pad, I decided to take the less risky approach and purchase a <a href="http://www.aliexpress.com/item/2014-high-quality-Bluetooth-Fully-Automatic-Upper-Arm-Digital-Blood-Pressure-Monitor-IOS-Android/32244885953.html" target="_blank">Bluetooth enabled meter</a>. Hopefully the Bluetooth interface won't be too hard to hack.</p><p>I've been spending a lot of time writing code for my CNC router project. The CNC router will make it much easier to make the pieces I need to continue building this robot.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4738/log/18578-collecting-additional-parts">Collecting Additional Parts</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4738/log/18578#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18578"><p>I've been putting a lot of my effort into getting my CNC controller working. Cutting out the parts needed for this robot will be a lot easier with a CNC router so time spent getting the router working seems like a good investment. I made some additional <a href="https://hackaday.io/project/4793-propeller-based-cnc-router-controller/log/18575-circular-motion">progress on the router lately</a>. I'm confident I can now control the steppers in a way to produce circular motion.</p><p>While the physical structure of this robot awaits parts to be produced by the CNC router, I've been acquiring various components I believe will be useful in a health maintenance robot. Besides the Bluetooth enabled blood pressure monitor, I also purchased one of <a href="http://www.adafruit.com/product/1963">Adafuit's Fonas</a>. I also purchased a couple of Adafuit's Wifi modules.</p><p>I figure the Fona will be used to call family members in times of need. I won't go into detail now about what will constitute a "time of need," but I plan to have the robot able to interface with the Fona to send text messages during these times. I may also use the Fona as a sort of speaker phone to allow family members or health care providers to communicate directly with the person using this robot.</p></div>
                            </div>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4738/log/19150-starting-to-add-a-cell-phone-to-the-bots-hardware">Starting to Add a Cell Phone to the Bot&#39;s Hardware</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4738/log/19150#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-19150"><p>I purchased another gadget to use with this robot. I want the robot to be able to call for help if the person being cared for were having trouble. </p><p>I purchased one of <a href="https://www.adafruit.com/products/1963" target="_blank">Adafruit's FONA</a> boards and a TING SIM card.</p><p><img src="https://www.adafruit.com/images/970x728/x1963-01.jpg.pagespeed.ic.hV8_cZsKRL.webp" alt="Adafruit FONA - Mini Cellular GSM Breakout - SMA Version - v1"></p><p>I want to use the FONA with a Propeller microcontroller so I wrote a program to eavesdrop on the communication going on between the Terminal and the Arduino and the Arduino and FONA. I <a href="http://forums.parallax.com/showthread.php/161193-Adafuit-s-FONA-with-the-Propeller" target="_blank">posted some details</a> about this in the Parallax Forum.</p><p>Adafruit's software is opensource and <a href="https://github.com/adafruit/Adafruit_FONA_Library" target="_blank">available on their GitHub</a>, but the code was not easy for me to follow. I decided it would be easier for me to figure out how to use the various FONA features by watching the data exchanged between the Arduino and the FONA.</p><p>For now I just have the code to monitor the communication running on the Propeller. I'm presently working on getting the Propeller to control the FONA directly.</p><p>The FONA has been a lot of fun to work with.</p></div>
