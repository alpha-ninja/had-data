                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Acetyl-salicylic acid  is on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines, the most important medications needed in a basic health system.  But still a very large part of the world’s population has inadequate or no access to such essential and life-saving medicines like acetyl-salicylic acid. In the following I will research a chemical synthesis route for acetyl-salicylic acid, which is feasible by everyone, everywhere, using a lab-on-a-chip system or a chem printer.</div>

                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><h1>The basic idea</h1><p>In common syntheses of aspirin, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salicylic_acid">salicylic acid</a> is treated with <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_anhydride">acetic anhydride</a>, causing a chemical reaction that turns salicylic acid&apos;s hydroxyl group into an ester group. Because acetic anhydride is used for the synthesis of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a> by the diacetylation of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>, acetic anhydride is listed as a U.S. DEA List II precursor, and therefore it is restricted in many other countries including mine, so I won&apos;t use it. Instead I&apos;ll follow a slightly different path as a starting point:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7936501430096942665.jpg"></p><table><tbody></tbody></table><p>As acetic acid is less reactive than acetic anhydride, the reaction yield will be lower. Furthermore the purity level of the acetyl-salicylic acid will be lower.</p><p>Balanced equation:<br></p><p><img alt="\color{White} \large C_7H_6O_3+C_2H_4O_2 \rightarrow C_9H_8O_4+H_2O" title="\color{White} \large C_7H_6O_3+C_2H_4O_2 \rightarrow C_9H_8O_4+H_2O" src="/api/latex?tex=%5Ccolor%7BWhite%7D%20%5Clarge%20C_7H_6O_3%2BC_2H_4O_2%20%5Crightarrow%20C_9H_8O_4%2BH_2O">Molar mass salicylic acid: 138.122 g/mol</p><p>Molar mass acetic acid: 60.052 g/mol</p><p>Molar mass acetyl-salicylic acid: 180.157 g/mol<br></p><p>Molar mass water: 18.015 g/mol</p><p>Theoretical yield:</p><p>138.122 g salicylic acid + 60.052 g acetic acid  &#x2192; 180.157 g acetyl-salicylic acid</p><p>But I expect the actual yield will be only half of the theoretical one.<br></p><h2>Synthesis of aspirin at a laboratory scale</h2><p><strong>1.</strong></p><ul><li>Measure 1 g of salicylic acid (&#xB1; 0.01 g) <span class="redactor-invisible-space"></span><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2523721430106721437.png"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6252801430106887883.png"></li><li>Place it under fume hood into 50-ml Erlenmeyer flask</li><li>Cover salicylic acid crystals with 2 - 2.5 ml glacial acetic acid<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2704491430107269698.png"><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1006691430107287721.png"></li><li>Swirl flask and add 2.5 drops of concentrated sulfuric acid <img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8140471430107410100.png"></li><li>Heat flask gently in a 70&#xB0;C hot water bath for 10 minutes<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6078671430107574154.jpg"></li></ul><p><strong>2.</strong></p><ul><li>Remove flask from water bath, add 15 ml distilled ice water </li><li>Allow mixture to stand for 5 minutes</li><li>Chill solution in an ice bath until crystallization of aspirin occurs</li><li>Stir occasionally</li><li>If crystallization does not occur, scratch walls and bottom of flask with a glass stirring rod to promote crystallization</li><li>If an oil appears instead of a solid, re-heat flask in hot water bath until oil disappears and cool again</li></ul><p><strong>3.</strong></p><ul><li>Set up a vacuum filtration apparatus with Hirsch funnel<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5456301430108748020.jpg"></li><li>Transfer solid aspirin onto filter paper</li><li>Add small amounts of distilled ice water and repeat until transfer of aspirin crystals is complete</li><li>Wash aspirin crystals on filter paper with 10 ml of distilled ice water</li><li>Pour filtrate in the sink</li></ul><p><strong>4.</strong></p><ul><li>Transfer aspirin crystals from filter paper to a 100-ml beaker </li><li>Dissolve crystals in a minimum volume of 10 ml pure ethanol<img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2991921430109315085.png"></li><li>Warm solution in a 60&#xB0;C hot water bath</li><li>Pour 25 ml distilled water into solution after solution has reached 60&#xB0;C</li><li>If a solid forms, continue warming until solid dissolves</li></ul><p><strong>5.</strong></p><ul><li>Remove beaker from heat</li><li>Cover beaker with a watch-glass</li><li>Set beaker aside to cool slowly.                     <p>Have you considered using PDMS for your lab-on-a-chip? It's pretty much the standard in academic work on microfluidics nowadays so there's lots of good information on using it available. And its properties make it perfect for what you are doing - it stands up very well to heat and most&nbsp;chemicals. I suspect it might be better suited to the task than laser cut parts that might leak, react, degrade, or contaminate.<br><br>If you haven't encountered it before, the basic procedure is: 1) Make your design as an image on a computer. 2) Print the design on an overhead transparency with a wax printer (I've also used laser cutter to etch acrylic&nbsp;with some success). 3) Put the printed design in a container and pour on your PDMS. 4) After curing finishes peel off the print from the PDMS. 5) Treat PDMS and a glass slide with oxygen plasma to remove organic&nbsp;surface contaminants&nbsp;(not as hard as it sounds - coronal discharge from high voltage works fine, or you can use a regular microwave to make the plasma). 6) Stick the PDMS down on the glass slide. 7) Done! Now you've got a very robust&nbsp;lab-on-a-chip. Poke some needles into it to supply and drain your chemicals.<br><br>I've played around with microfluidics&nbsp;for fun and have collected a lot of tidbits of information.
