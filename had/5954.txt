                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">Massively Multiplayer Small Scale Farming<br />=========================================<br /><br />The Problem<br />-----------<br /><br />We have a problem. Food is grown a long way from where it's eaten, and<br />cities naturally tend to grow on good farmland. In response we establish<br />vast irrigated monocultures on sub-prime land and truck the produce in<br />to the concrete-covered cities. The pests and diseases which afflict<br />monocultures are fought with pesticide and fungicide, and we feed the<br />resulting depleted, lifeless soil with carbon-intensive fertilisers.<br /></div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>A better vision for the future<br>------------------------------</p><p><br>Growing food closer to where it's eaten reduces the carbon footprint.<br>Most people living in cities have growing space available. Whether it's<br>a big suburban backayard or a pot on a window sill, most residences have<br>space to grow something. We're also going to be seeing more grow<br>factories like this pesticide-free one from Japan:</p><p><br><a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2014/07/140717-japan-largest-indoor-plant-factory-food/">http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2014/07/140717-japan-largest-indoor-plant-factory-food/</a></p><p><br>Software can help<br>-----------------</p><p>The path for global transition to this better future is going to be<br>helped by software. The sooner we build the software to facilitate <br>the transition, the sooner global uptake is likely to occur.<br><br>We are proposing to build an open source software system to efficiently<br>determine what to grow where. It will offer a database backend and a web<br>interface, with the option of sharing data globally, or with any<br>server(s) you choose (including none at all if you just want to use it<br>to track the fields on your own farm).</p><p><br>This will allow people to specify the growing areas they have available,<br>including details like size, soil pH and climate conditions. It will<br>also allow potential buyers to register interest in having particular<br>items grown in their area. The system will then match the growing areas<br>to a database of crop types, make recommendations as to what would grow<br>well in a given area, and give the grower an idea of how likely it is<br>that each product will sell in their local area. In the future it could<br>be expanded to co-ordinate delivery from disparate growers to disparate<br>buyers.<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
