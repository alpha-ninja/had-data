                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">750 million people around the world lack access to safe water; approximately one in nine people.  One source of clean water is harvesting it directly from the air.<br /><br />Dew point is the temperature at which the water vapor in a sample of air at constant barometric pressure condenses into liquid water at the same rate at which it evaporates. At temperatures below the dew point, water will leave the air. The condensed water is called dew when it forms on a solid surface.  <br /><br />Harvesting water from air is not a new idea however many systems rely on environmental conditions or large amounts of energy.  This system uses what is known about dew point water harvesting and tries to optimize the conditions in the most "energy convenient" way.  </div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>is system is to be powered by solar panels and rather than just relying on storage batteries most of the solar power would be used to cool liquid coolant in an insulated tank. This cooling process would only run while the sun shines, charging a smaller battery for controller and a condenser coil pump. A controller logs daily barometric pressure, humidity and temperature to calculate when to run the dew point water harvesting at the most optimum time.</p><p>The controller will be a small embedded computer (possibly a Raspberry pi or Arduino) that is pre-programmed to measure temperature and barometric pressure in order to determine the optimum time in which run coolant through the condenser coil.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2649981434849438131.jpg"></p><p>The vessel in which the condenser coil in enclosed is design to both catch dew as it forms on the condenser coil and establish a low air-pressure than that outside to increase conditions where water will leave the air (dew point). To achieve this, a filter is used on the inlet of the condenser vessel to both create an air pressure differential and to filter dust particles. A fan is used to draw air through the filter and condenser coil and maintaining low air-pressure inside the condenser vessel.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5220611439683599198.JPG"></p><p>It is important to recognise that Peltier Coolers are not very energy efficient however they are lightweight and small. Further the Peltier Cell itself is thermally conductive so when power is switched off the heat from hot side will quickly rush back to the cool side of the cell.</p><p>Consequently the efficiency of Peltier Cell cooling can be improved by ensuring the liquid coolant moves away from the Peltier Cell quickly when power is switched off. This is done by ensuring the liquid coolant runs back into the insulated tank when the pump stops ensuring the coolant isn&apos;t warmed by the Peltier Cell when it is switched off.</p><iframe width="560" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/9qMjwwPS8Z0" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen=""></iframe>A 136W dual peltier cell cooling system reduces the temperature of 10 litres of propylene glycol to -4C from ambient ~21C within 6 hours. Dew point can be achieved with a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dew_point">humidity below 30%</a> which is very dry at 10C of cause it&apos;s not as simple as that but -4C gives some wriggle room.<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Dewpoint-RH.svg/396px-Dewpoint-RH.svg.png">Note: this is a proof of concept prototype.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4329091439674826467.JPG"></p><span></span>
                                
                            </p>
