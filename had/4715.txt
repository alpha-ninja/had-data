                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><span class="tx"><strong>Let there be light &#x2013; multitasking</strong></span><br></p><p><span class="tx">Since </span><span class="tx">the TiRStove is insulated, you can easily <strong>boil drinking water</strong> and </span><span class="tx"><strong><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/1936-1charger">charge</a></strong> your equipment while fishing in a canoe. With the PPX you will </span><span class="tx">have</span> 10W of power enough to supply a very very strong <span class="tx">flashlight/<strong>searchlight directly</strong> (<em> if you were too lazy to charge the </em></span><span class="tx"><em>batteries ;-</em>)</span></p><p><span class="tx">The TiRStove with &quot;<a href="http://hackaday.io/hacker/38079-davidagner"><strong>Agge&apos;s fire-spider mod</strong></a>&quot; combined with <strong><a href="https://hackaday.io/project/1936-1charger">1Charger</a></strong> &amp; <strong><a href="http://powerpractical.com/collections/portable-power-products/products/powerpot10-thermoelectric-generator">PPX</a> </strong>charging an 10Ah LiFePO4 battery</span><br><span class="tx"></span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8526921426188300474.jpg"></p><p><span class="tx"><strong></strong><br><strong></strong></span></p><p><strong>Performance</strong><br></p><p><span class="tx">2L of water at 7&#xB0;C will start to simmer in 10 min and <strong>boil heavily in 16 min</strong>, stove &quot;warm&quot; at start ..and outside temp -2&#xB0;C.</span> ( A <span class="tx">cold stove</span> will<span class="tx"> need an </span><span class="tx">extra 10 min </span><span class="tx">)</span><span class="tx"> Almost the same boil result was achieved without insulation. I noticed an increased ext. stove temperature from 45 to 70&#xB0;C and a bit more smoke</span></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7254651426190278625.jpg"></p><p><span class="tx"><em>** best </em></span><em><span class="tx">boil time, is </span><span class="tx">2.8L of 7&#xB0;C water</span> to boil heavily in 11 min <span class="tx">with no insulation (just air ) and ext. stove temp 100 &#xB0;C, outside temp 1</span><span class="tx">&#xB0;C no wind</span><span class="tx"> and 10 mm pot-cross</span></em></p><p><strong>History</strong><br></p><p>A precursor to the rocket stove was the <strong></strong><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argand_lamp"><strong>argand lamp</strong></a> (1780). Dr. Larry Winiarski, Technical Director of Aprovecho, began developing the Rocket Stove in 1980 based on a <strong><a href="https://youtu.be/K7yKTqxHqjQ">VITA stove</a></strong>, designed by Sam Baldwin, and rediscovered the principles of the <strong>systems developed by the Romans in </strong><a href="http://www.pages.drexel.edu/~tk53/ae390/a5/hypocaust.htm"><strong>hypocaust heating</strong></a><strong></strong>.  </p><p><span class="tx"><strong>How it works</strong></span> </p><ul><li><a href="http://www.brainpickings.org/2012/09/27/richard-feynman-fire-trees/"><strong>Fire - &quot;stored sun&quot;</strong></a></li></ul><ul><li><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z2_rwr_qEkA&amp;feature=player_detailpage&amp;t=32"><strong>Gasification - no fire without &quot;smoke&quot; &amp; air ;-)</strong></a></li></ul><ul><li><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?feature=player_detailpage&amp;v=Z2_rwr_qEkA&amp;t=217"><strong>Chimney effect</strong></a></li></ul><p><em>by inventor Dr. Larry Winiarski </em><br></p><p><span class="tx"><em>&quot;</em></span><em><span class="tx">Rocket stove designs </span><span class="tx">consider both combustion efficiency and heat transfer </span><span class="tx">efficiency</span><span class="tx">. A key feature of Rocket Stoves is that the </span><span class="tx">fuel is </span><span class="tx">easily metered in so that </span><span class="tx">only as much surface is heated as necessary for the power production</span><span class="tx">, for wood this </span><span class="tx">means that <strong>only the ends</strong></span><span class="tx"><strong> are gasified</strong> and burn in the vertical <a href="http://vignette2.wikia.nocookie.net/solarcooking/images/e/e5/Rocket_Stove_details.gif/revision/latest?cb=20070302184638"><strong>combustion chamber. </strong></a></span><span class="tx">This is in effect a <strong></strong><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_effect"><strong>very hot chimney that pulls air in at a moderate velocity</strong></a>. In the </span><span class="tx">horizontal feed rocket stove the wood rests on a shelf with the ends overhanging, </span><span class="tx">forming a grate, or they can rest on a real grate at that</span><span class="tx"> position.<strong> Some air comes from </strong></span><strong><span class="tx">underneath the shelf in the feed tunnel and up through the glowing ends of the sticks </span><span class="tx">where gasification takes </span></strong><span class="tx"><strong>plac</strong>e. &quot;Secondary air&quot; comes in over the shelf and in </span><span class="tx">between the sticks. This air keeps the sticks cool and helps keep the flames blown </span><span class="tx">into the</span><span class="tx"> vertical chimney so that the fire does not back out the feed tunnel.</span></em></p><p><em>..<br></em></p><p><em><span class="tx">In convection heat transfer as packets of hot molecules touch the surface of the pot,they quickly cool down and slightly raise the surface </span><span class="tx">temperature of the pot as they reach a new equilibrium. Since the molecules and the pot&apos;s surface are essentially at the same temperature they </span><span class="tx">can no longer transfer heat to the pot&apos;s surface. So it is better that they be replaced by a new fresh group of hot molecules. </span><span class="tx">This is why <strong>we want </strong></span><strong><span class="tx">the hot combustion gases to slide as close as possible without choking the flow</span><span class="tx">. We do this by keeping the momentum of the gas flow as </span></strong><span class="tx"><strong>constant as possible. </strong></span></em></p><p><em>...<br></em></p><p><em><span class="tx">Keeping the cross sectional area constant under the pot means that we can taper the slope of the top of the stove. This gets the gases to flow</span><span class="tx"> closer and closer to the</span> bottom of the pot. When we put a skirt around the pot with a gap adjusted to have the same cross sectional area we heat<span class="tx"> the sides of the pot effectively. </span><span class="tx">As the gases cool</span> down, they become more dense, so the cross sectional area could be reduced slightly<span class="tx"> and still have the same momentum. This is the primary purpose of the pot skirt</span><span class="tx">. Some say that the pot skirt is a wind screen or that it is </span></em><span class="tx"><em>used to control excess air. This is a misunderstanding of it&apos;s primary purpose&quot;</em></span></p><p><a href="http://ethoscon.com/pdf/ETHOS/ETHOS2012/SatAM/Winiarski_HeatTransfer_RocketStoveSystem.pdf"><em>-Dr. Larry Winiarski       &quot;Combustion and Heat Transfer in a Rocket Stove System&quot;</em></a></p><p>More reading<br></p><p><em><a href="http://www.aprovecho.org/lab/rad/rl/stove-design/doc/22/raw">Stove Design - Stove Design - Aprovecho Research Center</a></em></p><p><em><a href="http://www.bioenergylists.org/stovesdoc/Ogle/nordicarocket.pdf">Global Modeling and Testing of Rocket Stove Operating Variations</a></em></p><p><em><a href="http://stoves.bioenergylists.org/content/pot-skirt-investigat">pot-skirt</a></em><br></p><p><br><a href="http://ethoscon.com/pdf/ETHOS/ETHOS2012/SatAM/Winiarski_HeatTransfer_RocketStoveSystem.pdf"></a></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2009181426188396251.jpg"></p><p><br><a href="http://ethoscon.com/pdf/ETHOS/ETHOS2012/SatAM/Winiarski_HeatTransfer_RocketStoveSystem.pdf"></a></p><p><span class="tx"><strong>Durable</strong></span><br></p><p><span class="tx">The </span><span class="tx">TiRStove is constructed to be <strong>durable,</strong> <strong>efficient </strong>and <strong>lightweight</strong>.
