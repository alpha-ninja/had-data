                            <p><h2>Attention 2015 Hackaday Prize Judges: </h2><h2>Please save yourself time, this entry has No video.</h2><p><strong>Thank you so much for your time all your hard work to make this community awesome!</strong><br></p><p>Setbacks have stalled this project, but I will attempt to continue to try to burn things after the Hackaday Prize.</p><p><strong></strong>Other than major setbacks, problems and failure, things are great! I love everything at Hackaday, I had a lot of fun following the Hackaday Prize, and the smaller prizes I won were very encouraging.</p><hr><p>Old Details:<br></p><p>The 2015Hackadayprize should be fair to everyone, so I hope to prove that lack of money should not stop a person from entering the contest. Maybe even igniting a sense of independence with financially challenged hackers worldwide, to overcome obstacles by taking advantage of their surroundings.</p><p>Broken Projection TV&apos;s Abound!<br>There are thousands of broken projection TV&apos;s hiding in peoples basements and garages, destined for land fills. I see adds for free ones online everyday. If the lenses (Fresnel lens screen) from all of them were converted and used for power instead of garbage, the energy would be immeasurable. I&apos;m saving one now. What if everyone did?</p><p>Safety Issues: This project is as dangerous as giving a kid a magnifying glass in a dry field on a hot sunny day. I will take precautions and make mechanical shutters to control the light.</p><p>Quality Issues: Some lenses work better than others. I don&apos;t know how good the one I have is, but we will find out. (uninformative Fresnel Lens Qualities link on side. Not mine.)<br></p><p>Budget: $0 &#x2013; Everything must be scavenged for free.</p><p>Motivation: To build a portable &quot;HomelessHackerSpace&quot; of tools made from free or dumpster bound materials.</p><p>Goals:<br></p><ul><li>Optimistic &#x2013; Cut and weld metal at over 2000F (1093C)</li><li>Realistic &#x2013; Cut and etch thin materials for artistic projects</li><li>Last resort &#x2013; I make the worlds worst reflow oven ever.</li></ul><p>Other tools to keep in mind while dumpster browsing: 3D printer, reflow oven, CNC router, oscilloscope, or anything coveted at the HackASpace.</p>

                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/4989/log/19188-implementing-the-teensy-lc">Implementing The Teensy-LC</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">2 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4989/log/19188#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>I want to thank Hackaday for the Teesnsy-LC I won in the <a target="_blank" href="http://hackaday.com/2015/05/07/were-giving-out-125-teensy-lc-boards-this-week/">"We're Giving out 125 Teensy-LC Boards this Week",</a> part of the <a target="_blank" href="http://hackaday.com/tag/50k-in-play/">$50K IN PLAY</a> contest! Thank you!  I am excited to use this <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/list/5845-2015-thp-freescale-parts">FREESCALE</a> based part!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1890491433785904436.jpg"></p><p><strong>Implementing TheTeensy-LC</strong></p><p><strong>1. To track the sun</strong>, aligned photo transistors or solar cells will connect to theTeensy-LC, which will relay a motor to rotate the lens. I decided aone axis tracking system would be the easiest and cheapest for now,and I will deal with the disadvantages of having to setup the correctangle of the sun each day.</p><p><strong>2. To find themelting point of commonly found elements</strong>, as mentioned on <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/5537-measuring-extreme-temperatures">https://hackaday.io/project/5537-measuring-extreme-temperatures</a>, by setting up fusetype system, breaking a connection of the remotely connectedTeensy-LC, to record temperature on an infrared thermometer.</p><p>First commonconductive elements to test:<br></p><ul><li>Zinc 419 °C</li><li>Aluminum 659 °C</li><li>Copper 1083 °C</li><li>Cast Iron 1260 °C</li><li>Stainless Steel 1363°C</li></ul><p>I want to set theseup as wires in rows on a firebrick, at a safe distance.<br>Also, if I can getthe temperature high enough, I would also see if I could duplicatethis sand conversion: <a target="_blank" href="https://hackaday.io/project/5230-solar-sand-converter-solving-the-sand-problem">https://hackaday.io/project/5230-solar-sand-converter-solving-the-sand-problem</a></p><p><strong>3. Eventually I hope tofind an alternative solar device</strong>, because of the underestimated powerof the sun.</p><p>I wanted a diagram,but being a little short on parts right now makes it difficult tonail down some of the details. I'm giving KiCad a try, so please excuse the mess:</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4631361434109972662.jpg"></p>

                            <h2><a href="/project/4989/log/18877-trying-to-focus">Trying to Focus</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;

                                0 comments

                            </small>
                            <p><h2>First Heat Test:<br></h2><p>My $10 Harbor Freight Multimeter just happens to have a temperature probe that I'm sure is good to +/-25C, so I taped it to the back of an old metal PC cover and we are good to go for some in apartment testing before the wife gets home. <strong>Boring video alert!</strong><br><iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/qcIbi9ad2ss" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" width="500"></iframe></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7929291433301680212.jpg"></p>I used my vertical blinds as shutters for instant light control. Turning the knob either way makes an almost instant change on the meter.<br><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1015161433301718246.jpg"></p><p><strong>Focusing the slit:</strong></p> <p>I was able to get the size briefly down to 80 x 3mm. I was constantly adjusting the position of everything, but couldn't get to a sweet spot. The key to me succeeding will be to find light weight and semi portable materials that will be ridged enough for near precision focusing.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4110421433301957529.jpg"></p><p><strong>Maxed out at 90C!</strong> (194F...                             <h2><a href="/project/4989/log/18822-the-hitachi-43fdx01b-teardown">The Hitachi 43FDX01B Teardown</a></h2>

                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/4989/log/18822#discussion-list">
                                4 comments

                            <p><p>This was too easy. There were no hidden screws, no hidden tabs snapping off, no tricky disassembly order, no blood and no cursing at all, so by definition its more like a take-apart than a teardown.</p><hr><p><br><strong></strong>Please Help! If you can, help me figure out what other projects I could make out of these parts! I will be happy to take more pictures, like of the large caps. I would like to keep it all, but will eventually have to reduce the size :(<br></p><hr><p><strong>Preliminary Focal Point Information:</strong></p><p><strong>- Screen: 43" diagonal, about 34.5" x 26"</strong></p><p><strong></strong>About 109cm diagnal, 87cm x 66cm<br><strong></strong></p><p><strong>- Focal point 26" from the lens.</strong> (66cm)<strong></strong></p><p>Closer than I imagined, so smoke on the lens will be a bigger problem.</p><p><strong>- Common vertical slit shaped point</strong> </p><p>About 4" high and 0.5" wide in the middle (about 10x1cm).</p><p>I was hoping for a small spot, even though it was a long shot, but this is much better than an unfocused blob some get.</p><hr><p><strong>Lets start with the back.</strong></p><p>The front screen lens assembly is held on inside by four metal bars screwed into the front plastic frame.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1084211433229670557.jpg"></p><p>Flash lens reflection.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9044511433229749776.jpg"></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/289431433229769832.jpg"></p><p>Back cover mirror.</p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2611491433229797912.jpg"><p>Very trapezoidal.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7005061433229846620.jpg"></p><p>The mirror is nice and thick, but the backing is a thin coating. I'm sure glad I always remember to teardown outside. I only found one dead bug.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/1854821433230193129.jpg"></p><p>Make that 200 years worth.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5638051433230979279.jpg"></p><p>Lots of shielded boxes all over.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4034711433230235123.jpg"></p>Wires and plenty of connectors.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9479111433230260970.jpg"></p>Power.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5441361433230292186.jpg"></p>Many side boards and lots of caps and resistors<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9752671433230330473.jpg"></p>Tube board.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4200991433231087542.jpg"></p><p>Original handmade wood strain relief.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7045541433231116739.jpg"></p>Lots of these furry things.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9516391433231193775.jpg"></p>Turns out they are heat sinks! 04686A?<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4454351433231213807.jpg"></p>IC's everywhere.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4266661433231467171.jpg"></p>Front speaker panel, With traces of "coffee".<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9229131433231514398.jpg"></p>Front panel velcro.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5424981433231539822.jpg"></p>Engineer 1: "Just two pieces of velcro for the front, really?"<p>Engineer 2: "Don't worry. It will hold for 30 years."</p><p>Yep, it still held for 30 years. Unfortunately the velcro glue backing specs had to be lowered for cost...</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9477241433231590854.jpg"></p>Two full range speakers :|<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7820901433231631071.jpg"></p>The adjustable side of the three projector lenses.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4092461433231672825.jpg"></p>Underneath are the three projector guns with circuit boards and large metal shields with holes.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9418511433231703162.jpg"></p>Closer view.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/8025441433231745244.jpg"></p>Holly resistor Batman! Now they can put three million SMT resistors on one real smaller than that gold band! Some of the caps are big also. <p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/2779591433232065824.jpg"></p><p>I finally found the reset button! Its right below that 90,000V line in the foreground. How convenient.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5821401433232141549.jpg"></p>The four metal bars holding the screen are off and two layers of the screen are both taped together and are also glued together, about 0.25" in from all the edges. A razor blade carefully slid around all sides between the layers separated the two pieces below.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7512131433232276954.jpg"></p>The clear protector looking front plastic (UV?) was hard to photograph.<p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/36801433232339200.jpg"></p>A closer inspection shows another layer glued to the lens I want.                     <p>It depends :) In this case it depends on the type of glass you use. Light, as in, visible light will not pose a serious threat on a thin pane of clear glass when it's about heat, anything that does absorb heat does though, and could kill your glass pane. Making sure your glass is clean is a must. Logically, the more dirt (smoke -&gt; carbon) on your glass pane, the more it absorbs heat. There's also reflection to take into account. If you focus a beam of sunlight, the more lenses/glass/transparent material you use to guide your beam, the more gets reflected, and basically is lost to heat.&nbsp;</p><p>Then there is the absorption of heat by glass itself, something about the infrared in the spectrum being either reflected or absorbed, you may want to read on that, or you may lose significant energy just but adding a layer of glass.<br></p><p>Instead of a glass layer ( =energy loss anyhow), you could place some fans that blow or suck away the smoke.</p><p>I hope you can pull this off, it certainly would be fun :) Post some pictures when you're at it.&nbsp;</p><p>Last thought is, the first collection of sunlight can be done with a reflective hollow mirror, perhaps something like a parabolic dish.
