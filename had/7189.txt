                <div class="section section-description">
                    <h5>Description</h5>
                    <div class="description">So this is yet another data logger for model rockets. The hardware consists of an Arduino, which uses a MPU6050 as an accelerometer/gyroscope, plus a BMP180 for altitude measurements.<br />The data is logged to an SD card. A radio module is used as a beacon on the UHF band, to allow quick recovery of the payload. I plan to use it as a telemetry module later on.</div>
                </div>
            

            
                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p><strong>Electronics/Hardware</strong><br></p><p>An ATMega328 microcontroller, with Arduino software, handles the communication between the different devices:</p><ul><li>MPU6050 Accelerometer</li><li>BMP180 Altimeter</li><li>SD Card Reader</li><li>Radio Transmitter</li></ul><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/7013101439500243571.jpg"></p><p>Not represented in the above diagram are the two LEDs: one for power check, and the other for status. <br></p><p>I chose every module with the size in mind, as everything has to fit in a 32mm diameter rocket tube. A 3D printed structure holds everything in place. </p><p>Everything is powered through a 450mAh, 7.4V LiPo battery. It may seem a tad oversized, but I want the transmitter to emit bursts as long as possible!<br></p><p><strong>Software</strong></p><p>The microcontroller is an ATMega328, running Arduino, for the sake of simplicity. The program has three main &quot;states&quot;</p><ul><li>Monitoring: it runs continuously, checking for a rise in acceleration indicating takeoff. The radio module continuously emits a tone to allow some fine tuning of the receiver.</li><li>Take-off/Coasting Phase: the data is logged from the accelerometer along with the altimeter into the SD card as fast as the ATMega can do. A timestamp is added. </li><li>Parachute Deployment: logging continues, the transmitter turns on and emits a continuous pulse.</li><li>Landing/Beacon: after a fixed delay (about 1min), the transmitter transmits short bursts and will continue to do so as long as it stays on. </li></ul>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                            <p><p>I ran into a small problem: the Arduino module is too big, and I can&apos;t fit the SD card reader inside!</p><p>So back to the drawing board!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/9213841439503918751.JPG"></p><p>I chose a smaller Arduino Mini board stacked on top of the SD reader. I had to chop some plastic off the support I printed to allow the slot to be exposed. The programming header is also broken out, to allow future debugging/reprogramming, without having to take the whole thing out of the rocket. </p>

                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7189/log/22708-printed-the-structure-microcontroller-in">Printed the structure, microcontroller in!</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7189/log/22708#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>The actual holder is pretty simple: a V-shaped structure hold the battery in-between the electronics. </p><p>One slot is used for the USB ports (for programming purposes), one switch and two LEDs (power and status) are also planned.<br></p><p>Another disc is snapped in at the bottom.<br></p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/706741439496727036.png"></p><p>Didn&apos;t take any pics of the printing in progress, but here it is after I started to fit the microcontroller.</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/6842331439497001333.JPG"></p><p>The altimeter is also in, along with the radio module!</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/5075861439497225034.JPG"></p><p>So far so good, the whole thing fits perfectly in the body tube, along with the batteries! </p><p>I still have to put in the SD reader and the step-down regulator.<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/7189/log/22706-challenges">Challenges</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">4 days ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/7189/log/22706#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>All right, so the first things I had to think about were the following:</p><ul><li>What do I want to measure?</li><li>How big do I want it to make (for what class of rocket?)</li><li>For what price?</li></ul><p>For the sensors, I settled for three measurements: </p><ul><li>Acceleration</li><li>Temperature</li><li>Height (calculated from the atmospheric pressure)</li></ul><p>The acceleration will be measured by a MPU6050, pressure and temperature by a BMP180.</p><p>I&apos;ll throw in an Arduino for the processing, a SD card reader for logging purposes, and a small radio module as a beacon (and maybe later telemetry...)<br></p><p>I want the whole thing to fit in a small-sized model rocket (C or D class engine). I settled for a BT-55 size, which uses a 32mm inner diameter tube. I&apos;d like the assembly to be able to slide in and out of the tube easily. </p><p>My initial budget will be around 20&#x20AC;, we&apos;ll see later on the total cost... I&apos;ll mainly use chinese breakout boards, to reduce the costs as much as possible...<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
