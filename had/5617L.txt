                            <h2><a href="/project/5617/log/18826-thoughts-on-hackadaycom-blog-feedback">Thoughts on Hackaday.com blog feedback</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                                <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5617/log/18826#discussion-list">
                                    0 comments
                                </a>
                                </small>
                            <div style="position:relative">
                                <div id="post-body-18826"><p>So <a data-mention-user-id="7" href="/hacker/7">@Benchoff </a>posted a well written <a href="http://hackaday.com/2015/05/31/hackaday-prize-entry-a-braille-computer/">article</a> about the Braille computer and the matching keyboard, articulating my ideas much better than I could myself. After the initial panic of seeing such a messy and early version of my project being seen by  people who's work I've been following for years, I started reading the feedback and its been really helpful. To clarify, my design has 60 servos placed along the x axis, which is pulled along the y axis by another servo, like a photocopier or desktop scanner in movement.</p><p>Here are some of the ideas and my thoughts on how I should progress. </p><p>I had planned of using SG90 Tower Pro servos as they are cheap and everyone uses them, until <a data-mention-user-id="61529" href="/hacker/61529">@Robert Guyser</a><a href="https://hackaday.io/Noirwhal"></a> showed me <a href="http://www.amazon.com/50pcs-totally-stepper-Assorted-Plastic/dp/B00NNM66GO/ref=sr_1_2?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1433186127&amp;sr=8-2&amp;keywords=miniature+stepper">these</a>. They are camera lens motors, and some are just 6mm in diameter and light at less than $1 each. I wont have to worry about clumsy mechanisms in order to reach the dots.</p><p>Electromagnetic displays were mentioned, but i have no idea how to deal with cross chatter at such a fine pitch. Single line displays use a bulky servo array.<br></p><p>A few people mentioned the pin art toys (block of metal pins people in the 90s used to copy their faces) if the pins were ridged a system similar to a zip tie could hold them in place, with a global per line reset.</p><p>[Rob] suggested electric stimulation in place of dots, don't think i would like to use it during a thunderstorm :)</p><p>[W] posted this...</p><p><em>"If your goal is a simple/cheap output device, why not ignore Braille entirely? Since people can use a chorded keyboard for input, why not use a chorded output? Think of 8 pins/buttons in the same pattern as the chorded keyboard, each lifted by a solenoid. Use the exact same patterns that you type on the chorded keyboard with.</em></p><p><em>You'd need a rate control, but that could be handled by a foot pedal or something. I agree it's nowhere near as convenient to use compared to a printed braille page, but it should be an order of magnitude cheaper than a braille printer and therefore more widely accessible.</em></p><p><em>If you were mechanically super-clever, the output device could also act as the keyboard."</em></p><p>This last one is quite interesting. Beyond the scope of this project, but it has loads of potential for mobile.</p><p>Thank you to everyone who left feedback, it was all appreciated :)<br></p><p><br><a data-mention-user-id="7" href="/hacker/7"></a></p></div>
                            </div>
