                    <div class="description">A solar sinter and crusher to convert desert sand to construction sand.</div>

                <div class="section section-details">
                    <h5>
                        
                            Details
                        
                    </h5>
                    

                    
                        <div class="post-content details-content">
                            <p><p>Construction uses river sand or sand from crushed rocks, both of which are cubic (or at least angular) and free from contaminants, but they are in short supply and people are being <a href="http://www.wired.com/2015/03/illegal-sand-mining/">murdered</a> over it. Sea sand is also angular, but can react with other chemicals resulting in dangerous buildings. Sea sand can be dredged in order to extend coastlines, but this causes environmental damage. Desert sand is round and does not bind well, resulting in <a href="http://www.quora.com/Why-is-desert-sand-not-used-for-construction">weak concrete</a>. <a href="http://www.scmp.com/news/hong-kong/article/1277483/hku-study-finds-glass-can-replace-river-sand-building-needs">Recycled glass</a> could be used, but needs preparing and is expensive.</p><p>Which leads me back to desert sand.</p><p>A few years ago Markus Kayser created the <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ptUj8JRAYu8">Solar Sinter Project</a>, which used desert sand and focused sunlight to 3d print objects. This same technique could be used to convert desert sand in to rocks, that can crushed in to construction sand, the same way rocks are already and glass can be.</p><p>To calibrate this project, I set up <a data-mention-project-id="5537" href="/project/5537">#Measuring Extreme Temperatures</a> <br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </div>

                            <h2><a href="/project/5230/log/18840-some-maths">Some Maths</a></h2>
                            <small class="authorship">3 months ago  &bull;
                            <a class="gray-link" href="/project/5230/log/18840#discussion-list">
                                0 comments
                            </a>
                            </small>
                            <p><p>So according to wikipedia "The reflectivity of bright aluminum foil is 88% while dull embossed foil is about 80%", this is far better than I thought. Markus Kaysers <strong></strong>SolarSinter Project, appeared to use 4 lenses of about 6ft x 4ft total area, while I doubt these are focusing all available light, I am going to assume 100% efficiency for his design, as any increase of power for me will be a bonus. </p><p>So I will assume that Markus design is 120cm x 180cm for a total of 21600cm^2 of light collecting area. At 80% efficiency i will need 27000cm^2 of light collecting area, or more if i need to.</p><p>So I plan on using a 200cm x 200cm square reflector, and loosing 20% for the focusing mirror, so 40000cm2 - 20% = 32000cm2 light collecting area.</p><p>Assuming Markus design is running at 100% and my own is 80%, this should give me at least 18.5% more power.<br></p>
                                
                            </p>
                        </li>
                    
                        <li>
                            <h2><a href="/project/5230/log/18542-total-re-think">Total Re-Think</a></h2>

                            </small>
                            <p><p>The lens I bought was awful. Its focal point was too close to the surface, so I couldn't use multiples along side, and large lenses were prohibitively expensive. This has to be repeatable, and rear projection tv's never really took off in the uk, and i suspect not in the target countries either. So plan B......</p><p><img src="https://cdn.hackaday.io/images/4002841432759402478.jpg"></p><p>(PS, I'm not 5, that just my handwriting)</p><p>So the idea is to use conventional foil to make a parabolic mirror in the style of a reflector telescope. The cost benefit should outweigh the loss of light and this could be made from a light wood frame , some papier-mache and a conventional mirror (and math of course)</p>
                                
                            </p>
